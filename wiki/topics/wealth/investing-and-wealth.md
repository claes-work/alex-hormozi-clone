# Investing & Wealth Philosophy

Alex Hormozi's synthesized view of how wealth is built and how it should be held.
Two distinct layers run through his thinking: **building** wealth (active income, the
business/leverage games, proximity to wealthier people) and **holding** it (buy-and-hold
indexing, keeping passive money passive, walling off a small speculation slice). Almost
every figure he uses is self-reported or napkin math cited from memory — flagged below.

## Buy-and-hold vs. active trading

Hormozi's holding-side thesis is blunt: do less, own the broad market, and don't touch it.
He frames this with a study he cites **from memory (self-reported, attribution uncertain —
he isn't sure if it was Fidelity or Charles Schwab)**: the best-performing brokerage accounts
belonged to investors who had *died*, and the second-best to investors who had *forgotten
their passwords* — inactivity beat trading in both cases [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]].

> "the first and highest performing portfolio activity were people who had purchased stocks and then died." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

> "the people who do the least amount have the most money in the account." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

He grounds the argument in his own confessed loss: **~$200,000 (title says $217,000) on a
short-term trade in 2021 (self-reported)**. He sold a position with gains, it dropped (so he
felt smart), then it climbed back to exactly his sell price — leaving him worse off, because
the within-a-year sale now owed ordinary-income tax [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]].

> "if i had literally just done nothing then i would have more money." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

Two supporting arguments:

- **Tax drag.** Short-term trading throws out favorable long-term capital-gains treatment and
  makes gains taxable as ordinary income (**he uses ~43-45%, cited from memory — self-reported,
  not verified**). His napkin math: a trader would have to turn $100 into **roughly $245** just
  to net the same **~$180** a buy-and-hold investor keeps after long-term capital gains — the
  trader's growth has to be ~45% higher just to break even against holding [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]].

  > "in order for me to to just match that not even beat it i would have to take my hundred dollars and turn it into 245." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

- **You're outgunned.** Retail traders compete against large quantitative firms with hundreds of
  analysts whose business is beating you; **~75% of people who try will lose money (self-reported)** [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]].

  > "there's huge quantitative firms that have hundreds of analysts who are trying to beat you and they make their money doing that." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

The behavioral prescription is to remove yourself from the loop:

> "maybe get a hobby and just let the money grow and pretend you're a dead person." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

> "if you can't pretend you're a dead person then throw away your password or give it to somebody that you would be ashamed to ask for your password back so that you just don't touch your stuff." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

## Play-money allocation (3-5%)

Hormozi doesn't claim to have conquered the trading itch — he quarantines it. **Self-reported
(2021): 3-5% of his net worth (he also says just 3%) is "investable assets" he plays with to
scratch an entrepreneurial/speculative itch; 95-97% sits in "really really stable stuff"**
[[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]. The speculation slice is explicitly framed as
damage control, not strategy:

> "put three percent aside for pure speculation which is basically just saying i'm a human being and i'm an idiot and this is how i can manage my idiocy." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

> "97 of my worth is in really really stable stuff." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

He notes — with self-deprecation — that even his own record confirms the thesis:

> "candidly all my passive stuff has outperformed my active stuff which is hilarious." [[../../sources/2021-05-19-yt-c5Lw12PXkgU]]

## Keep passive passive, keep active active

By 2026 the same principle appears as a portfolio-hygiene rule rather than a trading confession.
Passive holdings should stay genuinely passive; active energy belongs in the business. Real
estate and similar are fine **only as long as you're not actively running them** — he favors
REITs and funds with good partners, because buying another building or adding to the S&P
"changes nothing" about his day [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]].

> "Keep passive stuff passive. Don't make it active." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

> "Just let the real estate be the real estate, let the business be the business, and just keep them apart." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

A holding is only a **distraction when it changes what you do day-to-day** — when you start
checking it constantly and it eats your days. And critically, turning passive money active to
squeeze marginal returns is a bad trade: spending your time to earn ~5% better on passive
holdings is worse than deploying that same time into your active income, which yields far more
[[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]. This is the same buy-and-hold logic from 2021,
now stated as a time-allocation argument rather than a tax one — the two positions are
consistent across five years.

## Wealth = what you own, not what you do

The wealth-*building* side is a different game entirely from holding. Hormozi frames money-making
as a set of learnable "games," and the highest-leverage games are the ones where the product has
**zero cost of replication** — media, software, licensing — because leverage is baked into the
model [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]].

> "Most products that have zero cost of replication tend to have more leverage — so like media as an example, or software, even licensing stuff." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

> "You work just as hard to build the restaurant or the dry cleaning business as you do to build the software company. You just need different knowledge." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

He identifies **private equity as the single highest concentration of billionaires** and reasons
backward from that anomaly to its **arbitrage-stacking**: (1) buying businesses at a steep
discount to intrinsic value, (2) third-party/bank debt leverage, and (3) further leverage from
raising outside capital. Combined, these compound into 100x outcomes over a 5-to-7-year span with
very little of your own capital [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]].

> "There's multiple levels of arbitrage within private equity, and when you combine them together, that's when you can create these 100x outcomes." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

He contrasts this with real estate — which has **made more millionaires than anything but is a
far more efficient market**, so it rarely produces 100x — and notes the risk asymmetry both ways:

> "A business can go to zero. Buildings don't really go to zero." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

On the traits that build wealth in the first place (**self-reported blueprint**): focus over
opportunism (one avatar, one product, one channel — "say no to all the other Mistresses"), the
sequential skill stack of advertising → selling → product, and consistency formalized as the
**Rule of 100** — 100 primary actions/day (100 minutes of content, OR 100 reach-outs, OR $100
ad spend) for 100 days [[../../sources/2024-03-31-yt-FcUDVh6hr_o]]. The wealth angle of the Rule
of 100: consistent active output is what actually moves someone from six to seven figures — the
income engine that funds the passive holdings above.

> "I use something that I call the Rule of 100... 100 per day, and you do that for 100 days, and I promise you you'll be making six figures. Most people won't." [[../../sources/2024-03-31-yt-FcUDVh6hr_o]]

He diagnoses the main failure mode as fear and an unwillingness to sacrifice, not missing skill:

> "they can't make the short-term sacrifice for the long-term achievement." [[../../sources/2024-03-31-yt-FcUDVh6hr_o]]

## Proximity to wealth

Hormozi's **#1 self-reported lesson from the wealthy** is not a tactic but a positioning move:
consistently get around people who make more money than you, to watch what they pursue and,
just as importantly, what they say no to. He credits this — not any single pivotal event — with
changing his trajectory, and says it hasn't changed across his career [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]].

> "The most valuable thing for me was to get around people who are making more money than me, and that's just — and that hasn't really changed." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

> "I've always tried to get around people who make more money than me, just to see what they're thinking, what opportunities are they pursuing, what things do they say no to." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

Proximity is also how he learned the PE game in the first place — he saw wealthy people playing
it, flagged it as an anomaly, and went to study its rules [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]].

> "I learned about the private equity game. I was like, oh wow, the largest percentage of billionaires come from private equity. Weird. Okay, noted." [[../../sources/2024-04-07-yt-aNyuMP4jCWY]]

The same theme surfaces on the hiring side in 2026: scaling is **"the who game,"** with escalating
levels of talent, and "the best talent is always in the future" — the people who can grow your
wealth for you are ahead of you, not behind [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]].

> "The best talent's always in the future. The best people are always ahead of you, not behind you." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

## Trade-offs and finitude

Hormozi treats wealth decisions as trade-offs with no free lunch — the framing that ties the
holding and building sides together. Dissatisfaction, he argues, comes from wanting both sides
of a trade at once; the only levers are to want less or trade more [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]].

> "Either want less or trade more." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

> "If you change nothing, nothing will change." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

When choosing what to sacrifice, rank by recoverability: **short-term profit can be made back;
family time cannot**. He'll take a short-term profitability hit to bring in high-level talent,
but won't trade time that doesn't come back [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]].

> "You won't make up family time in the long term. You can make the profit up in the long term." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

The finitude argument underwrites the whole philosophy of focus and not-touching. **Self-reported:
he figures he has ~30-40 productive years left — only "four or five big runs"** — and invokes
Warren Buffett's 20-punch-card idea: if entrepreneurs treated opportunities as a limited punch
card, they'd choose far better. The cost of doing one thing big is every exciting new thing you
give up to do it [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]].

> "The cost of the big thing is all the new stuff you have to give up that you don't get to pursue." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]

Regret, in his view, is usually an illusion — imagining an upside without the cost you'd have paid
to get it — which is why he's comfortable saying no to opportunities the punch-card logic rules out
[[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]].

> "I think regrets come when we imagine the upside that we don't have without taking into account the cost that we didn't suffer." [[../../sources/2026-07-01-yt-88oj6ZlmEi8]]
