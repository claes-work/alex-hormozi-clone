# Building Wealth

Alex Hormozi's wealth-creation taxonomy: the **four paths to "mega money,"** the
**cash-flow-vs-net-worth** lens that ranks those paths, and the sequence he personally
took through them. The through-line: wealth comes from owning multiplied assets, not
from accumulating taxed income, and you pick one path and "play it for a decade" rather
than chase shortcuts.

Related: [[investing-and-wealth]] · [[../business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]]

> "Poor people stay poor because they want a fast way to get rich and instead the
> richest people that I know pick one of these four paths, play it for a decade and then
> end up with more money than everyone else that is just chasing for shortcuts."
> — [[../../sources/2026-06-04-yt-sL16tsGafcQ]]

## The 4 paths

Source: [[../../sources/2026-06-04-yt-sL16tsGafcQ]] (solo video, all Alex-attributed).

He frames wealth creation as a **2x2** on two axes:

- **Whose money** you use — yours vs. other people's
- **Whose business** you operate — yours vs. other people's

|                        | **Your business**       | **Other people's business** |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------- | --------------------------- |
| **Your money**         | 1. Bootstrapping        | 3. Investing                |
| **Other people's money** | 2. Raising capital    | 4. Fund management          |

Proof points (from the then-current Forbes top 11, paraphrase): Musk, Ellison,
Zuckerberg, Bezos, Page, Brin, and Jensen Huang **raised capital**; Ballmer (Microsoft),
Michael Dell, and the Waltons **bootstrapped**; Warren Buffett is **investing**. Fund
management doesn't crack the top 11 but appears a few spots deeper.

### Path 1 — Bootstrapping (your money + your business)
Fund the business from your own savings and cash flow, reinvest profit, take no outside
investors. Typical for low-cost-to-start businesses (services, agencies, home services,
education, e-comm, local, increasingly software). It is the **slowest** path but you keep
control, equity, and the choice of pace and exit (or never exit). Because money isn't
available to solve problems, you accrue more *non-cash* debt — management, leadership,
technical, data debt — and the capital constraint limits which opportunities you can
pursue. He recommends it for a **first** business so you "pay off ignorance" without
risking other people's money:

> "The main reason is just that you want to pay off ignorance. That the last thing you
> want to do is take your, you know, your friends and family's money and then lose it cuz
> you don't know what you're doing."

### Path 2 — Raising capital (other people's money + your business)
You run the company but sell equity slices to fund fast growth. Fits businesses with huge
upfront costs, later margin expansion, or winner-take-all / network-effect dynamics (tech
platforms, marketplaces, pharma). It lets you hire top talent, outspend competitors, run
negative acquisition costs, and chase rarer opportunities where fewer well-funded rivals
compete. Trade-offs: you now serve **two customers**, you dilute, and you can lose control
via board seats / liquidation preferences / ratchets — you can be voted out of your own
company (he cites Steve Jobs). Venture money is "grand slam money"; most founders who take
losses end up with nothing (n=1).

> "You now have two customers instead of one... your customer is both the end customer and
> the investors or venture capitalists. And so, that's one element is that I have to serve
> two masters."

> "There is a sea of tombstones of failed ventures and founders who gave five, ten plus
> years of their life and pretty much worked a job, but with way more stress for a long
> period of time and then ended up having nothing to show for it."

Companion principle — **capital follows deals, not the reverse** (advice from a mentor):

> "There is no lack of capital in the world, only a lack of good deals. And so if you find
> a good deal, capital will appear."

Ability to raise ≈ founder track record × the size and believability of the opportunity.

### Path 3 — Investing (your money + other people's business)
Use actively-earned cash to buy pieces of other people's businesses — cash-flowing
companies, public stocks, real estate. You fund, you don't run. It offers diversification
but caps upside (the best investors are actually **concentrated**, not diversified — he
quotes Dale Carnegie's "put all your eggs in one basket and then watch the basket"). It is
the **easiest lifestyle** (no boss) but the slowest path, and almost no one *makes* their
initial money this way — they earn high active income first, then invest. Real estate is
the "#1 path to creating millionaires but not billionaires," and it needs a very long time
horizon. When a genuinely good deal appears, "you just want to back up the truck."

### Path 4 — Fund management (other people's money + other people's business)
Raise a pool of capital from **LPs** (limited partners) and invest it in other people's
businesses, often adding debt for leverage. The **highest-leverage** path with the
**smallest personal checks** — the **GP** (general partner) often ends up richer than any
single LP. Structure: a hurdle / pref rate (typically **6–8%**) plus a GP/LP profit split.
Risks: enormous responsibility, a very long feedback loop, and accountability to LPs,
regulators, entrepreneurs, and customers:

> "You can be rich on paper, but the entire time you almost feel like a slave, which
> sucks."

*Self-reported / third-party notes:* the Forbes ranking and mentor advice are external
references, not Alex's own financials; a "ratchet" anecdote in the source is attributed to
his partner **Sherwin**, not Alex, and Alex himself hedges the Bezos / Gates early-help
claims as uncertain.

## Cash flow vs. net worth

Source: [[../../sources/2026-02-22-yt-krcUBz7kiUI]] (solo teaching monologue,
Alex-attributed). This is the lens that explains **why** the ownership paths above beat
grinding for income.

Two businesses can be **identical** on paper — $10M top line, $2M bottom line — yet make
their owners dramatically differently rich. The variable is whether the owner is *required*
to work in it or merely *owns* it:

- **"Frustrated Fred"** (hands-on) nets $2M, pays ~50% tax, covers living costs, and adds
  roughly **$500K/year to net worth** — "a slow way to accumulate cash."
- **"Wealthy William"** (owner-independent) holds a business that trades at a **6x profit
  multiple** — 6 × $2M = **$12M** — so he is "way richer" on the same top and bottom line.

The asymmetry **compounds on incremental profit**: an extra $500K of profit gives Fred
~$250K after tax, but gives William another **~$3M** of net worth (6x), taking his $12M to
$15M. Building wealth off regular income is "very, very inefficient" — taxed to oblivion
with **zero multiplication**. He puts the gap between a sellable and an unsellable company
at roughly **20x** (a company that sells for 10x vs. one that can't sell).

> "This guy adds $500,000 to his net worth every year. This guy has a business that is
> worth $12 million. This guy's way richer."

> "It's very, very inefficient to become wealthy off of regular income because it's taxed
> to oblivion and there's zero multiplication that occurs on it. You don't get 10 years of
> work. You get one year divided by two after taxes."

> "This is the game of wealth. This is how you get wealthy."

**The sellable-business thesis:** the wealth move is converting Fred into William —
building a business that runs without you so it becomes a multipliable, sellable asset. The
operational path (time study → self-inventory → red/yellow/green delegation →
if-this-then-that decision rules with spending-authority caps) lives in
[[../business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]]. A complementary rule from his coaching keeps
the two worlds separate — "keep active active, keep passive passive": making passive
investments active only adds cost, and active income beats squeezing a few extra points out
of passive money ([[../../sources/2026-04-24-yt-0EqJD2o-Mnk]]).

### April 2026 Q&A refinements: money as time, skill stack as moat

In April 2026 Q&A clips, Hormozi describes money as "bottled time": the reason he likes
making money is that it can buy back decades of labor and expand the amount of help he can
provide, not because spending is the end state [[../../sources/2026-04-21-yt-XzwNu7j6X0o]]
[[../../sources/2026-04-20-yt-hULiVdSEynY]]. He also frames wealth as a three-part equation:
create value, negotiate for a share of it, and make the skill stack scarce enough that the
market must pay for it. Time is the weapon because scarcity compounds when few people can
do the same thing for long enough [[../../sources/2026-04-22-yt-CS--glCpAhk]].

His inheritance view remains equal-opportunity rather than equal-outcome: he says he does
not want to remove struggle by handing down unearned wealth, because the skill and character
created by earning matter more than the money itself [[../../sources/2026-04-23-yt-YNy1pdCAGs4]].

## His own path

Source: [[../../sources/2026-06-04-yt-sL16tsGafcQ]] (self-reported unless noted).

Hormozi says he has moved through **all four** paths, roughly in this order:

1. **Bootstrapping** — got rich bootstrapping: the first gym / Gym Launch → **Prestige
   Labs** (supplements) → software ("Allan/Allen," spelling uncertain from captions), all
   funded from cash flow.
2. **Investing** — compounded that cash into pieces of other companies via **acquisition.com**
   (Acquisition Ventures).
3. **Raising capital** — co-founded **Skool**, which is venture-backed (= raising capital).
4. **Fund management** — acts as a **GP** on large real-estate buildings through **ACQ real
   estate**.

His summarizing advice:

> "No president, no economy is going to make you rich. You have to do that for yourself."

> "Pick the path that's right for you, and may the odds be ever in your favor."

*These are self-reported figures and company relationships; none are registry-verified in
the underlying source.*
