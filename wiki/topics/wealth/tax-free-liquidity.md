# Tax-Free Liquidity & the Leverage Ladder

How Alex Hormozi explains "the ultra rich playbook": a ladder of four escalating
leverage strategies for building wealth, culminating in extracting **tax-free
liquidity** from an owned asset while retaining ownership and control. Reverse-engineered
throughout to a ~$10M net-worth goal for a normal earner.

Primary source: [[../../sources/2024-05-08-yt-6x3re5-Ms1E]] ("The Ultra Rich Playbook [Legal & Tax-Free]", 2024-05-08).
Earlier companion source (2022): [[../../sources/2022-01-27-yt-w5g0JiO7OdE]] ("I paid 0% tax on 56% of my income", 2022-01-27) — see the [2022 view](#the-2022-view-appreciation-not-shelters-pay-and-build) and the [position-evolution note](#-position-evolution-2022-pay-and-build-vs-2024-leverage-the-asset) below.

> ⚠️ **Not financial advice; self-reported, US-specific, illustrative.**
> Every tax claim and figure on this page is Alex Hormozi's own stated understanding,
> framed as US-specific and rounded for illustration (67% "highest federal," 37% income,
> 20% qualified dividends, ~9% market return, ~4–5x EBITDA lending, 50% loan-to-value on
> stock). He explicitly hedges some of it (e.g. he says he is "not sure" whether dividend
> recap proceeds are tax-free). None of it is verified against tax code or registries.
> Treat as opinion and illustration, not tax or investment guidance.

## The 4 leverage tiers

Hormozi frames wealth-building as four tiers ("V1–V4"), each adding leverage on top of
the last. Cited from [[../../sources/2024-05-08-yt-6x3re5-Ms1E]].

- **V1 — pure income (zero leverage).** Earn, pay tax, keep the rest. At his stated ~67%
  effective top rate, netting $1B would require ~$1.5B of income; netting $10M would
  require ~$15M of income. Brute force, no compounding.
- **V2 — income + passive investing.** Earn income, pay tax, reinvest the post-tax
  remainder into public equities (S&P 500 index) at ~9%. Example: $1M/yr income →
  ~$670K post-tax invested at 9% → ~$10M after ~10 years. Leverage of the market's
  compounding.
- **V3 — income + private equity (own the asset).** Own a business, take distributions,
  then sell it at an earnings multiple. He cites ~12x at ultra scale and ~10x for a
  ~$10M-revenue business throwing off ~$1.2M/yr; real small businesses (HVAC, dental,
  accounting firms) sell at ~10–14x. Leverage of a **multiple** on your earnings — you
  monetize the asset, not just its cash flow. See [[../business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]].
- **V4 — borrow against the asset (retain ownership + control).** Instead of selling and
  taking the taxable check, keep the income-producing asset and have a bank/institution
  front capital against it. This is where **tax-free liquidity** lives (next section).

> "Everyone has this big dream to sell a company and get the check, but what they don't
> tell you is that you then have a check and then you have to do something with the
> check." — Alex Hormozi

The V4 meta-strategy = **an income-producing asset + an understanding of debt.** All of
its routes use an institution to front capital while the owner retains ownership and
control. Related: [[building-wealth]], net-worth-philosophy (page not yet created).

## Buy-borrow-die & loans against assets

The core mechanic across V4 is "buy-borrow-die"-style: rather than sell (a taxable
event), you **borrow against** an appreciating, income-producing asset. Because a loan is
not income, the liquidity is untaxed — you pay interest and repay principal instead. Cited
from [[../../sources/2024-05-08-yt-6x3re5-Ms1E]].

**Loans against public equities (route C).** Take the company public, sell only a small
slice (~5–10%), and the retained shares gain a real-time, mark-to-market value. Banks will
lend up to ~50% of that stock value, disbursed piecemeal (he claims within ~24 hours).
Because it is a **loan, it is tax-free.**

> "This is exactly what Elon did when he went to go buy X — he just took a loan out of his
> wallet and then just bought it in cash." — Alex Hormozi

**Unrealized = untaxed.** Hormozi argues taxing unrealized gains is like taxing a paycheck
you haven't received yet:

> "It's like having someone pay income tax on a paycheck they haven't gotten yet. I feel
> like that's a little bit ridiculous." — Alex Hormozi

**The risk he flags (important).** He explicitly warns this is not free money — loans carry
covenants and must be repaid; if the underlying business/stock declines you can lose
everything:

> "There's risk that's not being appropriately valued when people say, oh, these rich
> people just take loans off their stuff." — Alex Hormozi

## ESOPs, dividend recaps & entity structures

Two more V4 routes let a private owner pull liquidity **before** or **without** going
public. Cited from [[../../sources/2024-05-08-yt-6x3re5-Ms1E]].

**ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan) — route A.** Sell a **minority** stake (33–49%) to
employees; a bank finances the employees, backstopped by the equity as collateral. Per
Hormozi's stated understanding, the payment to the owner is **tax-free under US tax law**
(self-reported). The owner keeps majority control; employees get equity without fronting
cash and are incentivized to "act like owners." The multiple is smaller than a strategic
sale because banks price for downside risk, not upside.

**Dividend recapitalization — route B.** Same bank-financed structure, but employees are
cut out: the owner borrows directly against the whole business as collateral (his analogy:
buying a house with a mortgage). A bank may lend ~4–5x EBITDA — e.g. $100M/yr earnings →
~$500M loan. The key structural point:

> "The key point here is that the business carries the debt, not you personally." — Alex Hormozi

The owner takes the cash now, the business services the debt over years, and the owner
still owns the business at the end. Hormozi **flags uncertainty** on the tax treatment here:
he is unsure whether dividend-recap proceeds are tax-free (unlike the ESOP and public-equity
loan, which he states are tax-free).

**Qualified dividends & entity/debt structures.** Passive distributions taken as **qualified
dividends** are taxed at ~20% rather than at ordinary income rates, so passive income keeps
more per dollar. The through-line across ESOPs, recaps, and public-equity loans is an
**entity/debt structure** in which a bank or institution fronts capital against the asset as
collateral, letting the owner extract cash while retaining ownership and control.

**Tax framing summary (self-reported, not advice):**

| Route | Liquidity mechanism | Tax treatment (per Hormozi) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A — ESOP | Bank finances employees buying 33–49% | Tax-free (self-reported) |
| B — Dividend recap | Business borrows ~4–5x EBITDA; business carries debt | Taxable — but he's "not sure" |
| C — Loan vs. public equity | Bank lends ~50% of retained stock value | Tax-free (it's a loan) |
| — Qualified dividends | Passive distributions | ~20% (below income rates) |

## Reverse-engineering to a net-worth goal

Hormozi reverse-engineers each tier to a ~$10M net-worth target so a normal earner can see
the path. Cited from [[../../sources/2024-05-08-yt-6x3re5-Ms1E]].

- **V1 (income only):** need ~$15M of gross income to net $10M at his ~67% top rate.
- **V2 (income + investing):** $1M/yr → ~$670K/yr post-tax invested at ~9% → ~$10M in ~10 years.
- **V3 (own + sell):** a ~$10M-revenue business throwing off ~$1.2M/yr, sold at ~10x → ~$10M+
  (less capital gains). See [[../business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]].
- **V4 (own + borrow):** hold that same asset and borrow against it for tax-free liquidity
  rather than selling.
- **A fourth path for non-owners:** join a company pre-exit as an employee/partner accruing
  equity (e.g. 0.2%/yr × 5 yrs = 1%); if it goes public at ~$1B, that's ~$10M in stock
  acquired at very low tax cost — which you can then **borrow against** like the ultra-rich
  (you can't run an ESOP as a non-owner, but you can do the loan-against-equity move).

**Preconditions he stresses.** You must first *be valuable* and generate income by solving
problems and learning skills (he cites outbound sales → ~$150K/yr); passive-investing content
is "seductive but useless" on a $35K income. Live below your means to create the Delta of
excess capital you can deploy. He also debunks copying Warren Buffett as a template (three
"fallacies": Buffett bought his first stock at age 7 just after Pearl Harbor; invested through
the greatest growth period in US history; and had luck on top of skill).

> "The single greatest and most valuable skill that a human being can have is the ability to
> come to their own conclusion and have the confidence to act on it despite it being contrary
> to what other people are doing." — Alex Hormozi

> "The funny thing about opportunity is they look like risk while they're opportunities, and
> the moment they're no longer risky is when the opportunity has closed." — Alex Hormozi

## The 2022 view: appreciation, not shelters ("pay and build")

Two years before the "Ultra Rich Playbook," Hormozi made the *same* foundational point —
**wealth is built from the untaxed appreciation of what you own, not from income** — but
wrapped in a very different headline stance: **skip the tax shelters and just pay your
taxes while you build.** Cited from [[../../sources/2022-01-27-yt-w5g0JiO7OdE]] (2022-01-27).

> ⚠️ **Not tax advice; self-reported, US-specific, illustrative (as of Jan 2022).** Every
> figure below is Hormozi's own stated understanding, rounded for a slide deck, unverified
> against tax code or registries. He jokingly "thanks the IRS" because his core advice is
> *pay your taxes*.

**The "0% tax on 56% of income" worked example.** His centerpiece demonstration that
equity appreciation compounds tax-free (all figures illustrative, "move the zeros"):

- Business earns $10M EBITDA, valued at 5x = **$50M**. Dividends taxed ~37% → ~$6.3M after-tax.
- Next year EBITDA grows 20% to $12M; still 5x → value rises to **$60M**. Dividends
  $12M × 63% = ~$7.6M after-tax.
- Net-worth increase = $10M (equity value gain) + $7.6M (after-tax dividend) = **$17.6M**.
- Pre-tax that increase was ~$22M, after-tax ~$17.6M → **effective tax ~21%**, because
  **~56% of the net-worth increase was tax-free** (the appreciation of what he owns).
- Leverage of the multiple: at a 40x public multiple, +$2M EBITDA = +$80M net worth.

> "56% of your net worth increase was tax-free. It was based on the appreciation of the
> equities of the things that you own rather than the income that you make. Which is why
> what you build builds your net worth more than what you make." — Alex Hormozi (2022-01-27)

This is the same insight that anchors the V3/V4 tiers above (monetize a *multiple* on the
asset, and treat unrealized/appreciated equity as untaxed) — just arrived at by paying
ordinary tax on dividends rather than by borrowing against the asset.

**Why he rejected shelters — three unaccounted costs.** Hormozi says most shelters "cost
more than they save" once you count (cited from [[../../sources/2022-01-27-yt-w5g0JiO7OdE]]):

1. **Discovery & conversion** — sifting swampland ("95% of solutions are swampland, 5%
   legit… 19 out of 20 are not legit"), then vetting, creating entities, transferring
   assets, re-contracting clients, new banking.
2. **Upkeep** — ongoing attention to stay compliant (his captive-insurance example:
   quarterly allocations, claim decisions).
3. **Unwinding & fines** — the IRS is "3 to 5 years behind"; when it closes a loophole it
   can claw back taxes + fines + interest for prior years, plus legal cost to unwind.

He frames the tax code as **an incentive system** ("penalties and incentives so they can
move the economy"), and stresses that **intent** in using a clause is what triggers
unwinding.

**Where he says real, legitimate savings actually come from (three scenarios).** Cited
from [[../../sources/2022-01-27-yt-w5g0JiO7OdE]]:

1. **Give up ownership or control** — charitable giving and (properly done) trusts: you no
   longer own the money, so you don't owe tax on it.
2. **Depreciation** — the code lets you depreciate purchased assets like losses. Example:
   buy a $10M building, depreciate ~$4M in a year to offset ~$4M of income → zero tax. He
   claims real-estate fund GPs capture a disproportionate share of depreciation ("which is
   how Grant Cardone doesn't pay taxes"). *(self-reported attribution)*
3. **Deferral over elimination** — many structures only defer tax (often until death)
   rather than eliminate it.

**Shelters he examined and *rejected* in 2022** (cited from
[[../../sources/2022-01-27-yt-w5g0JiO7OdE]]):

| Structure | What it is | Why he passed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **IDGT waterfall** (Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust) | A "waterfall of trusts," ~$5M per trust, 3-yr wait to touch the money (past the IRS unwinding statute) | Could have saved ~$6M, but not worth the worry, restrictions, and audit risk |
| **Captive insurance** | Owner-controlled insurance entity | Marginal savings, heavy upkeep; the relative who pitched him got audited |
| **Puerto Rico / NYC day-counting** | Residency-day arbitrage (friends flying 104 days/yr, counting NYC days) | Trades valuable *attention/time* for less-valuable money; one friend missed a child's birth "to get their days in" |

**Headline stance (2022):** *anti-shelter — "just pay your taxes and build."* Chasing a 30%
tax saving is "thinking in increments, not orders of magnitude"; better to 10x the business
"and then you'll never have to worry about the taxes again."

> "95% of solutions are swampland and 5% are legit… The short and simple answer is pay
> them." — Alex Hormozi (2022-01-27, paraphrasing Charlie Munger)

### ⚠️ Position evolution: 2022 "pay and build" vs. 2024 "leverage the asset"

> ⚠️ **DATED POSITION EVOLUTION — read both with their dates.**
>
> - **2022-01-27** ([[../../sources/2022-01-27-yt-w5g0JiO7OdE]]): headline stance is
>   *anti-shelter / "just pay your taxes and build."* Tax-minimization is framed as a
>   distraction from 10x growth; the win is untaxed **appreciation of owned equity**, and
>   you simply pay ordinary tax on dividends.
> - **2024-05-08** ([[../../sources/2024-05-08-yt-6x3re5-Ms1E]], the rest of this page):
>   describes an elaborate **borrow-against-assets** playbook (ESOPs, dividend recaps,
>   loans vs. public equity — "buy-borrow-die") to pull **tax-free liquidity** while
>   retaining ownership.
>
> These are **not a strict contradiction**: 2022 warns against *artificial* shelters, while
> 2024 describes *liquidity via debt against real, income-producing assets* — and both rest
> on the same core insight that appreciation of owned equity compounds untaxed. But the
> **framing and emphasis evolved** (2022 = skeptic / pay-and-build; 2024 = leverage-the-asset),
> so do not present either as his single "current" tax view without checking the later record.
