# Community Building (the Skool / paid-community playbook)

Alex Hormozi's playbook for designing, launching, monetizing, and growing a paid
(or free-to-paid) online community — built almost entirely around **Skool**, the
platform he co-runs and promotes. This page synthesizes a cluster of 2024–2025
videos from the @TheSkoolSchool channel. Because Hormozi is an owner/promoter of
Skool, much of this material is **self-interested promotional content**; platform
statistics and success stories cited below are flagged as self-reported and are
not independently verified. The reusable *method*, however, is a complete and
coherent community-building system.

## Why the model (unit economics, retention, low-capital, endurance)

Hormozi positions the paid community as, in his framing, "the easiest business to
start today" and "the simplest business model in the entire world."

- **Radically simple funnel.** The whole business reduces to three steps: get
  traffic → send it to an "about" page → have a community people like and tell
  their friends about → "and then you make money. That's the business."
  ([[../../sources/2025-03-29-yt-hdawvvYqmYo]], verbatim)
- **Low capital, low skill, no prerequisites.** He argues it needs no startup
  capital (can start free), no inventory (no supply chain / drop-shipping), and
  no employees; and that it beats the three standard objections — "I don't know
  what to sell, I don't have an audience, and I'm not an expert."
  ([[../../sources/2025-01-18-yt-J7DWZsKr3bI]], paraphrase) He calls it the
  business that takes the *least time* ("make money in hours") and the *least
  skill* ("if you can invite people to a party you can make this business work").
  ([[../../sources/2024-12-18-yt-V5hkdejbybQ]], [[../../sources/2025-02-22-yt-_Nz_0qxIyok]])
- **All-in-one, zero external tooling.** "You don't need any external plugins,
  tools, resources. It's all here and it's built to work seamlessly together" —
  the platform hosts the community, the sales (about) page, and the traffic CTA,
  removing the usual funnel-software stack. ([[../../sources/2025-03-29-yt-hdawvvYqmYo]],
  verbatim; promotional)
- **Recurring revenue.** Pricing is monthly and recurring — "you charge a ticket
  for entry… and that's recurring monthly." ([[../../sources/2025-03-27-yt-C6d4BEyYszo]],
  verbatim) This ties the model to his continuity/recurring-revenue thinking in
  [[../business/money-model]].
- **Retention comes from product quality, not lock-in.** The retention thesis is
  simply: make the community "awesome relative to what you charge" and members
  stay and refer. Quality *is* the retention mechanism.
  ([[../../sources/2025-03-29-yt-hdawvvYqmYo]], verbatim)
- **Endurance economics — staying in business is the win.** "You don't win at
  business. The point of business is to keep the business going. It's to stay in
  business." Growth compounds from small monthly wins; "the only way to actually
  lose the games is to quit." ([[../../sources/2025-03-29-yt-hdawvvYqmYo]], verbatim)
  He extends this to the 12-month horizon elsewhere: most people quit within 12
  days, but "if you can wait 12 months, you can almost guarantee success."
  ([[../../sources/2025-04-18-yt-dY1Mp1LVO0o]], verbatim)

> ⚠️ **Self-reported / promotional stats (do NOT treat as verified):** across the
> VSLs he repeatedly cites "54.1% of people who start a paid community on Skool
> make money" and an "average paid-community owner earns $1,360/month," plus "tens
> of thousands of groups." ([[../../sources/2024-12-18-yt-V5hkdejbybQ]],
> [[../../sources/2025-02-22-yt-_Nz_0qxIyok]], [[../../sources/2025-01-18-yt-J7DWZsKr3bI]],

## Design (who it's for, free vs. paid, what's inside)

The design sequence from [[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]], also previewed in
[[../../sources/2025-02-15-yt-RmzMZGkDHCI]]: decide **who it's for → free or paid →
what goes inside**.

### 1. Who is it for?

- Don't overcomplicate the niche. Target people who share your interest and have
  the same goal or same problem. "The first question you ask is who is it for?"
  (verbatim)
- Easiest lens: **"scratch your own itch"** — build around a problem you
  personally want solved (day trading, house flipping, painting, fixing cars).
  "An easy way to think about this is scratching your own itch." (verbatim)
- **You do not have to be the expert.** Two paths: the *expert path* (you already
  know the skill, teach it) or the *crowdsourced path* (gather interested people
  and let the community find the answers). "The cool thing is is that you don't
  have to be the expert." (verbatim) — see the Wikipedia analogy below.

### 2. Free vs. paid

- If just starting: **start free.** Get people in, learn what they like, learn to
  actually run a community, then flip to paid once confident. The decision is
  reversible (switch in month 2, 3, or 6). If already confident you can deliver
  value, you can launch paid from day one. (paraphrase)
- Rationale: the audience is "the most valuable asset you have and that you will
  have over time" (verbatim); a paid community launched on top of a lively,
  contributor-rich free base "skyrockets."
- **Nightclub / party analogy:** clubs let people in free early, then charge once
  the party is full. "It's kind of like showing up to an empty party versus
  showing up to a party that's already rolling." (verbatim) The recurring **party
  analogy** runs through the whole corpus (see Recurring analogies).

### 3. What goes inside — the three buckets

All from [[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]] (paraphrase unless quoted):

- **Access** — access to *you* (Q&A calls; your time becomes scarce/valuable when
  100 people want it) AND access to outside guests the members couldn't reach
  alone.
- **Content** — because the community is closed/exclusive, you can post material
  you don't make public, created by you *or* by the members.
- **Events** — digital or in person, small or large (a group hike up to a big
  digital conference).
- Wrap these in a **schedule / cadence**. The lists aren't exhaustive — "do
  anything you can imagine that provides value." Source what to build by asking
  yourself "What would be epic?" and by asking members "What do you think would
  be cool?"

### What makes it *valuable* — the Value Equation

The three buckets are only valuable insofar as they improve the **Value Equation**
from *$100M Offers* (see [[../business/attraction-offers]]). The "who" work aligns
everyone on the same dream outcome; the other three levers determine value:
lower **risk** / perceived likelihood (e.g. teach members how to *not* blow up
their car), **faster** (compress a result from a year to 3 months), and **easier**
(don't make them stop what they love or start what they hate). For every piece of
access/content/events, ask: "How can I make it less risky for my audience? How can
I make it faster? And how can I make it easier?" (verbatim,
[[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]])

## Launch (how many members, and how to get the first ones)

From [[../../sources/2025-03-26-yt-ogMxa_2DWZI]] unless noted.

- **You need far less audience than you think.** "All good communities start
  small." Massive communities are the *hardest* to keep valuable — "there's so
  much chaos going on inside." (verbatim)
- **The threshold is 10 true regulars.** "All you need is 10 true regulars. It's
  10 people who contribute regularly to a group." (verbatim) His model example:
  a 13-person powerlifting group where everyone posted daily — "I looked forward
  to seeing the post every single day. And I would have paid."
  > ⚠️ Self-reported: the "10 true regulars" value threshold is attributed to
  > Skool platform stats — unverified.
- **Start with 3 paying members.** "Start with three… because three is on the way
  to 10." (verbatim) Three is the achievable first milestone that proves the model.
- **1,000 True Fans / niche stacking.** Invokes Kevin Kelly's *1,000 True Fans*
  (Alex says "A Thousand True Fans" — same essay): "You only need a thousand
  people." Against 8 billion you can afford to be extremely specific. **Stack
  interests to niche down** — "a vegan mom powerlifter who loves cats" has another
  thousand of the same; cross-pollinating interests narrows the audience but
  raises affinity. Joe Rogan example: JRE sits at the intersection of aliens,
  fitness, conspiracy, fighting, comedy; the more topics a fan shares, the harder
  they bond. (paraphrase + verbatim)
- **First tactic — text your phone contacts.** "You have 400 people or 800 people
  that you know, just text them and invite them to your community." Claim
  (self-reported): invite everyone in your list and "I promise you you'll get
  three. You'll probably get more than 10." The blocker is emotional: "It's not
  because you don't know how. It's because you're afraid… all we're doing is
  inviting people to a party." (verbatim)

## Monetization (pricing, grandfathering ethics, dual model, price × N)

From [[../../sources/2025-03-27-yt-C6d4BEyYszo]] unless noted. Hormozi frames "four
life paths": (1) stay free forever (valid, but not monetization), (2) start free
→ flip to paid, (3) run free + a separate paid community, (4) start paid from day
one. Paths 2–4 are the money-making strategies.

### Pricing

- **Market range: $19–$299/month.** "The majority of Skool groups price between
  $19 and $299/month" — a deliberately wide band you can exceed either way.
  (self-reported platform stat — flag)
- **Price by value discrepancy, not a fixed number.** Price is dictated by the gap
  between what you charge and what the member earns/saves. Digital marketing for
  agency owners doing $20k+/mo justifies a high price (big upside gap); teaching
  parents to watercolor with their kids justifies a low price (small budget, free
  alternatives online). (paraphrase)
- **Reverse-engineer N from a revenue goal.** Pick goal, pick price, solve for the
  number of members (N). Worked example on a **$10,000/month** goal (his stated
  "most common goal" new people bring): ~10 members at ~$1,000, ~100 at ~$100, or
  ~1,000 at ~$10 all hit it; the right mix depends on who you serve. "Once you
  know the price… all you have to do is reverse engineer the N." Then **break it
  into a daily quota** (~3 new members/day to add 100 in a month) — "and then
  guess what, you can increase your goal." (verbatim + paraphrase)
  > Caption note in source: exact price points were garbled in the auto-captions;
  > the round figures above are approximations, not verbatim numbers.

### Grandfathering ethics (free → paid flip)

- **You must grandfather free members in for free.** "You can't start everyone for
  free and then switch to paid and then all of them are getting billed — that
  would be unethical." "If you switch to paid, everyone who's free will be
  grandfathered in free." (verbatim)
- **Urgency pro-tips when inviting during the free phase:** (a) tell people up
  front the group *will* become paid — this creates urgency and a "big reason why"
  to join now; (b) tell them they keep the free price forever *as long as they
  stay in* — which incentivizes both joining and staying engaged. Late arrivals
  "join the party late after the party is already booming, and then you charge a
  ticket for entry — and that's recurring monthly." (verbatim + paraphrase)

### Dual free + paid model

- Keep the original free group intact and run a **separate paid community**
  alongside it with additional access/events/content. Conversion insight: "the
  best way to get people to want to join this one is to make this one awesome" —
  i.e., make the *free* community excellent so members level up into the paid one.
  (verbatim) This funnel logic echoes attraction-offer thinking in
  [[../business/attraction-offers]].

### Give-value-free, monetize the next problem

A distinct but complementary monetization philosophy from
[[../../sources/2025-04-18-yt-dY1Mp1LVO0o]]:

- "You got to give your best stuff and you got to keep giving it." Solve one
  problem free so people pay for the next; "when you can solve 10 problems, you
  give nine away for free and upsell the most valuable one." (verbatim)
- **Reject tit-for-tat:** "Help a lot of people and some of them will pay you and
  some of them won't. And that's okay." (verbatim)
- A single-problem business can be **chunked** into sellable sub-steps (selling a
  house → fix-its, cleaning, staging, landscaping, pricing, sales process); give
  some away, charge to finish.

## Paid-traffic economics & the free→paid conversion system (2026 MoreMozi Q&A)

From [[../../sources/2026-02-25-yt-OO1ACTnUh9U]] (all Alex-attributed, **paraphrase**
unless quoted; dated **2026-02-25**). This is the operational, benchmarked version of the
dual free+paid model above — the numbers and the conversion machinery are new here.

### Diagnose "my ads stopped working" as audience fatigue, not the offer

- **Ads cycling on and off is normal, not a failure signal.** "Ads start working then they
  stop working and they start working and they stop working" — it happens to lots of
  advertisers and is not by itself evidence something is broken. (verbatim)
- **Don't blame a price change you can't isolate.** Without knowing the sample size (how
  many people actually saw the offer), there's no evidence a price change caused the drop.
  Here the operator moved the price *back down* and ads still didn't recover — which
  suggests price was **not** the deciding factor. (paraphrase)
- **The real culprit is warm-pool exhaustion.** "Facebook probably starts by focusing on
  people who know who you are. And if you have a tiny little pool of people, then it'll
  quickly go through those people. You'll make your sales and then it'll go to one degree
  removed. And when it goes to one degree removed, those people don't know you at all, and
  they're less likely to convert." (verbatim) Perceived "ads stopped working" is usually
  this: the platform burned through the warm audience, then pushed to colder people who
  convert worse.

### The two-community model as the consistent outperformer

- "The people that I'm seeing consistently outperform are the people who have two
  communities. They have the free community because it's very easy to convert people into
  free communities." (verbatim) A **free** community is cheap to fill and becomes the engine
  that feeds a **paid** one. (This is the same dual-model structure as above, but here he
  frames it as the answer to *ad economics*, not just product design.)

### Benchmarks (new, concrete)

- **Free opt-in target: 20%+ of clicks (1 in 5).** "If you can get 20% or more to opt into
  the community, I think that's a good normal benchmark to shoot for, meaning one in five.
  And so, whatever your cost per click is, multiply by five, that's what your cost per new
  member is." (verbatim) → **cost per free member ≈ CPC × 5.**
- **Manual lead-work conversion: ~10% free→paid.** If you **manually work the leads** (which
  he recommends, especially for newer operators), you can get about **10% of free members to
  buy the next (paid) thing.** (paraphrase)
- **Viability formula: cost-per-free-member × 10.** Multiply your cost per free member by 10
  (the inverse of the ~10% conversion rate). If your paid price still makes money at that
  acquisition cost, the model is viable. (paraphrase)
- **Recurring-revenue test — "the first nut."** Because the paid community is recurring, "if
  I can cover the entirety of that first nut from the first payment, then every payment after
  that is profit." (verbatim) That's his core test for whether the paid membership is worth
  running against paid traffic.

### The four free-to-paid conversion mechanisms (inside the free Skool)

He likes **three** distinct mechanisms running simultaneously inside the free community, plus
an optional fourth (all paraphrase unless quoted):

1. **Onboarding / sales call.** Offer every new free member an onboarding call — functionally
   a sales call where you walk them through the material and show them what's going on.
2. **Five-video VSL / webinar sequence** posted as a "module" inside the free community:
   (1) an **origin story** that frames the opportunity / big idea behind what you do; (2)–(4)
   **three case studies** of representative people who each overcame a *different* obstacle,
   which he buckets as **"myself, other people, and circumstances"**; and (5) a **call to
   action** explaining what the paid community (or next step) is. Members who complete the
   sequence **self-book** calls.
3. **One live value call per week** into the free community: add value and answer questions,
   then **soft-toss the CTA** — because you just solved people's problems, you can mention
   that more advanced help exists in the paid community, and a handful convert each week.
4. **(Optional) DM nurture.** DM people who join the free Skool to open an authentic dialogue.
   Build rapport before soliciting — "take them out to dinner before you try and take them
   home." (verbatim)

## Membership unit economics & scaling a stuck community (2026 MoreMozi coaching)

From [[../../sources/2026-02-24-yt-6dED84QH8Yc]] — a live coaching clip where Alex diagnoses a
content-membership business end to end (~$20K/mo, ~300 members at $49/mo, ~12% monthly churn,
40–50 sales/mo). All Alex-attributed, dated **2026-02-24**, paraphrase unless quoted. The
questioner's own numbers are self-reported.

### Push, don't pivot

- **Fix the business you have before chasing a "blue ocean" avatar.** He's mildly more open
  than usual to a niche switch *because the business is barely a year old* — but still says
  **stay longer.** Pivoting means rebuilding an entire audience from scratch (new Instagram
  page, new game), and "you have a lot more to learn from this business before doing the next
  thing." (paraphrase)
- **Don't blame the avatar / market-size reframe.** Blaming the niche is "the obvious thing
  that everyone says." The "market leader" has only ~5,000 members out of ~1M realtors, so
  **nobody owns the market** — the real problem is **sales velocity** (~50/mo in a
  million-person market is very low; you could be doing ~1,000/mo), not the avatar.
  (paraphrase)

### Churn → average lifetime → LTV → CAC (applied to a membership)

- **Convert churn into a customer lifetime.** ~12% monthly churn ≈ **8-month average
  lifetime**. At $49/mo that's **~$400 LTV.** "We just know that we're going to get 8 months
  on average times 50 bucks, so we're going to make $400 per customer as long as CAC is, you
  know, $100 or less. … We have high margins, we're good to go." (verbatim) Rule of thumb:
  with high margins, the model works as long as **CAC ≤ ~$100.**
- 12% monthly churn is genuinely tough — "that's like you lose all the customers by the end
  of the year, almost." (verbatim)

### Annual prepay as a cash-flow and churn lever

- **When LTV ≈ 8 months, give ~4 months free on the annual.** "Right now, LTV is 8 months for
  you. So, you might as well give four and pull the cash flow forward since it's equivalent,
  and churn on those customers will be lower." (verbatim) It's economically equivalent to what
  you'd have earned, but it **pulls cash forward** and **lowers churn** on those cohorts. (The
  questioner was only offering 2 months.)

### Segment toward the sticky / serious members

- **Learn who churns vs. who sticks.** "There's probably two or three characteristics that
  separate the people who do versus ones that don't" — find them, then target and build the
  value prop around the stickers. (verbatim) Part-timers who are "barely business owners"
  churn; the serious full-timers stay.
- **Target recent buyers.** Aim at members **who closed a sale in the last 30 days** — not
  necessarily top-1%, but "on the way," reinvesting, and with cash to spend now. (paraphrase)

### Layer monetization on top of a low-ticket membership

- **Run ads to learn true CAC.** The competitor reportedly isn't spending much on ads, so
  it's untapped; measuring CAC tells you whether the ~$400 LTV math holds. (paraphrase)
- **Self-liquidating offer on the thank-you page, priced above the annual**, to help cover
  CAC. Concrete example: a **$197–$397 "high-ticket" digital product** (e.g. "the 100
  best-performing pieces of real-estate content over the last year"). (paraphrase)
- **A higher-tier ~$500–$1,000/month done-for-you upsell** built on heavy **AI and
  automation** so it keeps high gross margins. (paraphrase)
- Prescribed sequence: (1) learn which customers churn vs. stick, (2) build the annual-prepay
  incentive, (3) run ads and measure CAC, (4) add the SLO + high-ticket upsell — master the
  game and "you might just become the market leader." (paraphrase)

## Qualifications — who's in the room IS part of the offer (2026 MoreMozi)

From [[../../sources/2026-02-16-yt-AaUxdkGSB5Q]] — a MoreMozi interview clip, all Alex-attributed,
dated **2026-02-16**, paraphrase unless quoted. This is a distinct pillar of the community offer
that the design/monetization sections above don't cover: the *composition of the membership* is
itself a value lever, not just the access/content/events inside it.

### The mistake and the reframe

- **The biggest thing most communities miss is qualifications.** Operators lean too hard into the
  "grand slam offer" (the value/product) and forget that **who is in the community is part of the
  offer** — "part of the offer can be who is there, and I think that is the most underrated
  component of the offer of a community." (verbatim) The people in the room are the offer; almost
  nobody designs for that.

### Segment like a doorman

- **A community should have someone at the door.** "If you go to a party in the real world, there's
  somebody at the door who's saying you can't come in, you can come in. If you have a party where
  you just let everybody in, it's going to be a weird party." (verbatim) Many paid communities feel
  "weird" precisely because not everyone there should be there. (This sharpens the existing party/
  doorman analogy: elsewhere the doorman *collects money*; here the doorman *decides who gets in*.)
- **Segmenting is the whole point.** "The whole point of a community is to segment... you draw a
  line in the sand. You say everyone else not, but this. And so you create this tiny little world"
  — which you then have to protect. (verbatim)
- **Prune misfits — even high-paying ones.** Be more aggressive about removing people; frame it as
  fit, not judgment ("this isn't a fit for you, that's okay — we have a different vibe in here").
  Alex says he has kicked out members paying **$3,000/month** because they were "messing up the
  garden": "The people that I've kicked out were paying $3,000 a month. Like I don't give a [__].
  Like you're [__] up my garden." (verbatim) People under-prune because they like watching MRR go
  up — but revenue doesn't justify keeping someone who hurts the culture.
  > ⚠️ Self-reported: the "$3,000/month kicked-out members" figure is Alex's own claim — unverified.

### Say no to money for ~6 months (the gestation period)

- **Holding the line early compounds.** Draw the line earlier and the work of running the community
  later drops sharply. What goes *up* when you hold it: member **self-policing**, **stick/retention**,
  **gross margins**, and **affiliate referrals** (members tell friends "these people are just like
  us — you've got to join").
- **The hard part is saying no to money.** Like most great things it's slower at the start, "because
  you have to say no to money," which most people won't do. If you're willing to say no to money for
  **at least ~6 months**, you can curate the culture and **create the brand of the community during
  that gestation period** — the brand then proliferates, but you must keep protecting it.
- **Garden / rose-bush metaphor.** "It's a lot like a garden... it takes a while and then finally
  the good plants come in, but you still have to protect it and weed it." (verbatim) A whole rose
  bush with weeds all over it ruins the entire view — so protect and weed continuously.

### The accordion — solving cold-start (quantity ↔ quality)

- The chicken-and-egg problem (raised by the co-host, resolved by Alex): as a beginner it's hard to
  get even one person, yet quality demands selectivity. Alex's answer is the **accordion**:
  "It's the accordion that goes back and forth between quality and quantity. So first you have to
  get quantity, and then you're like, okay, of this can I select? And then you select a small
  amount, and then you overcorrect a little bit and you're like, okay, I need some more people in,
  and you kind of go back and forth." (verbatim) Oscillate: get quantity → select down to the
  high-quality subset → overcorrect and let more in → repeat.

### Objective qualification standards (B2B vs. B2C)

- Use **objective, stated** entry standards, not vibes:
  - **B2B**: a revenue threshold, a minimum customer count, a headcount, or investable assets (for
    an investing-oriented community).
  - **B2C**: demographic filters are legitimate — e.g. "you need to be a woman who's over 45 and
    have at least two kids."
- **Qualifying doesn't shrink the business if you have traffic.** There are "more than enough" of
  any given avatar; with enough traffic, narrowing the door eventually **snowballs** rather than
  starving the group.

### Mine Skool activity data to find your avatar

- **Let the platform's data tell you who to recruit.** Skool tells you who your **most active
  members** are. Ask "who are they?" then "how do I find more of those people?" — which in turn
  **narrows your marketing messaging** toward that ideal avatar.

### End state: snowball → network effect ("broadcast → network")

- The goal everyone wants: the **community itself becomes the magnet** for more of the same type of
  person. At that point "you stop having to provide value to the community, and that's when the
  community becomes... a true network effect." (verbatim)
- **Sequence over time:** short term, sell services and one-on-one calls to make money — "there's
  nothing wrong with that... you should do it." Then **shift from "me" to "you," from broadcast to
  network**: "at some point you want to shift it from me to you... From broadcast to network. And
  that shift has to come from the quality of the people who are inside of it." (verbatim) The quality
  of the members is what powers the shift. (Complements the member-to-member "build the net between
  members" thesis under Engagement & retention.)

## Growth & traffic (funnel, six sources, influencer arbitrage, referrals)

From [[../../sources/2025-03-29-yt-hdawvvYqmYo]] unless noted. The funnel:
**traffic → about page → likeable community**. See also [[../business/acquisition-channels]].

### The about page

- Only two components: **media (video/images) and words.** Pro tip: *demonstrate*
  value — "just show people what's inside" — because demos let buyers see value
  before purchasing. (verbatim)
- Copy in **bullets, not big paragraphs**; "state the facts and tell the truth.
  And if the truth isn't that compelling, then change what's inside until it is."
  (verbatim) Model the *structure* of big groups' about pages, never copy their
  content.

### The six traffic sources

1. **Warm outreach** — directly message people you already know (phone contacts,
   past emails, social followers/friends). No ad spend.
2. **Cold outreach** — same motion to people who don't know you (bought lists,
   scraping tools, shared-interest targeting). No ad spend.
3. **Paid ads** — any platform "that accepts money in exchange for eyeballs or
   earballs" (Facebook, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube).
4. **Content** — free posts whose bio/CTA points interested viewers to the about
   page. (See [[content-engine]] for the content method.)
5. **Influencer partnerships** — see arbitrage below.
6. **Referrals** — word-of-mouth from a great-relative-to-price community.

Warm/cold outreach, content, and referrals cost no ad spend, so the model can be
started with **time instead of capital**. (paraphrase)

### Influencer arbitrage / leverage

- "Many influencers have hundreds of thousands of followers and make very little
  money." Bring the productized community, run it, take a percentage, and monetize
  an audience that already exists. Concrete framing: an influencer making $500/mo
  off 100k followers could plausibly make $10,000/mo together. **Start with a low
  percentage; once you have proof, take a bigger cut next time** — "if you do it
  once, the next one you do, you can take a bigger percentage." "Big secret here,
  this is what I'm doing with Skool." (verbatim; the dollar figures are
  illustrative hypotheticals, and the Skool disclosure is self-interested)

### Referrals / word-of-mouth (compounding, zero-cost)

- The lever: make the community "awesome relative to what you charge" and growth
  self-sustains. His cited proof point (self-reported): his books "sell zillions
  of copies… 3,000 plus copies a day" with no ads, no outreach — "just people
  telling their friends the book is good."
  > ⚠️ Self-reported book-sales figure inside a promotional pitch — unverified.

## Engagement & retention (make it fun, peer cohort, public progress, member success)

- **Make it fun.** Rule #1 of the Skool Games: have fun (not hokey). He cites Tony
  Robbins' *Money Master the Game* — the billionaires interviewed all treated
  business as a game they loved; success "can't suck." Two force-multipliers:
  **find a friend** ("even if you do shitty stuff with a friend, it's still more
  fun than doing shitty stuff alone") and **partner on complementary skills** — he
  cites his own Skool partnership: founder Sam builds product, Alex does
  content/marketing; each fills the other's gap. (verbatim + paraphrase,
  [[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]], [[../../sources/2025-02-03-yt-ym8u5mN-_ww]])
- **Peer cohort + public progress as social proof.** Delivered inside a live Skool
  group, he uses a peer cohort (the "$100 test" / "speedrunners" group he monitors
  nightly) and public progress posts to create social proof and a gentle
  shame/deprivation prod — he cites that "88% of participants had already made
  money" as proof the system works. ([[../../sources/2025-04-27-yt-nO-CUYy-ry4]],
  paraphrase; the 88% is a self-reported in-community figure)
- **Radically simplify the core action.** Make the required action "as easy as
  humanly possible" so members can't hide from doing the work.
  ([[../../sources/2025-04-27-yt-nO-CUYy-ry4]], paraphrase)
- **Police "midwit" advice.** Actively curb members who over-complicate things
  ("midwitting") inside the groups, so beginners aren't overwhelmed by premature
  optimization ("2011 midwit marketing stuff" only matters once your time is
  maxed out). ([[../../sources/2025-04-27-yt-nO-CUYy-ry4]], paraphrase)
- **Member success is the real retention driver.** "As much as I'd like to think
  people love me, people stay because they make money."
  ([[../../sources/2025-01-18-yt-J7DWZsKr3bI]], verbatim) Consistent with the
  quality-over-lock-in thesis above.
- **Do epic shit and talk about it.** Members grow their own standing (and the
  community's) by doing real reps and posting them: "'I did 100 TikToks over 30
  days, here's what happened' outperforms 'here's how to make good TikToks' ~20
  to 1 because people believe you did the reps." Be a valuable member — post
  problems you solved, remove CTAs. He cites community member "Evelyn" as
  best-in-class at value-first posting. ([[../../sources/2025-04-18-yt-dY1Mp1LVO0o]],
  paraphrase + verbatim)
- **Free-community engagement features** (path 1, if staying free): guaranteed
  reach on posts, member-to-member interaction, leaderboards, and unlockable
  content that incentivizes sharing/referrals. ([[../../sources/2025-03-27-yt-C6d4BEyYszo]],
  paraphrase; promotional)

### 2026 Q&A refinement: build the net between members

In a May 2026 community-coaching clip, Hormozi sharpens the engagement mechanism:
community value should not be one-to-many broadcast from host to members; it should build
"the net between" members. He recommends prompts and threads around the granular everyday
problems unique to the group. For a remote-worker / global-adventurer community, examples
include "picture of your work desk," visas, Airbnbs, Wi-Fi, international connectivity,
and other logistics where members can help each other. The durable loop is
member-to-member reinforcement that survives even when the host is less central
[[../../sources/2026-05-07-yt-IkRG1lha-GU]].

### 2026 Q&A refinement: free-to-paid ascension and unscalable early activation

In April-May 2026 coaching cuts, Hormozi adds a more granular free-to-paid operating
sequence. For a community with an engaged free base, he recommends converting the most
active members into a paid free trial rather than splitting the paid group away from the
living free room too early [[../../sources/2026-05-05-yt-YHA8KJFytIQ]]. The launch cadence
is a 4-6 week promotion-first ramp: tell members the paid version is coming, move from
quiet "whispers" to louder promotion, and use the free period to create urgency and social
proof before the paid offer opens [[../../sources/2026-05-04-yt-4n4iSAhvF00]].

He also distinguishes three community-builder paths -- pain, profession, and passion --
and treats a free trial as gross merchandise volume rather than a vanity metric: it is a
conversion point that proves enough demand to keep improving the group
[[../../sources/2026-05-05-yt-XH5DbavDfNI]]. A separate ascension clip names four places
to convert: free community to trial, trial to paid, paid to higher help, and paid members
to referrals or reactivation [[../../sources/2026-04-28-yt-E_33PAl0AHo]].

For engagement, his prescription remains deliberately unscalable at the beginning:
personally DM members for permission to matchmake them, prompt picture posts, and create
small peer interactions that make the room feel alive before automation or scale
[[../../sources/2026-05-01-yt-L2rFSqxVFlw]],
[[../../sources/2026-04-30-yt-FsOYmruUaZ8]].

### 2026 Q&A refinement: sell personalization, not information

Across the same run, he repeats the content/community monetization rule in a paid-community
context: give away information and charge for personalization, troubleshooting, access,
and implementation [[../../sources/2026-05-01-yt-tJFqLdBAaIA]]. That makes one-to-small-group
delivery a bridge product: it preserves enough personal application to be valuable while
creating better economics than pure one-to-one work
[[../../sources/2026-04-30-yt-w9rzkvlMTj4]], and it explains why a free call can ethically
pitch the paid layer as the place where generic resources get applied to the member's
specific constraints [[../../sources/2026-04-27-yt-RPxzxc_YgFE]].

### 2026 Q&A refinement: you don't need courses to start — build the curriculum live

Answering "Do I need courses to start a community business?", Hormozi says **course-first is
the wrong order**. Do not stockpile pre-recorded modules before launching. Instead, **open
with an empty classroom and build the curriculum live**, session by session, as the community
grows — live sessions beat pre-recordings both for delivery and for pacing your build to
actual demand. His deeper point reframes what members buy: **they want the community over the
content** — the in-feed engagement and member-to-member discussion is the real usage, not the
course library, which reinforces the "members want community over content" and "build the net
between members" theses above. And you **find product-market fit by asking members what they
want, not by guessing** — the same "ask, don't guess" logic that sources what goes inside
(see Design → the three buckets) applies to the curriculum itself.
([[../../sources/2026-02-28-yt-HgeCheYgrW0]], paraphrase)

## The "community as skeleton key" — access leverage

A distinctive angle from [[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]]: once you
assemble a group (100 / 500 / 1,000 / 5,000 people), the community becomes a
**leverage asset** to reach people far above your status. Offer high-status guests
*distribution of their message* in exchange for a 30-minute video or Q&A. Win-win-win:
the guest gets reach, you get the favor and are seen delivering value, and members
gain access they could never get alone (a lone member emailing a master mechanic
gets ignored; "2,000 mechanics" gets a yes). "This is a skeleton key of life, for
you to gain access to people you otherwise wouldn't" (verbatim); "people who like
you will be able to punch way above your weight class because you can actually
provide something to them which is distribution." "You now have the collective
asking power of the whole community and you are their champion to go ask on their
behalf." (verbatim)

## Recurring analogies (voice markers)

Three analogies recur across the whole corpus and are useful persona/voice markers:

- **The party (+ doorman).** A paid community is "an online party" — "it's not the
  host that makes the party, it's the other people at the party. The host just
  invites the guests, provides the place… and sets the rules." "When the party
  rocks, you get all the credit even though you just put it together. And when the
  doorman collects the money to let people in, it all goes to you." Also used for
  the free→paid flip (charge at the door once the party's "booming") and for
  launch fear ("all we're doing is inviting people to a party").
  ([[../../sources/2024-12-18-yt-V5hkdejbybQ]], [[../../sources/2025-02-22-yt-_Nz_0qxIyok]],
  [[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]], [[../../sources/2025-03-26-yt-ogMxa_2DWZI]],
  [[../../sources/2025-03-27-yt-C6d4BEyYszo]])
- **Wikipedia.** "It's kind of like Wikipedia. Wikipedia is not the expert at
  anything. They just bring the people together, and then they are the online
  experts." — the core "you don't need to be the expert" argument.
  ([[../../sources/2024-12-18-yt-V5hkdejbybQ]], [[../../sources/2025-02-22-yt-_Nz_0qxIyok]])
- **Skeleton key.** The community as an access key to high-status people (above).
  ([[../../sources/2025-03-25-yt-1s1PtaUAE84]])
- **Test drive / house tour.** Risk-reversal for the free trial: "you can't truly
  make an informed decision… for the same reason you don't buy a house without
  looking inside of it first, or test drive a car before buying it."
  ([[../../sources/2025-01-18-yt-J7DWZsKr3bI]])
