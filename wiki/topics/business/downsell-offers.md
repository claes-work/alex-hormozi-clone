# Downsell Offers

Downsell Offers are the third stage of Hormozi's Money Model (attraction → upsell → downsell → continuity): the machinery for turning "no" into "yes". His definition is that "downselling tweaks the original offer to find the highest value solution for the customer's budget" — so **any offer made after someone says no is a downsell** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 127). Downselling operates on exactly two axes — change *how they pay* (balance pay-now vs. pay-over-time) or change *what they get* (quantity, quality, or something different) — and it is never the same stuff cheaper; that is discounting, which he explicitly forbids as a reflex ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 127, 129, 153). The section teaches three processes: Payment Plan Downsells and Trial With Penalty (how they pay), and Feature Downsells (what they get), all serving the same purpose — boosting 30-day profit by making sales that would otherwise be lost ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 130).

> "No means no for this thing, not no for everything."
> — ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 129)

## The Six Rules of Downselling

Hormozi opens the section with rules governing all downselling ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 128–130):

1. **Remember, They Said No To This Offer, Not All Offers.** Rejection of the offer isn't rejection of you; it's an opportunity to find what they really want — "No means no for this thing, not no for everything" (verbatim, PDF p. 129).
2. **Downsells Are Trades.** Work with the customer through give/get combinations until something matches: "If you're gonna give something, get something" (verbatim, PDF p. 129).
3. **Personalize, Don't Pressure.** Offer more of what they like, less of what they don't, priced to match. His soda example: if the large-soda upsell is refused, offer a small, a juice, or a coffee — "if I can better serve them, it would be offensive not to" (paraphrase + verbatim fragment, PDF p. 129).
4. **Offer The Same Things In New Ways.** Don't create "a hundred businesses' worth of products (and problems)"; downselling is a hundred ways to offer what you already have (PDF p. 129).
5. **Don't Drop Your Price Just To Get Somebody To Buy.** Dropping price is discounting, not downselling: "If someone wants what you have, and just doesn't want to pay the price—tough cookies" (verbatim, PDF p. 129). Payment plans — less now, more over time — are the acceptable alternative.
6. **Customers Talk About Price.** Deliberate price tests (specific price, specific number of people, planned ahead) are fine; caving in the moment out of fear is not — it upsets customers who find out and "becomes an ethical problem, at least to me" (verbatim fragment, PDF pp. 129–130).

## Payment Plan Downsells

**Definition (faithful):** downsell by offering the *same product* again with the cost spread out — charge some up front and put the rest into scheduled payments ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 133). The core insight: "it costs too much" usually means "this costs too much *up front*." Discounts appear to work because people pay less — but really because they pay less *in the moment*. Payment plans capture the best of both worlds: more buyers (less in the moment) AND full price paid over time (PDF p. 133).

**Origin (self-reported):** August 2013 was Hormozi's first real month in business, with exactly one month's rent in savings. He improvised his first-ever payment plan on a lead who could pay nothing that day — charging her in full on the 2nd (after her payday on the 1st), before his rent was due on the 5th. The charge worked. He says he has since made tens of millions of dollars with Payment Plan Downsells and still uses them ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 131–132).

**Risk model — "payment plans are a gamble":** one way to win, two ways to lose. Win: more customers who complete their payments. Lose: people cancel before you turn a profit. Lose most: people who *would have paid in full* take a plan and cancel early ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 132–133).

### The 7-step process (up to nine offers)

The sequence moves from most cash up front to least; stop as soon as they buy ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 134–136):

1. **Reward paying in full rather than punish paying over time.** Bake the interest into the presented price, then offer prepayment as a discount. Contrast of scripts: normal interest framing = "It's $10 if you get it right now, but it's $15 if you pay over time because we charge $5 in interest"; Hormozi's framing = "It's $15…but it's $10 if you prepay it. You save $5…that's what most people do." Same math, friendlier feel, plus a price anchor (PDF p. 134).
2. **Offer 3rd-party financing, credit card, or layaway.** 3rd-party financing (car-dealer model): another company pays you now, the customer repays them — work to set up but "totally worth the effort." Credit-card reframe script: "would you rather I decide your payment terms or you decide?" — they say they'd rather decide, so tell them to use a credit card. Layaway: the customer pays installments of any reasonable size and timeline but only gets the product after paying in full — most flexible for them, lowest risk for you (PDF pp. 134–135).
3. **Half now, half later.** Scripts: "When's the next time you get paid?" → "Wanna just put half down today and the rest down when you get paid?" → if not: "What's the most you can put down today?" → "Great. We'll put that down today and put the rest when you get paid. Fair enough?" Schedule off paychecks — most people are paid biweekly, and paycheck-timed payments boost 30-day profit far more than monthly payments (PDF p. 135).
4. **Check whether they still want the thing (temperature check).** Script: "Got it. So money is tight right now. Real quick. I want to make sure. On a scale from 1–10 how bad do you wanna do this?" At 8+, continue: "Awesome. Don't worry. We're gonna figure out a way to make this happen for you." At 7 or below: "Why not a 10?", then "You're right. I think we may have something that could be a better fit for you" — and pivot to Feature Downsells (PDF p. 135).
5. **Split into three payments.** One third now and one third on each of the next two paychecks — or one third now and one third in each of the next two months (PDF p. 135).
6. **Evenly spread payments over the remaining service.** Example: Gym Launch's program was sixteen weeks, so sixteen weekly charges (PDF p. 136).
7. **Offer a Free Trial** — the special way, i.e. Trial With Penalty (next chapter). The sale ends here for now (PDF p. 136).

> "This Payment Plan Downsell process makes up to nine offers. And if you think that sounds crazy, you're probably making way less money and serving way fewer customers than you could."
> — ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 136)

### Supporting tactics

- **"Seesaw" Downselling** (simplified version for fewer steps or less experienced salespeople): ask "Would you rather have giant monthly payments or tiny ones?" — they say tiny — then: "It normally costs XXX. And if you prepay it today, you get a huge discount and zero monthly payments. That work?" The plan is framed as the negative, prepaying as the benefit. If they can't afford it: "If you can't afford it up front, I totally get it. We'll simply adjust the down payment until you get a monthly rate you like." Bigger down payment = lower monthlies. If still no, confirm desire, then pull your chair to their side of the table and walk through options together — "The sale becomes a team effort" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 136).
- **Payment plans have built-in upsells:** periodically re-offer the original paid-in-full discount mid-plan — customers forget the option exists and some jump at it. Give salespeople the same bonus for closing the balance. Principle: "if you give people the option to pay slower, they will pay slower. If you incentivize them to pay faster, they will pay faster" (verbatim, PDF pp. 136–137).
- **Get fewer declined payments:** align billing with paycheck dates; if a charge declines, re-run it several times the same day (deposits land at different times). Learned from John, an early mentor (self-reported); it recoups about a third of declined payments ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 137).
- **Health metric — making sure plans MAKE money:** after adding payment plans, close rate should rise, but the number of paid-in-fulls must NOT drop (otherwise would-be paid-in-fulls just moved onto plans). Target: more total closes at the same paid-in-full percentage. Example numbers: 10 leads → 3 sales normally; with the downsell, 3 more (6 total) — up-front cash from the first three plus scheduled payments from the second three ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 137).
- **Profitwell churn data (14,000 businesses):** monthly billing (12x/yr) → 10.7% monthly cancellation; quarterly (4x/yr) → 5%; annual (1x/yr) → 2%. Presenting most-cash-up-front-first therefore also makes customers more valuable long-term: "start high (fewer bigger payments) and work your way down" (verbatim fragment, [[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 137).

**When it works best:** anywhere — "no matter how many zeroes the price tag has"; it is his "pay less NOW" answer. **Caveats:** default/cancellation risk (the gamble above), cannibalizing paid-in-fulls, and monthly billing raising churn ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 132–133, 137).

## Trial With Penalty

**Definition (faithful):** "In a Trial With Penalty offer, customers can try your product or service for free so long as they meet your terms." Customers pay only if they DON'T meet the terms ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 141–142). It is the inverse of Win Your Money Back (Attraction Offer #1): WYMB refunds money for meeting terms; Trial With Penalty charges fees for NOT meeting them — avoiding fees replaces winning money as the adherence incentive. The terms should mirror WYMB's actions and results, which produce excellent customers (PDF pp. 141–144, 150). Positioning is not "here's my thing—see if you like it" but "here's my thing, you get it for free so long as you do this stuff…which makes you a perfect fit for my next offer. And if you don't, then you have to pay for it" (PDF p. 142).

**Origin (self-reported):** Spring 2018, with Gym Launch scaling past 100 employees, Leila chose an HR software vendor because of exactly this offer — free onboarding if she did the training, paid onboarding if she skipped it. That company used it as an Attraction Offer; Hormozi prefers to *downsell* trials — offered only after a no. Done his way, it changes only what they pay *today*, not the total ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 140–141).

**Math example:** normally close 3 of 10 on the Attraction Offer; downsell another 4 onto Trial With Penalty; after the trial, upsell 3 of them → from 3 sales to 6 — "doubling your customers!" He adds: "I'm still irritated at the thousands of customers I've lost on free trials over the years before learning this. But now we can save them!" (verbatim, [[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 142).

**Example criteria sets** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 142–143):

- B2C — "28 Day Kick-That-Habit Blueprint": attend all consulting calls; post progress in the group weekly; journal daily in the app; attend feedback/transformation sessions (upsell opportunities).
- B2B — "5-Day Get Your First 5 Customers Challenge": send 100 outbound messages per day; report stats; attend daily training; post in the group daily after homework; attend the graduation call (upsell opportunity).
- Software — "$500 Onboarding for HR Software, then $99 Per Month Thereafter": skip the $500 up front IF you attend onboarding (three 60-minute Zoom calls — upsell opportunities), do the homework, activate the employer profile, and get employees set up by the end of the third call; otherwise pay the fee.

**Designing it:** the free portion should be your bare-bones offer (like the Decoy Offer) or your WYMB offer — "I'd recommend giving more rather than giving less—if you can afford it." Criteria should activate and retain customers; swipe them directly from Win Your Money Back. On fees: for a $500 product with ten to-dos, rather bill $50 per miss than one $500 fee on the first miss — unless a single miss truly wrecks their success, in which case the fee should reflect that. "I've seen both work" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 143–144).

### The 5-step downsell delivery

([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 144–147)

1. **Offer the trial last.** Script: "Hmmm…that sure is a pickle. I'll tell ya what. How about we just get ya started for free, would you be okay with that? We can just help you out, and if you like it, you can stay. Let me get your ID and we can get the process started—fair enough?"
2. **Always get a credit card.** Record their info, hold the ID, motion for the card: "What card do you wanna use?" If they balk: "That's just how we've always done it." If they still refuse — "wish them a lovely day and show them out" (verbatim fragment, PDF p. 145).
3. **Always sell staying and paying.** "If this program got you the result, will you stay long-term?" No agreement = no trial. For hesitators: "I don't want you to try it. I want you to get results… you're gonna have to stay on after. I just wanna make sure that you're not looking for a quick fix—because I can't ethically promise you that."
4. **Explain the fees AFTER getting their card.** Script: "We will do our part so long as you do yours. That's fair right? So now I just ask that you bet on yourself—if you miss or skip any stuff, your results will suffer. We charge to keep you on track… If you follow through, you get all this for free… Best of both worlds." Explaining fees before the card creates more resistance. Customers initial separately next to each fee clause — which forces salespeople to explain them.
5. **Make check-ins required.** Frame the check-ins (the upsell opportunities) with benefits: "Yep, and you agree to attend each of the three check-ins… Obviously we charge if you miss these because it's the only way we can get you results."

### Upselling from a trial — three outcomes

([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 147–148)

1. **They like it:** billing is already automatic; meet anyway and offer a longer-term or higher-value version (or both).
2. **They hate it:** ask what they'd have wanted different; agree; be angry at yourself, never blame them — "Only one person can be angry—and it needs to be you" (verbatim fragment). Ask for a chance to make it up and offer the higher-level thing that now fits their needs. He says he can sell about half of these people (self-reported).
3. **They didn't use it:** reach out multiple times first; require a meeting, offering to waive the fee if they attend; get them back on track or offer something better. "I don't like billing non-starters. A small fee isn't worth a 1-star review. But hey, it's your choice" (verbatim).

**Tuning knobs:** nobody takes it → lower requirements or penalties; they take it but don't follow through → better explain how fees help them and make sales meetings mandatory; they don't stay on the back end → sell staying-and-paying harder, deliver better, align front-end and back-end offers. "If you start printing money, don't stop" (verbatim, [[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 148). Also: let people make up for goofs — a make-up opportunity after a billed miss re-engages and converts; miss that too and billing is justified (PDF p. 149).

**Naming and scope:** customer-facing, just call it a "Free Trial" — "No one wants to be penalized." Deflection scripts: "This is just how we've always done it" / "People get the best results this way." When it works best: recurring products/services (use Pay Less Now or Pay More Later for physical products or one-time services), and only proven "in businesses where the customer has to do work to get results. If you find other types of businesses these work for…let me know!" (verbatim). A super-low discount price (e.g., "first month for $1" then $X/month) also justifies asking for the card and proves the card works before auto-billing starts ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 149).

**Economics reminder:** "You make money by getting people results and turning them into customers, not nickeling and diming people with fees." Trials With Penalties beat normal free trials because people use the product more and actually get value ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 150).

## Feature Downsells

**Definition (faithful):** "Feature Downsells lower prices by changing what customers get" — via lesser quantity, lower quality, lower-price alternatives, or cutting optional components. Not a discount (same stuff cheaper); the *stuff* changes ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 153). The formula: "take something away, lower the price, and in so many words ask 'how about now?'" (PDF p. 154).

**Value mechanics:** every feature has a price and a value. Remove stuff they hate and lower the price a lot → better deal → more take the downsell. Remove stuff they love and lower the price a little → worse deal → more take the original offer. People weigh money saved against value lost, so they see the value of what you removed only *after* they see the price difference. Clever feature downselling gets customers to **"re-upsell" themselves** onto the more expensive offer. Remove features from highest to lowest value ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 153–154).

**Case (self-reported, 2019):** an unnamed friend in high-ticket sales told Hormozi he "tripled my close rate from 25% to 75% last quarter" by cutting one feature — his full money-back guarantee. Script: "If you don't want the option to get your money back, you can pay less. Or, you can keep your money-back guarantee—which would you prefer?" Numbers: before, 100 calls → 25 buy (one full-price option). After: 35 buy the main thing + 40 take the downsell = 75%. It raised full-price buyers, total close rate, and up-front cash. "It changed my life" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 152–153).

**Example scripts by type** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 154–156):

- Product quantity: "Instead of a three-month supply, how about we start with just one?"
- Service quantity: "Instead of four sessions per month, why don't we start you at two?"
- Product quality: "Instead of the leather seats we can do vinyl, how does that sound?"
- Service quality: "Instead of 5-minute response times, why don't we just start you at overnight response times? You'll save some money and you'll still get your answers—just with a small delay."
- Removing entire features: "Instead of priority chat support, email support, and calls, why don't we just keep chat and email support but drop the calls to save you some money?"

**Service-quality levers** (features to trade down, PDF p. 155): time availability (days, times of day, session length — 15- vs. 60-minute calls); location availability (one vs. all locations); cancellation terms (reschedule fees vs. free); speed of response (minutes/hours/days); speed of delivery (line vs. priority, same-day vs. next week); service ratio (1-on-1 vs. one-to-many vs. many-to-one); communication method (text/chat/video); provider qualifications (owner vs. veteran vs. new employee); live vs. recorded; in-person vs. remote; DIY vs. DWY vs. DFY; expirations (forever vs. limited); personalization (generic vs. made for you); insurance/guarantee (length, coverage, conditional vs. unconditional) ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 155).

**DFY→DIY downsell:** if all service downsells fail, downsell a product solving the same problem. Examples: chiropractor → home massage tools, foam rollers, mats; painter → "why don't I just give you the paint and let you lease one of our spray machines for a daily rate?"; Hormozi himself → "Instead of me and my team buying your company and actively growing your business, why don't you just attend a workshop? (*Cough* Go to acquisition.com)" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 156).

**Important notes** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 156–159):

- **Never negotiate the price.** Pay less NOW → payment plan; pay less OVERALL → feature downsell; never "less just because."
- **Maintain the position of a helpful guide** — collaborative, not competitive. Pushy offers exhaust customers fast; a helpful guide "can downsell as many offers as necessary without exhausting the customer" (PDF p. 157).
- **Standardize over time:** learn which features customers value most and pre-plan feature combinations. His sequence: first cut something valuable and lower the price only a little (to make them reconsider the original), then keep removing and lowering until they buy. "I'd rather people get something rather than nothing" (verbatim, PDF p. 157).
- **Name your combinations:** aspirational names on top — "The Whale Package," "The Total Transformation," "High Roller" (airline analogy: First → Business → Economy). His cheapest is "The Minimum" — implying they must get at least that; "so nothing more than the minimum package then?" gets them to say no to say yes, like the Classic Upsell (PDF p. 157).
- **Temperature check after two downsells** (same 1–10 script as payment plans): 8+ → start Payment Plan Downselling ("Awesome. Don't worry. We're gonna figure out a way to make this happen for you."); 7 or below → "What would a 10 look like?" and recombine features. Alternating between payment-plan and feature downsells makes you "very difficult to refuse" (PDF pp. 157–158).
- **After each downsell, ask "Deal?" or "Fair enough?"** — it "works astonishingly well"; few people watch you change the offer for them and then call it unfair. He points to Episode 202 of his podcast The Game: "How to close everyone: downselling like a pro" (PDF p. 158).
- **Free orientations boost DIY downsells:** after all DFY refusals — "Even though we're not gonna work together on X, I still want to help. How about you just come to a free orientation on X tomorrow?" In his fitness business (self-reported), about half of refusers showed up and almost all bought supplements — "Free money for little extra work" (PDF p. 158).
- **Feature downsell your guarantees:** removing the guarantee reveals its value and often flips a no back to a yes (PDF p. 159).
- **Feature downsell current customers (churn save):** if a customer isn't using a feature, offer a lower price for only what they use — they either recommit or feel they got a better deal. "Fun fact: Customers who we've downsold into a lower package just for them have the second highest value of all my customers" (verbatim, self-reported, PDF p. 158).
- **Barter with reviews, testimonials, referrals:** a discount in exchange for advertising. Script: "I'll knock $100 off if you: 1) Leave me a review on all review sites 2) Leave me a video testimonial 3) Make a public social post at the beginning, middle, and end of our program showing your progress 4) Introduce me to two friends who you would want to do this with. Deal?" To him the advertising is worth more than $100; to them $100 is worth less than the advertising — "Win-win" (PDF p. 159).

> "People who demand to pay less for the same thing are business terrorists. I don't negotiate with terrorists."
> — ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 156–157)

## Doctrine

The section's closing principle: downselling is "a hundred different offers for the same product" — never the same stuff for cheaper ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 161). The comparisons Hormozi draws between offer types:

- **Discounting vs. downselling:** discount = same stuff cheaper (forbidden as a reflex); downsell = change the payment structure or change what they get (PDF pp. 129, 153).
- **How-they-pay vs. what-they-get:** Payment Plan Downsells and Trial With Penalty change *when/how* they pay (total unchanged); Feature Downsells change *what they get* (price lowered legitimately) (PDF p. 153).
- **Trial With Penalty vs. Win Your Money Back:** penalty fees for non-adherence vs. refund for adherence — the same criteria set, reused (PDF pp. 141–142, 150).
- **Pay Less Now or Pay More Later vs. Trial With Penalty:** the former for physical products or one-time services; the latter for recurring, effort-required businesses (PDF p. 149).
- **"The Minimum" close works like the Classic Upsell** — getting them to say no to say yes (PDF p. 157).
- **Trial With Penalty as Attraction Offer** (the HR software company) **vs. as downsell** (Hormozi's preference — it then changes only what they pay today, not the total) (PDF p. 141).
- **The two payment-and-feature processes interlock:** payment-plan step 4 (temperature check ≤7) pivots into Feature Downsells; the feature-downsell temperature check (8+) pivots into Payment Plan Downselling — alternating between them makes the seller "very difficult to refuse" (PDF pp. 135, 157–158).

The overarching bet: he takes the payment-plan gamble only when he knows he'll win — "I take the bet when I know I'll win. With this playbook, you can too" (verbatim, PDF p. 133). And the section's one-line law, teeing up Continuity Offers (Section V) with the epigraph he attributes to John, an early mentor — "You can shear a sheep for a lifetime, but you can only skin it once" (PDF p. 161):

> "Everybody buys something."
> — ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 161)
