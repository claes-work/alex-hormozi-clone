# Upsell Offers

Upsell Offers are the second stage of Hormozi's Money Model: "Upsells just mean whatever we offer next." His core mechanism is that every offer that solves a problem reveals a new one — "When an offer solves a problem, another appears. You upsell the solution to the problem your offer reveals" — so the upsell is made to existing customers at the moment of highest trust, right after they buy, and it often makes the majority of the profit: upsells "make or break a Money Model" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 89–90). His anchor example is McDonald's burger math: a $2.00 burger nets $0.25 profit — alone it would take ~10,000 burgers a day to survive. "Do you want fries with that?" adds $0.75; making it a meal with a drink adds $1.75 (total $2.00 profit, an 8x increase); supersizing "for just a buck more" takes total profit to $3.00 — an 11.6x increase over the naked burger. The lesson: the first offer doesn't always make the profit; the second, third, and fourth do ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 90–91).

> "If McDonald's didn't upsell fries and soda, there wouldn't be a McDonald's. If you want to win, you have to figure out your version of 'Do you want fries with that?' If you don't, others will." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 91)

Upsells fail for three reasons (or a combination): you offer something customers don't want (too different, doesn't solve their problem), at the wrong time (before they've experienced the problem), or in the wrong way (they don't believe you). What upsells tend to offer: *more* of what they just got (quantity), a *better* version (quality), or something *new/complementary* (different) ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 91–92). He teaches four types — the Classic Upsell, Menu Upsell, Anchor Upsell, and Rollover Upsell — with an upfront caveat: "Warning: This section is brutally effective and must be used ethically." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 92)

## The Classic Upsell

**Tagline: "You Can't Have X Without Y!"** Definition (paraphrase): the Classic Upsell offers a solution to the customer's *next* problem the moment they become aware of it. It's extremely profitable and easy because current customers are far likelier to buy than strangers, and "when timed right, customers upsell themselves" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 93–94).

**Mechanics.** Your core offer solves one problem and creates another; the upsell immediately solves the next one, framed as "You can't have X without Y" — you can't have a rental car without insurance and gas, can't have a good trip without late checkout. "Bottom Line: If a problem appears, and you can solve it immediately—in exchange for money—do it!" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 94–95).

**Origin story.** Summer 2016, dinner with his childhood mentor — a fourth-generation premier fur coat dealer (Hormozi once brushed "seven thousand fur coats in a row" working at his shop). The dealer's son advertises free earmuffs with coat storage, then when customers pick up the muffs: "Great. And we'll store those as well for $30. You don't want to store anything else do you?" Customers say "no" — which is actually a yes to the $30 storage. The "free" earmuffs cost materials and labor but customers paid $30 to store them: free bonuses create problems your upsells solve ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 93–96).

**Delivery lines from the book** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 95):
- Car wash → sealant: "You're not gonna wanna do the wash without sealant. You get way more for your money."
- Bicycle → helmet, lights, puncture-resistant tires: "You can't have a bike without a helmet!"
- Exercise course → nutrition course: "You can't out-exercise a bad diet…so you're gonna want our course on nutrition."

**Sub-tactics** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 96–99):
- **Actually Do It.** To businesses that sell only one thing: "You barely have a business—you have a front end. Figure out what you're gonna offer next." One business that added a single upsell "actually 5x'd their business" months later (p. 96).
- **Offer More Profitable Upsells First.** Pitch the highest-profit option first (p. 96).
- **"Say No To Say Yes."** "You don't want anything else do you?" — people are trained to say no; here no = yes. "So let the nos (yeses) roll in." (p. 96)
- **Surprise And Delight.** If you hold four bonuses for fence-sitters and add them one at a time, still give all four to customers who say yes early — everyone gets the same thing, no one feels left out later (p. 96).
- **Sell into the Hyper Buying Cycle.** Buyers entering something new (weddings, new hobbies, babies, moving) spend a huge chunk in a short window — keep making offers during it (p. 96).
- **Speed of access raises value.** "A $10,000 thing you get later is worth less than a $10,000 thing you get now." Put the product in their hands before they say yes: "It's way harder to give something back than it is to say no." (p. 97)
- **Bundle and Name.** One 'ask' for nine sales; name packages by customer type or result ("Fastest results," "Transformation package," "Minimum package"); later "peel" items out as downsells (p. 97).
- **Integrate Upsells Into Delivery.** His meal plans included optional supplement suggestions, so clients asked about supplements; Gym Launch training suggested optional softwares, so gym owners bought them (p. 97).
- **BAMFAM (Book-A-Meeting-From-A-Meeting).** End every appointment by scheduling the next. He quotes "my big fancy public CEO friend Sharran": "A customer should know the next time they see you—and why—before they leave." (p. 97)
- **Upsell as many times as makes sense.** "The second worst thing that happens is they say no. The worst thing is if they would've said yes but you never asked." (p. 97)
- **Upsell Guarantees, Warranties, Insurance.** Charge 5–50% extra for a guarantee instead of giving it free. An art studio that used to replace damaged portraits for free started charging an extra 10%; now 30% of customers buy what was free — "Pure profit baby." (p. 99)

**When it works best:** immediately after a purchase, the instant a new problem revealed by the previous offer becomes apparent ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 99). No specific caveats beyond the general ethics warning and the general failure modes (wrong thing, wrong time, wrong way) ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 91–92).

## Menu Upsell

**Tagline: "You don't need that…you need this."** Definition (verbatim): "In a Menu Upsell, you tell customers which options they don't need. Then, tell them what they do need, their preferences, and how to get their value from it." It combines up to four tactics in sequence: Unselling → Prescription Upselling → A/B Upselling → Card On File ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 101, 105).

**Origin stories.** All from his gym supplement sales ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 101–105):
- *December 2013 (A/B Upsell + Card On File):* supplements weren't selling despite ~$5,000 of shelf inventory and science-heavy pitches. After 19 failed nutrition consults in one day, on customer #20 he nervously forgot his script and asked, "You've got a protein shake for breakfast, do you like chocolate or vanilla?" She asked his favorite ("Chocolate") and bought; then "kiwi or strawberry lemonade pre-workout?" — second sale. Then, since her card was already on her membership contract: "You just wanna use the card we have on file?" — "Yep—that's fine." He then "sold the next twenty customers in a row" and emptied the shelf (pp. 101–103).
- *August 2014 (Prescription Upselling):* while upselling $5,000–$10,000 of supplements per challenge-group launch day, a question-heavy customer forced him to write step-by-step instructions on scratch paper; the next customer wanted the same. He wrote instructions directly on the order form (how much, when, next to each item), then: "I got all your instructions here, do you want to just use the card on file?" — she bought everything without being asked anything. His conclusion: "detailed and personalized instructions upsell more people than vague and general suggestions. I call this prescription upselling." (pp. 103–104)
- *November 2016 (Unselling):* launching gyms on the road, selling supplements to 40–50 people a day ("Two people. Every 30 minutes. 12 hours straight."); the supplement marathons alone covered his flight, hotel, and advertising costs. Sold out of an item, he pointed a customer to a cheaper alternative down the street ("about $20 less. It's not as good, but it'll do OK for the first month"), then crossed out the weight-gainer and testosterone booster she didn't need ("You're not trying to gain weight, right?") and prescribed from what remained — "She loved it and bought without hesitation." (pp. 104–105)

> "Later, I kept products just to cross them out! I call this process unselling." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 105)

**The four tactics** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 105–106):
1. **Unselling** — tell customers what they *don't* need (cross it out) to build goodwill and emphasize what they do need.
2. **Prescription Upselling** — tell them what they *do* need; works when offering a choice is inconvenient and one thing solves the problem. Two components: (a) explain how it integrates with what they already bought; (b) personalize and detail how to maximize its value — "you explain how to use it as if they already have," removing the not-buying option.
3. **A/B Upselling** — ask which of two options they prefer ("A or B") instead of yes/no; either answer is a sale. "Basically, when you give people the option to not buy, some don't buy."
4. **Card On File** — "Do you want to use the card on file?" lowers the hidden costs of buying (picking a card, taking it out, being reminded of past ugly buying decisions, rush hassle). "Just know if you make it easy for people to buy, more people will."
He closes: "This took me ten years to learn. May you get the same value in ten minutes." (p. 106)

**Example scripts** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 106–108):
- *Massage therapist:* Unsell — "We have a lymphatic massage available, but you're not pregnant or just out of surgery right? So we can cross that out." Prescribe — heat first, trigger points, dynamic stretches for the sore shoulder. A/B — "before work or on the way home?" Then: "Wanna just use the card on file?"
- *Dog food:* Unsell the small bag, puppy food, and redundant vitamins ("you've got a big dog!"). Prescribe joint chews at each meal, a heartworm wafer every 90 days, and book next month's visit now (BAMFAM embedded). A/B — "does your dog prefer beef or chicken flavor?" Card on file.
- *Digital product:* Unsell — "You don't need all eight courses yet. You just need to solve X, Y, & Z" — send free material for X and Y, sell one course for Z. Prescribe a particular way to do the course, an hour a day. A/B — "direct message or phone support?" / "start today or Monday?" Card on file.

**Further notes** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 108–109):
- **Make anything A/B sellable:** quantity (one bottle or two?), start dates (tomorrow or Monday?), payment (cash or card?), flavors, time slots, media (read or listen?), delivery speed, sizes, colors, materials, personnel (John or Sara?), communication (call or text?).
- **Add a Nudge** for inexperienced customers: "This is my favorite," "X is usually a safe bet," "a lot of people love this," "Tuesday's sessions are a little smaller if you like that," "Amy does great with high schoolers." Hint: nudge the product you want to move faster.
- **Sold out? Take payment and delay delivery** — collect cash, order it, set delivery expectations; sells more selection without carrying inventory. "You'd be surprised how well this works."
- **Employees love unselling:** encourage staff to help customers "game the system" on purpose — insiders showing customers maximum value. "Everyone wins." (The book references a visual, "The 'Economist' Play," illustrating the choice architecture.)

**When it works best:** "Menu Upsells work best when you have multiple offers available." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 111) **Caveat:** unselling the lower-margin items is what earns the right to upsell the higher-margin ones — without genuine fit, it would break the trust that powers it ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 104–105).

## Anchor Upsell

**Epigraph: "The only thing worse than making a $1,000 offer to a person with a $100 budget…is making a $100 offer to someone with a $1,000 budget."** Definition (verbatim): "With Anchor Upsells, you offer premium stuff first. If the customer gasps, you offer a cheaper but acceptable alternative." Presenting a premium version 5–10x the price of your main offer first makes the main offer look like a much better deal, so more people buy it. Two bonuses: anchored customers spend more than they normally would, and some customers actually buy the super expensive thing ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 112, 114).

**The 5 steps** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 114):
1. Present the Anchor — the really expensive thing.
2. Get "The Gasp" — expect the customer to freak out about the cost.
3. Come to the rescue — ask if they care about what makes it premium.
4. Present your main offer — expect relief; they see the better deal.
5. Ask how they wanna pay — "Which card do you prefer?"

**Origin story.** 2016, after starting Gym Launch but before making money — five prior years "showerless, in sweats and a tank top" — a fashionable friend ("Businessmen don't wear tank tops Alex") sent him to a suit shop. He budgeted $500. The owner put him in a suit whose tag read $16,000; his face turned red (The Gasp). The owner: "Do you care about the designer much?" — "Not at all." — and instantly draped the next suit: $2,200. Relief; he bought it, plus ~$300 of socks, handkerchief, and shirt — "after seeing the $16,000 price tag on the first suit, everything seemed cheap." Net: "I spent five times more than I had budgeted and felt OK about it." The pro move: the owner "already had the 1/8th price suit pulled before my reaction." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 112–116)

**Examples with numbers** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 115–116):
- *Lawn care:* anchor — owner's cell number, fancy mulch, natural pest control, bi-weekly maintenance at $1,000/week; main — team's number, generic mulch, normal pest control at $200/week.
- *Painting (physical product):* anchor — super protective packaging + 20-year insurance + gift wrapped = $1,000; main — normal packaging + 1-year insurance + sticker = $200.
- *Newsletter (digital):* anchor — all previous issues + new issues + 24 hours early = $199/mo; main — new issues only, on time = $19/mo.

**Sub-tactics and rules** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 116–117):
- **Don't fake the anchor:** "If You Treat The Anchor Like A Fake, So Will The Customer." Actually sell it and let them genuinely consider it; only move on after they pause, hesitate, or ask for something else. Glossing over the premium offer means it was never really offered.
- **Make a premium offer you'd genuinely be happy to deliver.** A friend's anchor failed because "He made up some BS that he didn't really want them to buy"; tweaking it to something he'd happily deliver tripled his profits. "And if they don't [buy it], you still anchored them."
- **The Gasp is good.** If there's no gasp, they find the premium reasonable — "just ask them if they wanna use the card on file (ha! Go for it!). Just don't do a gasp of your own when they say yes."
- **Feature engineering:** keep primary features identical between premium and main; change only secondary features. "Most people just want a suit. A few people want a fancy suit. A suit is the primary feature. The material, designer, etc. is secondary." Offering the primary features at ~1/5 the price after anchoring makes the main offer "basically the same thing for way less."

> "Gasps used to really stress me out. But then I realized something huge. The bigger the gasp, the more they bought." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 116)

**When it works best:** "Anchor Upsells work best when the lower-price offer has the same core functions as the premium one" (p. 114); an anchor 5–10x the main price is most effective (p. 117). **Caveats:** faking the anchor loses trust and wastes time ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 114, 116–117).

## Rollover Upsell

**Tagline: "Wanna just roll it forward?"** Definition (verbatim): "Rollover Upsells credit some or all of a customer's previous purchases toward your next offer. And this—in my experience—gets way more people to take it." You decide three things: **who** to upsell, **what** to upsell, and **how** to roll the credit over ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 119, 121).

**Origin story.** June 2014. He'd run a Win Your Money Back Offer ($600 fitness program, money back if they hit a goal) at his gym for a year. It "crushed" on sales but produced zero recurring revenue: winners put their $600 toward three months of membership and churned before ever paying out of pocket — effectively "buy six weeks get three months free," so revenue "started at zero every month." His friend Justin ran the same attraction offer but rolled winnings into a year-long membership: "We just give them fifty bucks off per month for a year." Winners still get their money back — it just takes a year — but start paying immediately. Hormozi spent two days at Justin's gym "spying" and calls it "the missing link in my Money Model": "This one thing, the Rollover Upsell, changed my life, thousands of gym owners' lives, and the lives of our customers." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 119–120)

**The WHO — four situations** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 121):
1. Re-engage customers who left a while ago.
2. Rescue upset customers as a better alternative to a refund.
3. 'Rescue' other people's upset customers (competitors').
4. Upsell regular/current customers.

**The WHAT and HOW.** Roll credit toward more of what they got, something better, or something new/different — "To make money: Roll their credit over to something more expensive." Apply all or part of the credit, up front or spread over time ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 121).

**Examples with scripts and numbers** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 121–122):
- *Chiropractor winback* (old patients, 6 months since last purchase, credit up front): "Hi Mrs. Banks, I wanted to give you your money back, do you have a minute? … I want to give you $500 of your money back as credit towards staying pain-free for good. Is that of any interest? Great…let's get you in…"
- *Dentist saving an upset customer:* paid $200 for a cleaning, teeth don't look whiter → front-load the $200 as credit toward a whitening package (multiple sessions + at-home kit + deep cleanings).
- *Software stealing competitors' upset customers:* find negative reviews and roll over what they owe the competitor as credit toward a longer agreement with you: "Hi John, I saw your negative review on their product and it really upset me. To make it up to you, I'll credit whatever payments you have left with them to switch to ours. This way, you don't lose a thing and you start getting the benefits now. Fair enough?"
- *Membership spread:* a $600 first purchase becomes a $50/month discount over a 12-month membership — best done immediately after the first purchase ("I can do the rollover upsell at any time, I just prefer to do it right then").

**Sub-tactics and rules** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 122–123):
- **Use rollovers to attract new customers:** credit what they paid somebody else; find leads by scraping contact info from negative product reviews. "Bonus: Create a way for people to complain about products in your industry… Then, Rollover Upsell all of them. Nasty."
- **Rollover before refunding:** "If you did a bad job (hey, it happens), roll over for a 'do over.'" Or roll toward something different.
- **Winback campaign numbers:** "I made personalized videos for 200 past customers offering them $4,000 of credit to return. We got about 20% to take the offer. One day of recording videos got us an extra ~$1,900,000 of annual revenue. Worth It." (p. 123)
- **Urgency — one-time only:** "A once-in-a-customer-lifetime offer. They don't get to sleep on it." If they pass, they can still pay full price later.
- **Pricing rule:** make the upsell at least 4x the rollover credit, so even a full credit is at most a 25% discount. "Bigger discounts make you less profit per sale, but they get more sales."
- **Partial credit is fine:** "I roll over whatever amount I think would incentivize them to buy the next thing. Test to find the sweet spot."

**When it works best:** paired with more expensive next offers ("when paired with more expensive offers, it skyrockets 30-day profits"); at the moment of first purchase, after complaints, or for lapsed customers. It works with or without a Win Your Money Back attraction offer: "You can Rollover Upsell anyone anything. (Even stuff people bought at other businesses…mwahaha)." **Caveat:** you must retain profit after discounting — discount depth trades margin for conversion ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 120–121, 123).

## Doctrine

Section conclusion principles ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 126):

- The purpose of upsells in the Money Model is to get more cash from customers, faster. If the Attraction Offer already covers acquisition and delivery costs, upsells stack profit on top.
- "Solve rich people problems, they pay better."
- The section closes by transitioning to Downsell Offers — what to do when they say no ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 126).

> "Many businesses go from burning cash to printing it—overnight." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 126)
