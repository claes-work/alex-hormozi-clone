# Attraction Offers

Attraction Offers are the first stage of Hormozi's Money Model: offers of something free or discounted that "generate leads and convert them into customers" and thereby "turn advertising into money" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 37). Their purpose, restated in the section's conclusion, is "to turn strangers into customers. And, to do it in a way that gets more cash up front" — ideally enough cash to cover both the cost of acquiring the customer and the cost of delivering the product "multiple times over," so the business can pay itself back and fund the next customer ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 88). The underlying logic: most businesses are cash-constrained because they "spend too much to get customers and make too little from them," so acquisition gets solved first; and since strangers cannot verify value but absolutely understand price, discounts make anything a great deal — "the greatest discount of all being free" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 36–37). Hormozi presents five favorite Attraction Offers: Win Your Money Back, Giveaways, Decoy Offer, Buy X Get Y Free, and Pay Less Now or Pay More Later ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 38–39). A sixth chapter in the section, the Free Goodwill Offer, is a meta-demonstration: a review-request chapter that itself is a goodwill offer ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 84–87).

## The Grand Slam Offer (foundational, 2021)

The attraction offers above are all vehicles for one earlier, canonical idea. In [[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]] (2021) — the foundational "what you sell" volume the later books build on — Hormozi defines the **Grand Slam Offer**: an offer that "cannot be compared to any other product or service available," combining an **attractive promotion**, an **unmatchable value proposition**, a **premium price**, and an **unbeatable guarantee** ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF p. 42). It lets you sell in a **"category of one"** — a value-driven rather than price-driven purchase — which raises response rate, conversion, and price at the same time, escaping the commodity "race to the bottom" ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF pp. 41–42). Verbatim thesis: "Make people an offer so good they would feel stupid saying no." ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF p. 25, quoting Travis Jones). The Win Your Money Back offer, above, was "the first Grand Slam Offer that Gym Launch taught."

## The Value Equation (2021)

The engine inside every Grand Slam Offer. **Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)** — raise the top two, drive the bottom two toward zero, and perceived value approaches infinity ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF pp. 80–81). The best companies compete on the denominator, making the outcome immediate and effortless (Apple, Amazon, Netflix) ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF p. 81). "Perception is reality" — the prospect must *perceive* the change ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF p. 82). Corollary on the Time-Delay driver: "The only thing that beats 'free' is 'fast.' People will pay for speed." ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF p. 88). (A ~2020 proto-version — believability + high ROI/value + a monetization model — is noted in [[money-model|money-model]].)

## Offer enhancers (2021)

Four levers that "enhance an offer without changing it" ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF p. 140):

- **Scarcity** (cut supply) — three types: limited total supply, limited supply of a specific bonus/class, and never-available-again ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF pp. 142–150).
- **Urgency** (limit time) — four methods: rolling cohorts, rolling seasonal urgency, promotional/pricing deadlines, and exploding opportunity ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF pp. 152–157).
- **Bonuses** — widen the price-to-value gap by stacking many named bonuses, each with its own value, rather than discounting; ideally at least **one bonus is worth more than the entire price** ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF pp. 159–167).
- **Guarantees** — reverse risk. Four families: **Unconditional** (no-questions refund), **Conditional** (refund only if the buyer did the work), **Anti-Guarantee** (all-sales-final, framed as a feature), **Implied** (performance/revenue-share). A stronger guarantee usually nets more even when refunds rise (worked math: 117 net vs. 95 net = 1.23x) ([[../../sources/2021-100m-offers|$100M Offers]], PDF pp. 169–184). This is the taxonomy behind the conditional guarantees used in the Decoy and Pay-Less-Now plays above.

## Win Your Money Back

**Tagline (verbatim):** "If you do x within y time within z rules, you can get it free." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 40)

**Definition (paraphrase of the book's):** "You set a goal for the customer and tell them how to reach it. If they reach it, then they qualify to get their money back or get it back as store credit" — the customer pays up front and can win the money back, as cash or (better) store credit ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 41).

**Origin.** June 2013: in a room of experienced gym owners, gym owner "Danny" told Hormozi how a difficult prospect proposed the deal himself — "I give you $500. You train me for eight weeks. And if I hit my goal, I get my money back. But in return, you can use my results to market your business." The client hit the goal, spent the refund on more training, and marketing his before/after pictures brought in **thirteen referrals**. Hormozi adopted the structure ("the core stayed the same: pay now with a chance to get your money back later") across private training, group training, and private/group nutrition coaching, then put it in ads: "My cost of getting customers went way down and my leads exploded!" It grew his gyms better than any other offer and became the first Grand Slam Offer that Gym Launch taught to gym owners ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 40–41).

**Three qualification structures** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 42):

1. **Results** — the customer wins if they hit the outcome no matter what they do (make $X/month, get Y customers, lose Z lbs). "They bet on their own ability to reach the goal."
2. **Actions** — the customer wins if they do what you ask regardless of result (attend all sessions, log progress, take pictures, do homework). "They bet on their ability to follow directions."
3. **Actions and Results** — both required. "They bet on their ability to follow directions and that your directions will get them the result." Rationale: goal-chasers often lack the skills to win a pure results bet.

Bottom line (verbatim): "Customers put money down. If they do the stuff OR they get the result OR both—they get it back as cash or store credit." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 42)

**Examples from the book** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 42–43):

- **B2C — "Free 28-Day Blueprint":** deposit X dollars; get it all back for attending all consulting calls, posting weekly progress in the group, journaling daily in the app, and attending the feedback and transformation sessions (calls and meetings double as opportunities to make more offers).
- **B2B — "5 Customers In 5 Days Free Challenge":** deposit X dollars; get it back for sending 100 messages per day, reporting stats, attending daily training, posting homework, and attending the day-5 consulting call.
- **Physical product — "Put 1,000,000 Miles On Your Car, And Get A Free Car":** drive a new car 1,000,000 miles, turn it in, take pictures and appear in a press release; the full original purchase price is credited toward the next car. The book notes "This was an actual offer."

**When it works best.** With businesses requiring continuous customer effort — "Stuff People Start And…Quit": starting businesses, learning new skills, losing weight, building fitness, beauty regimes, self-care, time management, mental health management. It works with new customers (steepest discount possible — 100%), current customers, and previous customers (bigger incentives win them back). Hormozi: "To this day, I have never seen a better way of setting up a program for results—a true win-win." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 44)

**Key numbers and mechanics** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 44):

- The offer "has generated over $1 billion in sales industry-wide."
- Data collected from thousands of gyms: about **10% of all customers will ask for their money back**. "If you can't stomach it, don't do this."
- It still makes money because (1) many won't qualify even under realistic conditions, and (2) qualifiers often stay as customers — but only if there's something else to buy, so have an upsell ready to apply their winnings.
- Testing showed **store credit and cash back got the same number of customers** — "so you might as well offer store credit." To still advertise "free" with store credit, pair it with an unconditional satisfaction guarantee; adding one "never materially affected the number of people who wanted their money back." Check with legal counsel.

**Good criteria have three characteristics** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 45):

1. **Easy To Track** — train customers on exactly what to do; ideally tracking happens automatically (phones track steps, word processors track word count, cameras date photos).
2. **Gets Customers Results** — realistic criteria do fine: "If you think the criteria look too easy, you've probably gotten close to realistic." Rule: whatever the best customers do to get the best results, make everyone do it.
3. **Advertises The Business** — bake advertising into the criteria: posting about participation, tagging on social media, referring, leaving reviews and testimonials.

**Applying store credit (flagged IMPORTANT in the book).** Spread winnings over a longer period or a bulk package costing more than the winnings. Worked example: product costs $200/month; customer wins $600 credit; instead of three free months up front, apply $600 over 12 months → a $50/month discount → they pay $150/month. Customers may use the credit however they want, but present this option first: "people fall off if they don't pay something. A discount over the long haul keeps them engaged over the long haul." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 46)

**Meetings are offer opportunities.** Make check-in meetings part of the money-back criteria. His gyms ran three: Nutrition Orientation (before pictures → supplement offer), Progress Check-in (membership offer), Transformation Feedback (after pictures → membership offer again; if already a member, a discount for prepaying a year) ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 46–47).

**Make everyone a winner.** Sell the program as strictly conditional, but about halfway through, make the next offer as if the customer already won — credit the program toward the next one "whether you hit the short-term goal or not." This lowers anxiety and keeps them longer. At the end of the program, let the "losers" win too: if someone fails and refused the first upsell, act like they won anyway ("You started. That's the biggest victory of all… we'll credit your entire deposit towards staying with us long term"). Keep this practice private so everyone stays surprised and grateful at the upsell ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 47, 49).

> "You'll turn that frown upside down and they'll love you for it. Remember—we don't get customers to make a sale, we make sales to get customers." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 47)

**Caveats.** Hard rule: "Only use a Win Your Money Back Offer if your refund rate is below 5%. Otherwise, fix your product before doing this. You risk giving too many refunds." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 48). Personal rule — "Don't Take Blood Money": if a customer asks for a refund, entitled or not, give it. "If someone doesn't want me to have their money, I want it less than they do. … Just focus on getting the next customer." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 45). The real money comes from people who succeed with the program and then have something else to buy: "The more results you deliver, the more money you'll make. Think long." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 48–49)

## Giveaways

**Tagline (verbatim):** "Many will enter…many will win." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 50)

**Definition (book's wording, paraphrased):** Giveaway Offers advertise a chance to win a big prize in exchange for contact information and whatever else you want; after picking a winner, you offer everyone else the big prize at a discounted price. Also goes by "scholarship," "sweepstakes," or "raffle" — all mean "enter for a chance to win" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 52).

**Origin.** August 2020: a phone call with the owner of a fitness certification business who advertised a full-ride scholarship. Applicants answered questions like "Why should we pick you for the full-ride scholarship?" One person won the full ride; everyone else got a call awarding a **partial scholarship** — "Most of them join our program on the spot." Because leads never knew the actual price beforehand, the discounted price against the full-ride's value read as huge savings. Demand was so strong they had to cap entries to service the sign-ups; the same play worked for the trainers they certified ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 50–52).

**Six-step mechanics** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 52–54):

1. **Pick a Grand Prize** — the thing you want everyone to buy; assign it a monetary value as a price anchor ("if you sell $5,000 worth of value for $2,000, then advertise the $5,000 value!").
2. **Pick your promotional offer** — the "partial scholarship": your core offer enhanced with a discount, a bonus, or a minor change from the Grand Prize "to ethically justify a price reduction." Bigger discount = more compelling; bigger assigned Grand Prize value = better.
3. **Ask for contact information** — plus permission to contact entrants for follow-up promotions; survey for eligibility ("Do you own a vet clinic?", "Why should you be selected?") and set qualifying actions (attend a call/event, make a post, enter a group) that promote the giveaway and demonstrate interest.
4. **Put the giveaway on a deadline** — he likes **three to seven days** from promotion start; update entrants daily across email, DMs, texts, and social posts with countdown, value, and social proof: "Keep the hype alive!"
5. **Announce the Grand Prize winner publicly, contact everyone else privately** — everyone else qualifies for the promotional offer (a "participation trophy"); give a reason-why if needed (their answers were so compelling you felt obligated to give them something); add a second deadline — the promotional offer expires in **seven days** — with a second countdown and a way to book a claim call.
6. **Explain the cost-to-value using their discount** — **rule of thumb: make your core offer discount equal to 10%–30% of your gross margins.** Worked example: Grand Prize valued at $5,000, retail $2,000; everyone else gets it for $1,800 (10% off retail), framed as $5,000 of value for $1,800 — "a 10% discount becomes a 64% difference in cost-to-value!"

> "Bottom Line: Remember, everyone that entered the Giveaway showed interest in your thing. And if somebody shows interest in a thing you have to offer—offer it to them." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 55)

**Examples with numbers** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 55):

- **Dentist — "Free Perfect Smile Giveaway":** Grand Prize free invisible braces ($6,000 retail); promotional offer: $2,000 gift card for braces.
- **Physical products — "Free Year Of Organic Dog Food":** Grand Prize $1,000 retail; promotional offer: $300 gift card usable only with a one-year subscription.
- **Services — "Free Ultimate Giveaway":** Grand Prize free 1-year package ($5,000 retail); promotional offer: $2,000 voucher toward a 1-Year Service Agreement.
- **Consulting — "Free 16-Week Turnaround Giveaway":** Grand Prize $12,000 retail; promotional offer: $6,000 Partial Scholarship.

**Refinements** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 55–58):

- **Eligibility questions double as sales ammo** ("Why should we pick you? Why this program? Why now? What's your goal?"). Trade-off: "the more work you make it to enter the fewer people will enter, but the more qualified they'll be—so find your sweet spot" (p. 56).
- **Failure diagnosis:** "If Your Giveaway Doesn't Work, It Means Your Grand Prize Wasn't Grand Enough." One of his portfolio companies gave away tickets to its own event — barely any interest. Retried with a **$50,000 bundle of equipment** from a well-known industry supplier plus their core product for a year — "this time, it crushed (surprise)" (p. 56).
- **Two prizes for twice the leads:** tell entrants that if someone they refer wins the Grand Prize, the referrer wins one too — endless "entries" via referrals, and referrers stay invested in their referrals' success, keeping quality high. Example given from Skool.com, "a platform I co-own for people to build and monetize communities" (p. 56).
- **Scarcity:** limit by time (e.g., seven days), entries (e.g., 5,000), or both — he likes both, matching entry caps to the number of people he can connect with inside seven days (p. 57).
- **Downsells:** some won't buy even discounted. Open the claim call saying they qualified for two prizes; present the promotional offer first; if declined, "offer the same discount by percentage on any other product you have that makes sense for them" (p. 58).
- **Recurring revenue variant:** apply their discount over the longest period they'll agree to; set the subscription to auto-bill at normal rates after the discounted period ends (p. 58).

**Legal caveats.** He's not legal counsel, but his no-brainers: somebody actually has to win the Grand Prize; make prize and qualification rules clear; make clear more than one person can win a prize; ask legal counsel about the rest ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 55).

## Decoy Offer

**Tagline (verbatim):** "Which one do you think will get you the best results?" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 61)

**Definition (book's wording, paraphrased):** Decoy Offers advertise something free or discounted; when leads ask to learn more, you also present a more valuable premium offer with more features, benefits, bonuses, and guarantees. Side-by-side placement makes the premium's extra value visible. "They either take the decoy version or the premium version. … either way, you can close everyone. This makes it cheap and profitable to get new customers. And any business can use it." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 63)

**Mechanics (two steps):** 1) advertise a lesser, smaller, or simpler version of your premium offer as the decoy; 2) when leads engage, offer both options but emphasize the premium one ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 63–64).

**Origin.** June 2014: John, "another early mentor" who had retired early, taught Hormozi on drives to his lake house — price vs. value, the trade-offs of low-cost offers, high-volume low-price models, recurring vs. one-time revenue, simplicity. John's tanning salons ran a **"5-Day $5 VIP Tanning Pass"**: everyone thinks they can get tan in five days but never as tan as they want, and rushing burns. Staff gave "the turkey talk" — a Thanksgiving turkey takes three hours; double the temperature to halve the time and you burn it — so the color they want takes five to ten sessions with rest days, always more than five days. Then: "Let's just credit your VIP pass toward your first month. Why buy so many $25 day passes when members get unlimited access for just $19.99?" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 61–62)

**The offer that saved his gym.** Five years later (circa 2019), fitness leads got too expensive — sellers were barely breaking even. Hormozi designed: option one **free** (one session per week); option two, an **"Ultimate" version for $399** (unlimited sessions, 1–1 coaching, more personalized elements, and a guarantee: results or repeat the program free). Result: "about eight out of ten people" took the $399 option — "We're crushing it." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 62–63)

> "You have to know what gets results for customers better than they do. This makes our premium offer the clear solution." (John's lesson, quoted by Hormozi; [[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 63)

**Examples** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 64–65):

- **Lemonade stand (physical products):** advertise "Free Week of Lemonade" or "$1 Week of Lemonade." Decoy: water + powdered lemon + corn syrup. Premium: organic, all-natural, vegan, gluten-free, imported Italian lemons, cold-processed and shipped to your door, plus other flavors like sparkling rosewater lemonade.
- **Float tank center (service):** "Free 6-Week Stress Release" or "$6 6-Week Stress Release." Decoy: one float per month + DIY at-home stress-relief exercises. Premium: floats twice a week for 6 weeks, 1–1 consulting, journal, sleep routine, satisfaction guaranteed.
- **Gym (local business):** "Free 21-Day Transformation" or "$21 21-Day Transformation." Decoy: once-daily workouts in a Skool.com group, general nutrition plan, recordings only, no support, no guarantee. Premium: unlimited workouts, personalized nutrition plan, 1–1 accountability, results guaranteed (or another 21 days free).

**How to build and sell it** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 65–67):

- **Build the decoy** with fewer components, older models, less personalization — and remove any guarantees. "Your Attraction Offer only has to get leads to engage. Nothing more." (p. 65)
- **Advertise benefits, not features:** sell the dream outcome ("a transformation in 21 days, not workouts and meal plans"); product details belong in the sales presentation, not the advertising. "Private jets and rowboats can both get you to an exotic island, but the premium option is certainly more enjoyable." (p. 65)
- **Four ways to advertise the same discount** (for a $100/month year-long thing sold at $900/year): Percentage Off (25% off), Absolute Amount ($300 off), Free Portion (3 Months Free), The Total Package (One Year For $900 ($1,200)). "They all mean the same thing. It's worth testing to see which one converts better in your market." (pp. 65–66)
- **Make the contrast huge:** make the decoy as basic as reasonable and the premium as awesome as possible — "The bigger the contrast, the better the deal, and the more customers will take it." (p. 66)
- **Free vs. discount economics:** free Attraction Offers get more leads; discount offers get fewer leads but a higher show-up percentage. Use discounts when show-up rates are low — "especially important for businesses where you have a high cost of someone not showing up (think doctors, lawyers, dentists, etc)." (p. 66)
- **Present premium first** if possible; keep the decoy "in your back pocket." If a lead asks for the decoy first (or you're legally required to present it), ask: "Are you here for free stuff or lasting results?" Most say results → skip to premium. If free stuff → present the decoy, immediately contrast it with the premium, and ask which will get them to their goal faster. (p. 66)
- **Selling posture:** get excited about the premium ("Present it as superior to the decoy offer, because it is") and use the assumed close: "This is what everyone does. This is just a formality. Let me get your ID and credit card so you can get your value. No hype. Just friendly disposition. Almost bored over how regularly people buy." (p. 67)
- **Surprise benefit (optional):** if someone takes the decoy, throw in a few low-/zero-cost premium features to build goodwill, over-deliver, and increase later upsell take rates — "Remember—they're still leads!" (p. 67)

**Caveat.** Money can still be made from decoy-takers (you learn to deliver the decoy and maximize its upsells). "Expect to make money fast. If you're not, then make the contrast between offers larger." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 68)

## Buy X Get Y Free

**Tagline (verbatim):** "Buy one puppy, get two puppies free!" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 69)

**Definition (book's wording, paraphrased):** when customers buy something, they get other stuff free — the more free stuff and the higher its value, the better it works. Key insight: free offers get far more attention than discount offers, but you can't just give your only product away — "by selling more than one thing at once, you can turn Discount Offers into even stronger Free Offers" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 71).

**Origin.** Downtown Nashville, 2020: the **Boot Factory**, with its giant neon cowboy-boot sign, advertised "BUY 1 PAIR GET TWO PAIR FREE." A decade earlier, as a kid on bar crawls, Hormozi thought it was dumb ("How could they give away so much stuff and stay in business?"). Now he saw it: boots marked down twice to a "final offer" of **$600 for the pair** — they had tripled the price of a single pair so it came with two more pairs, converting a fair price into a free offer. Bachelorette parties filed in for matching boots, and the store had grown much bigger. "It was brilliant." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 69–70, 72)

**The reframe math** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 71–73):

- **T-shirts, same price:** sell 3 shirts at $10 each ($30 total) OR sell 1 shirt for $30 and give 2 free — "It's the same price, but way more free stuff!"
- **T-shirts, discounted:** sell 3 shirts at $6.67 each ($20 total, a 33% discount) OR sell 1 shirt for $20 and give 2 free.
- **Boot Factory:** one pair = $200; the offer = buy one pair for $600, get two pairs free; customers still buy three $200 pairs for $600.
- **Services ("3 Pairs of Boots" for 18 months of service, all $1,800):** Good: "Buy 12 Months Get 6 Months Free"; Better: "Buy 9 Months Get 9 Months Free"; Best: "Buy 6 Months Get 12 Months Free." "Everyone pays the same price for the same amount of service. But, the third option is the most compelling. (Hint: It has the most free stuff!)"

**When it works best.** For "stuff that makes sense to buy more of or get longer access to" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 76). It also pulls cash forward: some of his service businesses used to take a whole year to make their money until "Buy 6 Months Get 6 Months Free" attracted far more customers than month-to-month — "Even better, they got paid up front for it!" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 73)

**Rules and refinements** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 73–77):

- **Raise prices first — actually:** "permanently raise prices to accommodate the discount. Don't lie. Actually raise your prices." Since all new customers come in on the offer, change pricing "for a season at least." Bonus: "plenty of people might still take your doubled prices a la carte and break your limiting beliefs around pricing. You're welcome." (p. 73)
- **Give more free than paid:** "Buy ten get two free is not as strong as buy two get ten free" — prefer "buy one get two" over "buy two get one." (p. 73)
- **Free things can differ from paid things:** mix and match — e.g., buy one $10 shirt, get $20 of free socks. More cheaper things can beat fewer expensive things ("Buy 1 Shirt, Get 3 Socks Free" still reads as "buy one thing get three things free"). (pp. 73–74)
- **Free beats percentage discounts:** rather than sell $10 shirts for $6.67 each (33% off), "you may get more interest (and make more money) by offering 'buy one shirt for $20 get two free.' Test it." Reason: "More people know the value of free than the value of one shirt." (pp. 74–75)
- **Fast cash from existing customers:** existing recurring customers will happily "Buy Ten and Get Two Free" even at current prices — but **limit the offer to 10% of your customers** to keep recurring cash flow healthy. (p. 75)
- **Prepaid customers keep buying:** "these are people who spend the most money. … Their wallets have been 'refreshed' with new money that is dying to make it into your pocket. Don't get in the way!" (pp. 75–76)
- **One-time buyers → go big:** the Boot Factory's tourists buy once ever, so make that purchase as large as possible and provide the value to back it up — "If you only have one shot, you may as well make it count!" (p. 76)
- **Retention effect:** the offer lengthens stay — "If normal customers stay for three months, then 'Buy 2 Get 2 Free' will keep them for four months (or whatever you set it at)" — creating more opportunities to make offers. (pp. 76–77)

**Caveat — cash management.** "Do Not Make Offers Like This If You Can't Manage Money." If you collect a year of payments in a month, budget to deliver the whole year.

> "Don't be a goon and buy yourself a house with the cash meant for servicing your customers. Selling stuff you can't deliver on breaks the law and ruins your reputation. Deliver on your promises." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 75)

## Pay Less Now or Pay More Later

**Definition (book's wording, paraphrased):** give people a choice to pay full price later OR a discounted price now. "This play works so well because we remove all risk from the customer. They pay later and only if they like it. So it combines the benefits of a delayed payment and a satisfaction guarantee." The chapter opens with the Benjamin Franklin epigraph "Time is money." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 78–79)

**Origin.** June 2016: Hormozi registered for the world's fastest reader's training, advertised as "Double your reading speed in 3 hours, or it's free." Registration options: (a) put your credit card down for $0 and get billed $297 tomorrow — cancel before then if your speed doesn't double, attendance required for eligibility; or (b) pay $97 right now and get the recordings as a free bonus, "which won't be for sale anywhere else." Hormozi took option (a); after two hours his reading speed doubled ("He earned his $297"); he declined the eight-week upsell but walked away with "a brand new Attraction Offer." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 78–79)

**Mechanics** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 79):

- **Pay Later:** advertised as "free" since they can choose to pay or not; gets lots of leads engaged; crucially, gets their **card on file**; they can cancel any time before the charge goes through if they hate it.
- **Pay Now:** offered as a follow-up after they accept Pay Later; carries a **20–50% discount** and greater bonuses; the card already on file makes paying easy.
- Either way "you've got customers, and likely, some profit" — but have something more, better, or newer to upsell when the time is right.

**Examples with numbers** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 79–80):

- **"Find Your First Real Estate Deal—Free 3-Day Workshop":** Pay Later: $0 for the workshop, billed $500 at the end unless they cancel. Pay Now: $299 plus recordings, a 1–1 call with a certified distressed-property expert, and printed materials. Upsell: **$30,000** to be taken through every step of closing the first deal within six months, plus legal templates, an advisor to vet the investment, and an inspection checklist.
- **Local service — "Trim Your Hedges For Free":** Pay Later: $0 lawn cut + hedges, then $599 after. Pay Now: $369 for lawn cut + hedges + lawn treatment. Upsell: $199/month lawncare. The rep estimates on site, offers both options, and upsells after the work is done.
- **Physical product — "14-Day Clothing Trial":** Pay Later: $0 now, billed $149 in 14 days (product must be returned in like-new condition before billing to qualify for the guarantee). Pay Now: $97 for the clothing plus a matching accessory. Upsell: monthly clothing subscription.

**Rules** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 80–83):

- **Promise a clear yes/no result** deliverable within the time frame — "If you don't, they will ask not to be billed. Duh." Example: shoulder pain rated 1–10 before and after. "Keep the promise simple, clear, and measurable. This avoids unnecessary cancellations." The promise must be "easy to track, difficult to refute, and a clear yes/no result."
- **Make the guarantee conditional:** customers can only cancel billing if they qualified (e.g., he had to attend the reading training) — "They can't say you suck if they never try it." Track conditions (attendance, appointments, turning in data) and make the criteria "what people do to get the most value out of the product. Win-Win."
- **Bonuses** are covered in a full chapter of $100M Offers; he points to free video training at acquisition.com/training/offers.
- **Optimization rule:** too many taking Pay Later → discount Pay Now more, add better bonuses, or both; too many taking Pay Now → do the opposite.
- **Recurring revenue variant:** pay a higher ongoing rate starting 30 days later, or pay less today and keep the lower rate for good, plus bonuses.

**Caveat — cancellation threshold.** "If More Than 10% Of 'Pay Later' People Cancel Their Payment. You promised too much, the guarantee conditions are too low, or the price is too high." Some cancellations are inevitable: "No matter how well you deliver, some people will cancel their payment. That's okay. Factor it in your costs of doing business and live your life." Give extra attention to customers claiming non-delivery before the deadline ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 81, 83).

## Free Goodwill Offer

Not a business framework per se — a meta-demonstration: a chapter that itself IS an attraction/goodwill offer, asking the reader to leave a review. Epigraph: "He who said money can't buy happiness hasn't given enough away." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 84)

**Mechanics of the ask:** it opens with a question ("Would you help someone you've never met if it cost you nothing, but you didn't get credit?"), frames the review as helping "a struggling entrepreneur you've never met," stacks six reader testimonials as social proof, gives platform-specific instructions (Audible: three dots top right → rate & review; Kindle/e-reader: scroll to the bottom and swipe up; otherwise Amazon), and applies urgency: "If you tell yourself you'll do it later, ins[t]ead, please do it now. It takes less than 60 seconds to change someone's life forever." It closes with identity/community framing — "you are my kind of people. Welcome to #mozination. You're one of us." — signed "- Your biggest fan, Alex." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 84–87)

## Premium cash plays: Fast Cash (2025)

The mirror image of a free/discount attraction offer: instead of the cheapest way in, a *premium* limited-time offer run to a business's **warmest** audiences (existing, past, and engaged leads) to "rip cash out of a business" fast ([[../../sources/2025-100m-fast-cash-playbook|$100M Fast Cash Playbook]], PDF pp. 7–8). Because those leads are already acquired and the price carries ~90% margin, the revenue drops almost entirely to profit.

- **"10x the 10%":** "If you get 10% of your customers to pay 10x the price…you double your revenue" — and it's nearly all profit ([[../../sources/2025-100m-fast-cash-playbook|$100M Fast Cash Playbook]], PDF p. 8). In his early days, cash plays were ~15% of revenue but "nearly half my profit."
- **"Add a zero" pricing:** take average cart/transaction value and add a zero (10x); "get frisky" and multiply by five (up to 50x). The price self-selects buyers with purchasing power ([[../../sources/2025-100m-fast-cash-playbook|$100M Fast Cash Playbook]], PDF p. 11).
- **Construction:** one-to-three core components plus three bonuses drawn from *unscalable* value (attention, personalization, convenience, status, speed, access, network, secrets), layered in one at a time; cap sales at ~5–10% of customers so it sells out (by day 3–4) ([[../../sources/2025-100m-fast-cash-playbook|$100M Fast Cash Playbook]], PDF pp. 10–11).
- **Two seven-day cadences:** **Push-to-Consult** (staged texts/emails at 7, 6, 5, 3, 2, 1 days + 12 hours out, each adding a bonus and a countdown, ending "sold out") vs. **Push-to-Automated-Checkout** (build launch-style tension, then open the cart to everyone at once, first-come-first-serve) ([[../../sources/2025-100m-fast-cash-playbook|$100M Fast Cash Playbook]], PDF pp. 12–14).
- **Cadence:** run **every 90 days** (once a quarter); a worked gym example turns four plays into +$229k cash and +22 customers (net income ~4x) ([[../../sources/2025-100m-fast-cash-playbook|$100M Fast Cash Playbook]], PDF pp. 18–19). Governing maxim: "Revenue is vanity. Profit is sanity. Cash is reality." (PDF p. 4)

## More attraction-offer types (Lost Chapters, 2025)

Cut offer chapters from the `$100M Series`, added to the five above. A split-test note frames all of them: across four independent tests (10 markets each), free vs. premium front ends had identical close rate and average ticket — only lead volume differed, with free front ends cutting lead cost 5x+ ([[../../sources/2025-100m-lost-chapters|$100M Lost Chapters]], PDF pp. 25–26).

- **Freemium** — give the core away free, monetize a paid upgrade tier ([[../../sources/2025-100m-lost-chapters|$100M Lost Chapters]], PDF pp. 93–96).
- **Free Pick-Your-Price** — let the buyer name their price (including $0); captures demand and data while surfacing willingness-to-pay ([[../../sources/2025-100m-lost-chapters|$100M Lost Chapters]], PDF pp. 97–101).
- **Free With Alternate Revenue Stream — "the no-sale sale":** give a rejecting prospect something free (a take-home program, then a free orientation) and monetize a *different* product (e.g. supplements). These no-buyers spent **~50% MORE** on the alternate product than regular clients, and whether the first upsell is taken is "the greatest predictor of back-end conversion" ([[../../sources/2025-100m-lost-chapters|$100M Lost Chapters]], PDF pp. 103–107).

## Doctrine

Section-level principles that run through all the Attraction Offer chapters:

- **Any offer works alone, in any order (Principle 7):** "Any Offer Can Be Used On Its Own, At Any Time, in Any Order." Each offer can be profitable standalone; sequenced correctly at the right times, they compound into a $100M Money Model ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 36).
- **Solve acquisition first:** most businesses are cash-constrained — they spend too much to get customers and make too little from them — so the Attraction Offer is always the first problem to solve ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 36).
- **The free / discount / $1 continuum:** anywhere Hormozi says "free" you can substitute "discount" or "$1" and vice versa — "They exist on a continuum because they all discount a product to some level—even if you discount it by 100%!" ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 37).
- **How free makes money:** people look for one thing and buy another by accident; Attraction Offers make them do it on purpose. "One free thing is awesome. Two free things are awesomer. And, maybe to get those two free things, they have to buy one. That's how we make money on free stuff." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 38)
- **Always have deadlines:** urgency belongs in three places — to enter, to claim, and to use. "I like hours, but I have gone up to five days. In short, always have deadlines." Pair with scarcity: limit by time, by entries, or both ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 57–58).
- **Make sales to get customers:** the thesis inversion — "we don't get customers to make a sale, we make sales to get customers." The real money comes from people who succeed with the offer and then buy something else; always have an upsell ready ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 47–48).
- **Keep customers paying:** spread credit and discounts over time — "You never want people to stop paying you." ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 49)
- **Protect your reputation:** unlike credit card debt, "you can't file bankruptcy to erase a bad reputation. Once you have a bad one, it sticks for life." Deliver on your promises ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 36, 75).
- **The payoff claim:** Attraction Offers "turned $1,000 into $10,000,000 in ten months because when I got returns I kept doubling down. A Grand Slam Attraction Offer changes your business (and life) forever." He applies them "at one time or another to every business I own." (Self-reported; [[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 88)
- **What comes next:** Attraction Offers hand off to Upsell Offers ("whatever we offer next"), which often make the majority of the profit — the McDonald's burger math takes per-customer profit from $0.25 to $3.00, an 11.6x increase ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 89–91).
