# Sales Communication (Tone & Delivery)

Alex Hormozi's framework for *how* words are delivered in a sale — the verbal/vocal
persuasion layer, distinct from the *what* (the script logic captured in
[[sales-frameworks]] under CLOSER and objection handling). His thesis: you underperform
in sales for one of two reasons — you say the **wrong words** (scripting) or you say the
right words the **wrong way** (tone). This page covers the tone side.

Primary source: [[../../sources/2025-11-12-yt-sGakuNs9mT4]] ("How to Speak So Well People
Give You Money"). Secondary: [[../../sources/2025-11-30-yt-spXH1kJ6q-Q]] (declarative vs.
procedural knowledge; the SPCL influence framework).

> Alex is the sole speaker in the primary source, so all of it is valid Alex-attributed
> voice/persona data.

**Prerequisite — you must have a script.** Tone only matters on top of consistent words.
He treats the script as non-negotiable foundation: *"It's like trying to build a skyscraper
on a foundation of sand"* — and claims 95% of sales teams lack one, which is why they aren't
world-class. The words come from CLOSER (see [[sales-frameworks]]); this page assumes them.

## The 5 voice levers

Hormozi rejects vague tone advice ("use a curiosity tone," "be more aggressive") as useless
and replaces it with five concrete, controllable levers: **three constants** you hold steady
and **two variables** you modulate. All five can be notated on a script with just three
punctuation marks (period, ellipsis, question mark).

**Three constants** (hold these steady the whole conversation — together they maximize
comprehension):

1. **Volume** — loud enough to be clearly heard. Matters more in person than on the phone,
   where the listener controls their own volume.
2. **Speed** — the sweet spot is roughly **135–185 words per minute** *(self-reported range,
   not sourced)*. Too fast → trust drops; too slow → *"they think you're an idiot."*
3. **Articulation** — enunciate and round out every word; don't mumble.

**Two variables** (modulate these to steer the moment):

4. **Pauses** — pause *length* signals *intent*. A short/soft stop draws attention to a point;
   a full stop expects a response. Notated `...` (short) vs `.` (full stop).
5. **Frequency / pitch** — voice *up* = a question, i.e. soliciting a response. Notated `?`.

**The buy-ask combines both variables: long pause + rising pitch.** After you ask them to
buy, it signals it's the customer's turn — then you stop talking:

> "Ask them to buy and you shut the [expletive] up. That's how it works."

Two coaching translations he uses to convert vague feedback into a lever:
- "Have more confidence" → **speak louder** (volume).
- "You sound nervous" → **you're speaking too fast** (speed); fully enunciating auto-slows a
  nervous fast-talker.

The **close needs the most tone management** of any moment — highest emotion/adrenaline, so
too loud+fast reads aggressive and too slow reads dull.

Tone changes meaning independent of the words — his demo: the sentence "I didn't say he hit
his wife" means something different depending on which word you stress.

> ⚠️ Self-reported / loosely-cited stats on this page: the **135–185 WPM** range, and the
> **"8-second pause after the ask → ~30% more closes"** claim (see below). Alex attributes the
> pause stat to "about three meta-analyses (one he recalls as Columbia)" but explicitly says he
> doesn't remember the exact sources. Treat both as self-reported, not registry-verified.

## Breathing the script

Delivery only becomes natural once the script is memorized to the point of automaticity —
what he calls **"breathing the script"** rather than reading it:

> "If you want to sound like you're not reading a script, stop reading the [expletive] script."
> "You will no longer be saying it. You will be breathing the script."

**The blackout memorization method:** read the full script aloud; cross out one word; reread
the whole thing; cross out another word; repeat until the entire page is blacked out. You end
up repeating the script as many times as it has words. In the sales-training context he pairs
this with role-play "until your eyes bleed" (~50–60 reps) with real-time interruption for
immediate feedback ([[../../sources/2025-11-30-yt-spXH1kJ6q-Q]]).

**Why it matters:** so you don't have to think about your own words and can instead *listen*.

> "A lot of sales is in your ears, not from your mouth. The person who speaks the most in the
> sale loses."

> "No one is above the script." — consistent words are the foundation; the rest is
> *"hullaloo and mythology."*

## Discovery: pulling teeth → chunking up

Discovery is **matching the prospect's problems to your products**, and it's where most sales
are won or lost — objection handling is only ~20–30% of the sale; *"you'll typically lose them
in discovery."* You ask questions until the prospect *states a problem*, because:

> "People believe what they say. They believe very little of what you say."

**Pulling teeth** (for uncommunicative prospects): alternate two questions —
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "Can you be more specific?"

— until you extract a concrete, *"meaty"* problem rather than a vague complaint.

**Chunking up** (once you have a meaty problem): recap it up into a category you sell against
— e.g. *"Sounds like you have a marketing issue... is that fair?"* Collect 2–3 such chunked
problems, then transition by getting them to agree that solving those would grow the business.
At that point, in his words, *"they've already accepted the sale."*

This connects to the [[../../sources/2025-11-30-yt-spXH1kJ6q-Q]] selling principle: **give
away declarative knowledge** (knowledge *about* the problem — builds trust in discovery and
content) but **sell procedural knowledge** (how to actually implement). Discovery surfaces the
declarative gap; the offer sells the procedural fix. (He credits the education system, not
himself, for the declarative/procedural distinction.)

## Universal closes

He teaches **seven universal closes** — memorizable, and workable against any *non-logistical*
objection (logistics, e.g. genuine scheduling/money-timing, are handled separately). Only one
is detailed in the source:

- **The "very reason" reframe:** whatever reason they give *not* to buy, reframe it as the
  reason they need it *most* —
  > "The problem that you're solving to not buy is the problem that we solve through you buying."

Underlying philosophy of closing:
- The reps who close the most are the ones who **ask the most times** — the skill is asking
  as many times as possible without annoying the prospect. Every objection overcome is just an
  excuse to ask again.
- You never win by being right:
  > "You're never going to sell someone by being right ever... People don't buy from people
  > they hate."
- **The 8-second silence** *(self-reported stat — see the warning above)*: after the ask,
  staying silent ~8 seconds reportedly closes ~30% more sales. Give people time to process;
  don't fill the silence.
