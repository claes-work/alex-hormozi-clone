# Business Math — Hormozi's Money-Math Canon

The set of "money-math" equations Alex Hormozi uses to evaluate, buy, and scale
businesses. He frames the whole thing as elementary arithmetic — "addition,
subtraction, multiplication, or division. There's nothing else" — and as a
**language** you must speak to make money: "This has to be your first language
if you want to get in business... Your ability to conceptualize ideas is
directly proportional to your vocabulary within a given domain."
([[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]). Everything below is
Alex-attributed (solo talks) and therefore persona-eligible.

The spine is a single ratio — **LTGP:CAC** — with the rest of the canon being
either its component parts (lifespan, gross profit, CAC), a rescaling of it
(ROIC, enterprise value), or a forward projection of it (sales velocity,
payback, the golden ratio).

> ⚠️ **Self-reported figures.** Most concrete numbers in these talks (Starbucks
> per-customer LTV of ~$14,199 and ~38,000 locations; "1 in 250 businesses
> reach $10M/yr"; Gym Launch's "$100K → $10M, 100:1" first year; the
> hair-extensions coach's ratios) are Alex's own claims, unverified against any
> registry or independent reporting. Treat all such figures as illustrative,
> not audited.

## LTGP:CAC — the fundamental economic unit

Alex calls **Lifetime Gross Profit to Customer Acquisition Cost** "the
fundamental economic unit of the business" and says this one number predicts how
far a business can scale, how profitable it will be, and how far it can outspend
competitors ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]],
[[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]).

- **LTGP = LTV − COGS.** In common speech people say "lifetime value," but "what
  they really mean is lifetime gross profit" — you must subtract the cost of
  *delivering* the good/service, not just multiply price by duration. Owners
  conflate LTV and LTGP because Silicon Valley / software runs ~100% gross
  margins, where the two numbers coincide; anyone with people, inventory, or
  manufacturing must run off gross profit
  ([[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]],
  [[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]]). See
  [[#gross-profit-not-revenue-churn--lifespan]] below.
- **CAC** = *total* cost to acquire a customer, including labor — not just ad
  spend. Add marketing payroll + ad spend + sales commissions over a period and
  divide by customers acquired. Measure over a **full year** to strip out
  volatility. Example: $10K ads + $10K marketing payroll = $20K ÷ 20 customers =
  **$1,000 CAC** ([[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]).
- **The ratio.** 1:1 means you spend a dollar and get a dollar back; 20:1 means
  $20 of profit per $1 in. Rule of thumb: **always want greater than 3:1**; 5:1
  is great — "otherwise you'll be too constrained to scale." A very high ratio is
  a "license to print money": at 100:1 you can be a tenth as efficient on ads and
  still make 10:1, because "you can put $1 in and get $100 back tomorrow." It also
  buys a legal/ethical "monopoly" — since ad markets are attention auctions,
  whoever can afford the highest CAC outbids every competitor for their customers
  ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]],
  [[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]).
- **CAC is roughly constant across a market; LTV is the variable.** The durable
  advantage of the biggest businesses is how much LTV they've *stacked*, not
  cheaper acquisition ([[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]).

### The two levers

Only two ways to move the ratio: **raise LTGP** or **lower CAC**
([[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]). See [[money-model]] for the offer
mechanics behind each.

- **Raise LTGP (eight ways):** raise price, decrease cost of goods, get more
  purchases, cross-sell, upsell (better), sell more quantity (more), down-sell
  (worse/cheaper to turn a "no" into a "yes"), sell fewer/smaller versions.
  Stacking a **back end** is the biggest lever: the hair-extensions coach had a
  34:1 front end ($2,000 course, ~$67 CAC) and a 7:1 coaching offer ($15,000).
  Lead with the cheap high-ratio offer, ascend ~20% of buyers into the $15K back
  end (back-end CAC ≈ zero): 20% × $15K = $3,000 added → $5,000 LTV, moving the
  ratio from ~30:1 to ~75:1.
- **Lower CAC:** improve efficiency down the funnel; the closest thing to a
  "silver bullet" is nailing the **offer**.
- **Directionality trick:** build **LTV back-to-front** (start from the most
  expensive thing, stack gross profit backward) but optimize **CAC
  front-to-back** (early funnel steps touch the largest % of people — you can
  lift a 1% CTR to 3%, but you can't take a 60% show rate to 180%). Exception:
  chase a clear outlier metric (a 5% show rate vs. a 60% industry average is a
  12x fix).

Choose the offer with the **best LTV:CAC to lead with**, then stack everything
behind it — unless the TAM is tiny (most owners are nowhere near saturation).

## Payback period & ROIC

**Payback period** — how fast you recover CAC. Faster payback = a healthier,
more resilient business, especially in a downturn. Four levers to pull it
forward ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]]):

1. Charge first + last month upfront.
2. Add a **fee** (onboarding / enrollment / setup / initiation).
3. Add an **upsell** (a fee is mandatory, an upsell optional — functionally the
   same: they drag revenue upfront).
4. **Third-party financing** that fronts the full amount for a slice of the risk.

**Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)** — the same LTGP:CAC ratio applied at the
**business/location scale** instead of the per-customer scale. Cost to open a
location (buildout, marketing, staff, recruiting — e.g. $100,000) vs. annual
profit at capacity. Wants at least 3:1, ideally 5:1 ($100K in → $500K/yr
profit). Because his own time and effort are involved, he demands ~300%/yr
minimum, not a passive-market 10%: "I'm not buying one customer, I'm buying a
custom printing machine... These are not passive investments for me."
Key sub-metrics: payback period, one-year return, profit at capacity
([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]]).

> ⚠️ **Internal CAC inconsistency (source QGcjweehrvU).** In the worked example
> the fully-loaded CAC is calculated as **$1,224** (5 employees at $50K/mo + $50K
> software & ads = $100K/mo ÷ 83 customers), giving a ~5:1 ratio against $6,000
> LTGP. But in the payback-period section he states the CAC "was $600." $600 is
> actually the **per-customer-per-month gross profit** figure from earlier in the
> same talk, so this reads as him reusing the round $600 number for
> illustration rather than a second CAC calculation. The source page treats it as
> a caption/illustration slip, not a corrected figure — flagged here so neither
> number is mistaken for a stable canonical CAC.

## Gross profit, not revenue; churn → lifespan

**Gross profit, not revenue**, is the single mistake Alex says most owners make:
"You don't want revenue to confuse you, and this is like one of the biggest
misunderstandings I see business owners make."

- **Definition.** Physical product: price − cost of goods sold. Service example:
  $10,000/mo revenue per employee (10 customers) − $4,000 employee cost = **$6,000
  gross profit = 60% gross margin** (the absolute number and the percentage are
  the same metric, two ways). Gross profit **excludes fixed costs** — rent and
  non-delivery staff are not subtracted; each additional unit's gross profit is
  what the owner reinvests or pockets
  ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]],
  [[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]).
- **The revenue trap.** Pen example: 10 pens at $10 = $100 revenue, but $9 cost
  each = only $10 lifetime gross profit; spending $50 to "make a $100 sale" is
  really spending $50 to make $10 of GP. The agency counter-example: a firm
  bragging about 5:1 ROAS was actually losing money — after subtracting ad spend
  ($1K) and delivery cost ($1,100/account) from $2,000 revenue, they'd "figured
  out a way to take $2,000 and turn it into zero"
  ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]],
  [[../../sources/2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]]).

**Churn → lifespan.** For subscription/service businesses, average customer
**lifespan = 1 ÷ churn**. Back-of-napkin: 100 customers at month start, 90 still
there 30 days later = 10% churn; 1 ÷ 0.10 = **10 months** average lifespan (works
on any interval). Product businesses instead use average number of purchases
(e.g. "average customer buys 4.3 times"). Then **LTGP = gross profit per period ÷
churn** — $6,000 GP ÷ 10 customers = $600/customer/mo × 10-month lifespan =
**$6,000 LTGP per customer** (equivalently $2,000/mo ÷ 20% churn = $10,000 LTGP).

Churn has outsized leverage: **5% → 10% cuts lifetime revenue in half; 10% → 5%
doubles it.** "That's why churn numbers and retaining customers are so
important." Use trailing 90-day / 6-month / 12-month averages because small
businesses are volatile ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]]). See
[[retention]].

### Gross margin is the master variable (2026-03)

By March 2026 Alex sharpens the gross-profit point into a stated hierarchy:
**net margin can never exceed gross margin**, so gross margin is the ceiling on
everything downstream ([[../../sources/2026-03-28-yt-XpqeenUIMHI]]).

- **Net ≤ gross, always.** A 50%-gross-margin business could only reach 50% net
  with zero acquisition, overhead, admin, or non-delivery staff cost — "basically
  impossible." Verbatim: *"Your net margins cannot exceed your gross margins."*
  Diagnostic corollary (paraphrase): if something is wrong with the business,
  "it's usually because your margins are off. You're mispriced."
- **The 80% minimum for services.** Alex states a hard personal rule — *"I will
  not get into a business with less than 80% gross margins. I won't do it"* — and
  cites fixing gym pricing to ≥80% as proof services can hit it. He treats
  60% vs. 80% not as "close" but as "a different stratosphere."
- **Worked pricing math (paraphrase).** Off a $100 delivery cost: 70% margin ⇒
  ~$333 price (~$233 GP/customer), 80% ⇒ $500 price, 90% ⇒ ~$1,000 price — small-
  looking margin gaps compound into materially different per-customer profit. Earn
  the high margin by de-commoditizing via branding + a real sales process (the
  pink-Himalayan-salt example); price off the value of the customer's solved
  problem, not off the ease of delivery.
- **Rule of thumb: 30-day cash collected ≥ COGS + CAC.** The gross profit
  collected in a customer's first 30 days should exceed cost of goods sold plus
  cost to acquire them; once it does, "that customer pays you back all of that
  cost" and funds the next acquisition — a self-financing growth loop and a
  tighter, front-loaded cousin of the [[#payback-period--roic]] idea
  ([[../../sources/2026-03-28-yt-XpqeenUIMHI]]).

## Enterprise value = customers × LTGP ÷ risk

The same unit economics projected forward and out to what the whole business is
worth. See [[scaling-and-enterprise-value]] for the full treatment.

- **Sales velocity × LTGP** = the "hypothetical max" revenue the business evens
  out at if nothing changes. 100 customers/mo × $6,000 LTGP shows the steady
  state — reveals whether a business will *grow into* or *shrink toward* its
  current revenue.
- **Sales velocity ÷ churn** = the hypothetical max number of customers (logos)
  it will sustain. 100/mo ÷ 10% churn = **1,000 customers**. Pairs with the
  previous equation (money at max vs. headcount at max)
  ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]]).
- **Opportunity = (potential units × LTGP) ÷ risk** (bonus 8th equation). TAM
  ("total addressable market") × LTGP sized against risk. TAM is "important but
  not knowable" (the Munger/Buffett knowable-vs-important framing); companies
  routinely start niche and expand (Amazon: bookstore → everything store;
  Facebook: college network → everything social). Focus on the knowable,
  important risks ([[../../sources/2023-12-20-yt-QGcjweehrvU]]).
- **Enterprise-value multiples** — the *same* $10M topline / $3M profit business
  is worth ~**$15M as a service** (5× earnings), ~**$20M as e-commerce** (2×
  topline), and ~**$100–200M as SaaS** (10–20× topline). Doubling each adds ~$15M
  / ~$20M / ~$200M respectively — "same amount of work, you get more for what you
  put in," the acquisition.com lever
  ([[../../sources/2024-04-19-yt-IFpHasZ7jN4]]). See
  [[scaling-and-enterprise-value]].

## The golden ratio (referrals ÷ churn)

A business's **passive growth rate = % of customers who refer ÷ % who churn/exit
per interval** ([[../../sources/2024-04-19-yt-IFpHasZ7jN4]]).

- 10% refer vs. 10% leave = **flat**.
- 20% refer vs. 10% leave = **doubles every interval, regardless of size**.
- Most businesses refer only ~2% and churn ~10%, so they must **buy ads to refill
  the bucket**.

Rule: only once **referrals exceed churn** should you "pour gas on the flame"
(spend to scale) — otherwise paid acquisition is just refilling a leaking bucket.
Alex says Skool's passive-growth metrics were the best he'd ever seen, citing
this ratio as one reason for his investment. This connects the acquisition side
(LTGP:CAC) to the [[retention]] side: referrals and churn are the two dials of
organic compounding.

## Hire-with-equity vs. raise-cash — one equation (2026-03)

Asked (March 2026) how to fund the hiring needed to scale a $1M coaching
business toward $5M, Alex reframes the classic build-vs-buy financing choice as a
**single math equation** rather than a philosophy: compare the **cost of equity
given to an employee** against the **cost of equity sold to an investor** to raise
the cash that would instead pay that employee in full cash
([[../../sources/2026-03-24-yt-norSbF8HNxY]]).

- **Cost of equity to the employee** = the delta between full-cash comp and
  cash-plus-equity comp — i.e. how much stock it takes to close the compensation
  gap you're *not* paying in cash.
- **Cost of equity to the investor** = the stake you'd sell to raise the cash to
  pay that person fully in cash instead.
- **Illustrative example (paraphrase).** Raise at a $100M valuation, sell 5% ⇒
  $5M cash. The question becomes: can that $5M pay everyone in cash for a net loss
  of only 5% of the business — *or* could you get everyone you need for **less than
  5%** in equity directly and skip the raise entirely? Whichever costs less equity
  wins. Verbatim: *"So, it's a math equation."* See
  [[scaling-and-enterprise-value]].
