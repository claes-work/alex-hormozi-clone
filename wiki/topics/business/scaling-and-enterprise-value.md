# Scaling and Enterprise Value

How Alex Hormozi thinks about growing a business past the founder, expanding *who* you
can sell to, and ultimately converting "a thing that makes money" into "an incredibly
valuable asset that can change your life forever"
([[../../sources/2023-08-31-yt-p9PD0m7a0DM|The Formula for Creating a $100M Business]],
2023). This page synthesizes his scaling and enterprise-value/exit frameworks. Individual
claims are dated because his positions evolve; direct quotes are marked as voice data.

---

## 1. The growth stage ladder: $0 → $10M

Source: [[../../sources/2026-04-28-yt-_TZEJAYUeCo|The Exact Roadmap From $0 to $10M]] (2026).

Hormozi maps the journey as a sequence of barriers, each one a different problem:

- **$0–$3M — the barrier is ignorance.** "People just don't know what to do."
- **Six figures = one channel, one product, one avatar.** Getting to six figures is
  "literally just like sell something to someone. Like that's it... one channel, one
  product, one avatar." Paraphrase: nothing else is required.
- **Seven figures ($1M) = do that same thing, plus "the word *consistently*."** Whatever
  acquired the customers — "reach outs... content... paid ads... affiliates...
  referrals" — just feed that input consistently and you get a consistent output.
- **$1M is deflated as "only 20k a week. It's not like a huge number"** — a signature
  Hormozi move of shrinking a scary number to make it attainable.
- **$1M–$3M = increase output of the one main thing a bit, add baseline efficiencies,
  and build the core team.** The dominant task is **getting the owner out of delivery**:
  "Going one to three is getting the person out of delivery to a large extent."
- **A ~$3M business is typically 5–15 employees**, "and there's usually really only like
  two-ish good ones and the rest are okay."

### The "$3M three boxes" (client-qualification criteria)

$3M–$10M is the minimum level **Acquisition.com** ([[../../entities/acquisition-com]])
takes clients on — "not because there's something magical about 3 million," but because
$3M typically signals three things have been proven (Hormozi live-corrects "two boxes...
I guess there's three boxes"):

1. **Product–market fit** at some level — people want what they sell and the sales
   resonate.
2. **At least one reliable acquisition channel** currently running.
3. **A core team in place** — possibly dysfunctional with few key players, "but at least
   they have some semblance of structure."

Meeting these minimums gives enough leverage to start helping the company grow.

> Related benchmark from an earlier source: Acquisition.com is described as investing in
> **$3M–$100M businesses** in [[../../sources/2023-07-01-yt-M2VP64Tc60w|5 Ways to Scale ANY Business]] (2023).

## 2. The five ways to scale (expanding your TAM)

Source: [[../../sources/2023-07-01-yt-M2VP64Tc60w|5 Ways to Scale ANY Business]] (2023).

Hormozi's core claim: **scaling = expanding your addressable market (TAM)**, and there
are only five ways to increase *how many people you can sell to*. He uses a triangle
metaphor — you can move up, down, sideways, broader, or narrower.

> "There are only five ways to scale how many people you can sell to for any business, and
> I'm gonna give you all five and a framework to think through you can apply to any
> business you ever start for the rest of your life."

**Sequence matters:** new businesses should **niche down first**, then expand over a long
horizon — "Apple/Netflix aren't niche" is a question of *sequence*, not a counterexample.

| Move | What it is | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Up-market** | Sell a higher/more leveraged version of the core market (salons → chains/franchises; Salesforce SMB → Enterprise) | Bigger deals, far less churn, no non-payment | Harder to sell, 6–18 month sales cycles |
| **2. Down-market** | Sell to the smaller/aspiring segment (salons → stylists; owners → aspiring entrepreneurs) | Endless, emotional buyers, "an ocean of them" | Hard to build recurring revenue, payment issues, unrealistic expectations, ~30% go out of business yearly |
| **3. Adjacent** | Same promise, different delivery (salons → nail salons; chiropractors → physical therapists) | Extracts max value with little product change | Slow, vertical-by-vertical; needs trench knowledge of the new vertical |
| **4. Broader** | Generalize the promise across all verticals (salons → beauty; gyms → fitness in general) | 10x your TAM overnight, scale faster | Harder to deliver same value; you fight every entrenched specialist |
| **5. Narrower** | Add qualifications to the same avatar (owner → owner with $30k/mo + ≥2 employees) | Can 3–4x profit with little new infrastructure | Requires disciplined 80/20 analysis |

Key quotes:

> "If you increase the quality of your customer you increase the quality of your company."
> (on going up-market)

> "The advantage of going down-market is that you never run out of customers — they're
> always making new ones every day."

> "If you're better at marketing and sales than you are at product and delivery, going
> down-market might be a better fit for you in the beginning until you get better at
> delivery."

> "If you want to go fast, go broad. But if you want to be good, you're staying narrow for
> a little and scaling out."

> "You're not changing the nature of your avatar, you're just adding qualifications to
> them." (on going narrower)

> "You have to know who you're not going to serve."

**Going narrower — the "Your First Avatar" method:** a fractal/80-20 customer analysis
(credited to the former head of packaging & pricing at Vista private equity): find the
20% of customers who bring the most money, find their common traits, and reverse-engineer
the buying process so every prospect becomes that higher-quality buyer. From a chapter cut
from *$100M Offers*.

**Bonus "door #6":** just keep getting better at what you do. Big businesses eventually do
all five moves; most operators should do only **one at a time**. "Growth just happened as
a consequence of not stopping and steady consistent improvements over time."

## 3. Scale zero, oceans-not-lakes, and one-vehicle focus

Source: [[../../sources/2021-03-25-yt-sKCrYIWPHLc|I Built My $100M Business WRONG...]] (2021).
Explicitly framed as *his* lessons for *his* season, not universal rules — from a
mastermind of eight entrepreneurs (>$500M/yr combined) where he was the second smallest
(self-reported ~$32M that year).

- **Scale zero.** To grow without becoming the bottleneck, the founder must reduce the
  business's need for him toward zero. "For me to get bigger I must get smaller within the
  company. I must become less and less and less important to the company's success in
  order for the company to grow without me being the bottleneck of it." / "Scale zero.
  Like get yourself down to zero, because only then I'll be able to scale."
- The founder progressively outsources, in order: **administrative work → back-end
  customer service → sales → marketing → messaging/positioning** — until only the biggest
  decisions remain.
- **Oceans, not lakes.** The bigger players all attacked large markets ("oceans") while he
  was in a single niche ("lake"). "They were all in oceans and I was in a lake." Note this
  is a *sequencing* nuance, not a contradiction of "niche down first" (§2): he says
  niching to gyms early was still the right call — "going after a lake when starting out
  is not a bad idea."
- **One primary vehicle.** Running three businesses as CEO was a "terrible idea" that cost
  him "probably two years of development." "For just about every human besides Elon Musk
  it's not feasible." People who make enormous money make it in *one* vehicle, then may own
  passive pieces of others (with a hired CEO/exec team) — they are not active partners in
  many.
- **"Simple scales."** The biggest businesses in the room sold simple things.
- **High-margin AND high-value.** Everyone successful sold high-gross-margin offers; the
  ideal (hard to find) is high margin *and* high value — "it's easier to have high margin
  with low value."
- **Talent hierarchy:** doers → managers → leaders → decision makers. He wants people who
  have "already run and won the Olympic gold," who plug in "pre-loaded with the solutions,"
  "batteries included."
- **"Decide" = to kill.** He reframes decision via the "-cide" root: "When you decide
  you're actively choosing to kill off other things... kill off all other options."

### Talent Grid predecessor (2021-05-31)

The 2021 **Talent Grid** is an early people-scaling framework that sits underneath "scale
zero." Hormozi grades skill and delegation across three parallel ladders: **What → How →
Who**, **Individual Contributor → Manager → Leader**, and **Doing → Teaching →
Systematizing**. The top level is not merely delegating a task, but building "the machine
that builds the machine." His practical test for a truly delegated portfolio company: he
is no longer in that company's Slack channel, only in a weekly operator meeting, and the
company grows between meetings without his direct involvement
[[../../sources/2021-05-31-yt-gpKz22P84iM]].

## 4. Leverage, the holding-company thesis, and out-sacrifice

Source: [[../../sources/2021-08-02-yt-ug5N9qmFVKQ|How I'm building a BILLION DOLLAR empire working 2-3 days per week]] (2021).
Aimed at entrepreneurs already doing $1M–$5M/yr.

- **Redefine growth as character, not novelty.** "We think growth means new when... we
  could redefine growth as meaning we develop the character traits to be able to persist
  and continue to do the same things over and over again."
- **The business is not there to fix your insecurities.** "The business doesn't exist to
  satisfy our internal deficiencies"; it "just needs to provide value to the customer."
  Starting new companies to scratch the novelty itch is the trap: "you have lots of
  businesses and you don't have any money."
- **Channel innovation into front-ends, hooks, creatives, and product lines** that attract
  more avatars — not into new businesses. "Why would I learn another business when I'm
  already really good at this one? I'd have to learn everything from ground one. That just
  sounds hard and stupid."
- **Compounding beats out-hustling.** A $100k/yr business growing at the market rate (~10%)
  for 40 years compounds into enormous wealth: "most people are always trying to beat the
  market when in reality if you just matched the market you would become extraordinarily
  crazy wealthy." "I'm not gonna outwork to get to a billion. You have to make good
  decisions and you have to let compounding work in your favor."
- **The holding-company / leverage constraint.** His stated goal: a **$1 billion/yr
  revenue portfolio while working 2–3 days a week** and training 1–2 hrs/day. Self-reported
  run-rate ~**$85M/yr** across the portfolio (2021) — an early articulation of the
  Acquisition.com holding-company thesis. Doublings every ~7 years put $85M near a billion
  ~28 years out.
- **Multiple metrics for success:** grow net worth AND have a good marriage AND stay in
  shape AND work limited hours. "Adding constraints makes the goals far more interesting."
- **Out-sacrifice, not out-work.** "I will out-sacrifice everyone. So when my competitors
  are going out to dinner, when my competitors are hanging out with their kids... I was
  like I will get ahead." Big goals begin with putting "everything you have on the altar of
  success."

## 5. Enterprise Value = customers × lifetime gross profit ÷ risk

Source: [[../../sources/2023-08-31-yt-p9PD0m7a0DM|The Formula for Creating a $100M Business]] (2023).
Origin story: he grew a business from **$0 to $4.4M/month in 20 months** and "it also
sucked" — a fragile rocket that depended entirely on him. An investment-banking friend told
him it could sell "for millions of dollars," but not in its current state. That launched
Acquisition.com's **"value acceleration method."**

The formula:

> **Enterprise Value = number of customers × lifetime gross profit ÷ risk**

- **Whom you're providing value to (for valuation):** "when we're talking about value
  within a business the person we're providing value to is the investor who's going to buy
  a share of it."
- **Customers:** "if you 10x the number of customers you will 10x the value of the
  business, sometimes more." To make more money you either **get more customers** or
  **make each customer worth more**.
- **Lifetime gross profit (LTGP)** = profit over the customer's lifetime, **not** lifetime
  revenue. Worked example: 10 meals at $10 = $100 lifetime revenue, but at $1 profit/meal =
  **$10 lifetime gross profit**.
- **Risk** = how likely the future cash flows are to continue. He invokes Buffett ("Uncle
  Warren"): "the value of a business is taking all the future cash flows that it will ever
  generate discounted into the present."
- A business that merely **"makes money"** is different from an **"incredibly valuable
  asset."** The method's whole point is converting the former into the latter — and there
  are **ten levers** that each "unlock another level of Enterprise Value." They are teased
  in this clip; the sibling video six days later enumerates all ten — see
  **§5b** below.

> "That's what began this journey of figuring out how to take something that just makes
> money into an incredibly valuable asset that can change your life forever."

## 5b. The 10 enterprise-value criteria ("the perfect business" / "Mosey Lisa")

Source: [[../../sources/2023-09-06-yt-HKbFUWJwEG0|I Reverse Engineered the Perfect Business]] (2023).
Six days after the §5 clip, Hormozi "reverse engineers" the maximally *valuable*, sellable
business — the **"Mosey Lisa"** (his Mona Lisa pun) — enumerating the ten levers §5 only
teased. Same **"value acceleration method"** and same origin story ($0 → $4.4M/month in 20
months but "it also sucked"). Each criterion turns a money-making *job* into a sellable
*asset* by moving one term of the §5 equation: **EV = customers × lifetime gross profit ÷
risk** — it adds customers, raises LTGP, and/or (most often) cuts **risk**. The umbrella:

> "You get paid for what you do. You get returns on what you own."

> "It's not about selling more, it's not about making them worth more — it's about making
> sure that those never stop happening."

1. **Leadership team running the day-to-day** — the owner can leave and the business
   maintains *and grows* without them; most small businesses die if the owner leaves, so no
   one can buy them. His litmus: step away and the company grows for **six straight months**
   without him jumping in; hire for a purpose, under-title (manager → director → VP → COO),
   and weight track record over "would we be friends."
2. **Marketing without the founder** — if you *are* the ads/content, that's a job, not an
   asset; deliberately **transfer the brand association** to someone else (his gym-launch
   ads went Alex → Alex + Kale → Kale alone over ~12 months) and keep your personal brand
   separable from your primary income source if you ever want to sell.
3. **Delivery without the founder's face** — replace yourself not with one clone but with
   **two-to-five subject-matter experts** who each own a slice (a "Hydra"); edify them to
   customers and give the top ones small equity (0.1–0.5%), which also makes non-competes
   hold weight.
4. **Multiple reliable acquisition channels** — "multiple lines in the water"; one channel
   is one platform ban from death. Run one-offer / one-avatar / one-channel until
   **$1M/year**, then go "more and better" before adding a second, and **"don't kill your
   business trying to save your business"** — pick the next channel by what peers your size
   already run profitably. Cross-ref [[acquisition-channels]].
5. **Reliable recurring revenue** — one-off sales plateau; recurring compounds, and the real
   goal is **net negative churn** (revenue grows even at zero new customers, e.g. Salesforce
   expansion). Governed by the **nine C's of stickiness**: consumption, collateral, cost of
   switching, choice, control, cause, community, contracts/commitments, communication.
   Cross-ref [[retention]] (the 9 C's) and [[continuity-offers]].
6. **Diverse customer base** — no whale too large; ideal **no customer >20%** of revenue,
   his personal preference **<5%** (self-reported). A 20%-of-revenue customer can be ~100% of
   *profit*, so losing it breaks you; either chase minnows or a whole herd of whales (10–100)
   to stay diversified. "Sometimes a big shiny whale is just another woman in the red dress."
7. **Automated metric tracking** — "if you don't have data you can't make good decisions";
   move off scattered Google Sheets into a real CRM (Salesforce/HubSpot), give the numbers
   **one owner** ("one chest to poke"), and game-plan via **theory of constraints** (add a
   fixed % to each metric, attack the one that moves total throughput most). Cross-ref
   [[business-math]] and [[business-diagnostics]].
8. **High cash flow, profitable, growing — with a story** — Newton's first law: buyers
   prefer growth already in motion. He's a **cash-flow investor** (distinguishes paper profit
   from cash); the **story** should track a **trend** (AI/defense tailwind). Brick-and-mortar
   move: audit each product's gross profit/margin and push the best — "no silver bullets,
   many golden babies."
9. **Audit-ready financials** — a third party can independently recompute your profit and it
   matches. Three levels: (1) have financials at all, (2) accountant + move **cash-basis →
   GAAP**, (3) audit-ready "quality of earnings" (a "blue check mark" on your numbers). "You
   will literally not sell your business for anything material" without this.
10. **$5M+ in EBITDA** — most institutional investors won't buy smaller; reaching $5M+ EBITDA
    usually *requires* a real management team, and analyzing a small deal costs about as much
    as a big one. Portfolio stats he cites (self-reported): within 12 months revenue **+1.8x**
    / profit **+3.01x**; within 24 months revenue **+2.8x** / profit **+4.7x** on average.

Note: criteria **1–3** are three faces of the "get the owner out of the business" throughline
(§3 "scale zero"); **4–6** de-risk *where the revenue comes from*; **7–10** make the value
*legible and buyable* to an institutional investor. All ten reduce to Hormozi's close: sell
more customers, make each worth more, and make sure "those never stop happening" **without
you**.

## 6. Talent-side LTV:CAC — recruiting as acquisition

Source: [[../../sources/2026-06-16-yt-Ln94ZnvPDOQ|How to Scale a Dental Business From $15M to $50M]] (2026).
Live coaching of a ~$15M/yr dental group owner; only Alex's advice is persona data.

- **All businesses are demand-constrained — including on the talent side.** "In one way or
  another, all businesses are demand constrained. Even your business. It's demand
  constrained on the talent side." Supply-constrained fields (HVAC, home services, dental)
  are just "specialized labor."
- **Solve the binding constraint first.** "You can't solve doctor time you don't have. So
  let's assume it's doctors." Manpower gates growth before leads do.
- **The talent-side equivalent of LTV:CAC:** "what is our lifetime gross profit per
  employee to our cost of acquiring talent." A dentist at $400K gross profit/yr × ~6-year
  tenure = **~$2.4M lifetime value** against a small acquisition cost. Paying $50K / $100K
  / $200K to acquire that person is an 8:1 / 4:1 / 2:1 return "and then next year I'm also
  going to get 400 grand too."
- **"It cost me nothing up to this point" is the trap.** "Everyone's like, 'Well, it cost
  me nothing up to this point.' And I'm like, 'Yeah, and that's why you are stuck.'"
- **Bomb the constraint.** Once identified, attack it aggressively — headhunters, 10
  recruiting agencies, specialty forums, paying community owners to post recruitment.
  Portfolio example: an outsourced sales team brought in-house in 90 days (hired 80, kept
  50; ~$500K to 10 agencies at ~$10K/head) added ~**$5M/month**. "We paid 500 grand to...
  make 5 million a month after the fact. Great trade."
- **You pay for growth in profit or in growth rate.** "You either pay in profit or in
  growth rate. So if you want it to happen slower, pay less." Pulling the future forward
  (2 years instead of 7) means a big up-front number and lower near-term profit.
- **The EV multiplier is the real prize.** A dental group trading at ~10x: a ~$250K
  investment (5 orthodontists × $50K) yielding ~$1M extra EBITDA produces **~$10M in
  enterprise value** — "the cash is just gravy." (Directly applies the §5 EV formula:
  recruiting adds customers/capacity → LTGP → EV.)

## 7. Going up-market: price to the worst month

Source: [[../../sources/2026-07-07-yt-ndne_8qRh8o|Helping a Marketing Agency Scale from $1.2M to $8M]] (2026).
Live coaching of Stephanie Fox, a $1.2M/yr agency owner; only Alex's advice is persona data.

- **The "which service?" question is an avatar question.** "I actually think it's an
  avatar question, which will solve the service the product question." Deciding *who* you
  serve solves *what* you sell.
- **Find the 80/20:** the clients who "pay the most, that you like the most, and that stay
  the longest" — start there.
- **Best moves at sticking points are counterintuitive.** "The intuitive thing, if it were
  the correct answer, you would have already done it."
- **For a service agency, up-market is the only way to scale.** Say **no to smaller paid
  clients, yes to bigger high-LTV clients**, and position as serving only businesses above
  a revenue threshold (he suggests requiring $10M/yr clients, or at least $5M). The
  everything-for-everyone path **caps at ~$3–5M** and gets *more* painful (margins
  compress, talent gets scarce, offloading top clients to junior reps degrades service).
- **Narrow to where LTV:CAC becomes absurd** — clients who never want to leave: "then you
  have a money printing machine. And that's the only way that agency scale is going up
  market."
- **Price to the worst month.** "You have to get priced to their worst month, not what
  they'll say yes to. Cuz otherwise you create a churn factory."
- **Two polar extremes for agencies:** cheap high-volume SMB, or premium up-market. SMB
  ($400–600/mo) only works if you engineer costs down from day one (the Walmart analogy):
  "It's not I do this thing and charge less. That is a terrible business. It is I figure
  out a way to make this cost me nothing and then I charge something." A well-built SMB
  service can hold **30–40 month LTVs** on cheap acquisition — but the whole business must
  be built around volume.
- **Career tell:** "You can pretty much determine how far along someone is in their
  entrepreneurial career... by the price point they charge and the customer level they
  serve." "Look at the biggest agencies in the world... they serve the biggest customers.
  So you have one way and it's up."

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (scope-dependent, not a reversal): In
> [[../../sources/2023-07-01-yt-M2VP64Tc60w|5 Ways to Scale ANY Business]] (2023) Hormozi
> presents **down-market** as a fully legitimate scaling move (an endless ocean of
> emotional buyers, good if you're stronger at marketing than delivery). In
> [[../../sources/2026-07-07-yt-ndne_8qRh8o|the 2026 agency coaching]] he says the *only*
> way agencies scale is **up**. These reconcile by scope: the 2026 "up is the only way"
> claim is scoped to **service agencies** (people-driven, low-moat), where down-market
> becomes a "churn factory" unless costs are engineered near zero from day one. The
> general five-ways framework still treats down-market as viable for other business types.

## 8. Exit / M&A philosophy: the $10–30M valley, cut complexity 80%, then franchise

Source: [[../../sources/2026-06-21-yt-S1Lq8ITwtHQ|Why a $25M Exit Is Harder Than a $100M Exit]] (2026).
Live coaching of a two-location dance-school owner; only Alex's advice is persona data.

- **The hardest amount to sell for is $10M–$30M.** "It's just not big enough for people who
  have real money and it's too big for people who don't have real money." So counterintuitively,
  "it's easier to get like a hundred million dollar deal done than it is to get a 25 million
  dollar deal" — the big-deal buyer has a bigger checkbook and doesn't sweat it because it
  "barely moves the needle for them."
- **Service-/talent-heavy businesses are hard to sell for a lot.** Value rests on the
  quality of trainers/coaches/instructors. By his standard there are **no super-successful
  high-service gym businesses**; Orange Theory was the closest before declining in COVID.
- **They're easy to sell cheaply, though** — like restaurants, "somebody else always has
  the dream." A doctor with money "who has no idea about business but thinks he does" will
  buy it — but only for "10 with a bunch of hair on it," earn-outs, and clauses.
- **You can acquire such businesses for free.** "You can get gyms for free. Studios for
  free... so many people are in pain and don't want to run them... that's how I expand,
  like I got them for free." (self-reported expansion method)
- **Three expansion paths: M&A, de novo/organic, and franchise/licensing.** Franchise and
  licensing are **far more sellable** than the other two. (Licensing ≈ franchising, just a
  different legal structure.)
- **The prescription: "How could I cut down the complexity by 80%? And then franchise
  it."** Take the most profitable pricing/packages and strip to the fewest people who can
  run the unit.
- **Case study — Alloy / Rick Mayo:** a $4M single facility (smoothie bar, merch, PT,
  semi-private, large group). To franchise, he measured **revenue per square foot**, found
  **semi-private training** highest, fired every other client, and piloted with only that
  service. The replicable unit is a **three-person model** (one manager doing
  mornings/evenings + sales, plus a morning and an evening assistant) running ~$500–600K/yr
  at ~50% margins that cookie-cutters.
- **Resist adding services.** The owner's instinct *not* to add daytime/side services is
  "100% right" — strip down, don't bolt on.
- **Warning:** almost every multi-location fitness operator he knows **never sold the whole
  thing** — "every one of them piecemealed it: one to a trainer, one to a customer, one to
  a competitor" when they got tired.

## Cross-cutting throughlines

- **Get the owner out of the business.** "Scale zero" (§3), "getting the owner out of
  delivery" ($1M→$3M, §1), and de-risking future cash flows (the *risk* denominator, §5)
  are the same idea from three angles: the more the business needs the founder, the less it
  scales and the less it's worth.
- **LTV:CAC is the master ratio, applied everywhere.** Customer-side LTV:CAC (§7), talent-
  side lifetime-gross-profit-to-cost-of-hire (§6), and the EV formula's
  customers × LTGP ÷ risk (§5) are one lens: acquire high-LTGP units (customers *or*
  employees) at a favorable cost, and enterprise value follows.
- **Simplicity and focus compound; novelty and complexity destroy.** "Simple scales" (§3),
  one vehicle (§3), don't-start-new-businesses (§4), and cut-complexity-80% (§8) all say
  the same thing about what makes a business both scalable and sellable.
- **Counterintuitive moves at sticking points** (§7) and "small exits are harder than big
  ones" (§8) share Hormozi's recurring rhetorical hook: the obvious answer is usually
  already exhausted.
