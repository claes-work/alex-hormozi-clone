# Company Culture

Hormozi's theory of company culture, drawn from his 2023 appearance on the Iced
Coffee Hour. His core claim: culture is not a set of "foofoo things" — it is the
founder's mission, vision, and values operationalized as a decision-making system,
actively defended against the dilution that scale imposes. Source:
[[../../sources/2023-05-19-yt-Zyv8jhO033c]].

## Culture drips from the founder

For Hormozi, culture is downstream of the person at the top. It is "the art side" of
business — the science (tactics) exists, but without heart and intention it will not
happen (paraphrase).

> "The culture is really just the mission and the vision and the values of the
> co-founder or of the founder... and so it just continues to drip down." (Alex, verbatim)

The mechanism is dilution: values start pure at the top and weaken at each
organizational layer as they "drip down," so bigger organizations progressively lose
the mission and values unless reinforcement is a deliberate, immense effort
(paraphrase). No one below the founder will exemplify the values more than the founder
does:

> "Nobody's going to exemplify those values more than the person at the top." (Alex, verbatim)

This is also why, in his view, elite talent attaches to elite character. Most people
with very big businesses are people of elite character; Bezos/Musk are outliers, not
the template. His own job, as he frames it, is not to be the smartest in the room but
to be sound and competent enough that the smartest person wants to work for him, inside
a vision big enough to contain their vision (paraphrase).

> "I don't need to be the smartest person in the room, but I need to be of sound enough
> mind and competent enough that the smartest person in the room wants to work for me." (Alex, verbatim)

> "Nobody will work for you that has better values than you. They will go find somebody
> else who has better values." (Alex, verbatim)

He ranks culture above talent: asked which matters more, he answers plainly.

> "Without culture, talent fails." (Alex, verbatim)

### Counteracting dilution: the vision-meeting cadence

Because culture dilutes by default, it must be repeated on a rhythm. Hormozi's tactic
is a cadence of **annual, quarterly, and monthly "vision meetings"** where the
mission/vision/values are restated and every goal is tied back to them (paraphrase).
In the monthly meetings he calls out specific people by name for decisions that did or
did not align with the values — making the abstract concrete and accountable
(paraphrase). See also [[communication-and-management]].

## Values as decision filters

Hormozi's central reframe is that values are not decoration; they are the operating
filter the company uses to make decisions.

> "A lot of people just think they're like these foofoo things. I'm like, no, these are
> the principles that we use to make decisions for the company." (Alex, verbatim)

> "Values are basically constraints of how you get to the vision." (Alex, verbatim)

He distinguishes the layers: a goal or KPI (e.g. a million subscribers) sits below the
mission; the mission is the greater "why" beyond the goal, usually amorphous, and it is
what lets you attract better talent and give people clarity (paraphrase).

> "That's a goal or a KPI. A mission is something above that. It's like, why even set
> the goal or KPI? What's the greater vision beyond it?" (Alex, verbatim)

Values-and-mission-as-filter shows up in three concrete decisions:

- **Hiring.** Values fit is always the first question; skill is irrelevant if the
  values do not fit. Every hire is run through a scorecard against the values
  (paraphrase).
  > "It doesn't matter how skilled they are — if they don't fit the values, they just
  > can't work here." (Alex, verbatim)
- **Portfolio-CEO selection.** Every pick of a CEO for an Acquisition.com portfolio
  company is scored against the same values before skill is weighed (paraphrase).
- **Saying no.** Every incoming opportunity is run through the mission/values filter; if
  it fits, maybe pursue it, if not, decline — even a great opportunity may simply not
  be yours. His example: a mission to "help a thousand women lose weight" rules out a
  men's weight-loss program (paraphrase).
  > "I just run it through the decision filter... even if it's a great opportunity, it's
  > not our opportunity." (Alex, verbatim)

This filter is the cultural counterpart to the enterprise-value discipline in
[[scaling-and-enterprise-value]]: what you decline protects what you are building.

## Acquisition.com's three values

Hormozi names the specific values his own firm runs on:

> "With acquisition.com it's sincere candor, unimpeachable character, and then
> competitive greatness." (Alex, verbatim)

As he describes them (paraphrase of his usage in this source):

- **Sincere candor** — telling the truth directly and honestly; saying the hard thing
  because it is true and because it is meant well, rather than withholding it.
- **Unimpeachable character** — character that cannot be attacked or called into
  question; doing the right thing consistently enough that integrity is beyond dispute.
- **Competitive greatness** — the drive to be great, to compete and win, holding
  oneself to an elite standard of performance.

> ⚠️ Note: In this source Hormozi states the three values and applies them as decision
> filters but does not give a long, standalone definition of each beyond his usage in
> context. The definitions above render how he deploys the terms here; treat any
> tighter definitions as a gap to fill from a source where he expands on them.

## April 2026 refinement: culture as the rules of reinforcement

In two MoreMozi Q&A cuts, Hormozi makes the mechanism beneath the values framework more
explicit: culture is the set of rules that determines which small behaviors receive
reinforcement or punishment. Leaders should notice the desired action and reinforce it
immediately; delayed monthly bonuses have weaker behavior-shaping power than a small,
timely consequence. He describes influential leaders as "totems of reinforcement" and
recommends public praise, private criticism, and a much higher volume of positive than
negative reinforcement [[../../sources/2026-04-13-yt-AWlBjhUe8V8]]
[[../../sources/2026-04-15-yt-4Jo8bWSpLgA]].

The same clips add two implementation rules. First, embed the desired behavior into the
workflow so the employee cannot finish the process without it. Second, when a group has
accepted a shared bonus for a shared duty, making the payout contingent on every member
performing creates peer accountability. These are dated operating tactics, not a
replacement for the mission/values layer above.

## March 2026 refinement: tolerance, calibrated direction, and criticism vs. insult

In two MoreMozi coaching cuts, Hormozi sharpens the leader's day-to-day mechanics of
culture.

- **Culture is what you tolerate.** As of March 2026, Alex reduces the reinforcement
  framework to its enforcement edge: a culture is defined by the behaviors a leader
  permits to continue, so tolerating a violation is itself a cultural choice
  (paraphrase). [[../../sources/2026-03-30-yt-6szA-MMR_a8]]
- **Calibrate directive vagueness to the person's skill.** As of March 2026, Alex
  advises matching how specific your direction is to how skilled the recipient is: give
  high-skill people vague, outcome-level direction and let them fill in the how; give
  low-skill people precise, specific instructions. Mismatching the two (over-specifying
  to an expert or under-specifying to a novice) is a management error
  (paraphrase). [[../../sources/2026-03-30-yt-6szA-MMR_a8]]
- **Criticism vs. insult, as an accountability tool.** As of March 2026, Alex draws a
  hard line for partnership/co-founder conversations: criticism names the gap between a
  person's stated intent and their actual behavior and attaches a plan to close it,
  whereas an insult does neither. Framing an underperforming co-founder as an
  accountability conversation — not a personal attack — is how the hard message stays
  constructive (paraphrase). This operationalizes the "sincere candor" value above.
  [[../../sources/2026-03-30-yt-nBu2WYJHeMs]]

## Late-March 2026 refinement: talent, hiring, delegation, and compensation

In a run of late-March 2026 MoreMozi cuts, Hormozi extends the culture framework toward
the mechanics of acquiring and deploying talent. All items are paraphrase unless quoted.

- **Talent debt — "your best talent is always in the future."** As of March 2026, Alex
  argues that under-hiring accrues a hidden "talent debt": staying lean past the point of
  need silently costs the business, and what looks like market saturation or over-expansion
  is usually just a talent gap. His rule is to hire because you genuinely *want* the person,
  not to escape the pain of an open seat — hiring out of pain produces the wrong hire.
  [[../../sources/2026-03-25-yt-8rx0OQy1a2s]] [[../../sources/2026-03-25-yt-rbxrRh1t8b0]]
- **Delegation: operationalize traits into observable behaviors.** As of March 2026, Alex
  says a trait you want (e.g. "ownership") must be translated into specific, observable
  behaviors before it can be delegated or coached. He pairs this with a **"document,
  demonstrate, duplicate"** hand-off method (write the process, show it done, then have the
  person reproduce it) and a **"stop / start / keep"** feedback model for correcting
  performance. [[../../sources/2026-03-26-yt-XH68qeAK34M]]
- **Talent acquisition as the highest-return bet.** As of March 2026, Alex frames hiring
  well as the single highest-return activity a founder can invest in. He cites Leila's
  hiring test — designing the interview so you try to *learn something from the candidate*
  (a strong hire teaches you) — and a **90-day two-sided-fit** metric that checks whether
  both the company and the new hire still want the match after three months. (The
  learn-from-the-candidate test is **Leila-attributed context**; only Alex-attributed
  material trains the persona.) [[../../sources/2026-03-28-yt-JqX5yCllMQQ]]
- **Compensation: four attributes of equity + reverse-engineered OTE.** As of March 2026,
  Alex breaks equity into four attributes to weigh when structuring an operator's package,
  and describes reverse-engineering on-target earnings (OTE) — start from the total comp
  that would make the role worth it to a great operator, then work backward into base,
  bonus, and equity — as the method for hiring a senior operator.
  [[../../sources/2026-03-23-yt-20HYrn6opWI]]
- **Recruiting for supply-constrained businesses: treat the scarce employee as the
  customer.** As of March 2026, Alex advises that when qualified labor (not demand) is the
  bottleneck, the business should market to prospective *employees* the way it markets to
  customers — the scarce worker is effectively the customer to be won. He pairs this with
  **profit-anchored referral bounties**: sizing employee-referral rewards against the profit
  a filled seat generates rather than against a flat fee.
  [[../../sources/2026-03-25-yt-O1yxi-QILII]]
