# Sales team scaling

Alex Hormozi's end-to-end system for **building and managing a sales team** — as
opposed to the individual-closer script covered in [[sales-frameworks]]. This page
covers hiring/interviewing, a 14-day onboarding ramp, the team's CLOSER script and the
per-rep coaching loop, and the culture/performance-management rules that keep a team
productive. Source: [[../../sources/2022-09-27-yt-2lA_A8BGRRs]] ("How To Build Large
Sales Teams (Starting from 0)", 2022-09-27, solo talking-head — all material is
Alex-attributed).

> All claims below are **self-reported** — Alex's own account of the systems he says he
> and his portfolio companies use; not independently verified.

For the mechanics of the close itself (word-for-word script, objection handling,
questioning), see [[sales-frameworks]]. For the communication/leadership discipline this
draws on, see [[communication-and-management]] and [[sales-communication]].

## Recruiting as mirrored acquisition (2021 predecessor)

Before the dedicated 2022 sales-team buildout video, Hormozi had already framed talent
acquisition as a mirror of customer acquisition. In the 2021 recruiting framework, the five
external functions (lead generation, lead nurture, sales, fulfillment, retention/ascension;
credited to Alex Tarfin) map internally to **application generation, application nurture,
interviewing, training, and talent retention/ascension**. The same six channels apply to
customers and candidates: paid ads, earned media, owned media, manual outbound, affiliates,
and word of mouth. The practical line is "you don't need a what, you need a who": acquire
the person who already has the missing skill, then sell the candidate on the company and
train/ascend them like a customer journey [[../../sources/2021-11-15-yt-Lxp-e5NionA]].

## Hiring & interview criteria

Per [[../../sources/2022-09-27-yt-2lA_A8BGRRs]], Alex screens interviewees on four
traits (paraphrase):

1. **Speed of response** — a proxy for work ethic. He tests how quickly a candidate gets
   back to him, treating himself as a stand-in prospect:
   > "You can test work ethic by how quickly they get back to you because you are a lead
   > to them."
2. **Intelligence** — a rep who "sounds like an idiot" projects that impression onto your
   brand.
3. **Listening / ambiversion** — socially aware, not a motormouth; can read the prospect.
4. **Coachability** — tested via a live **role play**: give the candidate feedback, then
   ask them to try the same thing again. Taking feedback without ego *and* visibly
   improving on the second attempt = coachable.

**Prospect knowledge over product knowledge** (paraphrase): most teams overemphasize
product/technology training and underemphasize understanding *who* they sell to. Focus
on the person more than the product — understand the prospect's problems so deeply they
believe you intrinsically understand them:
> "If you can explain to somebody exactly the problem that they're going through they
> will buy whatever you have because they will believe that you so intrinsically
> understand what they're going through."

## The 14-day onboarding ramp

New hires spend onboarding **"bathing in good sales"** — listening to good recorded sales
calls all day (if you have none, record your own first). By roughly **40 calls** listened
to, a rep has a good working feel for the motion. Between listening sessions they study
the script.
> "You want them to be bathing in good sales."

After a rep **passes the role play**, put them on a **half schedule**: the first half of
the day they burn through lots of leads (volume of real reps); the second half they
**review their own recorded calls**. In week two, add **manager meetings twice a day**.
Cadence in the first 14 days is **1–2 touches/day**. A rep **graduates** to a full
schedule + weekly meetings once they hit the minimum close-rate quota.

## The team's script (CLOSER) & coaching loop

### The script — CLOSER (big picture)

The script Alex teaches is a **question-based framework**, the **CLOSER** structure
(from *$100M Sales*), presented here big-picture rather than word-for-word. Full
mechanics live in [[sales-frameworks]]; the team-facing outline (paraphrase):

- **C — Clarify** why they're there.
- **L — Label** them with the problem.
- **O — Overview** their past experiences and pains.
- *(Transition once you have enough info: "I think you'd be a good fit, can I tell you
  about it?" — get invited to sell.)*
- **S — Sell** via short analogies that solve 2–3 of their stated problems, then ask for
  the sale.
- **E — Explain** away concerns.
- **R — Reinforce** the decision.

**Boil the decision to its most basic unit** so the prospect doesn't have to work out
what they're deciding — for a B2B service, "can we help you make more money than you
could on your own, yes or no?" A rep who keeps asking "do you have any questions for me?"
signals they can't close (they invite reasons *not* to buy instead of asking for a
decision).
> "You don't want them to try and think about what the decision is, you want to give them
> the decision that they have to make by the end of the call."

### The per-rep coaching loop

Alex frames sales management as **motivate + train**, *not* weekly pipeline check-ins.
The recurring per-rep coaching loop (paraphrase):

1. **30 min before** the coaching session, listen to **1–2 of the rep's recorded calls
   at 2x speed** and take written notes.
2. Get on the coaching call **immediately after** the rep's own call (feedback while it's
   fresh).
3. **Prioritize the 1–2 highest-impact fixes** (bold them) — ~5 min to communicate.
4. Spend the remaining ~25 min **role playing** the specific changes.
5. **Next day**, check whether the rep actually did it, then **drill specific
   objections** — "I need to think about it," "I have to talk to my spouse," "that's a
   lot of money."

The point is a small number of concrete fixes drilled to habit, tracked week to week — a
disciplined feedback cadence rather than passive status updates (see
[[communication-and-management]]).

## Culture & performance management (PIP)

### Culture triad

Culture is maintained by three things (paraphrase):

1. A **visual, always-up-to-date leaderboard** — reps are competitive.
2. **Clear KPIs + a bonus**, plus a **recurring competition every ~4–6 weeks (30–42
   days)**. The objective that worked best across Alex's portfolio companies is
   optimizing for **cash up front**.
3. **Cut the bottom 10% quarterly.** Not cutting communicates that mediocrity is
   acceptable:
   > "If you're not cutting you're communicating with the team that's okay to suck and it
   > will drag down the culture of the team."

### Management as tolerance, and leading by example

> "Sales management is a skill of tolerance — the person who tolerates the most is the one
> who has the weakest team."

A new sales manager sets and enforces new standards (e.g. mandatory early training, on
time to every call, clean pipelines daily, call notes in the CRM) — and enforces them
**even against the best closer**. Managers must lead by example:
> "No one respects a sales manager who says do all these things and doesn't do it
> themselves."
> "If it's worth doing it's worth doing well."

The **best salesperson is rarely the best sales manager** — managers tend to be more
giving, more emotionally stable ("the rock"), first to show up and last to finish the
checklist.
> "I have rarely found that the best salesperson is the best sales manager."

### Meeting stack & cadence

- **Daily morning huddle** (training; optional with strong culture, mandatory for
  trainees/PIP) + **end-of-day huddle** (short check-in, clean pipelines) + **weekly
  1-on-1s** (~1 hr per rep/week) + **monthlies**.
- After each 1-on-1, message/email the salient points; keep a **per-rep tab dated week to
  week** tracking the 1–2 assigned fixes and whether the rep improved.
- Cadence graduates with tenure: 1–2 touches/day (first 14 days) → full schedule + weekly
  meetings (after hitting quota) → every-other-week only once a rep is clearly killing it
  after ~a year.

### PIP / performance management

Drop **below KPI for two weeks → PIP** (performance improvement plan): the rep regresses
to **trainee-level attention**. Still not improved after **two more weeks → out**. Alex
sets this "dangerous place to work / we expect excellence" expectation **at hire**.

### Conviction as the failure mode

Sales goes cold when reps **lose belief**. Alex keeps conviction alive with competitions
and with client results/testimonials — having former clients hop on sales calls, watching
testimonial videos as a team in the morning huddle, or having the rep who sold a client
read that client's testimonial aloud. Sell from genuine empathy, pushing through the "no"
because you believe in the transformation — not from ego tied to closing.
> "You want to sell from a place of empathy — genuine empathy, not nice guy, but like I
> really want to help you, which means you need to make a decision."

## March 2026 hiring & recruiting refinements (MoreMozi coaching)

A cluster of March 2026 live-coaching cuts extends this page from *managing* a sales team
to *staffing an org around one* — diagnosing when the felt constraint is talent vs. process,
and how to build the recruiting and leadership layers. All self-reported.

### The talent bottleneck is really a script/process bottleneck

Coaching an agency owner who believed his ceiling was a shortage of seven/eight-figure
sales directors, Alex reframes the constraint as a **process problem, not a talent-supply
problem**: an over-**consultative** script forces reliance on rare "unicorn" salespeople,
whereas a tightened, repeatable **follow-the-script** process lets ordinary trained reps
win [[../../sources/2026-03-19-yt-v0ROYlEwE2A]].
> "I would bet that your sales process is a bit too consultative. And so as a result, it's
> relying on people with specialized skill rather than a more refined process… you need
> more unicorn style sales people rather than like follow the [__] script."

Refining the script is an **arbitrage/TAM unlock**: once the process (not the person) does
the selling, you stop paying a premium for scarce specialists and the hireable pool
explodes — he points to the large pool of door-to-door sellers who already sell harder
things, for whom moving to phone/computer selling is a cheap, teachable learning curve
(mostly language and script, not delivery knowledge).
> "Fix the scripting around the sales so that you don't need a $60,000 person and then all
> of a sudden this arbitrage will get really juicy and you will massively open up the TAM
> of potential sales prospects."

**Sales-director-as-culture-and-training** (paraphrase): directors don't hop on phones and
need *less* product/delivery knowledge than frontline reps, because "teaching sales is
teaching sales." Reps don't need to know the delivery or the client's business better than
the client — only how to sell "how we can help you." **Span of control:** ~15 reps under
one director is "a little high"; Alex starts splitting around **10** (e.g. two managers with
7–8 reps each) [[../../sources/2026-03-19-yt-v0ROYlEwE2A]].

### Own your core-economic-engine roles; rent the rest

Sizing a hiring cadence (six offices/year ≈ one hire every ~two weeks) justifies a dedicated
in-house recruiter. Alex's **build-vs-outsource recruiting rule**: use **headhunters for
specialized one-time roles** (rent their existing network rather than rebuild it), but keep
recruiting/hiring/onboarding/training **in-house for the repeated roles that are your core
economic engine** [[../../sources/2026-03-17-yt-HUsN0HMfOoA]].
> "If I have a specialized one-time role, headhunters all the way… I don't want to
> outsource that, cuz it's core to the economic engine that I have."

His favorite sourcing tactic: find a recruiting firm that does it, then go **one level lower
on LinkedIn** to the individual actually doing the work and hire them to do it exclusively
for you ("I'll pay you better and treat you better").

### The leadership-layer hiring playbook (bottom layer vs. top layer)

Coaching a $4.5M/month brand that had already fixed its *frontline* sales-hiring bottleneck
with an in-house recruiter and now wanted the same for its leadership tier, Alex diagnoses:
**"You built the bottom layer, you're missing the top layer"** — the absent leadership tier
is what pulls the owner into daily fires and blocks strategic work
[[../../sources/2026-03-17-yt-J4tdCR_PjL4]].

- **Network-vs-headhunter split.** Source roles you have a network and pattern for through
  that network (sales/marketing leaders); for roles you lack the expertise to *judge*
  (finance, tech/analytics, people/HR), pay a **blue-chip headhunter** to run the technical
  screens, and you assess only culture/fit. For domains outside his expertise (e.g. legal)
  he pulls in already-paid outside experts — e.g. asking his attorneys to vet a candidate on
  human judgment.
- **C-suite "six-week sprint."** For a big hiring constraint, he and Leila pull themselves
  out of the business to do nothing but interviews for six weeks — **only for C-suite**, not
  directors/managers.
- **CEO cold-outreach.** Headhunting is "an outreach game," and response rates are
  "significantly higher" when the CEO reaches out personally; his low-pressure intro script
  flatters the target and asks if they "know anybody exactly like you" — letting them opt in
  without pressure.
- **Sequence by "most on fire."** Don't replace all reports at once; rank them and recruit
  the role "most on fire" first, then the next.
- **Pattern recognition = scaling speed.** "A big thing that dictates how fast you move in
  business is your ability to recognize patterns with people and talent" — you rebuild fast
  where you already know what a role looks like, and slow down at levels you've never hired.
- **Hiring cost is an investment, not an expense** (risk-reward): "Would I pay 40 to make
  400? Yes… which is why we get compensated when it works." In-house recruiting (~$10k/mo)
  also smooths the cash hit vs. headhunter fees of 20–25% of a $100–200k package.

> ⚠️ POSITION EXTENDS (does not contradict) the 2021 "recruiting as mirrored acquisition"
> frame above: the 2021 material treats *all* talent acquisition as one customer-style
> funnel; March 2026 keeps that ("recruiting is advertising" — find talent with the same
> volume, funnel, and outreach you use for customers) but adds a **layered** nuance —
> own the funnel for core-engine and pattern-recognized roles, rent a headhunter's funnel
> for judge-outside-your-expertise roles [[../../sources/2026-03-17-yt-J4tdCR_PjL4]].

### A-player razor & candidate volume (March 2026 MoreMozi)

Two supporting cuts from the same March 2026 batch: run a **14-day A-player razor** — decide
fast, and "fire the sixes so the eights can become nines" rather than let mediocrity anchor
the bar. Filter every hire on **two questions — does it make money, and what does it cost
culture?** And **"keep dating candidates"**: maintain candidate volume continuously (recruiting
is advertising — never let the pipeline of talent go cold), so you're never hiring from
desperation (March 2026, @MoreMozi).

## April 2026 operating refinements: utilization and speed

In an April 2026 Q&A, Hormozi places a sales team near **70-75% calendar utilization**:
enough load to produce, but enough slack to preserve show-rate recovery, follow-up, and
pipeline work. Overbooking can look efficient while lowering the downstream conversion
work that turns appointments into revenue. When the pipeline supports it, add reps before
every calendar is full [[../../sources/2026-04-17-yt-G_M5eenRzQs]].

A separate cut treats response speed as an LTV:CAC lever rather than etiquette: contacting
a new lead within roughly 60 seconds can be worth the staffing or routing cost when the
incremental conversion value exceeds it [[../../sources/2026-04-16-yt-iT4lqNIckcE]].
These figures are dated heuristics, not universal capacity standards.
