# Hiring & Recruiting

Across a dense cluster of mid-March 2026 MoreMozi live-coaching teardowns, Alex Hormozi
laid out a coherent, recurring theory of hiring: **talent acquisition is the same
discipline as customer acquisition**, run on the same math, the same volume, and the same
funnel infrastructure — and for most profitable, demand-rich businesses it is *the*
binding constraint. This page consolidates that body of thought: how he prices a hire,
why he treats the recruiting bottleneck as a resource problem rather than a scarcity one,
how he sources and pays for scarce talent, and how he retains it once hired. For the
leadership-layer hiring playbook specifically (network-vs-headhunter, C-suite six-week
sprint, CEO cold-outreach, sequence-by-most-on-fire), see [[sales-team-scaling]].

## Talent-side LTGP:CAC — a hire is customer acquisition run backwards

Alex's signature move is to reframe hiring as an acquisition-math problem identical to
customer acquisition. Every business, he argues (March 2026), is ultimately demand
constrained — and a "supply-constrained" business is simply demand constrained *on the
talent side*.

> "In one way or another, all businesses are demand constrained. Even your business. It's
> demand constrained on the talent side." — [[2026-03-15-yt-t7aSRUZXEMQ]]

So the customer LTV:CAC ratio has an exact mirror in what he formalizes as **LTGP:CAT** —
lifetime gross profit per employee to **cost of acquiring talent** (March 2026,
[[2026-03-15-yt-6w60tMzWf-E]]).

> "LTGP to CAT, which is cost of acquiring talent. So just like we did LTGP to CAC on the
> customer side, it's the exact same equation on the other side — which is how much am I
> willing to pay to acquire talent, and how much do I make in talent per year?" —
> [[2026-03-15-yt-6w60tMzWf-E]]

The engine of the argument is that **a hire is a one-time cost against multi-year gross
profit** — an investment trade, not an expense. His stock analogy (March 2026):

> "Imagine I have a stock investment where I said, 'Hey, you give me 600 grand a day, and
> for the rest of your life, you're going to make 2.8 million in profit per year on this
> investment.' You'd be like, 'Fuck yeah.' ... And for whatever reason, we don't do that
> with our businesses." — [[2026-03-15-yt-6w60tMzWf-E]]

Worked examples recur across the cluster: an orthodontist producing ~$400k gross
profit/year (≈$2.4M over a ~6-year tenure) justifies paying $50k–$200k to acquire — an 8:1
return before compounding ([[2026-03-15-yt-t7aSRUZXEMQ]]); a "director of social" worth
~$1.25M/year in gross profit justifies "$125k one time to add an extra $1.25M per year all
day," with a blue-chip recruiting firm charging ~30% of salary (~$60k on a $200k role)
([[2026-03-15-yt-FJXvgK6uMYw]]).

Relying on **free referral hiring is precisely why owners stay stuck** — paying $0 to
recruit caps growth:

> "It cost me nothing at this point. And I'm like, yeah, and that's why you are stuck." —
> [[2026-03-15-yt-t7aSRUZXEMQ]]

**The enterprise-value multiplier.** Because a business trades at a multiple, recruiting
spend that adds gross profit creates enterprise value far larger than the cash it costs. A
~$125k hire adding ~$5M of lifetime gross profit at an ~8x multiple adds ~$10M of tax-free
EV ([[2026-03-15-yt-FJXvgK6uMYw]]); a ~$250k recruiting spend adding ~$1M EBITDA at ~10x
creates ~$10M of EV — "the cash is just gravy" ([[2026-03-15-yt-t7aSRUZXEMQ]]); putting
$1.2M into 20 lawyers can yield ~$32M in tax-free EV ([[2026-03-16-yt-idmIkZ04gdo]]). See
[[scaling-and-enterprise-value]].

The core trade-off underneath all of it:

> "You either pay in profit or in growth rate. So if you want it to happen slower, pay
> less." — [[2026-03-15-yt-t7aSRUZXEMQ]]

## The bottleneck is a volume/resource problem, not a scarcity problem

When a profitable, demand-rich business names hiring as its constraint, Alex treats it as a
**pure resource issue** solved by volume — not proof that talent is unavailable.

> "When I'm in your situation, I just see this and this... this is a pure resource issue.
> Like spin up three times the recruiters." — [[2026-03-16-yt-idmIkZ04gdo]]

His scale playbook (March 2026): run far more recruiters than feels normal (10, not 2–3);
go **up-market** on the recruiting firms you engage ("you can get recruiters all the way up
to... million-dollar-a-year recruiters"); multiply outbound reach-outs 10–20x; and decide
headcount by working backward from **demand saturation** ([[2026-03-16-yt-idmIkZ04gdo]]).
The first thing he looks for is a **recruiting-metrics gap** — a business that only reaches
out "when we find the right candidate" has no metric and therefore no lever.

He contracts **many recruiting firms in parallel** and cuts **bulk wholesale deals** with
replacement guarantees. The canonical proof story recurs in three tellings:

> "We had to build a 50-person sales team in 90 days… Well, we hired 80 guys. We kept 50.
> And we had 15 headhunting firms all spun up at the same time." — [[2026-03-15-yt-FJXvgK6uMYw]]

> "We had to spin up a team of 50 activated and good salespeople. So we ended up hiring 90
> in 12 weeks... what we did was we hired 15 different recruiting firms and we did bulk
> wholesale deals with them saying like, 'Give us five people. We'll get a discounted rate
> cuz we want five.'" — [[2026-03-16-yt-idmIkZ04gdo]]

That same portfolio company insourced sales by paying ~10 recruiting agencies ~$10k/head
(~$500k total) to add $5M/month: "We paid 500 grand to make five million a month after the
fact. Great trade." ([[2026-03-15-yt-t7aSRUZXEMQ]]). The mindset line for supply-constrained
owners: "that's the constraint. Let's bomb the hell out of it."

**Decision rule — repeated vs. one-off roles:** for high-volume repeated roles build true
end-to-end recruiting infrastructure; for a one-time role, just pay a recruiter.

> "If you think about every role as a new customer that you have to build an acquisition
> process for, I will build out infrastructure and true processes around repeated roles…
> If you're looking for one role one time, I just pay the money." — [[2026-03-15-yt-FJXvgK6uMYw]]

## Supply-gen mirrors demand-gen — build the recruiting funnel

For businesses scaling a repeated role (typically sales), the infrastructure is a **parallel
"supply-gen" structure that mirrors the demand-gen funnel** — an internal sales academy /
recruiting machine (March 2026, [[2026-03-16-yt-6Tru99awhhM]]). App gen → app nurture →
interviewing → onboarding → retention & ascension of salespeople mirrors lead gen → nurture
→ sales → onboarding → retention & ascension of customers.

> "Just like we spend money reliably in our demand gen side, we need to be able to spend
> money reliably on the supply gen side. And so those just have to be parallel functions
> that exist within the business." — [[2026-03-16-yt-6Tru99awhhM]]

You track supply-side metrics (recruiting-funnel conversion) with the same rigor as
demand-side metrics (CTR, opt-in, show %, close %) so you can spend on hiring as reliably as
you spend on ads. The ROI is among the best in business: "Some of the best returns you get
in the business are just your cost to acquire talent versus lifetime gross profit per
employee" — a rep driving ~$1M gross profit for ~$200k pay is a 5:1 return
([[2026-03-16-yt-6Tru99awhhM]]).

## You're under-pricing your talent

For supply-constrained-but-profitable businesses, Alex's diagnosis flips the owner's framing:
if you're making money hand over fist and *still* can't fill roles, the compensation is the
problem, not the labor market (March 2026, [[2026-03-16-yt-2IGBCUHCX8c]]).

> "Compensation is you can be over-compensated or you can be under-compensated. So if you
> are supply constrained and you are making money hand over fist, you are under-pricing
> your talent." — [[2026-03-16-yt-2IGBCUHCX8c]]

His compensation test: calculate how much more you'd need to pay to get a skilled worker to
relocate and train; if you keep good margin at that pay, do it; and if passing the raise
through to price doesn't change acquisition, "jack the price, jack the talent." A parallel
argument runs in the trades — a welder role with ~$200k of gross profit on the line is worth
paying $150–180k (not $100k) to fill reliably and attract more qualified applicants — and in
a supply-constrained market "the supply has the upper hand... you're going to have to pay
them more" ([[2026-03-15-yt-6w60tMzWf-E]]).

**Sourcing scarce skilled labor:** the "alpha" is in a *dying trade* full of skilled artisans
who lack business sense. Just call the owners directly.

> "There's all these people who are dying on the vine who can barely make ends meet, and then
> I'm over here printing money with the exact same skill set they have." — [[2026-03-16-yt-2IGBCUHCX8c]]

**Sequencing rule — supply first:** fix delivery capacity before scaling demand. "No, supply
first. Get the people first." ([[2026-03-16-yt-2IGBCUHCX8c]]).

## Pay more to get better talent — the A-player premium

His enduring thesis is to pay above market for A-players, which also improves retention:

> "This is why I still believe that to this day, the best way to beat the market is to find
> A-player talent and pay them well. You'll also retain them longer, all that stuff." —
> [[2026-03-15-yt-6w60tMzWf-E]]

He calls the premium one of the last great arbitrages (March 2026):

> "One of the biggest arbitrages that still exist in business is paying 20 or 30% more for A+
> talent because they're worth more than three or four times B talent." — [[2026-03-16-yt-oiUNB6q8_GI]]

> "Everyone's afraid of spending the extra 30, but it's like you can't afford not to." —
> [[2026-03-16-yt-oiUNB6q8_GI]]

## When the founder should step out — the delegation-ROI test

The trigger for a founder to step out of a function is not a revenue stage but a **break-even
calculation**: the time freed must generate more than the manager costs, and the reclaimed
time must have a productive use (March 2026, [[2026-03-16-yt-oiUNB6q8_GI]]).

> "If it costs you $100,000 in bottom line… you're going from 450 to 350. And that means that
> you have to make $400,000 back just to break even on this guy. Right? Can you do that with
> the time?" — [[2026-03-16-yt-oiUNB6q8_GI]]

The same logic drives his key-man-risk fix: a **time study** (log activity every 15 minutes
for a week, rank by revenue value/uniqueness, shed the bottom half), because "key man is the
number one risk of every business" ([[2026-03-15-yt-9xvNzvABzuQ]]). Premium customers →
premium prices → excess margin → the ability to buy premium people, so "you have to lower
your tolerance for mediocrity on your team." His recruiting unlock there: rather than only a
firm, "run national ads and then offer a really generous relocation package" structured as a
signing bonus (e.g. $25k now / $25k at month six) — and keep such workers W-2: "go more
legit, not less legit." See [[communication-and-management]] for the management-diamond
diagnostic.

## The debt-choice framework for bootstrapped hiring

For undercapitalized founders, Alex reframes the whole question through his debt thesis (March
2026, [[2026-03-15-yt-mtJd5Ub1nBE]]): every business incurs debt; the only choice is which
kind. Declining financial debt means taking on **talent debt, infrastructure debt, and tech
debt** instead.

> "Every business always incurs debt, period. The question is just what type of debt you want
> to incur." — [[2026-03-15-yt-mtJd5Ub1nBE]]

> "The reason that founders get disproportionately compensated is because we have to do more
> jobs better than most people for an extended period of time to make up for the debt that
> the business has to incur." — [[2026-03-15-yt-mtJd5Ub1nBE]]

Options for hiring above your cash level: take on capital debt; give away equity as a
founder-skill-levered bet ("I would rather have 10% of this guy's thing than 100% of my own
thing"); or work overtime yourself to grow cash flow first. His key sequencing counsel is to
**backfill yourself, don't hire the rainmaker** — because "rainmakers ain't cheap" and the
truly great ones don't need you.

> "How do I hire the person to do what I'm already doing so that I can go grow the business is
> probably the better question." — [[2026-03-15-yt-mtJd5Ub1nBE]]

And the deeper diagnosis: a hiring bottleneck is often really a **missing acquisition
channel** — "the real thing that you're lacking is a reliable acquisition channel."

## Directive-generality — the measure of talent level

The clearest test of someone's level is **how general a directive they can execute**: the more
senior and capable, the less you have to specify (March 2026, [[2026-03-15-yt-wpt-F4s13_Y]]).

> "If I'm bringing in somebody who's going to be a C-suite who's going to run this whole
> function, I shouldn't tell you how to do anything. Like you should know how to do
> everything. You should be bringing things I don't know to the table to make this function
> better." — [[2026-03-15-yt-wpt-F4s13_Y]]

> "You can tell how advanced someone is by how much, how many directives you have to give and
> the level of the direction you're giving someone." — [[2026-03-15-yt-wpt-F4s13_Y]]

Two corollaries: as you climb the ladder, "your level of talent that you can attract and the
level of mediocrity that you repel just gets more and more extreme"; and past ~$1M/year, real
A-players **arrive with their own teams, networks, and Rolodex** — Alex says if recruited he'd
only go if he could bring six of his own people.

## The world-class EA as founder leverage

A world-class executive assistant is a distinct, high-leverage hire defined by three traits:
**speed, intelligence, and matched intensity** (March 2026, [[2026-03-16-yt-ToWsyHoH6S8]]).

> "I needed someone who will always respond to me within 60 seconds, no matter when I
> message... the moment I message you, I need full resources, full attention because in that
> moment, you are my constraint." — [[2026-03-16-yt-ToWsyHoH6S8]]

Intelligence earns the trust to delegate real cognitive tasks — "I can get 80% of that done,
and then I can tweak, rather than having to just truly do everything de novo" — and the EA
acts as an open-loop / project tracker, chasing follow-ups. His concrete guidance: "Pay more
than you expect. Look for somebody who's more intelligent, and say speed is non-negotiable."

## Sales-god → sales-training/recruiting-god — codify the founder's "black box"

For owner-dependent sales firms, the unlock is an **identity shift** from being the best
closer to building closers — treating every deal the founder personally closes as a failure
(March 2026, [[2026-03-15-yt-DHmicoejBt4]], [[2026-03-15-yt-Jd3hPZw4e6k]]).

> "At some point, your identity will have to shift from sales god to sales training god." —
> [[2026-03-15-yt-DHmicoejBt4]]

Because winners often don't know why they win ("Michael Jordan, way better player than
coach"), you must codify your winning behaviors into a scripted, documented **"black box"**
someone else can execute — "Right now, you don't have the black box, cuz it's up here." Two
concrete levers: build a sales process that is **based on the prospect, not the salesman**
(the rep asks questions and gives no answers, leading the prospect to their own decision,
which lets you hire cheaper reps who don't need founder-level authority); and **kill
commissions on founder-assisted closes** so reps stop pulling the founder in.

> "A sales process that scales is based on the prospect, not the salesman." — [[2026-03-15-yt-Jd3hPZw4e6k]]

> "I would consider not paying commissions on sales that you help close. So that'll probably
> get them to real quick stop asking you to close." — [[2026-03-15-yt-Jd3hPZw4e6k]]

The end state: a brand big enough to attract already-good reps — "take heroes and make them
superheroes" rather than taking people from zero to hero ([[2026-03-15-yt-DHmicoejBt4]]). See
[[sales-team-scaling]] for the full sales-org build.

## Career-path / promotion-ladder retention

Once talent is hired, Alex's retention mechanism is a **long career ladder** built on two
parallel columns — **title/perks** and **money** — advanced in many small, milestone-gated
increments (March 2026, [[2026-03-15-yt-ky2xQWLXKNk]], [[2026-03-15-yt-3GIXMxy3428]]).

> "When I think about career pathing, I think about two columns that are kind of in parallel.
> So you've got title / perks... and then money." — [[2026-03-15-yt-ky2xQWLXKNk]]

Split each role into junior/standard/senior tiers, with each rung shedding a menial duty the
lower tier still does (his example: editors no longer clean camera lenses); ~6 tiers yields
~12 progressions, and a 3-rung ladder can be expanded to 9 or 18 rungs by alternating title
and pay bumps ([[2026-03-15-yt-3GIXMxy3428]]). The design target:

> "What happens is they're always three to six months away from another promotion. So
> whenever you're about to leave, there's always career advancement that exists." —
> [[2026-03-15-yt-3GIXMxy3428]]

Tie promotions to objective cumulative-volume milestones, and make the **first promotion a
"6-inch putt"** — deliberately easy, placed right at the start — to survive the highest-churn
first 30–45 days, then expand the reinforcement cycle so each milestone stretches further out.
Gating rungs behind **in-house mini-certifications** (the med-spa / nail-salon model) unlocks
higher-value work and keeps people feeling they're "always learning" — one nail-salon chain
lifted average tenure from an industry-standard ~6 months to ~4 years this way
([[2026-03-15-yt-ky2xQWLXKNk]]). The principle: "Just give them more ways to win."

## Get in the door — the stand-out candidate playbook

The flip side of the recruiting funnel is what a candidate can do to break through it. Alex's
canonical example (March 2026, [[2026-03-04-yt-djDqESv_yIY]]) is the 18-year-old who landed a
job at Acquisition.com by **reaching every decision-maker directly with a personalized pitch**
— he emailed everyone in the company individually, and applied on his birthday to stand out —
rather than dropping one application into the queue.

The doctrine underneath it: **remove the hirer's blocker fast, then out-work the room once
you're in.** Get past the gatekeeping by going straight to the people who decide, make it
trivially easy to say yes, and once inside let output do the talking. He pairs this with a
warning to founders about who they let in early: **the first 3–5 hires set the culture**, so
those seats matter far more than headcount alone ([[2026-03-04-yt-djDqESv_yIY]]).

## How to make more money as an employee — become an internal entrepreneur

For someone who wants to maximize earnings *without* starting their own company, Alex's advice
(March 2026, [[2026-03-03-yt-Z_hOgtlFmss]]) is to engineer the job like a business owner would.

First, **pick a big-enough vessel** — join a company large enough (he cites the $10–20M+ range)
that there is real value to capture; a great operator inside a tiny business is capped by the
size of the pond. Then become an **"internal entrepreneur"**: tie your own pay to the revenue
or output you generate rather than to a fixed salary, and **think of yourself as a vendor, not
an employee** — a vendor prices on the value delivered, not on hours.

The concrete mechanism is to make **performance-based, percentage-of-value asks**: propose comp
structured as a share of the results you produce, so your upside scales with what you create.
The governing trade-off mirrors his equity/debt thinking on the owner side — **the more risk
you're willing to own, the more upside you can claim** ([[2026-03-03-yt-Z_hOgtlFmss]]). This is
the employee-side complement to the "you're under-pricing your talent" section above: the same
value-based logic the owner should apply to compensation, the employee should apply to the ask.

## Own your core-economic-engine roles

His build-vs-outsource rule (March 2026, [[2026-03-17-yt-HUsN0HMfOoA]]): headhunt peripheral
and specialized one-time roles, but own the recruiting/hiring/onboarding/training process
in-house for the repeated roles that *are* your economic engine.

> "If I have a specialized one-time role, headhunters all the way. They already have networks
> that I don't want to rebuild that." — [[2026-03-17-yt-HUsN0HMfOoA]]

> "I don't want to outsource that, cuz it's core to the economic engine that I have." —
> [[2026-03-17-yt-HUsN0HMfOoA]]

A hiring cadence of roughly one hire every two weeks (six new offices/year plus staff and
leadership) is enough volume to justify a dedicated in-house talent-acquisition person. His
favorite sourcing tactic: find a recruiting firm, then go one level lower on LinkedIn to the
person actually doing the work and hire them exclusively — "I'll pay you better and treat you
better."

## The barbell hiring strategy — staff the two ends, thin the middle

Asked how founders should resolve the classic "hire scrappy young talent vs. hire expensive
experience" dilemma, Alex answers with a **barbell** — deliberately staff both ends of the
experience spectrum and keep very little in the middle (Feb 2026, [[2026-02-22-yt-xOARq03-5eE]]).

> "I think it's a barbell strategy where you have people on either ends and very little in
> the middle." — [[2026-02-22-yt-xOARq03-5eE]]

One end is **young and hungry** — there for growth, want to learn, over-compensate with hours
and reps. The other end is **late-career** people who have already made their money, could get
a job anywhere, and are there because they genuinely love the work (not to be done a favor);
they bring experience and create leverage through strategy. The **careerist middle** is where
most failed hires come from:

> "The people in the middle are the ones that tend to be the pain in the ass for everybody." —
> [[2026-02-22-yt-xOARq03-5eE]]

Critically, this is **perspective, not age** — the split is whether someone is there for growth
or there to protect/share what they already know. Alex dislikes "careerists" fixated on title
and promotion who bring a tit-for-tat, transactional relationship.

> "I don't think this is actually an age thing. I think it's a perspective thing. So it's just
> are you here for growth or are you here to share what you know?" — [[2026-02-22-yt-xOARq03-5eE]]

_(The same clip also lays out his customer-success **"four Rs" — retain → review → refer →
resell**; that framework belongs to [[retention]], not hiring — cross-linked here only.)_

## The interview: assume you're bad at it, then decentralize and grade the ladder

Alex's interviewing method starts from a deliberately humbling premise — **assume you are bad at
hiring** (biases, blind spots) — which pushes the decision *outward* rather than trusting your
own read (Feb 2026, [[2026-02-24-yt-wN0Sq8Y3HcQ]]).

> "I live with the permanent assumption that I'm bad at hiring... And when you start with that
> assumption that you are really, really bad at hiring, you try and decentralize the decision." —
> [[2026-02-24-yt-wN0Sq8Y3HcQ]]

**Decentralize onto a panel of the best operators you know.** For a sales hire, build a ~5-person
interview panel — one may be you, the rest the best salespeople you've ever personally encountered
("who sold you your house?"). **Source elite talent via a referral cascade:** ask each expert for
the five best people they know at the skill, then ask *those* people the same, and repeat until
the same "god-tier" names recur. He cites Skool: non-technical founder Sam interviewed ~600
developers this way to surface Daniel, who became CTO/co-founder ([[2026-02-24-yt-wN0Sq8Y3HcQ]]).

**Run a senior interview like a consulting "rapid learning" session** — present the candidate the
actual problems of the role and ask "how would you solve these?", which yields free consulting
*and* reveals their thinking. Then grade on a **three-part rubric**:

> "One is the quality and quantity of metrics that they track. The second is the behaviors that
> they're going to do to influence those metrics... third, how do those metrics and these
> behaviors influence the revenue in the business? If they can explain that clearly, you have a
> winner." — [[2026-02-24-yt-wN0Sq8Y3HcQ]]

Experts think in **funnel components, not binaries** ("low CTR / unqualified traffic / weak page
conversion," not "ads don't work"), so the quality and quantity of metrics someone tracks is
itself a skill signal. Drill vague answers ("I'll coach the guys up") down to concrete actions —
"the more detailed they get, the more I believe they can do it." A complementary diagnostic works
for *any* role: ask how their work makes the company money; a support rep who answers "great
support → more referrals, retention, and cross-sell" is operating above their title.

## Recruiting senior / co-founder talent — the four elements of equity

Persuading an exceptional, well-paid, comfortable senior person to leave security for an early,
cash-poor startup splits into a **hard side** (compensation) and a **soft side** (the emotional
sell) (Feb 2026, [[2026-02-24-yt-0U6nqErQcRM]]). On the hard side, equity is not one thing but
**four separable elements** — each of which can be granted as actual shares *or* contracted around
synthetically:

> "You have control, so who gets to call the shots? You have risk, what happens if everything goes
> wrong, who's liable? You have profits, which is the cash flow of the business, who gets
> distributions. And then basically sale or enterprise value... who gets to participate in that
> upside? Those are... the four elements of equity." — [[2026-02-24-yt-0U6nqErQcRM]]

In practice a senior hire usually won't take the **risk** and won't get **control** (if it's your
company), so what you're really offering is **profit distributions plus sale upside** — which you
can structure without ever handing over stock. His negotiation opener is to stop guessing and just
ask — a frame that also *assumes the yes*:

> "My favorite question for these types of scenarios is asking what would it take?... because I
> don't know — he'll tell me." — [[2026-02-24-yt-0U6nqErQcRM]]

On the soft side, recruiting a co-founder or early leader is **fundamentally a sale — the same
pitch you'd give an investor**, except they invest time, life, and expertise instead of capital:

> "That pitch that you're giving to a potential co-founder... is the same more or less that you'd
> give to an investor... except instead of investing capital, they're investing time... their
> life... their expertise. But fundamentally, you're asking for investment." —
> [[2026-02-24-yt-0U6nqErQcRM]]

His emotional archetype is the Steve Jobs → John Sculley "keep selling sugar water, or come change
the world" recruiting line. This complements the [[#The debt-choice framework for bootstrapped
hiring|equity-for-talent]] thread above: that section frames equity as the *founder's* debt choice;
this one gives the *mechanics* of what you actually hand a senior hire.

## Widen the hirable pool — reduce required skill, recruit adjacent, cap span of control

When an owner names "not enough qualified sales directors" as the constraint, Alex reframes it as a
**self-imposed belief**, not a real shortage — especially when the recruiting math already works
~10:1 (Feb 2026, [[2026-02-21-yt-uzdBX8r7-zA]]). Two levers widen the hirable TAM:

**(1) Refine the script to lower the required skill.** A too-consultative process forces reliance
on rare "unicorn" reps; a more refined, repeatable script lets ordinary trained reps succeed —
which is where the arbitrage opens up.

> "Fix the scripting around the sales so that you don't need a $60,000 person and then all of a
> sudden this arbitrage will get really juicy and you will massively open up the TAM of potential
> sales prospects." — [[2026-02-21-yt-uzdBX8r7-zA]]

**(2) Recruit adjacent pools.** Door-to-door salespeople already build and manage hard-selling
teams in worse conditions than selling from a computer at home — "the learning curve would just be
teaching them" the category's language, not the craft of selling. (This is the reduce-the-skill-bar
mechanism behind the prospect-not-salesman scripting in the [[#Sales-god → sales-training/recruiting-god
— codify the founder's "black box"|black-box]] section.)

**Span of control.** Directors are **culture and training, not phone work** — they "arguably need
*less* product knowledge than frontline reps because teaching sales is teaching sales." Alex caps a
director at **~10 reps** (15 is high) and starts splitting a team once two managers each carry ~7–8
reps ([[2026-02-21-yt-uzdBX8r7-zA]]).
