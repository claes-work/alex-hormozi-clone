# Communication & Management

Alex Hormozi's frameworks for **internal clarity** — articulating thoughts precisely,
giving instructions that actually change behavior, and diagnosing why an employee didn't
do what you asked. This page covers his *management-and-articulation* doctrine; it is
distinct from [[sales-communication]], which covers the *delivery* side of communicating
with prospects (framing, tonality, objection handling). Here the object is your own team
and your own precision of thought, not the buyer.

Source: [[../../sources/2024-09-11-yt-s6tkRztZwYc]] (Skool "school day" Q&A excerpt,
2024-09-11, L2). Attribution: Alex is the dominant speaker; the management-and-articulation
material below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.

Related: [[../mindset/mental-toughness-and-learning]] (his observable-behavior /
consequences view of how skills are learned underpins this framework).

## Articulation: debundle terms into behaviors

Hormozi's core communication move is **debundling** (a.k.a. unbundling) vague "bundled"
terms into observable behaviors. Soft-skill labels — *charismatic*, *patient*,
*motivated*, "being a dick" — are not single things; each is a bundle of concrete,
observable activities. Communication fails because people trade in the bundle instead of
naming the behaviors inside it (paraphrase, [[../../sources/2024-09-11-yt-s6tkRztZwYc]]).

The practical rule: never give the label as the directive. Name the specific behaviors to
start or stop.

> "Rather than saying be charismatic, say I need you to do these six things, or stop
> doing these six things that you are doing." — verbatim

Worked example — "be charismatic" debundles into observable behaviors like: use vocal
range, pause for emphasis, say people's names, make eye contact, nod (paraphrase). "Stop
being a dick" is the same failure in the other direction:

> "Stop being a dick is actually a pretty terrible directive... they're like, you just
> want me to not be me, and you're like, kind of." — verbatim

Behavior change comes from specific instructions, not insults or "be nicer." Hormozi says
he deliberately **erased "feelings, intentions, beliefs" from his behavioral vocabulary**
and reasons only from the observable:

> "I don't care if you feel motivated or not. If you do these activities, people describe
> you that way." — verbatim

> "I want to be clear: I don't care if you're a dick, I don't care if you hate everyone,
> as long as you behave this way, doesn't matter to me." — verbatim

> "If you reason up from what can I observe with my eyes, it makes life a lot easier."
> — verbatim

**Soft skills and hard skills are all just skills** — soft skills are only harder to teach
because they are *bundled language*, not because they are inherently harder ("Soft skills
and hard skills are all just skills. It's just that soft skills people just have a harder
time teaching because it's bundled language." — verbatim). Operationalize a soft skill
with a **checklist**: score people against an explicit list (he cites an 11-item example);
if a low scorer follows all items and still underperforms, the checklist — i.e., the
manager's training — is wrong, not the employee (paraphrase).

**Why this makes content clearer than 99% of people.** Hormozi ties debundling directly to
why his content outperforms: he *defines the terms* other people leave undefined. Most
communicators pass bundled words back and forth; defining them into observables is the
articulation edge (paraphrase, [[../../sources/2024-09-11-yt-s6tkRztZwYc]]).

## The logic / evidence / utility filter

To cut through imprecise language, Hormozi runs any claim through three questions
(paraphrase, [[../../sources/2024-09-11-yt-s6tkRztZwYc]]):

1. **Logic — "what does this mean?"** Force the vague term to resolve into something
   concrete. (e.g. dismantling "I'm stressed because I'm lonely" by asking what each word
   actually refers to.)
2. **Evidence — "how do you know?"** Demand the observation behind the claim.
3. **Utility — "so what?"** If it resolves and is true, does it change what you'd do? A
   claim that survives all three is worth saying; one that fails any is noise.

This is the diagnostic companion to debundling: debundling produces observable terms, the
logic/evidence/utility filter decides whether a statement built from them is worth
uttering at all. He uses it both inbound (testing others' vague statements like "make a
customer worth more in three ways") and on his own thinking.

## The Management Diamond (5 reasons)

Hormozi frames the single most valuable skill as getting other people to do things for
you:

> "The single most valuable skill set you can have is getting other people to do things
> for you." — verbatim

When someone *didn't* do what you asked, the **Management Diamond** is his five-reason
diagnostic. Each reason has a matching fix (paraphrase,
[[../../sources/2024-09-11-yt-s6tkRztZwYc]]):

| # | They didn't do it because… | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | They didn't know you **wanted** it | Communication |
| 2 | They didn't know **what** to do / **how** to do it | Training |
| 3 | They didn't know **when** it was due | Deadlines |
| 4 | They weren't **incentivized / motivated** | Reinforcement / incentives |
| 5 | Something was **blocking** them (circumstances) | Remove the impediment |

If more than one reason applies, fix more than one. Hormozi presents running through the
five as a **script for hard conversations** — walking the diamond turns a confrontation
into a diagnosis, which makes the conversation "not hard" (paraphrase).

Two supporting principles from the same source:

- **Behavior is driven by consequences, not antecedents** — "People do things because of
  what happens after they do them, not because of what happened before." (verbatim).
  Managers must artificially insert feedback loops into roles whose natural loops are slow;
  tight-loop roles (sales, editing) self-reinforce, slow-loop ones don't ("No one cold
  calls in their free time." — verbatim). This connects reason #4 to reinforcement design.
- **Motivation comes from deprivation** — "Motivation fundamentally comes from
  deprivation — which is, what are you most deprived of." (verbatim). Reinforce each person
  with what they're most deprived of (e.g. praise); money is a "conditioned universal
  reinforcer" (paraphrase).
- **The more skilled the person, the vaguer the instruction can be** — "The more skilled
  the person is, the bigger the instruction can be." (verbatim). Experts have more ways to
  be rewarded in the same context; low-skill people need highly specific instruction.
  Training usually fails because you assumed a skill the person didn't have (paraphrase).
  This is the bridge between articulation (how specific to be) and management (whom you're
  instructing).

### 2026 Q&A restatement: diagnose motivation last

In a May 2026 Q&A, Hormozi restates the Management Diamond in a shorter four-part
operator form: employees fail because they do not know **what**, do not know **how**, do
not know **when**, or are blocked. Motivation should be diagnosed last, not assumed
first; the manager should find the missing condition before attacking the person
[[../../sources/2026-05-09-yt-IuOjYZMQu_I]]. The same clip adds two hiring implications:
hire for the smallest skill deficiency the company can afford to train, and treat
intelligence operationally as rate of learning, not as a vague trait
[[../../sources/2026-05-09-yt-IuOjYZMQu_I]].

## Delegation vs. abdication

Hormozi distinguishes delegation from abdication by what happens to the **output quality**
after the handoff (paraphrase, [[../../sources/2024-09-11-yt-s6tkRztZwYc]]):

- **Delegation** — after the handoff, output stays neutral or gets *better*. You actually
  transferred the work.
- **Abdication** — after the handoff, output gets *worse*. You just dumped it.

> "If you hand it off and it gets worse after you hand it off, it's because you didn't
> delegate it, you abdicated it — you just said, here you go, good luck." — verbatim

The common failure mode: entrepreneurs verbally dump instructions once, expect 100%
retention, then are surprised weeks later that it wasn't done (paraphrase). Proper
delegation is the Management Diamond applied *up front* — communicating the want, training
the how, setting the when, building the incentive, and clearing impediments — rather than
diagnosing after the fact.

## Operator-context note: crisis communication and one-on-ones

> Attribution: this July 2025 Operator Podcast episode is **not Alex primary speech**.
> The transcript is Leila Hormozi / Sharran Srivatsaa operator context and should not be
> used as Alex persona voice data unless later corroborated by Alex-attributed sources
> [[../../sources/2025-07-26-yt-GmJQyCFCz1k]].

The context note is still useful for the Acquisition.com operating environment. In a
public crisis, the hosts argue for fast truth-telling, accepting responsibility, avoiding
PR evasions that compound the lie, tying the apology to violated core values, and then
rebuilding reputation through consistent changed behavior over time. The market-leader
version is to be first with useful guidance for the whole industry, so the brand becomes
associated with the solution rather than the panic
[[../../sources/2025-07-26-yt-GmJQyCFCz1k]].

For internal operating cadence, the same episode treats one-on-ones as prepared working
sessions, not casual check-ins: use a running log, require an agenda or memo, use
end-of-week reporting so blockers are visible, adapt structure to seniority, and protect
the person's current major focus when that focus matters more than a broad agenda
[[../../sources/2025-07-26-yt-GmJQyCFCz1k]].
