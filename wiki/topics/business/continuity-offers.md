# Continuity Offers

Continuity Offers are the fourth and final stage of Hormozi's Money Model — "how to keep
them buying for good." They provide ongoing value that customers make ongoing payments
for until they cancel: "you sell once, but get paid again and again"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 161–162).
His core illustration of why recurring beats one-time: sell a $1,000 thing to 100 people
and 10 buy — $10,000 now, $0 over time. Repackage the same thing at $50/month and 40 of
the 100 buy; keep them 20 months and each is still worth $1,000 — $2,000 now and $40,000
over time, plus four times the customers to upsell later
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 162–163).
The tradeoff is that continuity makes "way less money now": used as an Attraction Offer
it leaves you strapped for cash today. So Hormozi deploys continuity LAST, after the
cash-rich Attraction, Upsell, and Downsell Offers, then upsells prepaid bulk time on top
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 163, 167, 191).

> "You can shear a sheep for a lifetime, but you can only skin it once." — John, an
> early mentor ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 161)

> "All offers depend on getting customers to buy. But, Continuity Offers depend on
> getting customers to keep buying."
> ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 163)

Fit rule: only some products make sense for continuity. Paying forever for a one-day
workshop is "silly" — that's a payment plan. But "if your customers get ongoing value,
it probably makes sense for them to make ongoing payments" (paraphrase of pp. 163).
Continuity can attract new customers, upsell/downsell current ones, or re-engage old
ones — but it works best last. He teaches three types: Continuity Bonus Offers,
Continuity Discount Offers, and the Waived Fee Offer
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 163–164).

## Continuity Bonus Offers

Tagline: *"If you like this, you're gonna love what I have next…"* Definition: give the
customer an awesome thing if they sign up today, where the bonus itself is typically
worth more than the first continuity payment. Two levers work together — bonuses add
value, discounts lower costs; anything offered free can also be offered as a discount —
and it "all works better with a dash of urgency—if they join now"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 165, 167).

**Origin story (Fall 2019, self-reported).** A struggling gym in Hormozi's Gym Launch
program suddenly outperformed his top performers. Instead of selling the marketed
six-week challenge, the owner pitched it, then offered it *free* if the prospect became
a member — plus member-exclusive bonuses (better class times, tanning booth, VIP
events) — then upsold a discounted six-month prepaid membership ("wanna save even more
money?"). Numbers: before, 34 of 100 signed up for the challenge and about half (17)
converted to memberships weeks later. After, only ~15 bought the challenge, but 40 went
straight into continuity, and ~8 of those 40 took the six-month prepayment upsell — the
gym "tripled membership sales" while keeping up-front challenge cash and stacking
prepaid membership cash
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 165–167).

**Mechanics / script** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 167, 170):

1. Run big-cash Attraction, Upsell, and Downsell Offers first.
2. Sell the benefits of the amazing bonus first (not the membership), using its
   high value as an anchor — "It may shock them—and that's okay."
3. Ask: "Do you want to know how you can get this for free?"
4. Reveal: become a member today and get it all as a free gift for joining — or buy it
   standalone for $XXX. "Which would you prefer?"
5. Stack more bonuses ("on top of that…when you become a member you'll get…"), naming
   each bonus's individual dollar value to anchor.
6. For joiners, upsell a bulk prepaid discount to boost 30-day profits.

**Standalone-vs-continuity pricing curve.** Hormozi's tested data ("I've tested this a
ton"): price the standalone one-time option above the continuity price to steer buyers
into continuity — 1.33x → 50% choose continuity (e.g., $399 standalone / $199 per
month), 1.66x → 60%, 2x → 70%, 2.33x → 80%, 2.66x → 90%. The principle over the
numbers: the smaller the standalone price relative to continuity, the more people buy
standalone; the larger, the more choose continuity. Key insight: "people pay 33% more
to avoid continuity" — even at +33%, half still buy the one-time option
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 171–173).

**Best practices** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 169–173):

- **Advertise the bonus, not the membership.** "'Join my membership program' isn't
  nearly as compelling as 'get this free valuable thing.' So advertise that."
- **Bonuses work like upsells** — more of the same (past newsletters), complementary
  (free nutrition with fitness membership), or upgrade (free gold tier with bronze).
- **Keep bonuses related to the core offer** — a free t-shirt to upsell tech services
  attracts the wrong customers; a free t-shirt to upsell t-shirt printing makes sense.
- **Use things you already have and do** (past content, onboarding you deliver anyway):
  "slap a price on it and give to 'em as a bonus. If you value it, they will too."
- **Cross-medium bonuses** (physical-on-digital, digital-on-physical) often lower the
  cost of acquiring a customer more than the bonus costs — "that's the point."
- **Realistic bonus pricing.** Made-up ridiculous values don't anchor and destroy
  trust; use real prices of products you've actually sold.
- **Titles as zero-cost bonuses** (silver/gold/diamond/double diamond at 3/6/12+
  months): a friend of his found customers cared about the title more than any other
  bonus — they introduced themselves by it.
- **Bulk prepaid discounts** (e.g., buy five months get one free): "Only one out of
  every eight people has to take the upsell to raise 30-day profits by 50%!"
- **Trade bonuses for 3/6/12+ month commitments** — "This nets fewer sales, but more
  committed customers. This is the trade you make."

**Works best** as the final stage after up-front-cash offers, and when you have existing
high-value assets to bundle. **Caveats:** low up-front cash if used alone; unrelated
bonuses attract wrong leads; inflated bonus values kill trust; some customers take the
bonus and run (mitigated by lower acquisition costs)
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 169–173).

## Continuity Discount Offers

Tagline: *"If you sign up today, you get X time free."* Definition: give products or
services away free if the customer commits to buying more over time — "Buy X Get Y
Free" done continuity-style. It attracts loads of potential customers and "makes an
easy sale anyone can close"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 176, 178).

**Origin story (Spring 2018, self-reported).** Hormozi's neighbor in a nice Austin
suburb — backwards hat, flip flops, Ferrari in the garage — was a trash entrepreneur
who won the local commercial market by offering big apartment complexes a whole year of
trash service *free* if they contracted him for the next five years paid ("Well, I had
a truck, took my credit card, and I gambled"). Fronting the free year was brutal — no
investors, family called him crazy — but after the one-year mark "the cash came
floodin'," and years later he sold the company "for a pretty penny"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 176–177).

**Two prerequisites:** decide (1) how you'll apply the discount and (2) your
cancellation policy, "because people don't always keep their commitments"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 178).

**Four discount placements** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 178–181):

1. **Up front** — free time first, official term starts after. Best in industries with
   a history of enforcing contracts (cell phones, storage, real estate, equipment,
   anything with collateral). Skip if churn is historically high: "It gets customers,
   but delays cash."
2. **At the end** — bonus time equal to the discount, appended after the term,
   conditional on every payment being on time. "They earn their free time." Converts
   fewer, lowers churn.
3. **Spread over time** — e.g., three months free on a one-year commitment at
   $200/month = a $600 discount; $600 / 12 = $50 off each month. Optional kicker: make
   every payment on time and keep the discount for life. Keeps cash flowing while
   giving the full discount.
4. **After the first 1–2 payments** — collect a few full payments first to cover
   advertising and delivery costs (framed as "first and last month" or an activation
   fee), then apply the discount. Also verifies the customer's payment method works —
   "a small but important detail."

A structural rule: discounts should **extend** the term, not eat into it — "three
months free on a year" means pay 12 get 3 free (15 months total), not pay 9 get 3 free.
Start with the extended version, then Feature Downsell a shorter one
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 182).

**Cancellations.** Decide the policy ahead of time. Hormozi's favorite, since everyone
enters on a discount: **the cancellation fee equals the discount they received** — got
$600 in discounts for committing, pay $600 to cancel anytime ("Simple to explain"),
putting them back at the month-to-month rate. Make sure customers know how to cancel:
"If customers have nowhere to complain inside your business, they will definitely
complain outside of your business" — a clear path means fewer vanishings, fewer 1-star
reviews, and a chance to save them
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 183–184).

**Churn tradeoffs:** front-loaded discounts convert more but churn more; back-loaded
discounts convert fewer but churn less; spreading keeps cash flowing with the full
discount. Light cancellation terms bring more sign-ups and more leavers; harsher terms
the reverse. Works best where recurring need meets contract-enforcement history
(internet, pool cleaning, gyms, landscaping, "anything rentable") — but he says it can
work in any business if placement and cancellation policy are nailed
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 178, 184–185).

## Waived Fee Offer

Tagline: *"You can sign up month-to-month with a setup fee, or I'll waive it if you
commit to a year."* Mechanics: charge a startup fee — typically **3–5x the monthly
rate** — for a month-to-month program, then offer to waive the entire fee for a longer
commitment (a year minimum). If they cancel inside the term, they pay the fee
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 186–187, 190).

**Origin story (January 2021, self-reported).** Hormozi met a "legend of high-ticket
sales" — a lifestyle operator making a few million a year with zero employees ("I don't
need to feed my ego, I just collect monthly payments and chill") — who presented every
customer two options: month-to-month with a big setup fee ("It covers the cost of
getting you started, but you can leave whenever"), or commit to a year and the fee is
waived, with the buyer initialing that quitting early means paying it. The fee is huge
on purpose: "It costs a lot to quit in the beginning, so that keeps them engaged," and
past a certain point it costs about the same to cancel as to stick it out — so they
stick it out ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 186–187).

**Risk logic.** The business takes greater risk on month-to-month customers, so the
startup fee covers it; the customer takes greater risk by committing, so the waived fee
covers that. Early quitters simply pay as if they'd chosen month-to-month from the
start ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 187).

> "Bottom Line: Customers will stay longer if leaving costs more than staying."
> ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 188)

**Worked example:** 12-month commitment, $1,000/month, $5,000 fee. Option A: pay a
one-time $5,000 fee plus $1,000 for the first month, then $1,000/month, cancel anytime.
Option B: the $5,000 is waived with a 12-month commitment at $1,000/month — payable
only if the commitment is broken early
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 188).

**Tuning and caveats** ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 188–190):

- Fees get them to start (avoiding the fee is immediate value: "People want to avoid
  fees. So, more people sign up to continuity. Mission accomplished.") and to stick
  ("if the cost to quit exceeds the cost to stay, they will probably stay").
- Present the fee via setup costs: "It costs us money to get you started. If you only
  wanna test us out, you cover those costs. If you commit longer, I'll cover them."
- **Health check: if more than 5% of customers want to cancel early, look into the
  product.** Pricing should nudge, "not handcuff people into paying for something they
  hate. Then, they'll just hate you."
- Want more up-front cash? Use a smaller fee (1.5–3x monthly) — more people go
  month-to-month. A larger fee drives more commitments.
- Drop the fee once the commitment is fulfilled — completing the term earns free
  cancellation. "This makes it equitable."
- Works best on 12+ month commitments and slow-payoff services (SEO, investing, weight
  loss) — "It keeps people committed when they get emotional."
- Optional spite play: donate early-cancel fees to a cause the customer hates ("What
  cause do you absolutely hate?...Great. If you cancel early, I will be donating your
  setup fee to them.") — two reasons to stay: money and spite.

## Retention and billing tactics

The Continuity Discount chapter contains what Hormozi labels his **"Highest Value Per
Word Note In This Book"** — subtitled "Skip this if you hate money": **bill every four
weeks instead of monthly.** 13 four-week cycles vs. 12 monthly cycles = 8.3% more
revenue per year, with identical conversion ("$100 every four weeks" converts the same
as "$100 a month"). At 20% margins, that 8.3% "skyrockets annual profit by 41%"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 181–182).

> "What else can you do legally that makes so much money for so little work? This has
> literally made me millions in pure profit. So yeah, do it."
> ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 181–182)

The rest of the retention/billing stack
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 182–184):

- **3% processing fee** — five extra words ("Yea, it's $X plus a 3% processing fee").
  He claims he has never had anyone not buy because of a processing fee; at a
  10%-profit business, +3% topline is +30% profit.
- **Two forms of payment** — recurring businesses "lose mountains of cash" to payment
  problems (expired/changed cards, insufficient funds). Fix: offer to waive the 3%
  processing fee in exchange for a second payment method ("if you save us time, we pass
  the savings onto you"). Get ACH if you can — bank-linked, "the cheapest way to
  transact besides cash."
- **Gift cards** — issue the discounted time as a physical gift card redeemable after
  the first ~3 payments, giftable to a friend (a built-in lead magnet). Many forget to
  use it: "you just got a full-priced sign-up. Nice!"
- **Lifetime discount at the average churn point** — advertise a permanently lower rate
  earned by staying past month X, where X sits just beyond where the average customer
  cancels. Real-world sighting: a rice company with a one-time price, 5% off
  subscription, and 15% off after five straight subscription months.
- **Cancellation fee = discount received** — the fee to cancel anytime equals the
  discount the customer got for committing; simple to explain and puts them back at
  the month-to-month rate.
- **Exit interviews** — "Some people like to vent. Let them. Get more angry about the
  problem than them. They may try to calm you down. Sometimes, they will save
  themselves!" Waive the cancellation fee in exchange for the interview ("I'll waive
  your cancellation fee if you come in and tell me what I could do better"), solve
  solvable complaints, and rollover-upsell customers who actually wanted higher-tier
  features. **His metric: "I routinely save a third of customers that agree to exit
  interviews."**

## Doctrine

The section's conclusion restates the sequencing philosophy: run a profitable
Attraction Offer, maximize cash with Upsell and Downsell Offers, then make the
Continuity Offer last — "a little cash today and tons of cash tomorrow." After the
customer accepts continuity, upsell prepaid bulk months at a discount; when the bulk
time runs out, they automatically continue on regular continuity. "The only thing
better than getting someone to buy once, is getting them to buy again"
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 191).

> "Continuity Offers work with rewards or punishment. I prefer rewards."
> ([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF p. 191)

Two of the three offer types (bonuses, discounts) run on rewards; the Waived Fee Offer
fits where a more traditional contract makes sense. The section closes by previewing
Section VI — combining all four offer types into a complete $100M Money Model
([[../../sources/2025-08-16-100m-money-models|$100M Money Models]], PDF pp. 191–192).
