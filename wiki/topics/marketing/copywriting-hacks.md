# Copywriting Hacks

Alex Hormozi's copywriting/advertising playbook, drawn primarily from his solo
masterclass ["14 Years of Marketing Advice in 35 Minutes"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JDR-R--4HhM)
(2025-05-07). He frames it as the internal checklist his portfolio companies run every
piece of copy or ad through. See [[marketing]] for the domain hub,
[[../content-strategy/hormozi-writing-guidelines]] for the general writing rules, and
[[../business/sales-frameworks]] for the persuasion tactics these overlap with.

> ⚠️ Self-reported: The authority framing — "14 years in business," "tens of millions per
> year in paid ads across companies he actually owns" — is his own on-camera claim
> (source: [[../../sources/2025-05-07-yt-JDR-R--4HhM]]), not registry-verified here.

## The 12+1 hacks

His copywriting checklist — 12 core hacks plus a bonus 13th
(source: [[../../sources/2025-05-07-yt-JDR-R--4HhM]]):

1. **Headlines come first.** Quoting Ogilvy: *"Once you've written your headline, you spent
   80 cents of your advertising dollar."* A great headline can beat the control by 2–5x
   (the "law of small numbers": lifting CTR 1% → 3% triples everyone downstream).
   Curiosity is king, different is ideal, sexy works. *"All ads have headlines. There's
   just bad ones and good ones."* Model the first 3 seconds (visual + verbal) of working
   organic content and ads; "steal like an artist" from other industries.
2. **Say what only you can say — proof beats promise.** *"Competitors can copy your offer,
   but they cannot copy your proof. That has to be earned."* Be one of a kind by
   controlling the pond you are one of a kind in. (Expanded in
   [Proof beats promise](#proof-beats-promise) below.)
3. **Always call out who you're looking for** — and, more powerfully, who you're *not*
   looking for (disqualify by revenue/weight/etc.). Polarize; don't be vanilla. Advertise
   to *one* person so the reader thinks "how does he know my secrets?" Ladder through the
   [5 levels of awareness](#5-levels-of-awareness). Name the *moment* they feel the pain,
   not vague "are you overweight?" language.
4. **Always have a reason why.** The word "because" is one of the most influential words in
   English (Langer/Cialdini copier-line study: any reason beats no reason). The bar for a
   reason is low — it need not be good, only present. Sell one step at a time; every line
   of copy sells the next line. *"A confused mind does not buy, and a confused mind
   certainly does not click."* Bonus if the reason ties to urgency/scarcity.
5. **Damaging admissions** (possibly his favorite). Owning your flaws makes you more
   believable because it's rooted in truth. *"What you'll sacrifice in promise, you'll gain
   in trust. And trust trumps promise."* (Expanded in
   [Damaging admissions](#damaging-admissions-but-mechanic) below.)
6. **Show, don't tell.** Break down the reasoning and describe the *moment* and sensory
   experience, not the abstract benefit — "running out of order forms because customers
   read their cards too fast" instead of "get more sales." Describe the future good
   problems they'd prefer to have. Unique moment-description = naturally fresh copy no one
   else writes.
7. **Tie benefits to status.** Go past benefits-not-features to *who awards the status*
   (family, friends, colleagues, rivals). Humans are competitive/status-seeking (mating +
   resources). Status is subgroup-specific (a mom's excelling kids vs. a hustle-bro's
   Lamborghini). Use the past/present/future and "what/who/when" framework (*$100M Leads*,
   p.140) for endless benefit angles.
8. **Urgency and scarcity** (distinct). See [Scarcity vs urgency](#scarcity-vs-urgency).
9. **Implied authority.** Credentials, longevity, client rosters, "as seen on," tiny local
   "best of" awards (they let you open a sales convo), aggregate numbers ("in aggregate
   we've done over 20 years of advertising"). Proof/social proof beats promise — 4.6 stars
   with 4,000 reviews beats 5.0 with 3 reviews.
10. **Always have a PS statement** ("power sentence"). The headline and the PS are the two
    most-read parts of any ad; the PPS is third. Top plays: (a) a fun email sign-off that
    trains readers to always read/click the PS; (b) a disclaimer of who it's *not* for;
    (c) a one-sentence recap of the whole pitch.
11. **Clear CTA.** Tell them exactly what to do next, stupid-simple, step by step, and tell
    them what happens next. "Say-do correspondence": when what you predicted comes true
    after they act, you gain influence (the Martha Stewart recipe effect — make promises,
    keep promises, repeatedly, contingent on their action). *"The reason I called my whole
    company acquisition.com was so that the name of my company was the call to action."*
12. **Third-grade reading level.** Assume the prospect is rushed, distracted, doesn't care,
    and reads below third grade. *"Fancy equals friction, simple equals sales."* Short
    sentences, small words, big promises, big proof; few adverbs, better verbs ("sprinted"
    > "quickly ran"). But *"simplicity trumps concision"* — never pick fewer words over
    easier words. Nth-grade copy auto-disqualifies ~two-thirds of readers. Run every ad
    through a free reading-level tool.

**Bonus (13) — Use humor.** Entertainment goes broad, education goes deep (education
changes behavior → the click). The Old Spice ad is his highest-converting example. Humor
is the one he'd cut first (the other 12 convert without it) but it's the extra sizzle. Test
humor like comedians build specials — front-load the bits that land, iterate over many rooms.

## 5 levels of awareness

Borrowed from Eugene Schwartz (via hack 3), the awareness ladder tells you where the reader
starts so your copy can meet them there and move them one rung at a time
(source: [[../../sources/2025-05-07-yt-JDR-R--4HhM]]):

1. **Unaware** — doesn't know they have a problem.
2. **Problem-aware** — feels the pain, doesn't know a solution exists.
3. **Solution-aware** — knows solutions exist, not yours specifically.
4. **Product-aware** — knows your product, not yet convinced.
5. **Most-aware** — ready to buy, just needs the offer/CTA.

The more aware the reader, the more directly you can pitch; the less aware, the more you
must lead with the problem-moment. Specificity ("name the moment they feel the pain") is
how you make an unaware/problem-aware reader think *"how does he know my secrets?"*

## Scarcity vs urgency

Hormozi treats these as two distinct levers, defined by what they restrict
(source: [[../../sources/2025-05-07-yt-JDR-R--4HhM]]):

> "Scarcity is a function of quantity. Urgency is a function of time."

- **Scarcity = quantity** (limited seats/units/spots). *$100M Offers*, p.104.
- **Urgency = time** (deadlines; the book covers four types). *$100M Offers*, p.112.

> ⚠️ Self-reported: the specific *$100M Offers* page numbers (scarcity p.104, urgency p.112)
> and *$100M Leads* p.140 are his own on-camera citations of his own books.

The only "secret" is making both **legitimate**. Say no to buyers after the deadline/cap to
protect reputation — *"You're digging your well before you're thirsty. I would always prefer
to undersell my demand so I always have more later."* Manufactured/fake scarcity burns
trust and undermines every other hack.

## Damaging admissions ("but"-mechanic)

His self-described *"single favorite technique in persuasion"* — deliberately state a true
negative first, then use "but" to amplify the positive that follows. Detailed in a dedicated
talk (source: [[../../sources/2025-03-15-yt-Ihfq36wuxQY]]) and hack 5 of the masterclass
(source: [[../../sources/2025-05-07-yt-JDR-R--4HhM]]).

The linguistic mechanic:

> "Everything that happens here after the word 'but' is amplified, and everything that is
> before the word 'but' is diminished."

So **reverse the usual order.** Most people write *positive-but-negative* ("you'll make a
ton of money, but it'll be a ton of work"), which spotlights the negative. Flip it to
*negative-but-positive* ("you'll go through hundreds of hours of video and spend five hours
a day, **but** you'll make more money than you ever have"). The clause after "but" is what
lands. (His illustration: "markers smell bad *but* write longer" vs. "write longer *but*
smell bad.")

Why it works — it's rooted in truth, and trust drives the buy:

> "The degree to which they will buy is directly correlated with how much they trust you."

> "What you'll sacrifice in promise, you'll gain in trust. And trust trumps promise."

- The more negatives you admit up front — the more true and damaging they are — the more
  believable the positive that follows. *"You're just saying all of the things that someone
  is going to find out eventually — you might as well use those things to increase the
  persuasiveness of your argument."*
- **Claim your flaws first** so the critic has nothing left — he credits Eminem's 8 Mile
  final battle, where Eminem pre-empts every insult against himself.
- **The Viagra archetype:** the "erection lasting longer than 4 hours" warning states a
  genuine extreme risk that amplified the product's perceived power.
- **In reviews/social proof:** negative reviews give credence to the positive ones (see
  [Proof beats promise](#proof-beats-promise)).
- **Where to use it:** weave into copy near the CTA, and into sales presentations.
- Add humor for bonus points. Overlaps with the CLOSER sales tactics in
  [[../business/sales-frameworks]].

## Proof beats promise

The through-line behind hacks 2 and 9: anyone can *promise*, but proof must be *earned*
(source: [[../../sources/2025-05-07-yt-JDR-R--4HhM]]).

> "Competitors can copy your offer, but they cannot copy your proof. That has to be earned."

- Proof is the moat — offers are copyable, proof is not. In an AI world, "AI avatars can lie
  but can never have *done* something"; verified story and track record grow more valuable as
  trust erodes.
- **Volume of proof beats perfection of promise:** a 4.6-star rating with 4,000 reviews beats
  a 5.0 with 3 reviews. (He restates this elsewhere as preferring a 4.7-star restaurant with
  3,000 ratings over 5 stars with 19 — source: [[../../sources/2025-03-15-yt-Ihfq36wuxQY]].)
- **Implied authority is proof:** credentials, longevity, client rosters, "as seen on," even
  tiny local awards, and aggregate numbers all substitute earned credibility for claimed
  benefit.
- Ties back to **say/do correspondence** (hack 11): the strongest proof is the reader's own
  experience — predict what happens after they act, be right, and influence compounds.

> "If you want to stand out in a crowded marketplace, tell the truth."
