# Paid Ads

Alex Hormozi's paid-ads playbook, drawn from his solo masterclass
[[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI|"Learn Paid Ads in 30 Minutes!"]] (2024) and
grounded in his written canon: the paid-ads chapters of [[../../sources/2023-100m-leads|`$100M Leads`]]
(2023, the foundation), the [[../../sources/2025-100m-goated-ads-playbook|`$100M GOATed Ads Playbook`]]
(2025, the assembly-line system "on top of" that foundation), and the
[[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook|`Proof Checklist Playbook`]] (2025,
proof-led creative). The through-line: paid ads are won on **creative**, not on clever media
buying — so the bulk of the work is systematically reskinning, re-cutting, and *assembling*
proven winners, not inventing new ads. Pair this with [[copywriting-hacks]] (the ad-copy inputs — hooks, proof, awareness)
and with [[../business/acquisition-channels]] (paid ads as one of the `$100M Leads` core
channels). Hub: [[marketing]].

## Creative

Creative is the primary lever. Two doctrines dominate:

**Reskin proven winners (~80% of resources).** His teams spend the large majority of their
creative effort taking ads that already work and making variations of them, not making
net-new ads (self-reported allocation).

> "For us, we spend 80% of our resources taking winners and just reskinning them."
> — [[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI]]

The "kale ad" example: one strong testimonial spawned ~100 versions — black-and-white,
sepia, reordered back end, swapped hooks / headlines / subheads / fonts / copy. Run
winners longer and harder, in more variations, until they stop converting entirely.

> "I will use and abuse it until it doesn't work at all. When you have your winners, you
> usually just need to run them way longer, way harder, in a lot of different variations."
> — [[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI]]

**Hook splicing (first 3 seconds).** To manufacture more winners, pull the **top 5–10% of
ads**, isolate their **first 3 seconds**, and graft those proven openers onto 100+ other
ads — yielding roughly 10–50x more creative per proven hook. Scaling spend is downstream of
scaling creative volume:

> "If you want to get to 20 million a year, it's going to be you doing 10 times or 50 times
> more creative in terms of what you're putting out."
> — [[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI]]

**Pain-led hooks, proof over promise.** Lead every ad with the **prospect's specific pain**,
not with yourself (e.g. for dentists: not wanting to work weekends, not wanting to sell to
private equity). ~80% of the ads his companies run are about **proof**, not the founder —
"no one cares" about you; buyers want to see people like them who got the result. Proof
sits on a believability continuum (in-person > virtual, live > recorded, looks-like-me >
anonymous, exact-result > close result — the hardest-to-fake proof is most compelling). The
differentiator is proof, not the promise:

> "Your promise is not unique; your proof is. Everyone in the marketplace promises the same
> thing. The question is whether they can back it up, and that is unique to you."
> — [[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI]]

You only lose trust by saying something untrue; naming the prospect's own fact of life
costs you nothing. (See [[copywriting-hacks]] for the copy-level treatment of proof,
awareness levels, and damaging admissions.)

The canonical source for the proof-over-promise doctrine is the **Proof Checklist
Playbook** (2025). Its origin story is itself an ad diagnosis: when the fund that bought
Gym Launch reported GL's ad costs had **doubled** (July 2024), Hormozi ranked his top-50
ads and found the one variable separating winners from losers was proof — **~80% of the
winning ads carried no education, weren't offer-focused, and didn't put him center-stage;
they told one story: "This. Product. Works."** So proof-led creative reliably beats
offer-led or education-led creative.

> "80% of our winning ads... Told one story - This. Product. Works."
> — [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]

## The ad assembly line (GOATed Ads, 2025)

The **$100M GOATed Ads Playbook** (2025) industrializes the reskin doctrine into a
production line. Its thesis: a business that stalls on paid ads has not saturated the
market — it has hit a wall in **ad quality**. The fix is not more spend per ad but
dramatically *more and better* ads.

> "You haven't saturated Facebook. But you have gotten all the low hanging fruit. You've
> hit a wall with your ad quality, not with the market. The better your ads, the bigger the
> audience they'll convert." — [[../../sources/2025-100m-goated-ads-playbook]]

The origin case: a portfolio founder made "at least five" new ads a month and believed he
had saturated the market; Hormozi took him from ~5 ads/month to **~500** and "the company
doubled in two quarters" (self-reported). The mechanic is to stop *creating* ads and start
*assembling* them from proven parts:

> "I wasn't really creating ads at all. I was assembling them."
> — [[../../sources/2025-100m-goated-ads-playbook]]

**The core formula.** Chop winners into three modular parts — **hook + meat + CTA** — and
mass-produce combinations: **50 hooks × 3–5 meats × 1–3 CTAs = 150–750 ads per week**. A
side benefit is that competitors can't reverse-engineer your top performers from the
assembled output.

**Prep split: 80% hooks / 20% meat / ~0% CTA.** "90% of the work is preparation," and the
prep is research, not shooting:

> "Six-Pack-Abs are made in the kitchen, not in the gym. And like six-packs, ads are made
> in research, not in recording." — [[../../sources/2025-100m-goated-ads-playbook]]

**Five sources of high-converting hooks** (in priority order): (1) your own previous winning
ads, (2) your own free content, (3) other people's ads (saved to a phone album), (4) other
people's free content, (5) platform ad libraries (last resort). Winning hooks cross
industries; when in doubt, go broader (meme-like content pulls the widest audience slice).

**Match the hook type to awareness (Eugene Schwartz's 5 levels).** The colder and larger
the audience, the more varied the ads must be. Hormozi maps each awareness level to a hook
type:

| Awareness level | Hook type |
| --- | --- |
| Most Aware | offer-driven ("$50 off this week") |
| Product-Aware | proof-driven (result / testimonial) |
| Solution-Aware | promise-driven (the outcome) |
| Problem-Aware | pain-driven (name their specific pain) |
| Completely Unaware | curiosity-driven (a pattern-break) |

He treats the pyramid loosely — "I've never met these people in Eugene's pyramid. Labels are
clean. Reality is messy." (Awareness levels also live in [[copywriting-hacks]].)

**Five meat formats** that worked across every industry: **Demonstration, Testimonial,
Education, Story, Faceless.** The meat only gets ~20% of attention and rotates less often —
3–5 per weekly session is enough.

**CTA — "Clear > Clever."** Spell out the next step and *show AND tell* it. A complete CTA
covers what to do, how, when, what they get, and (optionally) what happens next. Test it by
running three identical hook+meat combos changing only the CTA.

> "If your ad got them interested, then your audience will have huge motivation… for a tiny
> time. Take advantage. Tell them exactly what to do next. S-P-E-L-L it out."
> — [[../../sources/2025-100m-goated-ads-playbook]]

Scale by **repetition** — new prospects enter the market daily, so re-running a winner is
still their first exposure. The next level up, teased but not taught in this playbook, is
**Kaleidoscope Ads** ("how I take ads to the moon").

## Budget split (70/20/10)

Allocate creative time and budget in a **70/20/10** split:

- **70%** — the main proven ad(s).
- **20%** — close adjacent permutations / variations of the winner.
- **10%** — wild new bets / experiments.

He credits Google's experimentation culture as validation of this split. It operationalizes
the reskin doctrine: most resources compound on what already works, a slice explores nearby,
and a small slice hunts for the next breakout.

## Targeting & lead quality

**Platform default — Meta first.** Facebook + Instagram, because its targeting is the
easiest to figure out (TikTok's is "getting much, much better"). Google/PPC is intent-based
(higher quality); Meta is interruption-based (lower intent, huge reach, skews older — good
for dentists, roofers, homeowners).

**Lookalike audiences.** Upload your **customer list → build a lookalike audience** off it.
If the target ("dentist") is named in the copy and the video, Meta's AI reinforces delivery
to that segment. An email list over ~1,000 is enough to seed a lookalike.

**Friction tunes lead quality.** A sales process doesn't create qualified customers so much
as **filter** for them. Good filters (e.g. a VSL, an application) raise cost *and* quality;
bad filters (e.g. a slow-loading page) raise cost without quality.

> "It's really just filters for good ones. The more filters you add, the more expensive the
> leads will be if they're good filters." — [[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI]]

The perfect-funnel tuning rule:

> "If you get too many that are bad, add friction. If you don't get enough, remove friction.
> And if you have almost no friction and there's still bad leads, the targeting is off."
> — [[../../sources/2024-07-20-yt-fSbqaTlWaYI]]

**Adjacent tactics.** Own your **PPC branded terms** — a campaign on your own terms "always
makes money." Run **global retargeting** (the "shadow funnel"): install every platform's
pixel and retarget site visitors across GDN, Instagram, Facebook, YouTube — the cheapest,
almost-always-positive-ROAS traffic because you already paid for it. For local businesses,
trust is already sky-high, so ads are very **offer-driven** and brand-building matters less.

## Scaling (ROAS-gated)

**Canonical foundation — `$100M Leads` (2023).** In the Core Four taxonomy, paid ads are
the **1-to-many cold** channel — the hardest but most scalable. The book's paid-ads chapters
("Making An Ad" and "Money Stuff") are the canonical base this page's YouTube and playbook
material sits on top of (GOATed Ads is framed explicitly as running "on top of the paid ads
chapters in `$100M Leads`. Not instead of"). Two load-bearing ideas from it:

- **Scaling runs in three phases — Track Money → Lose Money → Print Money.** Hormozi says he
  has lost money on ads more often than made it, but he "know[s] when to shut it down" and
  when to hit the gas, **10x-ing** winners. ([[../../sources/2023-100m-leads]])
- **LTGP:CAC gates everything.** Measure lifetime *gross profit* against cost to acquire;
  every struggling company he buys sits below **3:1**, and fixing it (usually by raising
  LTGP, not cutting ads) is what makes ads take off. If a customer pays back acquisition +
  fulfillment cost within ~30 days (**Client Financed Acquisition**), cash stops constraining
  scale — "the key to limitless scale." ([[../../sources/2023-100m-leads]])

> "Advertising is the only casino where, with enough skill, you become the house."
> — [[../../sources/2023-100m-leads]]

**When ROAS is healthy, spend more.** With strong ROAS and capacity to fill (7:1, 10:1,
20:1), the answer is simply to dial spend up to your fill rate; if seats/capacity stop
filling, crank spend then throttle back. For a printing location model, raise capital /
bring in investors to open more locations faster.

Two levers to improve ROAS besides raw spend:

- **Drive CAC down** — via creative (the hook-splicing / reskin engine above).
- **Push LTV / average cart value up** — e.g. **bundle** recurring consumables with a
  high-ticket purchase ("buy a year of ink with the printer"); even a 30% take-rate adds
  meaningful ACV, and a max-cart bundle can nearly double spend.

**Compliance-audit tactic (regulated niches).** In health / wealth / relationship spaces,
claims trigger auto-shutdowns. Hire a **compliance-audit firm** (~$5–6K, self-reported);
check their post-audit approval rating (90%+ for good ones). They audit sites, videos, and
collateral and often have a Google back-channel to push compliant accounts through.
