# Proof

Alex Hormozi's framework for making prospects believe you, drawn from the
*$100M Proof Checklist Playbook* (2025), subtitled "How to Get Strangers to Believe You
Instantly" (source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]). It grew out of a
July 2024 diagnosis: after Gym Launch's ad costs doubled, Hormozi ranked his top 50 ads of
all time and found the single variable separating winners from losers was proof. See
[[marketing]] for the domain hub, and [[copywriting-hacks]], [[../business/sales-frameworks]],
and [[paid-ads]] for the tactics this overlaps with.

> ⚠️ Self-reported: the datapoints, origin story, and results below are Hormozi's own
> playbook claims (source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]), not
> registry-verified here.

## Definition and thesis

> "Proof is data that supports your advertising claims."

An *advertising claim* is anything you say has happened, might happen, or will happen about
what you sell; a single instance of supporting data (a client's week-to-week weight loss) is
a **proof point**. The more compelling the claim, the more proof — in quantity and quality —
it requires (source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]).

The thesis:

> "Your promise is not a differentiator. Your proof is."

Anyone can copy a claim; no one can copy your results. In a competitive market the offer and
the "unique mechanism" get copied, so proof — earned results others cannot reproduce — is what
actually cuts through. Hormozi frames it as the up-and-comer's biggest weapon
(source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]).

> "Anyone can copy an advertising claim, but no one can produce the exact same results to
> prove it!"

## The Belief Continuum

Belief is not all-or-nothing — it is a continuum from **100% doubt to 100% certainty**. The
prospect's biggest obstacle to buying is *risk*: the fear the thing won't work for them, the
way they want, on the timeline they want. Marketing and sales exist to move the prospect along
this continuum, and proof is the thing that moves them toward certainty. Belief is therefore a
question of *how much*, not *whether* (source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]).

## The 13 proof-compelling dimensions

Each dimension is a preferred form; push every piece of proof toward the left side
(source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]):

1. **In-Person > Virtual** — seeing with one's own eyes beats a digital version; fly best
   customers to live events.
2. **Live > Recorded** — a live testimonial ("living proof") beats the same story on video.
3. **Raw > Processed** — least-tampered, iPhone-shot testimonials read as most authentic.
4. **Show > Tell** — a visual before/after beats any description (why unboxing videos convert).
5. **Others Saying It > You Saying It** — you are the most biased person alive; let customers
   say it instead.
6. **Identical to Them > Opposite of Them** — feature people who literally look like the target
   market; the cast determines who you attract.
7. **Personal > Generic** — specific, vivid pain ("this is me") beats generic language.
8. **Big Results > Small Results > No Results** — bigger is more compelling, but small still
   beats none.
9. **Newer Proof > Older Proof** — stale testimonials imply no recent wins; refresh constantly.
10. **More Proof > Less Proof** — quantity can overcome quality limits; walls and galleries of
    hundreds-to-thousands of reviews.
11. **Third-Party Verification > Zero Verification** — accreditations, awards, certifications,
    endorsements ("believe them, not me").
12. **Numbers > No Numbers** — plainly stated numbers (with how/when collected) are hard to
    argue; visualize with charts.
13. **Metaphors > Jargon** — connect the unknown mechanism to the known via 2-3 metaphors
    instead of technical jargon.

## Claim your proof

The pro tip **"Match Your Claims To Your Proof"**: collect the data *first*, then make only the
claims the data already supports. Rather than straining to prove an escalating claim, invert it
(source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]):

> "Instead of trying to prove your claim—claim your proof. And if you're proof isn't
> compelling—fix reality."

"Fix reality" means improving the product or delivering results (e.g. working for free to the
extent affordable, which delivers results *before* the prospect buys) rather than escalating a
claim war with competitors. Working for free sits at the extreme end of the compelling-proof
spectrum (source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]).

## Datapoints (numbers-as-proof)

Hormozi's own examples of "Proof With Numbers" (2025, self-reported):

- **54.1%** of people who start a paid community on Skool make money (pulled July 2024).
- The average **Gym Lord** adds over **$100,000** in profit to their gym in its first complete
  year.
- The average **Acquisition.com** portfolio company increased its EBITDA by **3.1x** in its
  first 12 months.

## Why it matters

The July 2024 Gym Launch diagnosis found 80% of winning ads were pure proof — no education,
not offer-focused, not Hormozi center-stage — telling one story: "This product works." Proof
compounds across the whole funnel (click → opt-in → sale → retention) and is one of the rare
business assets you can never have too much of (source: [[../../sources/2025-100m-proof-checklist-playbook]]).

> "80% of our winning ads... Told one story - This. Product. Works."

> "Proof is like an animal in the wild. It may only be there for a moment. You have to capture it."

## WISBY — designing for the maximally skeptical viewer

**WISBY** — *"Why Should I Believe You?"* — is a mnemonic Hormozi keeps posted above his
creative room so the team always designs content assuming the viewer arrives with maximum
skepticism ("everything's fake, everyone's lying")
(2026-02-18, source: [[../../sources/2026-02-18-yt-nypmbn0mmGQ]]). It is a *named
default-skepticism assumption*: the burden of proof always sits with you, so every piece of
content is built to preempt disbelief rather than assume goodwill.

> "I use the acronym Wisby — like 'why should I believe you' — and I just have that above… in
> our creative room… so the guys know it's like we should always be thinking, like, everyone
> comes in with a huge amount of skepticism."

## Extreme proof, degraded down

A believability mechanic distinct from the 13 dimensions above: **lead with your single most
extreme capability demonstration, then let the prospect degrade it down to their own smaller
need** (2026-02-18, source: [[../../sources/2026-02-18-yt-nypmbn0mmGQ]]). People shop for the
capability that handles the extreme case and then mentally discount it to their modest real
need — so the extreme feat halos onto the everyday job and they believe. His examples:

- A **jacket that survives the tip of Everest** → "it'll probably keep me warm from the car to
  my front door."
- An **off-road vehicle** → "so if it snows I can still get to work."
- **Red Bull's Stratos space jump** → "it'll maybe wake me up a little when I'm tired."
- On skateboarding: **land a 1200 first**, *then* say "anyways, here's how you do an ollie" —
  establish believability with the extreme feat before delivering the basic lesson.

Extreme demonstrations are how brand and authority get built: "if that guy vibe coded a $10
million app, he can probably help me automate my email." This extends "Big Results > Small
Results" — you don't just want big proof, you want the *maximal* proof, because the prospect
does the down-scaling for you.

> "We will often look for the jacket that can keep you warm on the tip of Everest, and then
> degrade that down… 'it'll probably keep me warm on the way from the car to the door of my
> house.'"

## SPCL — the four-component influence framework

Where proof answers *"will it work?"*, **SPCL** ("special," the memory hook) is Hormozi's
model of *influence itself* — the four independent levers that make an audience act on what you
say (2026-02-19, source: [[../../sources/2026-02-19-yt-gpNw_tlM6Sk]]). Earlier notes across the
wiki referenced "SPCL" without spelling it out; this defines the acronym. He restates the same
four as a "reinforcer" framework — do these and you condition yourself as a *reinforcer*
(something that gets someone to repeat a behavior)
(2026-02-19, source: [[../../sources/2026-02-19-yt-fvjyOngt5ic]]):

1. **Status** — the number of *reinforcers you control* (how much of "the stuff people want"
   is under your control). Even unearned wealth confers it. (Reinforcer restatement: *visibly
   demonstrate you control the things your audience wants to control.*)
2. **Power** — *say–do correspondence*: you give directions, people follow them, and a good
   thing results, so future compliance grows. Built purely from how good your advice is.
   Hormozi's pick if he could have only one — "how well can you give people directions in order
   to get something good to happen." Archetype: **Martha Stewart** (years of instructions that
   reliably worked). (Restatement: *give behavioral instructions they can follow to a good
   outcome.*)
3. **Credibility** — *third-party / real-world proof that what you say is true.* This is the
   component the rest of this page is about; proof is the credibility engine of influence.
   (Restatement: *have third-party credibility that what you say is true.*)
4. **Likeness** — how *similar* the audience is to you (demographics, appearance, speech). Maps
   directly to the proof dimension "Identical to Them > Opposite of Them." (Restatement: *be
   like them.*) Hormozi's audience skews male and near his age from likeness; Leila's covers
   similar topics but skews ~54% female.

The four operate independently but compound. In his running example, a rich person (**status**)
who gives 10 stock tips that all work earns **credibility + power**; pivoting to a real-estate
tip forfeits topic-specific **credibility** but keeps **power** (past directions worked).

**Brand as a history of positive reinforcement.** Hormozi defines branding as "building a
history of positive reinforcement with a large audience," which *pre-programs* behavior so
people comply with requests (source: [[../../sources/2026-02-19-yt-gpNw_tlM6Sk]]). A brand's
**three elements** are **reach** (how many see it), **influence** (% likely to comply), and
**direction** (for or against you) — Trump = very high influence but polarizing/both-directional;
parents = huge influence, tiny reach. At sufficient brand strength you can make an offer with no
promises ("everything else you bought from me was good, you'll like this too") and people buy,
provided you keep the promise and re-reinforce the loop.

> "You have SPCL, special is the easy way to remember it. You have status… power, which is say
> do correspondence… credibility… is there a third party world that proof that what I'm saying
> is true? And then fourth is likeness."

> "Fundamentally branding is building a history of positive reinforcement with a large audience
> that ideally has a high likelihood of purchasing from you in the future."

**Anti-cult stance (cross-link — belongs more to branding/content-strategy).** In
[[../../sources/2026-02-19-yt-fvjyOngt5ic]] Hormozi argues you should *not* aim to build a cult:
be product-obsessed and let a passionate following form as a byproduct, and if you must have an
enemy make it a *concept* (e.g. complexity), never a person or company. This is a
branding/audience position rather than a proof point; see
[[../content-strategy/content-strategy]] and [[marketing]] for its proper home.
