# Skool

> **Context entity.** This page neutrally archives what the corpus establishes
> about Skool, the community platform Alex Hormozi co-owns. It is not a persona
> page. Almost everything below is **self-reported by Alex in promotional videos
> on Skool's own channel** (`@TheSkoolSchool`) and is **NOT registry-verified**.
> Treat all figures as self-reported unless explicitly marked otherwise.

Skool (skool.com; Alex frequently says "school.com" in captions) is a community
platform for hosting paid online communities — courses, discussion, and
gamification in one place. Its founder is **Sam Ovens** ("Sam"). Alex Hormozi
became a co-owner via what he calls his single largest investment to date.

## What Skool is

- **An all-in-one community platform**: courses + community discussion +
  gamification, with native payment processing and content delivery built in.
  A creator can "run the entire business on the platform." *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]], [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*
- **Pricing: a flat $99/month per group.** Platform revenue = number of groups
  × $99. Deliberately kept simple (no tiers) to maximize the count of students
  and teachers during "growth mode" rather than profit; Alex says $99/month "is
  not the monetization place" long-term. *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]], [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*
- **Members pay a monthly fee to join a community.** Alex cites a range of "$1
  to $999" (elsewhere "$1 to $9,999") per month that a community owner can
  charge their members. *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]], [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*
- **Standardized page layout** mirroring what Amazon, Zillow, and Airbnb
  converged on (video, image carousel, reviews, buy button, bullet points) —
  removing design/technical work for beginners. *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]], [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*
- Positioned in the **education / community space**; stated mission (per Alex)
  is to make **business online fun and easy for everyone**, starting in
  education. *(self-reported — [[../sources/2025-03-23-yt-YoGOongsiD4]],
  [[../sources/2024-12-15-yt-5BqqXjl11V8]])*

## Why he invested

Alex frames the decision through **Charlie Munger's "Lollapalooza effect"** —
explosive outcomes come not from one silver bullet but from **"a hundred golden
BBs,"** several stacked factors that each add an incremental boost but multiply
into an outsized result. His stacked reasons:

- **The "golden ratio" (passive growth).** His litmus test for anything he
  promotes: it must grow on its own without ads. Passive growth rate = **(% of
  customers who refer a new customer per period) ÷ (% who churn/exit per
  period).** If 20% refer and 10% leave, the business doubles every interval
  regardless of size; most businesses run ~2% referring vs. ~10% leaving and so
  must refill the bucket with ads. Once referrals exceed churn, "pour gas on the
  flame" with marketing. He says **Skool's metrics "blew him away"** — the best
  passive-growth metrics he'd ever seen across the "several thousand businesses a
  month" that pitch Acquisition.com. It was "growing without me, based on word of
  mouth." *(self-reported — [[../sources/2024-12-15-yt-5BqqXjl11V8]],
  [[../sources/2025-03-23-yt-YoGOongsiD4]])* → [[../topics/business/retention]]
- **The education "crisscross."** Education is a huge market (his approximate,
  "directional" figures: **~$10T global / ~$1.3T US**), splitting into **formal
  education ~$850B US, shrinking ~6%/yr (losing ~$50B/yr)** vs. **alternative /
  online / e-learning ~$200–250B, growing ~20%/yr (gaining ~$40B/yr)** — a
  near-symmetric transfer of buying behavior he wants to be on the right side of.
  Money moves from the "mediocre middle" of universities down to creators
  teaching a proven niche skill. *(self-reported, explicitly rounded —
  [[../sources/2024-12-10-yt-iRKZEelCS5M]], [[../sources/2025-03-23-yt-YoGOongsiD4]],
  [[../sources/2025-01-12-yt-fD4mPziE1vU]])*
- **Education is the un-disrupted industry.** "Hotels got Airbnb, taxis got
  Uber," but no one has disrupted education — it's still fragmented with no single
  place everyone goes to learn. His bet: Skool becomes that company. This is
  also **Acquisition.com's investment thesis** — fund the best practitioner in a
  niche (mechanic, salon owner, lawn-care operator) to teach peers. *(self-reported
  — [[../sources/2025-03-23-yt-YoGOongsiD4]], [[../sources/2025-01-12-yt-fD4mPziE1vU]])*
  See [[acquisition-com]].
- **Personal + mission fit.** His prior at-scale exits (Gym Launch, Prestige
  Labs) served creators/owners; his audience is business owners and aspiring
  owners; his mission "to make real business accessible for everyone" aligns with
  Skool's mission. *(self-reported — [[../sources/2024-12-10-yt-iRKZEelCS5M]],
  [[../sources/2025-03-23-yt-YoGOongsiD4]])*

## Platform economics & mechanics

- **Gamification is core.** Members earn **points** for likes/comments, gain
  real **status**, and **unlock abilities "like a game."** *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]])* → [[../topics/content-strategy/community-building]]
- **The dev team is made up of gaming developers.** "The core of the development
  team at Skool is actually all gaming developers, because we wanted to make
  business fun." *(self-reported — [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]])*
- **Skool Discovery directory** ranks communities "just like Google ranks web
  pages," so people searching to learn a skill find and join groups — organic
  customer acquisition that Skool (not the group owner) generates. *(self-reported
  — [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]])*
- **Losing money by design.** The platform has been **unprofitable**, aggressively
  reinvesting in engineers, designers, and developers to make the product
  "absolutely exceptional." *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]], [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*
- **Cheaper to run than a free Facebook group.** Native payments, automatic
  removal of non-paying members, and gamification-driven member-helping-member
  support cut community-management cost; Facebook also throttles paid reach.
  *(self-reported — [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]])*
- **Transparency thesis.** Because Skool owns the platform, member revenue
  numbers are visible — "no one's fronting" — which Alex (and Sam) frame as
  bringing honesty to an industry "shrouded in deception." *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*

## The Skool Games

A recurring competition Alex built with **Sam Ovens** to help beginners make
their **first dollar online**, gamified rather than "just try this out."
*(self-reported — [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]],
[[../sources/2025-03-24-yt-q6nUhd51NuA]])*

- **Structure: 14 days free inside a 30-day competition.** People who feel it
  isn't working can dip out at two weeks; those who finish and rank **top 10 get
  flown to Vegas** (elsewhere framed as a mastermind loop), where the team helps
  them grow and gathers product feedback.
- **Leaderboards rank by MRR *growth* over the month, not total** — so newcomers
  can compete against larger communities; lose a month, reset, play again.
- **Onboarding milestone: first three paying members** unlocks the top-10
  trainings — "get the first dollar across the bridge," because proof-of-possible
  changes what you believe.
- **The give-it-away playbook** behind the Games: "look at what everyone else
  charges for, make it better, make it free" — they gave away coaching, courses,
  community, and tools free.
- **Weekly free live onboarding call** (Mondays), typically ~200–300 attendees.

## Skool as a business model

Alex's **"easiest business to start in 2025"** thesis pitches a paid Skool
community as the simplest first online business.

- **Turnkey, recurring, beginner-grade.** No inventory, no third-party
  integrations, no website/Stripe setup, no technical knowledge, no staff — money
  is processed and delivery happens on-platform; it "works out of the box" and
  generates **monthly recurring revenue (MRR)**. He also frames this as an
  **online service business** — cheapest to start, near-zero cost to deliver.
  *(self-reported — [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]],
  [[../sources/2025-03-24-yt-q6nUhd51NuA]], [[../sources/2024-12-29-yt-C6iedcsF_sU]])*
- **The core funnel: content → community → MRR.** "You post content. You siphon
  the traffic to school. School converts it and turns it into money." Goal he
  sets: **$10,000/month** (e.g. 50 × $200, or 20 × $500). *(self-reported —
  [[../sources/2025-03-24-yt-q6nUhd51NuA]])* → [[../topics/content-strategy/community-building]]
- **Borrow-the-platform's-credibility.** As trust in Skool compounds, Skool
  pages will "convert better than any page you could build on your own."
  Supporting analogy: his book converts ~2–3% on his own Shopify page vs. ~30%
  on Amazon (~10× more efficient) because of inherent platform trust. *(self-reported
  — [[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]], [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]])*
- **Low-risk front end / ascension.** A low-priced community lets a customer get
  value first, then ascend to higher-priced offers — versus asking a stranger for
  $50,000 upfront. The #1 reason people cancel or never start is **overwhelm**, so
  Skool is designed to funnel action into clear step 1 / step 2 / step 3.
  *(self-reported — [[../sources/2025-01-08-yt-LgmiVRU7j2E]],
  [[../sources/2024-12-29-yt-C6iedcsF_sU]])*
- **Marketing challenge Alex notes:** the benefit (a platform that scales with
  you) is hard to sell because beginners "aren't suffering from the problem yet."
  *([[../sources/2025-01-21-yt-sDqOC2YtnKE]])*
