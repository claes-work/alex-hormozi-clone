# Why Life Feels Difficult

## Summary
Podcast/panel clip in which Alex reflects on what actually made a recent season of his life hard — and it wasn't work ethic or time constraints, which he says are baseline and which he genuinely enjoys ("me writing a book is my favorite thing in the entire world"). The culprit is **overhead**: all the non-work obligations that pile up around the work he loves so he can get back to doing it. He argues that at every level of entrepreneurship he hits periods where the overhead grows "bigger and bigger and bigger until I fix it," and when that overhead becomes a disproportionate share of his time, life *feels* difficult. He narrates the concrete stack of adversity from early 2026: eight or nine lawsuits within about 30 days; a health scare in which a garbled message made him briefly believe Leila had cancer (it turned out a front-desk employee had cancer and had mis-communicated a scheduling delay — Leila did not), which he uses as a live illustration of gratitude ("you don't have cancer"); and a printing mishap that ruined roughly 1.5 acres of trees' worth of his forthcoming book because of errors, forcing him to destroy the run rather than ship a flawed product. His resolution has two moves. First, he distinguishes whether he got "better at gratitude" or simply "solved the problem" (building a legal team so he won't have to endure the same overhead again) — and honestly admits he doesn't know which. Second, and central to the clip, he reframes the *narrative*: a friend told him to stop calling 2025 "a bad year," because it was really "a handful of moments over five months that were spread out," with the back seven months still to win. After adopting that reframe on ~May 1st, his HRV rose about 15 points off a poor ~32 average despite changing nothing in his diet, training, or routine — the reframe was the only variable. He ends genuinely energized ("we're turning the ship around"), then gives the standard acquisition.com roadmap promo.
