# 'I Just Quit My Job. What Business Should I Start?'

## Summary

Interview clip (interviewer appears to be Steven Bartlett — "Steve," Dragon's Den references) in which Alex Hormozi answers the classic beginner question: someone who just quit their job asks what business idea to pursue. Alex offers a "three Ps" origin framework — **pain, past profession, passion** — and explains the trade-off between them: the past profession is the *proven* path (the economy already paid you for that skill), while pain and passion are less proven but carry more upside because they give you deep, visceral customer knowledge. He then pivots into a second, connected theme: **why a founder's personal story beats a features/benefits pitch**. Using his own lifelong nasal-breathing problem as a running example, he argues that origin-story narrative ("I had a problem, I tried everything, I solved it for myself") is far more compelling to both customers and investors than rational benefit claims — and that this story becomes your marketing campaign for the next five years. He ties this to Jeff Bezos's "missionaries vs. mercenaries" framing, to a demonstration-beats-description meta-point (he notes he just walked the listener through a live pitch), and closes on **obsession** — a friend who ran Olympic-team nutrition told him champions differ not by what they have but by what they lack: an "off button." Ends with the standard acquisition.com roadmap CTA.

## Key claims (2026-02-16, Alex-attributed, paraphrased unless quoted)

- ★ **Business ideas come from one of three Ps: pain, past profession, or passion.** A fresh, memorable packaging of his business-selection advice into a single mnemonic — the "three Ps" origin taxonomy is the distinct contribution of this clip.
- **The past-profession route is the most proven** way to make money: the economy has already shown people will exchange money for that skill because they paid you a salary for it. It is "a bit of a shortcut."
- **Every business can be dismantled into jobs to be done**, and every job can be fractionalized — sales, marketing, customer success, support, product, design, web pages. You only need to learn *one* of those to have a skill you can trade for money without a boss (the solopreneur / gig-economy path).
- **Pain and passion are less proven but often have significantly more upside**, because they hand you deep customer knowledge.
- **Deep knowledge of the prospect is paramount for building exceptional products.** You get it one of two ways: a ton of research, or *being* the prospect. Being the prospect gives a "visceral feel" that research can't.
- **Passion and pain routes both create deep understanding** — with passion you spend all your discretionary time on the thing; with pain you've lived the problem for years.
- **A founder story beats a benefits pitch** — "20% more durable, 20% wider nostrils" is "multiples less compelling" than the story of a kid who couldn't breathe and solved it for himself.
- **Your origin story is your marketing campaign for the next five years** on TikTok/Instagram: "I had a problem, I solved it, I tried everything."
- **Lead with a story, not logic** — a story-led pitch is far more compelling to an investor than a logical/benefit-led one.
- **Missionaries vs. mercenaries (credited to Bezos):** mercenaries reason their way in via market trends and surveys and *some* make it work; missionaries — who viscerally experienced the problem and don't want anyone else to suffer it — tend to make the most money. A business still needs an economic engine, but obsession is the edge.
- **Investors bet on obsession / total product knowledge.** He recounts an investor backing a single-theater cinema operator (later Regal Cinemas) purely because the man knew the business "down to how much the cost of a kernel of popcorn was" — "this guy can't fail, he just knows too much about this business."
- **A demonstration or model is always more compelling than description** ("a really good meta concept"): narrating his own nasal-product journey *was* a live demonstration of how to pitch a business.
- **To decide what to do with your business, build your narrative of why you're doing it** — specifically why *you* care about the problem. Investors respond to visible obsession even in categories they personally don't care about.
- **Obsession is required to get where you want to go**, and in his view has been unfairly chastised (more so in the UK than the US); the people who are all-in obsessed are the ones who change the world (or at least their world).
- **Champions lack an "off button."** A friend who handled a national Olympic team's nutrition/supplementation said the real question isn't what champions *have* that others don't, but what they *lack* — they can't stop; everything in their life is geared toward one goal.
