# Why You Should Ignore the People Closest to You

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says people close to someone are right most of the time when they discourage
unusual paths, but not more right, because the one exception can matter more than all the
ordinary failures. He argues that local critics play a smaller status game, while the
person pursuing a wider game should treat much of that input as noise.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Advice from people in the same current situation is usually right in ordinary cases but
  can be wrong on the one exceptional case that matters.
- Parents and friends may be right many times when they predict an idea will not work,
  but that does not mean they are "more right."
- Low expectations and cynical conformity can lower the bar rather than help.
- When critics try to pull someone into their local status game, Alex recommends refusing
  to play by their rules.
- He uses "you're right" as a way to end unproductive criticism.
- The opinions of local critics are often disproportionate to the wider game being
  played.
- Alex thinks often about death and uses funeral imagery to reduce the weight of other
  people's opinions.
- Six months after death, most people will have moved on; he finds that freeing rather
  than hopeless.
- If one's actions will be forgotten, there is more freedom to do unusual things now.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "They are right more times, but they are not more right."

> "You're playing a much wider game."

> "Why on earth would I listen to them while I'm alive?"
