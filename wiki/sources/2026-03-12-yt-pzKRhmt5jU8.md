# How to Build a Brand

## Summary
Short solo clip on brand maintenance. Alex's thesis: **a brand has to be constantly reinforced** — you must keep "pushing the envelope to stay relevant," because "something is only a big deal if you make it one." His running example is his own record-breaking **launch** (the deadlift world-record stunt / product moment), which he says almost nobody knew about despite his roughly 16–20 million followers across channels and his trying "really, really hard" — proof that reach alone doesn't guarantee attention and that relevance must be actively manufactured and re-earned. He reframes success as **defending a title** rather than reaching a summit: like Chris Bumstead winning the Olympia, winning once doesn't mean you're done — you have to defend the belt, take on new title belts, and perform on ever-bigger stages ("in business the stages can only keep getting bigger"). He pushes back on the idea that climbing is the hard part: "holding the top's way harder than climbing it." He then owns a concrete **failure**: he had planned a big PR blast-off on the back end of the launch, but it "fell flat" — the PR firm got no attention/effort from him, so few articles covered "some internet person doubling the world record." He frames it as an eminently fixable miss (just needed PR queued up in advance) and vows to "do better next time." Ends with the standard $100M scaling roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, a brand must be constantly reinforced; to stay relevant you have to keep "pushing the envelope" and keep going bigger and bigger.
- As of March 2026, "something is only a big deal if you make it one" — attention/significance is manufactured, not automatic.
- As of March 2026, even with ~16–20 million followers/subscribers across all his channels, the vast majority of people didn't know about his launch despite him trying very hard — reach does not guarantee awareness.
- As of March 2026, success is like defending a title, not summiting once: winning the Olympia (his Chris Bumstead analogy) doesn't mean you're done — you must keep defending and taking on new "title belts," on progressively bigger stages.
- As of March 2026, "holding the top is way harder than climbing it" — maintaining relevance/position is harder than the initial climb, and there are always levels to the game.
- As of March 2026, Alex counts a PR miss on the launch: he'd planned a big PR blast-off but didn't give the PR firm his attention, so coverage of "an internet person doubling the world record" fell flat — a fixable failure he attributes to not queuing up PR in advance.
