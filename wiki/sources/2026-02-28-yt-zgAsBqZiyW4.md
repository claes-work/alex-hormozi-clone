# You Need to Ask Every Employee This Question

## Summary
Short solo clip. Alex offers a "very powerful frame" for talking to anyone in a business about their contribution: ask each employee **"How do you and your role make the company money?"** He warns that if you ask your team today, many won't be able to answer — and that should frighten the owner. Some roles are obvious (sales: get people to give the company money). The frame gets interesting on **ancillary/support roles** that don't directly generate revenue. His worked example: the camera operator filming him. Marketing generates money, but the camera person doesn't "do marketing" — they hold the camera. So the follow-up is: *in what ways does this role make us money?* Alex reasons through it by inverting to cost: bad camera work wastes the whole team's time, makes the media perform worse (off-center, bad lighting), wastes footage, leads to ill-fitted equipment and lost/unsaved files — all "huge cost setters." You then reverse each potential mistake into a positive standard/metric: what percentage of the time was footage saved correctly, named correctly, saved to the right drive, timestamped, lit/framed correctly, and started on time? If that's 100% of the time, "you're making us money, and here's how." His broader point: people crave *impact* ("I wish I had more impact"), but if you have a job in a company you already have impact — you just may not know what it is. Explaining that connection is "a very big unlock" because it gives meaning and lets people see the value their role provides to the whole.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, Alex recommends asking every employee: **"How do you and your role make the company money?"** — a simple frame he says most team members won't be able to answer, which should alarm the owner.
- As of February 2026, direct revenue roles (sales) answer this easily; the frame is most valuable for **ancillary/support roles** (finance, tech, media, marketing operations) where the money connection is indirect.
- As of February 2026, for a role that doesn't directly generate revenue, find its money impact by **inverting to cost** — enumerate what bad performance would cost (wasted team time, worse-performing media, wasted footage, ill-fitted equipment, lost files) — then reverse each mistake into a positive standard.
- As of February 2026, those reversed standards become **measurable metrics** (e.g., for a camera operator: % of the time footage was saved correctly, named correctly, saved to the right drive, timestamped, lit/framed correctly, and started on time). Hitting 100% is how you show that role "is making us money."
- As of February 2026, Alex claims people who say they "wish they had more impact" usually already have impact through their job but don't know what it is; explaining how their role connects to how the company makes money is "a very big unlock" that gives their work meaning.
