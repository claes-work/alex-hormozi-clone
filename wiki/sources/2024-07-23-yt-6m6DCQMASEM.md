# 10 Millionaires Asked Me How to Get Richer

## Summary

Alex Hormozi runs a live Q&A with ten business owners doing between $1M and $10M/year,
diagnosing what constrains each of their companies. Across a fitness coach, several ad
agencies, a used-car dealer, a web-dev agency, a clothing/community brand, a cannabis
agency, and a dental-implant practice, his advice repeatedly reduces to a handful of
moves: raise price, sell the goal not the program, fix the model before hiring, install
a script-driven sales-training system, and buy back time by hiring finance/ops people
who "find their own salary." The millionaires' individual questions are context; only
Alex's coaching is persona data.

## Key claims (Alex's advice to already-wealthy operators)

- **Sell the goal, not the program.** For the fitness coach, switch from selling
  packages to a diagnostic sale: price per week ($129/wk), map the client's goal (e.g.
  40 lbs at ~1 lb/week = 40 weeks), and guarantee you keep working with them until they
  hit it. Selling the outcome converts a far higher percentage.
- **Add a supplement/affiliate line.** A fitness coaching business can usually add ~50%
  in supplement sales with zero delivery cost — but sell it on the second appointment
  (a nutrition consult), using an assumed close tied to the client's daily habits.
- **Don't turn off a channel that's making money — add on top.** To the agency wanting
  to move off Meta ads, he says keep the profitable Meta flow running and test LinkedIn
  in addition; LinkedIn "will for sure murder" for finding executives.
- **Fix LTV / add a performance component before chasing new channels.** Adding a
  performance-fee piece can take a customer from ~$3K to ~$10-12K, turning a 3:1 return
  into 12:1; even with diminishing returns at higher spend you stay far ahead.
- **Sales training is broken almost everywhere.** ~95% of companies can't actually train
  sales — they hire and hope. People improve by (1) watching a ton of game tape of great
  sellers (modeling, like children) and (2) immediate corrective role-play, drilling one
  part of the script until they breathe it, then moving to the next.
- **The sales-killer is usually the wrong sales manager.** The best closer is often a
  "psychopath" who wants to win, not teach; a good sales manager is a different persona —
  a caring, attentive closer who genuinely wants to develop people.
- **The script is the Bible.** To turn around a sales team, get everyone following one
  script first — only then can you tweak it, because changes actually propagate. If no
  one follows a script, the script (and the training) is irrelevant. Cut scripts hard;
  "most times when we look at a script we cut half of it."
- **A sales-driven CEO must know sales.** Whoever runs sales has leverage over you until
  you can do it yourself; get in and take calls so you can iterate and teach the script.
- **Six problems = a people problem.** When an owner lists many constraints, the real
  answer is usually "you need people to help you," not a tactic.
- **Hire the finance person first — they carve out their own salary.** Tell a good
  controller/CFO "I can't afford you; find your salary and cost in 90 days," and they
  will, funding the next hire (ops). Basic finance discipline can find $250K on the top
  line and roughly double margin.
- **Buying back time gets more expensive as you scale.** Early trades are cheap
  low-skill hires; later you must "make a bet" and pay for higher-value time. Talent
  levels ($250K, $500K, $1M/yr) are like new ad-spend territories you break into.
- **Partnership conflict = a role and accountability problem.** Divide roles cleanly and
  don't step on each other's territory. If a partner owns sales and there are no sales,
  that gap is the problem: "either you change what you do or I stop being here."
- **Insult vs. criticism.** An insult attacks character; a criticism is a discrepancy
  between stated and reality ("you said you'd do this; this is what happened"). Framing
  feedback as observation of fact, not attack, defuses partnership conflict.
- **Fund the "universe" from the economic engine, but cap it by ROI.** For the
  clothing/community brand, treat character/IP/media building as marketing spend funded
  by the clothing sales; it must attract more buyers or it isn't working. Mickey Mouse
  has to sell shirts. Study Walt Disney and Gary Vee's VeeFriends.
- **Model problem, not people problem.** For the cannabis agency at ~$90K/yr profit,
  hiring is impossible — the model is broken. Either 10x the price for what you deliver
  or make delivery 10x more efficient before trying to scale.
- **Cracking a new channel: start with vendors.** For local lead gen, go vendor-first
  for PPC (stable once cracked, well-priced locally), and bring social/Meta ads in-house
  (local creative burns out every ~21 days, so you need volume). His playbook: buy the
  agency's service, tell them you'll take over in ~6 months while they train your person.
- **Agency → consulting transition extends LTV.** Move a done-for-you client to a
  consulting fee (~20-30% of the original, ~3% of ad spend) once they bring it in-house;
  stay on consulting until their team outperforms you. Shifts you from subservient
  service provider to teacher and aligns interests.
- **To land high-value accounts, go outbound / niche content / your own ads.** Use harsh
  targeting messaging ("if you're not spending 100 grand a month, please just scroll
  past this ad") to filter for the right buyers. LinkedIn has high-quality people.
- **Raise your price — his favorite move.** Price increases are pure profit. For the
  dentist: double the price and work the same hours. In an acquisition he doubled a
  company's price (settled at +50% after nine calls with the founder) and close rates
  went *up* because the offer was mispriced.
- **You already have a sales process — you just haven't documented it.** Record 50 calls
  side by side and you'll see the repeatable structure; write it down so the team can
  carry the baton.
