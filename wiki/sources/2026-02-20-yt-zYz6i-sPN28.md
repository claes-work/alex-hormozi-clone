# How to Give Feedback

> ★ **L3-candidate.** Contains two concrete named feedback mechanics — the
> **criticism-vs-insult distinction** ("discrepancy between actual and desired")
> and the **Stop / Start / Keep** correction format — plus the "kind, not nice"
> culture principle. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/business/` (management/
> communication) and possibly `persona/beliefs.md` during synthesis.

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi clip (with a brief interviewer setup) on why leaders should
give frequent, specific, "petty" feedback and how to do it without insulting
people. His core claim: most managers over-index on being *nice* and therefore
withhold correction, which is actually *unkind* — because people rarely lose
jobs over one thing, they lose them over a hundred small uncorrected things.
Fixing those small things fast maximizes an employee's likelihood of success.

He draws a hard line between **criticism** (objectively pointing out the gap
between actual and desired behavior) and **insults** (attaching a character
judgment to that gap). To correct behavior you remove the insult, name the
observable behavior, and tell the person exactly what to do instead — using a
**Stop / Start / Keep** frame. He illustrates with a real story of a high-
performing director everyone called "a dick," whom four executives had failed
to fix by just telling him to "stop being a dick" (an insult you can't act on).
Alex instead translated the complaint into 3–4 specific conditional behaviors
(e.g. "when someone is talking, wait until they finish; if unsure, ask if
they're done") — and the man changed within a week. A second worked example
("Sandy," who seems "lazy") shows converting a character label into observable
data (slow Slack replies, incomplete deliverables, disheveled on Zoom) and
issuing concrete behavioral asks (respond within 10 minutes 9-5; use the
standard company background; look professional from the waist up).

Ends with an ad read for his free acquisition.com/roadmap (10-stage 0→$100M+ map).

## Key claims

_All dated 2026-02-20. Paraphrased unless quoted._

- Most companies, entrepreneurs, and founders are scared to give **petty
  feedback** — pointing out a small error feels rude or dictatorial, so they
  avoid it. (paraphrase)
- Many managers' central focus is too much on being **nice**, and they don't
  want to offend people — this is the wrong optimization. (paraphrase)
- **"Kind, not nice"** is a repeated internal cultural principle at his company:
  you are actually being *unkind* to someone by not maximizing the likelihood
  that they succeed in their role. (paraphrase of a stated company value)
- People typically don't lose jobs over one thing — it's usually **a hundred
  small things**. Immediately fixing those small things maximizes success,
  because most people want to do a good job, succeed, and move up. (paraphrase)
- Set the expectation of a high-correction culture from **day one** — e.g.
  telling a new hire up front, "we'll get through a third of the script and I'll
  stop you 30 times to correct you" — so fast, frequent fixing feels normal.
  (paraphrase)
- **Criticism = a discrepancy between actual and desired.** Example: "you were
  supposed to be on time and this week you were late twice" is the discrepancy;
  "you're lazy" is the insult. Pointing out the discrepancy is objective
  criticism; the character judgment attached to it is the insult. (paraphrase of
  his definitions)
- To improve a team: **remove insults, focus on criticism, and tell people what
  to do instead.** (paraphrase)
- Easiest correction frame: **Stop / Start / Keep** — name what to stop, what to
  start (do instead), and what to keep doing. (paraphrase — named framework)
- **Teaching must come from a condition + a behavior.** To change someone, give
  them the *conditions* under which to change: "when someone says X / if this
  occurs (condition), do this (behavior)." Most training does none of this — it's
  just people shouting "level up / increase your performance," which is
  unactionable. (paraphrase)
- The hard part of feedback is identifying the **actual observable behavior**
  behind a label. Ask yourself "why do I think he's a dick / she's lazy?" — you
  reach for the label because you want to insult; instead, gather the underlying
  data. (paraphrase)
- Insults are useless to the receiver: told "stop being a dick," there's nothing
  to *do* — "you just get insulted and then you have nothing to do." (paraphrase)
- Worked example (the "dick" director): a high-performing director everyone
  disliked was fixed not by four executives telling him to stop being a dick,
  but by naming 3–4 specific behaviors and their replacements — e.g. **when
  someone else is talking, wait until they finish; if unsure, ask if they're
  done**; and **don't give unsolicited advice on how to run someone's department
  — ask if they want it, and if they say no, say nothing.** He changed within a
  week. (paraphrase of his anecdote)
- Aside on firing: "**HR will fire you if you're going to get fired**" — an
  employee worried about being fired wouldn't be meeting with Alex about it.
  (paraphrase)
- Worked example (the "lazy" employee, "Sandy"): convert the label into three
  data buckets — (1) slow Slack responses (1–3 hours), (2) incomplete /
  not-fully-fleshed-out deliverables, (3) shows up disheveled on Zoom — then
  issue concrete behavioral asks instead of the label. (paraphrase)
- You can run a **performance improvement plan (PIP) without announcing it** —
  don't say "you look lazy, level up or you're on a PIP"; just give the specific
  behavioral requirements directly. (paraphrase)
- Example concrete asks given to "Sandy": **use the standard company Zoom
  background** (so her bedroom isn't visible), **look professional from the waist
  up** (defined explicitly as a collared shirt and hair back), **turn on Slack
  notifications and respond within 10 minutes during work hours (9-5)** — after
  hours is fine. (paraphrase, with the "waist up / collared shirt / hair back"
  detail near-verbatim)
- On objections that specific appearance/behavior requirements clash with "woke
  politics": he waves it off ("no idea, deal with it in your country") but
  asserts you should have a culture where you *can* say things like that.
  (paraphrase)
- The end goal of all of it: "**I will get everyone to stop calling you lazy**" —
  i.e. fix the perception by fixing the observable behaviors. (paraphrase)
