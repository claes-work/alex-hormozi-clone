# Sounds like a scam': Pitching $200K in savings to CEOs

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a nonprofit health-benefits business that gets paid from savings generated for employer clients. The founder says the direct pitch sounds too good to be true and that proof is constrained by tax/privacy sensitivity. Alex suggests making the savings mechanism more believable by explaining the nonprofit margin structure, then "chunking up" acquisition: treat CPAs, financial advisors, and other centers of influence as the real customers, and build launch-plus-integration affiliate motions around them.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A savings pitch needs a believable mechanism; a nonprofit passing industry profit margin back to the customer may be easier to believe than a huge absolute savings number.
- When warm introductions drive acquisition, the seller should treat centers of influence as the primary customer for acquisition metrics.
- Marketing and sales should measure the cost and process of acquiring a center of influence, not only the end employer.
- Affiliate programs have two components: launch and integration; integration is usually more valuable.
- A launch can create the first internal case study for the affiliate.
- Integration means becoming part of the affiliate's onboarding process so every relevant new customer is routed through the offer.
- If 170 partners produce only about one account per month, most partners are not active and need launch/integration work.
