# The Education System: Why It's Failing Tomorrow's Leaders

## Summary

A multi-speaker panel/compilation on why conventional education fails and what
should replace it. Alex Hormozi argues education is fragmenting into practical,
niche-down verticals (the way media did) and lays out Acquisition.com's thesis of
funding the best practitioners in trades to teach others — connecting directly to
his investment in a peer-to-peer teaching platform (Skool). He frames school's core
failure as training convergent thinking (one right answer, one method) when the real
world rewards divergent thinking, and predicts the fix will come from an alternative,
technology-enabled schooling system rather than reform of the existing one.

> ⚠️ ATTRIBUTION: This video is a **compilation of multiple speakers**, not a solo
> Alex interview. **Neil deGrasse Tyson** and **Graham Stephan** are also present
> (see Notes). Only Alex-attributed segments below are persona training data.

## Key claims (Alex Hormozi — education-disruption thesis)

- **Hire for demonstrated ability, not credentials.** A degree can be an indicator of
  capability but isn't necessary; he cites Gates, Ellison, and Jobs (non-graduates) —
  look for a track record of exceptional achievement, which tends to continue.
- **Education is fragmenting into niche verticals, like media did.** The future is
  practical, niche-down education, not generalist education: the best mechanic teaching
  mechanics to run better shops, the best salon owner teaching salon owners, the best
  lawn-care operator packaging and selling his method — "there's no college for that,
  and there should be."
- **This is Acquisition.com's investment thesis.** He states the portfolio does over
  $100M/year and that funding niche practical educators is what they do now.
  *(self-reported; company/revenue figures unverified here.)*
- **Purpose of education = teach thinking, not memorization.** If you can pass a test
  with an iPhone in hand, the test was poorly designed. The "product" of the education
  system is people who can add value to society; the raw input is humans. The current
  system is inherited from the factory-worker era.
- **Convergent vs. divergent thinking.** School trains convergent thinking (multiple
  variables, one correct answer, and increasingly one accepted method). The real world
  runs on divergent thinking (multiple valid answers, some "more right" than others —
  hard to grade). Convergent tasks are already being automated; people get paid for
  creative/divergent thinking.
- **Conventional education should be massively overhauled** — organized around divergent
  problem-solving, explaining the *why* of what's taught, and making learning
  engaging/gamified to sustain lifelong curiosity.
- **It won't be fixed from within** due to power dynamics (teachers' unions, school
  funding, misaligned incentives); cites the documentary *Waiting for Superman* (says
  it made him cry). The fix will be a **new alternative schooling architecture** enabled
  by technology.
- **Naval Ravikant principle:** "technology democratizes consumption and consolidates
  production" — the best in the world can teach everyone at zero cost of duplication
  (Zoom / virtual). Displaced roles (analogy: telephone switchboard operators) are
  offset as new problems create new jobs (references web3).
- **Testing should match real-world conditions** (allow the phone), be self-paced, and
  have the best practitioners teach. Localization is "just semantics" — it can be one
  system; the best/most-relevant teacher by language or niche wins (cites Graham
  Stephan's personal-finance channel).
- **The system's harm:** students take on debt for "a piece of paper" that doesn't equip
  them to repay it; teachers are underpaid and forced to teach to tests they know don't
  help students. "I hate all of it."
- **His response (the Skool connection):** make free educational content, and invest in a
  **platform where any expert can teach peers** — "how to paint," "how to fix Hondas."
  One person can't fix education, so he's funding a platform so people can teach one
  another. He calls this his "whole next year."
