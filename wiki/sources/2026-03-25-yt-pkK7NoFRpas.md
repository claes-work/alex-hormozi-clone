# Helping an E-Com Founder Decide When to Exit

## Summary
An e-commerce founder with two LOIs on the table asks Alex Hormozi how to decide when to sell. The founder reports ~$6.1M revenue and ~$2.3M EBITDA this year, all one-time (non-recurring) sales acquired via ads. Hormozi frames the exit as a purely personal risk-and-goals decision, stresses knowing what you'll do afterward and how daily behavior will change, and notes deal terms (all-cash vs. seller financing/earnout, whether you must keep working) determine whether it's a good deal.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames deciding when to exit as one of the hardest business decisions and a "purely personal" risk decision that only the owner can make, weighing personal goals against how reliable and growable the business is.
- As of March 2026, Alex's rule: if you're confident you can keep growing, don't sell; if you doubt future growth, sell.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the underrated second question is "what will you do after?" — exiting without a plan tends to cause a crisis of meaning, while those who know their next move get right back to work.
- As of March 2026, Alex states that the day after selling Gym Launch he started Acquisition.com, and that a lot of time plus a lot of money plus nothing to do is a bad combination for entrepreneurs.
- As of March 2026, Alex says deal terms matter: an all-cash deal you can walk away from may be fine (e.g., 10x bottom line for one-time cash transactions), whereas seller financing, buying 51%, or being required to keep working means "you basically have a job either way."
- As of March 2026, Alex offers the frame "how will my daily behavior change?" — if what you do every day doesn't change, there's no reason to sell; his own sale terms required that he stop working the day the check cleared.
- As of March 2026, Alex asserts that businesses "always go up in value until the day they don't and then they're worth nothing" — growing or consistently maintaining businesses are worth more the longer held, but once a business declines "no one touches it."
- Caller claims (unverified): the business does ~$6.1M revenue and ~$2.3M EBITDA this year, all one-time sales with no recurring revenue, customers acquired via ads; they had backed out of an earlier deal after watching Alex's own long exit-decision video and now have a better offer with mostly cash up front plus a little seller financing and an earnout, structured so they exit once they sign.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes he made a ~46-minute YouTube video (roughly 2-3 years prior) documenting his full decision process for why he sold.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you were like, for sure we're going to be able to grow, then don't sell. If you're like, I don't know if we're going to be able to grow from here, then sell. And only you can know that."

> "The day after I sold Gym Launch, I started Acquisition, literally the next day. And I'm very happy that I did that. I think having a lot of time and a lot of money and nothing to do, bad combo for entrepreneurs."

> "How will my daily behavior change? If what you do every day doesn't change, then why sell it?"

> "Which is why for me the terms of my sale were like, I will stop working the day I get this check. And if you can't do that, then I will not sign the deal."

> "Businesses will always go up in value until the day they don't and then they're worth nothing. So a business that grows will always be worth more. Even a business that maintains is worth more, the track record they've been consistent. But the day the business goes down, no one touches it. So that's the knife's edge."

> "Sometimes it's like you flip the coin and then when it's in the air, you decide what you want to do. I think it's a terrible way to make decisions, but it works for some people."
