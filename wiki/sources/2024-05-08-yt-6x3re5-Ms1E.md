# The Ultra Rich Playbook [Legal & Tax-Free]

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out four escalating strategies ("V1–V4") the ultra-rich use to build and preserve wealth, then reverse-engineers each to a $10 million net-worth goal for a normal earner. The core mechanic is the "buy-borrow-die"-style idea: retain ownership of an income-producing asset and borrow against it (via ESOPs, dividend recaps, or loans against public equities) to extract tax-free liquidity while keeping control. He closes with meta-lessons on income generation, selectivity, "measuring sticks," and why copying Warren Buffett is a fallacy. Framed as his own experience and opinion — self-reported, not financial advice.

## Key claims

- **Four leverage tiers to a wealth goal:**
  - **V1 — pure income.** Zero leverage. To net $1B you'd need ~$1.5B in income (at his stated 67% top US rate); to net $10M you'd need ~$15M in income.
  - **V2 — income + passive investing.** Earn income, pay tax, reinvest the post-tax remainder in public equities (S&P 500 index) at ~9%. E.g. $1M/yr → ~$670K post-tax invested at 9% → ~$10M after 10 years.
  - **V3 — income + private equity (own the asset).** Own a business, take distributions, then sell it at an earnings multiple (12x at ultra scale; 10x for a ~$10M-revenue business throwing off ~$1.2M/yr), pay capital gains, keep the after-tax proceeds. Notes real small businesses (HVAC, dental, accounting firms) sell at ~10–14x.
- **V4 — three ways to get liquidity while retaining ownership/control:**
  - **(A) ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan).** Sell a **minority** stake (33–49%) to employees; a bank finances the employees, backstopped by the equity as collateral. Payment to the owner is **tax-free under US tax law** (self-reported); owner keeps majority control; employees get equity without fronting cash and are incentivized to "act like owners." Smaller multiple because banks price for risk, not upside.
  - **(B) Dividend recap.** Same bank structure but cut employees out — borrow directly against the whole business as collateral (analogy: buying a house with a mortgage). A bank may lend ~4–5x EBITDA (e.g. $100M/yr earnings → ~$500M loan). **The business carries the debt, not the owner personally**; owner takes the cash, the business pays the debt off over years, owner still owns the business at the end. (He is **unsure whether the recap proceeds are tax-free** — flags this uncertainty explicitly.)
  - **(C) Loans against public equities.** Take the company public, sell only a small % (5–10%), and the remaining shares get a real-time market value. Banks will lend up to ~50% of stock value, disbursed piecemeal within 24 hours. Because it's a **loan, it's tax-free** (you pay interest and repay principal). Cites Elon Musk borrowing against stock to buy X.
- **The V4 meta-strategy** = an income-producing asset **+** understanding debt: all three routes use a bank/institution to front capital while the owner **retains ownership and control**.
- **Tax framing (self-reported, not advice):** ESOP payment = tax-free; dividend recap = taxable payment (though he's unsure); loans against public equities = tax-free because they're loans. Argues taxing unrealized gains is like taxing a paycheck not yet received. Caveats the real risk: loans have covenants and must be repaid; if the business declines you can lose everything — "risk that's not being appropriately valued."
- **Qualified dividends** from passive distributions are taxed ~20% rather than at income-tax rates, so passive income keeps more per dollar.
- **A fourth path for non-owners:** become an employee/partner (e.g. 0.2%/yr × 5 yrs = 1%) at a company that goes public for ~$1B → ~$10M in stock acquired at very low tax cost, which you can then borrow against like the ultra-rich (can't do an ESOP as a non-owner, but can do the loan-against-equity move).
- **Common themes / meta-lessons:**
  - You must first **be valuable** and generate income by solving problems and learning skills (e.g. outbound sales → $150K/yr); passive-investing content is seductive but useless on a $35K income.
  - **Three fallacies of Warren Buffett:** (1) he bought his first stock at age 7, weeks after Pearl Harbor; (2) he invested through the greatest growth period in American history; (3) luck — the very top has all the skills/traits **plus** luck.
  - **Live below your means** to create the Delta of excess capital you can deploy aggressively.
  - **Rich parents' greatest gift** = teaching kids which opportunities to say **no** to; the #1 predictor of success is the zip code you're born in (proxy for surrounding conditions/associations). The wealthy pursue high-**leverage** opportunity vehicles (an "ultra salesman" sells companies at ~4% commission — $8M on a $200M deal — vs. a car salesman).
  - **Measuring sticks:** the wealthy measure over longer time horizons and in bigger units (Wall Street slang: a "stick" = $1M). Being around people who talk in millions raises the leverage of the opportunities you consider — and the things you say no to.
  - To make obscene money you won't do it the way someone already did; the most valuable skill is coming to your own conclusion and acting on it against the crowd. "Opportunities look like risk while they're opportunities."
