# Just do MORE

## Summary
Solo monologue adding "another layer of paint" to Alex's **more/better/new** framework. His core thesis: **new is incredibly risky**. He illustrates it with a candy analogy — throwing six pieces of candy and hoping they land perfectly sorted by color and stacked is statistically possible but wildly unlikely; a working business is exactly that improbable, well-ordered stack of variables. The taller/more evolved the stack, the higher the likelihood that any *different* move is the wrong move (his marketing version: a control that has won 36 straight A/B split tests is unlikely to be beaten by a change). Therefore the **highest risk-adjusted move is to do more of the thing that already works**; "better" is a tiny variation, but truly *new* rarely works and consumes the most resources. He allots himself **one new thing per year** (this year: the book launch) and says the bigger/more-resourced the company, the *fewer* new things it should do — you only want to "hit refresh" a lot when nothing works; once something works you want less deviation. He warns that many owners destroy their businesses solving **artificial/manufactured problems** that haven't occurred (COVID survivors know they adapt), when real existential threats give warning and are rarer than feared. The "another layer" for 2026: the highest-return question is **"why can't I do more of the thing that's working?"** — the easy mores run out, and the next "more" is a *harder* problem than doing something better or new, so people distract themselves with "reasonable problems no one will blame you for solving." The real risk to a business isn't a stupid decision (unlikely) but doing the **2nd/3rd/4th most profitable thing instead of the single most viable one**. Solving the true constraint often means **giving time time** — long, painful fixes (18-month culture rebuilds) that feel worse at month 6 but shouldn't be abandoned; don't reach for a new solution before the first bridge is finished. Two personal examples: early Gym Launch turning over 11 of 11 directors (and the teams beneath) over 12 months, killing morale that year; and, stuck at $30–50M/year for 3 years, deciding to **build a brand** — a multi-year bet that cut his income "by a lot" for the first two years. Point 11 pivots to the **third-marshmallow fallacy**: the marshmallow study made people assume delaying gratification is *always* right, but delay it indefinitely and "you work your entire life for nothing, and then you die" (the ant with a big stack of crumbs). Once you've *learned* to delay, the harder skill is knowing **when to accept gratification / begin reaping** — winners get dose-dependently rewarded for delaying and never stop. He argues **it's okay to just make money for its own sake** and to risk-adjust future outcomes (cash today beats a speculative payout 5–10 years out, because you get 5 more years to compound it — he passed judgment on a friend's hundreds-of-millions deal on this logic). Examples: his first gym's single $4,000/month cash personal-training client that "paid for me to eat" even though it wasn't scalable; and a creator with a big Twitter following who refused all CTAs to "protect goodwill" yet built the brand to make money — "how do you expect to make money if you never ask for it?" For lifelong **sowers/savers**, you must draw a line: today I begin reaping. Invest-forever/consume-nothing and consume-everything/invest-nothing are both wrong; the answer is personal but lives in the middle. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
