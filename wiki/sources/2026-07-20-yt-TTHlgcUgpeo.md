# Helping a $2M Coaching Business Cut Dead Weight

## Summary
In this coaching exchange, a caller named Jesse — who with his wife runs clinical training and business coaching for cash-based pelvic floor physical therapy practices ($2.1M last year, mostly from the $1.6M coaching side) — asks Alex Hormozi for help deciding whether to shut down their smaller brick-and-mortar physical therapy practice. The practice does $1.7M topline and $350K profit a year but consumes disproportionate time and emotional energy (Jesse attributes the attachment mainly to his wife), on top of two other businesses (a conference, and the coaching/education arm) and two young children.

Alex walks the numbers rather than just rendering a verdict: he has Jesse quantify revenue and profit across all ventures, then reframes the choice as an opportunity-cost problem rather than a "sunk cost" one. Rather than simply shutting the practice down, he suggests transferring it to a pelvic floor therapist in Jesse's own network — a low-effort exit that preserves a "clean exit story," gives clients continuity, and can be sweetened with a free membership to the coaching program as part of the deal — noting that shutting a business down is actually harder than transferring it. He also addresses the couple's belief that they can't credibly coach others on running these practices without keeping their own "city on the hill" location, calling it false, and illustrates the opportunity-cost logic with his own story of selling all six of his gyms for the price of one during the early days of Gym Launch, arguing the decision let him build a business doing $1M/month within six months. He closes by telling Jesse that a single brick-and-mortar location can't run seamlessly on its own — that only works at a scale (he cites ~20 locations) where management layers make the economics work — so holding onto the $350K/year practice is costing Jesse an estimated $2–3M/year in forgone opportunity.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-20. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ Jesse and his wife run clinical training and business coaching for cash-based pelvic floor physical therapy practices; total revenue was $2.1M last year, with about $1.6M from the coaching side (a six-month coaching program upsold into a longer continuity program, sold entirely without a closer — "they just buy"), delivered via one in-person gathering a year plus one-on-one coach calls.
- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The couple also runs a brick-and-mortar pelvic floor therapy practice ($1.7M topline, $350K profit last year, 25–30% margins), a related conference in Atlanta (roughly $500K in profit, "in the same world" as the therapy business), and are raising a three-year-old and a one-year-old — four ventures total.
- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ Jesse believes Alex will recommend shutting down the brick-and-mortar practice because it's the lowest-margin, highest-headache business and the one he and his wife are most emotionally attached to; he says he wants Alex to say it so he can relay it to his wife.
- (2026-07-20) Framed the practice decision as a math/opportunity-cost conversation rather than an emotional one: walk the actual revenue and profit numbers for each business before deciding anything.
- (2026-07-20) Rather than shutting the practice down outright, proposed transferring/selling it to someone already in Jesse's network (a pelvic floor physical therapist) — even at low or no price — in exchange for being fully released from it; noted that shutting a business down is actually harder than transferring it to someone else.
- (2026-07-20) Suggested sweetening a practice handoff by including a free membership to the coaching program as part of the deal, so the new owner has no added cost, the exiting owners get "a clean exit story," it becomes "a good success story for the clients," and employees get continuity.
- (2026-07-20) Challenged the couple's belief that they can't coach other practice owners without keeping their own flagship ("city on the hill") location — calling that belief false — and offered the reframe: "would you guys rather me be purely focused on getting you the highest returns possible, or occasionally have to fix someone's pelvis?"
- (2026-07-20) Recounted (self-reported) that during the early days of Gym Launch he owned six gyms, sold all six "for the price of one" within 90 days, and within six months was doing $1M/month in Gym Launch revenue — despite advisors telling him to wait ~18 months and polish the P&L first, which he estimates would have gotten him only ~$2–3M (maybe $3M) for the gyms in total.
- (2026-07-20) Generalized from that story: holding onto a smaller, lower-margin business can represent a large opportunity cost — told Jesse he should be making an extra $2–3M/year and is instead holding onto $350K, which is preventing him from making the extra $3M.
- (2026-07-20) On why a single brick-and-mortar location can't run seamlessly on its own: there isn't enough margin on one location to support the management layers needed; that only works with scale (he cites having ~20 locations), where one senior manager can oversee roughly five locations and the economics work out — unless the underlying model "prints money," which this one does not.
- (2026-07-20) Named "the opportunity cost" explicitly as the lens for the whole decision, contrasted with treating the business as something to hold onto because of sunk effort or emotional ownership.
