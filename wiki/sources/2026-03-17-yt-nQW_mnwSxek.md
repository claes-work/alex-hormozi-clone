# How to Start Your First Business

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host asks the questions; all substantive advice is Alex's). Alex lays out his end-to-end playbook for first-time founders. Starting a business is mechanically trivial: **four steps** — get an LLC, take it to a bank for a bank account, connect that account to a payment processor, then "go ask a stranger if you can do something in exchange for money." People waste years "going to start a business one day" when they could do all of it tomorrow. To get the first customer, he prescribes **warm personal outreach**: open your phone's ~400 contacts and your ~300 Facebook friends, send a *personalized* video or text ("Hey, I'm starting this thing, this is what I'm solving — do you know anybody?"), and ask for *referrals* rather than pitching friends directly. ~90% of the time the person replies "you could do that for me." He frames the whole learn-to-earn arc: do the first ~10–20 clients **for free** with explicit **risk reversal** ("I'm doing the first 10 free to get testimonials and to learn") — you're not working for free, "you're learning and then you're earning," and by the 19th client you've paid down 60–70% of your mistakes. Then create **artificial demand**: when you run out of time to give away, tell people who still want you, "I can't take more people — if you want, you can pay," and make your first sale with confidence from prior results. From there, **bump price 20% every five people** until they stop saying yes; that's your current price. He reframes the "I don't want to work for free" block as **entitlement masking insecurity** — you don't yet know how the full delivery lifecycle feels, so you're scared to sell more. Two levers make free work not-free: **education** (you're getting paid in learning) and **terms** (there's price, and there's terms — require feedback throughout plus a glowing review in multiple formats). Testimonials are worth more than cash: business owners would pay $500+ for a great testimonial, and your first 5–10 reviews will out-earn a one-time $1,000. His signature tactical offer is **"10 by 10"**: give 10 people 10 hours of one-on-one work free (or 5×5), the least-scalable, trackable free labor — compelling because even minimum-wage labor has understood value, and one-on-one needs no tech/calendaring. Pre-frame on call one ("what's the problem? if this is good, can I tell you what I do at the end?"), extract "ammunition" (other problems to solve) over the hours, then on the final hour recap the progress and pitch a paid engagement — reciprocity makes them say yes. Only give free work to **qualified** people, defined by **BANT** (budget, authority, need, timing — IBM's ~70-year-old framework). Analogy: timeshares sell over six-hour blocks because trust for a high-ticket sale takes time. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

- As of March 2026, "consistency is the currency of credibility" — people won't refer a "fly-by-night" operator because a referral risks their own social/relational capital.
- As of March 2026, he recommends doing the first ~10 clients free with explicit risk reversal ("I'm doing the first 10 free to get testimonials/reviews and to learn"); being upfront lowers the stakes for everyone.

- As of March 2026, he names supply/demand and leverage as the two biggest forces in business; a beginner should create *artificial* demand by giving lots away free, then convert when out of time to give.

- As of March 2026, he reframes "I don't want to work for free" as entitlement (believing you deserve something for nothing) sitting on top of insecurity — you haven't run 10 clients through the full delivery lifecycle, so you're scared to sell 100.
- As of March 2026, he distinguishes price from terms: even unpaid, the client pays you in (1) your free education and (2) terms — required feedback throughout plus a glowing review in three different formats (video + text).
