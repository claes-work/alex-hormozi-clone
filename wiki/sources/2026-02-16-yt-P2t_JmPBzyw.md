## Summary

In this interview clip Alex Hormozi walks through how Acquisition.com is actually
structured and makes money, and how the model evolved. He positions himself as a
**B2B content brand** who is repeatedly mistaken for a course-selling self-help guru
but sells no course — the strategy is to make his free material better than others'
paid material. He recounts the money story: he took ~$40M in distributions out of Gym
Launch before selling it, then sold the company for $46M, and used that to start a
**family office / holding company, Acquisition.com**. The original thesis was to make
content to attract businesses for **minority (10–20%) deals**; he did **24 deals in
24 months**, found it didn't work, exited almost all of them (kept ~4), and pivoted to
**more meaningful stakes in fewer, far better businesses** — Skool being the flagship
example (he is co-founder; cited at **17 million users**). In January 2024 he launched
an **advisory practice** serving lower-mid-market SMBs (~$500K to $50M revenue), which
he says is now a very big business. He also details buying premium domains:
**acquisition.com for $400K** and **ACQ.com for $500K** (the latter owned since 1996 by
an unnamed Fortune 100 company he was finally able to broker a deal with).

## Key claims (dated 2026-02-16, paraphrased, Alex-attributed)

- He **sells no course** and never has; people assume his free content is a funnel to a
  paid course, but the deliberate strategy is: *what if all your free stuff is better
  than everyone else's paid stuff?* His content is hardcore **B2B**.
- He is **co-founder of Skool**, a software platform for online communities / starting
  businesses online; cited at **17 million users**. Communities range far beyond
  business/crypto — canning/pickling, crochet, BMX, Pokémon cards; "the other half is
  just consumers doing consumer stuff."
- He bought the domain **acquisition.com for $400K (≈$400,000)**.
- He bought the domain **ACQ.com for $500K (≈$500,000)**; it had been owned **since 1996
  by a Fortune 100 company** he is contractually not allowed to name. He'd been trying to
  acquire it for a long time; the company had denied every prior approach until they
  finally agreed to sell, brokered via an internal contact. (Skool's domain, spelled with
  a K, cost less than either.)
- He took **~$40M (≈$40 million) in distributions** out of Gym Launch **before** selling
  it, and then **sold the company for $46M (≈$46 million)**.
- With those proceeds he started a **family office** that would be the holding company,
  **Acquisition.com**.
- **Original Acquisition.com thesis:** make content to attract businesses so he could do
  **minority deals — roughly 10–20% chunks** of companies, no brand or operational
  involvement, just capital.
- That minority-stake model **"ended up sucking"**: he did **24 deals in 24 months**,
  then got rid of almost all of them **except ~4**.
- **Pivot:** move to **more meaningful (larger) stakes in fewer, far better businesses.**
  Skool is the archetype of the new approach ("a deal that makes a ton of sense").
- He notes Acquisition.com is **"pretty good at building business,"** which is why the
  advisory arm made sense.
- **Advisory practice launched January 2024** ("January of last year, 2024"); now a very
  big business.
- Advisory is **industry-agnostic / open**, serving **SMBs from ~$500K on the low side to
  ~$50M revenue** as the sweet spot — explicitly the **low-to-mid market** that sits below
  the top-end consultancies (he name-checks Gartner, McKinsey, Deloitte) and above the
  "guru world."
- **Advisory delivery model:** clients fly out to meet directors specialized by function
  (paid media, content, etc.). Example given: a ~**$14M plumbing business** told what
  director roles to hire, how recruiting ads should look for specific roles, and how to
  solve supply/technician constraints.
