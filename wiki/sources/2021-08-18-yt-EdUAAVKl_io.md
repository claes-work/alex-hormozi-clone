# I Built a Business That Runs Without Me (2021-08-18)

## Summary

Hormozi shares the single mental model he used to turn active income into passive income:
the "jump two steps forward" delegation method. Instead of delegating his current job and
personally learning the next higher-value role (jumping one step), he hires someone who
already has the next skill too — delegating what he does now *and* placing the person who
does what he'd do next — so the business keeps growing without him. He ties this to a
poor→rich→wealthy progression (do wrong things → do right things → own right things).

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- **The runs-without-me method = "jump two steps forward."** Normal entrepreneurship
  delegates your current role and you learn the next one yourself (one step). Instead,
  delegate what you do now *and* find the person who already has the skill for the next
  thing you'd do — then the business grows without you. This is his core proto-**systems /
  delegation framework**.
- Poor people do the wrong things; rich people do the right things; wealthy people *own*
  the right things. (Stated as the video's thesis.)
- Many people overvalue their business because most of their income is really a
  high-skilled *job* they surrounded themselves with — they're self-employed, not owners.
  Returns come on the small slice they truly own as an enterprise, not on their labor.
- You must learn to do each role well enough to *recognize talent* and make a judgment
  when hiring — skill gives you the perspective from which to judge others.
- Self-reported metric: two fitness-space businesses did ~$17M profit in 2018; in 2021
  they'll do ~$8.1M (partly COVID-affected) — yet they're *more valuable now* because he
  does nothing in them. He went from "very rich because of what I did to wealthy because
  of what I own."
- **"Who not how"** (cites the concept): ask *who* already has the skill rather than *how*
  do I learn it. Connects to [[2021-08-16-yt-MLkFOaxIdxM]] "assemble a team."
- Owners *assemble* businesses; self-employed people *build* them. "Words matter,
  dialectics matter" — how you describe your role shapes it.
- Hiring nugget: if you interview ~20 people for a role and learn nothing, don't hire any
  of them. A good interview process teaches you what you're missing; the person you hire
  should end up knowing more than you about that role.
- A business owner who knows a lot about a role sets a higher bar and keeps strong hires
  "on their toes"; an owner who knows nothing invites slacking.
- Self-reported belief (flagged by him as possibly wrong): the degree to which you've
  grown a business yourself caps where it plateaus once you stop running it — a $50k/mo
  owner who delegates too early likely won't grow as fast as they want.
