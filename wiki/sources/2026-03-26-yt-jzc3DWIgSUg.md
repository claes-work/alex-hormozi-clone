# Stop Looking for Revenge

## Summary
In this clip Alex Hormozi reframes the desire for revenge as an internal project: rather than blaming external forces for a bad life, the truest "revenge" is punishing the past version of yourself by acting in the exact opposite of the habits that got you there. An unnamed interlocutor prompts and paraphrases his points, framing it as a pushback against victimhood culture and the stoic dichotomy of control. Hormozi extends the idea to self-acceptance, arguing that authentic acceptance means accepting the ideal version you can live up to, not the "shitty version" you are today.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that "wherever you point the finger of blame, power follows" — blaming others hands them control over your circumstances, so turning the blame inward is what restores agency.
- As of March 2026, he holds that the only thing you can control is your own actions, so even if you were genuinely wronged or dealt a bad hand, it "doesn't matter" for the purpose of changing your life.
- As of March 2026, he defines "revenge" as identifying what the past version of you did to get you here and doing the exact opposite as many times as possible; the pain of rejecting those old habits is the real revenge.
- As of March 2026, he frames his position as a pushback against externalizing the locus of control (blaming politics, the economy, the dollar, "the patriarchy") — claiming you get 100x–1,000x returns by directing effort at changing yourself rather than the world.
- As of March 2026, he advises treating your entire surroundings as immutable and yourself as the only mutable thing in the world; changing yourself changes the world because it changes how you see it.
- As of March 2026, he critiques the self-acceptance movement: people feel "icky" about it because they sense they are accepting a version of themselves that falls short. He reframes authentic self-acceptance as accepting the ideal you can live up to, not the current shortfall.
