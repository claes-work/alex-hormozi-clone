# you need to focus

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says entrepreneurs usually underestimate how long changes take to execute
and absorb. He argues that changing a process, script, landing page, or operational
system often creates an immediate performance dip, so small improvements are often not
worth the disruption. His current bias is toward stability unless a change is likely to
produce a large enough gain to justify the cost.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Entrepreneurs often think a change should take six weeks when it may take six months
  or a year.
- The longer someone does business, the fewer new things they can responsibly take on.
- Teams struggle to execute changes when they did not participate in deriving the final
  solution.
- Alex says many operational changes create an immediate performance decrease before
  improvement appears.
- A change should clear a high expected-gain threshold before disrupting a team.
- Constant changes can trap a team below baseline because nobody gets enough time to get
  good at the current process.
- Alex says he now over-indexes on stability because stable systems let people improve
  at their jobs.
- Service-business process changes are especially hard because people have to execute
  them repeatedly.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "What you think should take you 6 weeks often takes 6 months, sometimes a year."

> "Some things will never get fixed."

> "I traditionally will over index now on stability."
