# Stop Trying to Build a Cult-Like Following

> ★ **L3-candidate.** Contains a crisp, genuinely contrarian audience-building position (reject cult-building as a goal; the "reinforcer" 4-step framework) plus a repeatable model of how value gets attributed to a creator. **Potential tension for the synthesizer:** Hormozi elsewhere praises passionate communities/movements and himself has a famously devoted following — here he argues you should *not* aim for a cult, *not* build an us-vs-them enemy, and that the follower-attraction is a *byproduct* of product obsession. Reconcile with existing content-strategy/audience and branding pages (movement, community, "attractive character") rather than treating as a straight contradiction.

## Summary

A coaching-Q&A clip in which a dating coach ("the Tinder guy," referred to as Hunter) asks Alex Hormozi how to build a "cult-like following" so his audience has as much conviction as he does — noting people dress as Hormozi for Halloween. Hormozi reframes the whole premise: you don't want a cult, you want people you've provided value to who trust you'll keep providing value, so they follow your suggestions. He gives a four-step "reinforcer" framework for becoming someone an audience acts on, explains how content lets a creator get credited for a slice of the audience's real-world outcomes (Martha Stewart's recipes as the model), rejects the "create an enemy / us-vs-them" cult tactic as bad and negative (translate any "enemy" into a concept, e.g. complexity, not a person or company), and argues the cult-like following is a *byproduct* of obsessing over the best product (Steve Jobs, Elon/Tesla, his own books). He closes on hate being unavoidable and increasing with influence. The clip ends with an ad CTA for acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2026-02-20)

- **You should not try to build a cult.** The goal is: people you have provided value to, who believe you will provide value to them again, and therefore adhere to your suggestions. Wanting a following mainly so they'll "go to war for you in the comments" is the wrong (narcissistic/negative) framing — build the best thing and a passionate community forms around it on its own.
- **The four-step "reinforcer" framework** — do these and you condition yourself as a reinforcer (something that gets someone to do a behavior again): (1) visibly demonstrate that you control the things your audience wants to control; (2) give behavioral instructions they can follow to get a good outcome; (3) have third-party credibility that what you say is true; (4) be like them (like your audience).
- **Content lets you get credited for others' outcomes at scale.** If you remove the friction blocking someone from what they want and get them through it with your instructions, they attribute a portion of that outcome to you — and content does this at scale. Over time you shift from "the only value is what's in the video" to *being* a portion of the value the audience has created by following you.
- **Martha Stewart as the model:** she gave recipes day after day, people followed them and got good outcomes (praise from family), and a portion of the credit went to Martha. She "changed behavior" over a long period — that's why people say she changed their life.
- **Consistency compounds.** Repeat the same thing, passionately, over a long time, and stick to it; people recognize "this is the thing," a community forms, and some will adopt its markers (Hormozi's own uniform makes him easy to imitate; Skool has its cat mascot / Skool Games tattoos). Keeping appearance/routine somewhat consistent is valuable because people pick up on it.
- **Reject the "create an enemy / us-vs-them" cult tactic** — Hormozi calls it "kind of bad advice": it's negative and puts focus on something else. If you must have an enemy, make it a *concept*, not a person or company: e.g. Skool's enemy is complexity and not-having-fun ("fun and simplicity is our thing; complexity and not-fun is our enemy"), never pointing at "that guy." He's for being willing to out-work competition, not victimhood.
- **The cult-like following is a byproduct of product obsession, not a goal.** Steve Jobs wasn't trying to start a cult — he wanted the best computer; the turtleneck came from not wanting to think about clothes, not from styling. Hormozi wears the same shirt for comfort and to avoid decisions so he can build the best platform. Elon didn't try to build a cult; Tesla's cult-like following comes from his commitment to the thing.
- **Product-first is the true North Star.** Hormozi says he wouldn't have his brand without the books; he made them as simple as humanly possible because he genuinely wants people to win, and audiences can tell when a creator/engineer truly cares. "Put the product with everything and everything else takes care of itself" — the hard part is resisting distractions.
- **Content length rule: "No such thing as too long, only too boring."** People binge for hours (Netflix) as long as it's rewarding. Hormozi's team released his sales trainings and "brutally honest" clips as single multi-hour videos (~8.5–9 hours) and they performed well — a successful friend consumed 8.5 hours "in the background" on a long trip. As you condition yourself as a reinforcer, you earn more leeway: people consume because of *you* and your history of helping them.
- **Edutainment + humanistic connection.** Pair education with entertainment (being funny, relatable, "a normal dude"); the humanistic component builds a deeper connection than the content alone — people who like you as a person copy your choices ("if you told me you bought those shoes, I'd buy them; if you started swimming, I'd swim").
- **"Good advice generalizes."** Give great advice in one domain and people extend their trust to your adjacent advice (dating → fashion → etc.); after two or three generalizations they just follow your advice broadly — that's when you become an "influencer."
- **Hate is unavoidable and only grows.** The more you believe in anything, the more counter-resistance it draws; hate scales up until you "become news" like Elon or Trump (newsworthy regardless of what you do). Even universally-praised things get ~10% detractors, because disagreeing is how some people manufacture their own uniqueness/difference. Many people don't actually want that endpoint.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You don't want a cult. You want people who you have provided value to, who believe that you will provide value to them in the future, and as a result, they adhere to your suggestions."

> "You need to demonstrate visibly that you control the things that they want to control. Number one. Number two is you need to give behavioral instructions that they can follow and get a good outcome. Number three, you need to have third-party credibility that the things you say are true. And number four, you need to be like them... If you do those four things, you condition yourself as a reinforcer."

> "If you remove all the friction that someone is encountering that's blocking them from the thing they want, and then you help them get through that friction with your instructions, they will attribute a portion of that outcome to you. And with content you can do that at scale."

> "No such thing as too long, only too boring."

> "Fun is our thing and simplicity... complexity and not fun is our enemy. But we're not pointing at a dude and being like, 'That guy.'"

> "I'm also not trying to start a cult... I don't think Steve Jobs was either. He was just trying to make the best computer in the world."

> "The more you believe in anything, the more you will have counter resistance to that thing no matter what... eventually you become news. Like Elon or Trump — it doesn't matter what they do, they are news."
