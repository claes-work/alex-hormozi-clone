# How to Spot Bad Friends

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex offers a single litmus test for friends worth cutting: **do they talk about your future, or only your past?** Friends who can only reminisce about what you did together — never what you or they are going to do — are, in his framing, "living in the past," with their best years behind them; a growth-oriented person wants people looking at the same forward-facing goal. Keep the ones who talk about the future, cut the ones who talk about the past. From there he widens the lens to *whose opinion to listen to at all*. His claim: people "don't want the best version of you, they want the version of you who best serves them." Even parents — who are more aligned with your long-term goals than strangers — are not *completely* aligned; the only completely aligned party is "future you," so he uses that as his litmus test for whose opinion to weigh. When two parents want different things, he says the tell is which option serves each parent's own worldview, beliefs, or community status rather than you. He argues we care about others' opinions because as children we *had* to orient to the world through what others told us ("What's that?" → "This"), and the maturity move is to turn that off, re-examine inherited beliefs, and keep only the ones you choose. Parents are structurally **risk-averse** on your behalf (evolutionary drive to keep you alive; "playing not to lose"), so to "play to win" you must take different bets. He then pivots to a **facts-only** discipline: ignore intentions and internal narratives, focus only on observable actions and results — "would it be admitted to a court as evidence?" Saying you were offended is not evidence; the exact words said are. He extends this into a sustained attack on **self-labels** ("I'm bad at math," "I'm a neat freak," "I'm an early bird," "hothead"): labels change behavior more than behavior changes labels. His own example — he told people he was bad at math until 25, then decided he wasn't, ditched the calculator, and scored as well on the GMAT math section as on verbal. He ties this back to environment: family and hometown people "speak things over you" and reinforce old labels, which is why *changing your behavior means changing your environment.* He closes preferring **ideals over binary labels** — not "courageous or not" but "how courageous," taking daily steps toward chosen values — then the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
