# Your biggest advantage is no one knows who you are

## Summary

Solo Hormozi monologue arguing that being unknown and small is a competitive
advantage, not a handicap. He gives three reasons the underdog wins: (1) an underdog
has risk-free unlimited upside and a real positioning edge against the market leader,
(2) a smaller business is easier to "nail" (fix the retention/offer unit) before you
scale it, and (3) your competition only gets more sophisticated as you grow, so being
small means fewer, easier opponents. Along the way he re-teaches familiar frameworks
(Rule of 100, size-of-the-pie fallacy, nail-it-then-scale-it, the Grand Slam Offer
problems-and-solutions exercise) and closes on the personal cost of success with
friends and family. Heavily interlaced with promotion of his `$100M Money Models`
book and the August 16 launch event.

## Key claims (the obscurity-advantage argument)

- **Every position has an advantage; play the cards you're dealt.** Believing your
  starting position dictates the outcome is "only a loser's perspective."
- **Adopt an underdog mentality even when you're big** — underdogs win; market leaders
  get "fat and happy" and get taken over. Cites Bezos's "it's always day one at Amazon."
- **The unknown challenger's positioning edge vs. the market leader**: the leader sells
  proof/track record ("Coca-Cola Classic"), but the challenger can reframe the choice as
  "me personally vs. the junior rep they'd assign you" — direct access, dedication, "my
  cell phone number." You're not competing company-vs-company but you-vs-their-rep.
- **Nothing to lose = everything to gain.** The underdog has "risk-free unlimited
  upside" and unlimited shots on goal (miss and you're back at zero, where you already
  are). In game theory this makes them "the most dangerous player on the board because
  they can't lose." This is the logic that got him to quit his job: worst case, he gets
  the job back.
- **Most entrepreneurs fail repeatedly first.** He had "nine businesses that amounted to
  almost nothing" before his first big win; failures get progressively less bad (no
  sales → first sale → revenue but no profit → finally profit around year five).
- **Volatility is a symptom of inadequate volume.** One customer a month means it takes
  ~4 weeks of ad activity to get one; the fix is to compress that into one day. Volume
  negates luck and creates consistency.
- **Size-of-the-pie fallacy** (book p.168): markets feel saturated only because you
  define your market as one method/platform/medium. There are many advertising vehicles
  (warm/cold outreach, content, ads, affiliates, referrals, employees, agencies) and
  many platforms within each — nearly all of them empty for you. "No one knows you exist,"
  so advertise more.
- **Rule of 100** (p.164): 100 reachouts/day, or 100 minutes/day making content (and
  post one per day), or 100 minutes/day making paid ads. Ad spend/month should be
  relative to what you're willing to pay per customer.
- **Comment strategy update** (from the Skool Games): comments are less rate-limited than
  DMs — leave the best, most thoughtful comment on posts relevant to your avatar to
  "hitch a ride" on someone else's views. Give people flowers rather than dunking (his
  personal preference, explicitly flagged as opinion).
- **Nail it then scale it.** Company A (100 new/yr, 100% retention) compounds to 300+
  customers with better margins; Company B (100/200/300 new but loses all of them) has
  worse margins and must keep acquiring at ever-rising cost. Acquisition only gets more
  expensive over time (colder, later-adopter, more price-sensitive audiences). If you
  can't keep customers happy/referring/paying, there's no point scaling.
- **Grand Slam Offer exercise** (offer book): define the dream → list all problems
  blocking it → convert each problem into a named solution with brainstormed delivery
  options → trim-and-stack by value vs. cost. Update to the book: he's now more willing
  to include high-value / some-cost solutions, because the zero-cost solution market is
  crowded and the medium-cost-high-value space is not.
- **The 20% change-decrement rule** (pro tip): any operational change causes ~20% drop
  in performance immediately, so only make changes you're confident will yield >20%
  gain. He now over-indexes on stability; smaller teams suffer less decrement, another
  underdog advantage.
- **Your competition changes as you grow** — a stacked ladder of "bosses": yourself
  (the lazy version) → friends and family (who don't believe you / resent your changed
  expectations) → recruiting/selling employees on your dream → marketplace changes and
  regulators (cites FTC suits against Amazon, Fortnite/Epic, Reebok). Underdogs don't
  have to worry about the later ones yet.
- **"Everyone believes in the American dream until it comes true."** Personal thesis:
  people want you to do well, just not better than them. Hold on to the rare friends who
  truly want you to win.
