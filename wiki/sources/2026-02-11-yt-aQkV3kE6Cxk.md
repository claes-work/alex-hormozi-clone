# I Have a Software Business and a Coaching Business. Should I Shut One Down?

## Summary
Live business Q&A on @MoreMozi in which a founder asks whether his coaching/community offering is splitting his focus from the software business he really wants to build. His setup: a free group that teaches AI automation using his own tool (software doing ~$90k/month), with a money model that upsells into a paid "School" community adding ~$60k/month; he spends about an hour a day on community service delivery and wants a ~$100M software exit one day. Alex first mirrors another guest (Sam) by challenging whether there is even a problem to solve, then reframes the setup as not two businesses at all — it's software plus a paying community that feeds product feedback. His core counsel is focus and patience: he cites David Senra's two-word summary of 400 founder interviews, his "coats of paint" analogy for depth of thinking, his self-imposed "one thing a year" limit, and Acquisition.com's Bezos-style memo culture, using his $100M-in-3-days launch (planned over two years) as proof that nine-figure outcomes come from giving one problem enormous surface area. He closes by pitching his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed] The questioner runs a free group teaching AI automation with his own tool; that tool does ~$90k/month, and a money model upsells people into a paid community adding ~$60k/month. [self-reported figures]

- (2026-02-11) You manufacture problems by listening to too many conflicting internet opinions and then concluding there's a problem when there isn't one.
- (2026-02-11) The right first question is whether there is even a problem to solve — Alex explicitly mirrors Sam's framing.
- (2026-02-11) David Senra, after ~400 interviews on the Founders podcast, summarized all of them into two words: focus and patience — a summary Alex says he stands by. [Senra-attributed]
- (2026-02-11) Success requires doing one thing for an inordinate amount of time; most people jump to the next feature instead of polishing, but quality comes from depth.
- (2026-02-11) "Coats of paint": next-level thinking needs the previous coat to dry — you leave a problem, see more, and return to it with a fresh frame; you can judge solution quality by the number of attack vectors you approached it with.
- (2026-02-11) It's rare to hit all the attack vectors on the first pass; the fifth and eighth passes of thinking are what really unlock outsized results.
- (2026-02-11) Most people can't achieve order-of-magnitude outcomes because they split their time and never give a problem enough surface area.
- (2026-02-11) Alex gives himself only one thing per year, saying that's all he can really do — despite having more resources and team talent than the questioner. [self-reported]
- (2026-02-11) Acquisition.com runs a memo culture — all decisions are written down in memos (attributed to Bezos's practice) so the thinking can be defended and fully thought through. [Bezos-attributed as origin]
- (2026-02-11) The company was on memo seven for next year's launch plan and memo three for the rollout, expecting ~memo eleven and ~memo six by launch. [self-reported]
- (2026-02-11) The $100M launch did $100M in 3 days because the plan had been refined over two years of "coats of paint," repeatedly controlling for risks and building contingencies from things that went wrong in other businesses. [self-reported]
- (2026-02-11) Asked what he'd have done differently about that launch, his answer was "nothing," because everything went exactly to plan. [self-reported]
- (2026-02-11) If you want a nine-figure exit, that deep level of thinking is what your competitors are doing, so you should protect it at all costs.
- (2026-02-11) Reframe: the founder is not running two businesses — he has a software product plus some people who pay more within a community and give feedback on the software. Power users spend the most, take the upsell, and generate most of the feature requests.
