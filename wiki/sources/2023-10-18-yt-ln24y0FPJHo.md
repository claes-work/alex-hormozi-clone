# Brutally Honest Advice to My Younger Poorer Self

## Summary

A long-form, monologue-driven video of Hormozi's core mindset material addressed to his younger self: work ethic and time horizon as the great money-making advantages, embracing uncertainty as the true nature of entrepreneurship, reframing haters/competition/envy as feedback, and skills as "income insurance." Densely quotable and heavy on his signature frameworks (the slot-machine time-horizon analogy, the seven levels of advice, sadness vs. anxiety, give-until-they-ask).

## Key claims

- "Work ethic and time" is the most dangerous money-making combo in the world; going from good to great takes so much work that you can only be great at one or two things.
- He shifted his measure of work to "counting in hundreds" — hundreds of minutes/hours/days toward a specific goal — which forces far more volume.
- The uncertainty *is* entrepreneurship: "what makes entrepreneurship isn't the work itself, it's actually just not knowing if the work will work." Feeling behind/sleepless/unsure means you're on the right path, not doing it wrong.
- The slot-machine analogy: local restaurant vs. billion-dollar app take nearly identical work/stress but have different payouts and payout time-horizons; most people line up for the small, fast payout.
- Skills are "income insurance" — the biggest lesson of his life came after losing everything (sold the gyms, put the ~$46M-era nest egg into the next thing, lost it all, then made $100k the next month on a maxed-out credit card): what he'd built was his skills, not the gyms.
- Weekends: "you don't need a weekend... what you really need is work that you don't need a weekend from"; not going backwards on the ~104 weekend days is a massive compounding edge. Think work-life balance on a 7-year, not 7-day, horizon.
- Hate/competition are feedback, nothing more: "winners focus on winning, losers focus on winners." Envy = wanting what someone has (people behind you); jealousy = fearing losing what you have (people ahead of you).
- Seven levels of whose advice to weigh, from #7 (anonymous social-media comments) up to #1 (someone who has been there AND taken people just like you there multiple times).
