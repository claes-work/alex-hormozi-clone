# Building a Content Team From Scratch

## Summary
In a live Q&A on @MoreMozi, a member asks how Alex internally manages his organic content machine — recording cadence, number of editors and managers. Alex describes his current setup: he records once a week by going live for three or four hours, doing back-to-back YouTube videos and taking open Q&A at the end, all in one sitting, because the live/interactive format keeps his energy high (versus recording to camera in front of his crew, which drains him). That single session yields roughly 30 hours of raw material his team clips from. On team structure, he prescribes headcount as the answer to wanting more output, and describes a per-platform staffing model — a YouTube shorts person, a YouTube long person, a YouTube long strategist and editor, plus dedicated Instagram, LinkedIn, and Facebook people — because the platforms have fragmented and content no longer cross-performs. Each person owns their platform: sourcing clips from the recorded library and adding platform-native copy. Early on one or two generalists cover multiple platforms; specialization matters as you scale. His central principle is that pre-production (idea, packaging, research, key points, stories, visuals) is where all the leverage is — "an ounce of pre is worth a pound of post" — and that most creators' content is a rambling "four-minute voice memo" that could have been thirty seconds if they'd thought about what to say first. Production itself is straightforward; post-production is decentralized, cheaper, and near-automatic if the pre and the capture state are right. He warns that maximized content production for one person "will kill you" and eventually forces a media/production house, and advises picking the format you're most energized by so the talent stays in a good state on camera.
