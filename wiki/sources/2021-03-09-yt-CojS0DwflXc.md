# 3 Simple Steps To Close Any Sale (2021)

## Summary

In this early-2021 solo video, Hormozi teaches that when closing a complex sale, only
**three types of statements** should come out of a salesperson's mouth: **questions**,
**restatements** (reflecting back what the prospect said), and **short stories/analogies**
that break beliefs — never declarative teaching. He frames selling as coaching a prospect
to conclude *on their own* that they must work with you, argues that "breaking beliefs"
provides more value than tactical advice, and insists you never pitch until you already
believe the prospect will say yes. The talk originated from a sales-team coaching session
where he was reviewing "game tape" (call recordings) with his reps.

## Key claims

All claims paraphrase unless in a quote block. All dated **2021-03-09**.

- **The higher-level / more complex the sale, the more expertise the seller needs** (2021).
  Low-level transactional B2C sales (e.g. supplement sales over the phone) run on highly
  scripted, minimum-variability processes; complex business deals require deep understanding
  of the prospect's business.
- **Only three statement types belong in a close** (2021): (1) questions, (2) restatements,
  (3) short stories/analogies. None of these are declarative statements of fact.
- **Teach vs. coach distinction** (2021): teaching is one of the biggest mistakes in a
  close because prospects won't listen or execute until they buy; you coach them to a
  conclusion instead.
- **Questions are the primary tool and the only way to change beliefs** (2021). Leading
  questions guide the prospect to conclude *themselves* that they should work with you, so
  it feels less forced.
- **Restatements prove listening** (2021): saying the prospect's problem back to them
  ("so what I'm hearing is…") signals understanding.
- **Belief-breaking beats tactics on value delivered** (2021). Hormozi cites an unnamed
  "good friend": *everyone* benefits from having beliefs broken, but only ~10% of people
  benefit from tactics — so questioning beliefs services 100% of prospects.
- **Self-reported early mistake** (2021, recalling the start of Gym Launch): he used to get
  on calls and tell gym owners exactly what was wrong with their pricing; they took notes,
  didn't buy, and ignored the advice because they didn't know or trust him. He thought he
  was helping but wasn't.
- **Short stories/analogies close the deal** (2021): once questioning is exhausted, pre-
  packaged mini-stories illustrate what the prospect will *experience as a result* — not
  how the mechanism works. Examples he scripts live: "teach a man to fish" (Gym Launch),
  "learn to like working out more than TV" (weight loss), "find foods you like more than
  what you eat now" (nutrition).
- **Don't technobabble the mechanism** (2021): explaining programming, periodization,
  software, etc. loses the prospect; they don't care and won't listen. (He credits **Russell
  Brunson** for the term "technobabble" — borrowed, not original to Alex.)
- **Never pitch until you believe they'll say yes** (2021): analogies to lawyers not asking
  questions they don't know the answer to, and people not proposing marriage without knowing
  the answer. If you don't think you'll close, stay in the question/restate loop.
- **"Closers ask hard questions"** (2021) — a line he has his team say. Challenge the
  prospect's paradigm; if they're on the call to prove nothing will work, agree with their
  pessimism so they take a contrarian stance and sell *themselves* on why it *can* work.
- **You can usually tell within the first 5–10 minutes** whether you'll close (2021). If not,
  you need a "state shift" / "frame shift," achieved by asking challenging questions.
- **Retake the frame** (2021): if a prospect commands you ("tell me how the program works"),
  decline until you have enough context — otherwise you won't close.
- **Detach ego from close rate** (2021): approach every call intending to provide value by
  breaking beliefs whether or not they buy; this makes you sell more.

## Topic notes

This is an **early (2021) proto-version** of the closing methodology later codified as
**CLOSER** — see [[../topics/business/sales-frameworks]]. It predates the book/framework
codification and uses a looser "three types of statements" (questions / restatements /
short stories) rather than a named acronym.

Proto-framework flags:

- **Proto-CLOSER structure.** The live example call flow here — ask context questions ("why
  are you here / what have you tried / how's that working / why does it matter") → restate
  and label the problem → tell short outcome stories → get to "how do I work with you?" —
  maps directly onto CLOSER's **Clarify → Label → Overview past pain → Sell the vacation**
  sequence documented in the 2020 and 2022 sources, but without the acronym. The restatement
  step ("so what I'm hearing is… does that sound about right?") is a clear precursor to the
  **Label** step ("you now have cancer, I can cure it").

- **Proto "logical closing" / belief-transference.** The 2021 core thesis — "they will only
  execute if they have their beliefs broken and believe you can help them, and then trust
  what you're saying" — is an early statement of the later **"sales is a transference of
  belief/conviction over a bridge of trust"** formulation (2022–2023 sources in
  [[../topics/business/sales-frameworks]] §2 and §4). The 2021 version already contains
  belief + trust as the two ingredients, but not yet the "transference" metaphor or the
  "closer or further?" master frame.

- **Continuity with the five C's.** The framing device — reviewing "game tape" / call
  recordings with a sales team in a coaching session — is consistent with the **Call
  recordings** and **Comms** pillars of the 2020 "five C's" ([[../topics/business/sales-frameworks]] §6).

- **Borrowed term:** "technobabble" is credited to **Russell Brunson** (do not train as
  Alex's coinage). The "everyone benefits from breaking beliefs, only 10% from tactics"
  stat is credited to an unnamed friend.
