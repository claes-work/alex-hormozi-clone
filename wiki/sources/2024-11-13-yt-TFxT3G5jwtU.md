# It's Actually Pretty Easy to Get Ahead of 99% of People

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex argues that beating 99% of people is easy because most people work on the wrong things. His central thesis: growth comes from doing the one highest-leverage task ("A+ problem") instead of the pile of easy "B+ / C-level problems" that feel productive but never move revenue. He grounds this in the Theory of Constraints and Frank Slootman's one-objective management, then gives a tactical four-step process (prioritize, create urgency, eliminate everything else, ramp communication) illustrated with a live paid-ads/pixel-tracking turnaround he was between calls on while filming.

## Key claims

- Most "growth-isms" are wrong; if they were right, everyone would do them and grow. Most real growth is counter-intuitive. (Alex)
- Business-as-a-tree analogy: revenue = tree height. Side offshoots (B+ problems) siphon resources sideways so revenue stays flat; "pruning" (cutting them off) sends all resources into the trunk. Pruning to keep a tree healthy is even referenced biblically. (Alex)
- "Commitment is the elimination of alternatives" — and therefore the recipe for growth: focus all resources on the single highest-return path. (Alex, repeated framing)
- People default to solving B+ problems because they know how and get fast dopamine; the A+ problem is hairier, more complex, soaked in the unknown, and requires systems/analysis/iteration. (Alex)
- If you solve the #1 problem first, items 2–5 on the list often become irrelevant — or a new item suddenly becomes #1. So always reassess after solving, rather than pre-ranking a top-10 you'll never work through. (Alex)
- Theory of Constraints: one thing limits your growth; fix that constraint before anything else. Pixel-tracking example: if the pixel feeds bad data, no conversion-rate optimization downstream matters. (Alex)
- The goal of strategy = prioritize limited resources against unlimited opportunities. Excellent strategists are excellent prioritizers; the best see moves others miss (a hidden 10x vs. an obvious 2x). (Alex)
- Speed that matters is distance covered per unit of effort, not how fast you do tasks — the big-wheel vs. small-wheel analogy (one turn of a huge wheel beats ten of a tiny one). (Alex)
- Four-step process to pull the future forward: (1) prioritize the one thing, (2) create urgency (tighter meeting cadence — daily or twice-daily instead of weekly, deadlines), (3) eliminate everything else / let other fires burn, (4) increase communication frequency + make winning visible (dashboards, thermometer) and add a "pot of gold" reward. (Alex)
- As the boss, absorb accountability: declare "amnesia" so people stop protecting their turf and will let lower-priority fires burn on your authority ("tell them I said it's fine"). (Alex)
- "Graveyard of half-built bridges": businesses lose most money starting work they never finish — no dollar ever crosses the river to the bank account. (Alex)
- Increasing information transparency (financials, margins, conversion rates) aligns teams faster; entrepreneurs' fear of sharing is irrational — public companies show everything and still function (cites Sam Walton's autobiography). (Alex)
- Portfolio show-up-rate example: lifting event show-up to industry benchmark would raise revenue ~35% and, at ~10% margin, more than triple profit — so it became the company's single priority and the business grew >35%. (Alex)
