# How To Create Luck (2021-02-16)

## Summary

Framing a coaching session with his 18-year-old neighbor (a beginner cold-caller
in real-estate wholesaling), Hormozi argues that "luck" is a placeholder word used
by people who don't understand probability, and that sufficient volume mathematically
negates both luck and skill deficits. He reframes early failure as expected, insists
the first dollar is always the hardest because it requires learning many small skills,
and uses a "laying bricks on a bridge" metaphor for persistence.

## Key claims

- (2021) "Volume negates luck": if you do enough attempts, probability turns in your
  favor and outcomes stop looking like chance. To become luckier, simply increase
  `n` — the number of attempts.
- (2021) Volume also negates a skill deficit, because skill is a byproduct of volume —
  the more reps you do, the better you get, and therefore the "luckier" you become.
- (2021) Beginners are supposed to be bad; expecting to be a millionaire in year one
  is what stops ~99% of people, because their ego can't tolerate being bad, so they
  quit and say "this isn't for me."
- (2021) The first dollar / first deal is always the hardest, because you must learn
  many small skills before the big payoff; most people learn one skill (or none),
  quit, and start a new "half-built bridge."
- (2021) People measure progress as binary (closed the sale or didn't) when in reality
  there are many "bricks on the bridge" — incremental skills — before the first dollar
  crosses.
- (2021) Getting lucky too early can hurt you: an early fluke win can make you
  second-guess a working process, and you may have to sift through extra "no's" to
  account for the win you pulled forward.
- (2021) His only condition for letting the neighbor quit: prove the entire industry
  (real-estate wholesaling) has disappeared — otherwise the opportunity still exists
  and only skill (acquired via volume) separates him from what he wants.
- (2021) Illustrative rule-of-thumb math he offered the beginner: roughly ~1,000 cold
  calls per deal early on; do enough that it becomes "unreasonable" to still be bad.
  (Presented as a motivational estimate, not verified data.)

Note: Hormozi does not cite a formal "types of luck" taxonomy here; his argument is
purely probability/volume-based.
