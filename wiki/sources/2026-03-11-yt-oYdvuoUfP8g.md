# If You Can't Buy It Twice, Don't Buy It Once

## Summary
Solo monologue on Alex's personal spending/affordability discipline. His rule of thumb: "If you can't buy it twice, don't buy it once" — really buy it three, four, five times over, or don't buy it at all. The habit that reshaped his behavior was pricing purchases in **post-tax hours of his own labor**. He tells the origin story: as a teenager working at Smoothie King as a "blender tender" making $6.75/hour (just raised from $6.25), his after-tax wage was ~$6/hour. His lunch runs next door to Cosi (a sandwich shop) cost ~$13, meaning he "gave up two hours of my six-hour shift every shift" — a third of his daily earnings — just for lunch; framed that way, he'd never make the trade, yet he made it every day. Same logic on a $30 Gap shirt: a whole shift for a shirt he didn't need. Feeling the *pain* of the labor a purchase would cost — **before** buying — completely changed his buying behavior. He's explicit that saving isn't wealth-building on its own: "saving money won't make you money, but saving money allows you to have money," and having money lets you look further out, take bigger risks, and *those* make the money. He updates the math for adults: at $100/hour, would you work two full days for a $500–$1,000 purchase, or two days just to "go out with the boys for three hours on Saturday"? Whenever the honest answer was "I'd rather just not work than do that thing," that was his signal not to buy it. He closes with his early budgeting hack: when poor, he only spent cash — a personal-training client paid him $2,000/month cash, and that wallet cash was the only money he'd let himself live on (all business/credit-card income just accumulated in the bank), and being scared/frugal he'd always keep a buffer above that in case a payment came a day or two late. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's spending rule: "if you can't buy it twice, don't buy it once" — ideally you should be able to buy a thing three-to-five times over before buying it once.
- As of March 2026, his core reframe is pricing every purchase in **post-tax hours of his own labor** ("how much time it took me to make the amount of money post tax to buy whatever I wanted").
- As of March 2026 (origin story), at Smoothie King he earned $6.75/hour (raised from $6.25), ~$6/hour after taxes; a ~$13 Cosi lunch cost two hours of a six-hour shift — a third of his daily earnings — a trade he'd never make consciously yet made daily until he did this math.
- As of March 2026, feeling the *pain* of the labor required to afford something **before** buying it is what changed his buying behavior — a $30 Gap shirt read as "a whole shift" for something he didn't need.
- As of March 2026, saving is not itself wealth creation: "saving money won't make you money, but saving money allows you to have money" — and having money lets you look further out, take bigger risks, which do make more money.
- As of March 2026, the adult version of the test: at $100/hour, ask whether you'd work two days for a $500–$1,000 purchase (or two days for three hours of fun); if the honest answer is "I'd rather just not work than do that thing," don't buy it.
- As of March 2026 (early budgeting method), when poor he spent only cash: a personal-training client paid $2,000/month in cash, and that wallet cash was the only money he allowed himself to live on while all other income accumulated; frugality/fear led him to always keep a buffer above it against late payments.
