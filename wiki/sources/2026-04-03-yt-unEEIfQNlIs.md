# The Problem With High Ticket

## Summary

Alex advises a Quebec marketing company to return its attention to a low-priced website
subscription with strong reported retention instead of diverting resources into a new
high-ticket service. He argues that very small businesses are structurally volatile and
that serving many of them modestly can be a better model than pursuing a larger promised
transformation with short retention.

## Key claims

- The owner self-reports $2.1 million in sales, about $950,000 net profit, 700 legacy
  website customers at $200 per month, and 85% annual retention; all are unverified.
- As of April 2026, Alex says very-small-business customers are intrinsically unstable
  and cites Shopify retaining about 50% annually as an uncited best-in-class comparison.
- He says the low-ticket model works when high gross margins and low prices are designed
  into the entire operation, rather than treated as a temporary entry offer.
- The company moved to high ticket partly because the work felt robotic, but customers'
  continued payment is stronger evidence of value than the founder's own preferences.
- He frames strategy as prioritizing limited time, effort, and money across unlimited
  options; uncertainty about the top priority remains the entrepreneur's responsibility.
- Rather than build an ascension service, he recommends directing the same energy toward
  growing legacy acquisition from roughly 40-50 to 200 customers per month.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Strategy is prioritization."

> "There is always only one thing that is the most important in the business."

> "They have demonstrated with their dollars that they value what you give them."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: founder-preference versus revealed customer value; low-price/high-margin design for volatile very-small-business customers; concentrate resources on the proven model.
