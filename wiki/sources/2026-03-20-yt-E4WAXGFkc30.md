# Helping a Local Fast Food Chain Scale

## Summary
In a live Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a 22-year-old owner of a single pizza-and-ice-cream location (self-reported $2.4M revenue, 30% margin, ~500–700 pizzas/day) who is weighing opening a second location versus maxing out the first. Hormozi applies his "nail it then scale it" principle, urging the owner to first prove a general manager can sustain or grow the flagship location without him before expanding. He also gives benchmarks for privately-held brick-and-mortar manager compensation and warns that over-expanding before the first store is systemized is very hard to undo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reiterates "nail it then scale it": before opening a second brick-and-mortar location, prove the existing one is fully maxed out and can run without the owner.
- As of March 2026, Alex's litmus test for expansion: the GM must be able to do the owner's job in its entirety and the business must sustain or grow; only then is it safe to open the next store.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends the owner take a 3–6 month "on vacation" period where he does only back-office work; if the flagship location dips at all during that window, that signals it is not yet time to expand.
- As of March 2026, Alex says untracked/data-poor operations should be fixed up front, because each decision made without data carries greater stakes as you scale, and it only gets harder and messier later.
- As of March 2026, Alex's benchmark for a privately-held (non-franchised) brick-and-mortar store manager: on-target earnings around $100k all-in, ~$120–130k if they crush it, ~$75k if they underperform, plus a profit share; he says he has never seen it work without that structure.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues over-expanding is far harder to undo than delaying: taking an extra year to nail it now can "shave four off the back."
- Caller claims (unverified, self-reported): single location doing $2.4M revenue at 30% profit margin; ~500–700 pizzas/day; owner holds 67% (bought in with his father who holds 33%); deal structure gives owner 50% of a second location and 100% of a third; a new GM starting in two weeks earning 5% profit share on KPIs rising 1%/year capped at 10%.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you have a single brick-and-mortar store, like we say nail it then scale it."

> "You have to get it so that the general manager can actually do the job in its entirety without you, and the business sustains or grows. If those things occur, then you've got the right guy, and you can open up the next one."

> "I like to give 3 to 6 months of I'm on vacation because you're going to be doing this other thing."

> "Just because I don't train the sessions doesn't mean I don't own the business or I don't run the business. It's the same thing as the CEO versus the people who are actually delivering services in a business. There's still a job, it's just not customer-facing."

> "You can ask anybody here who has over expanded — when you have two and now you have something wrong at both, it is way harder to undo."

> "If it takes you an extra year now, it'll shave four off the back."

> "The privately owned brick-and-mortar almost always has a manager that has a profit share that's in place. Typically OTEs or on-target earnings should be somewhere in the neighborhood all in of like a hundred-ish."

> "You're the type person who's going to win either way. If you win, you win no matter what because you're a winner."
