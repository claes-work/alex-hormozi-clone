# More Followers Won't Make You Rich, But This Will

## Summary

In this group Q&A / mastermind-style session (Alex fielding questions from a room of
community/Skool creators), Hormozi lays out the content-strategy shifts he's making
after a 90-day test and ~35,000 pieces of content. Core thesis: follower count and raw
views are vanity — throughput from content to qualified buyers is what matters, so he's
pivoting to almost exclusively business (and business-adjacent) content and abandoning
"branded" content in favor of proof-led content. He details his give/ask ratio,
platform-specific consumption behavior, his Twitter-as-home-base repurposing engine, and
the cost of fame. Several community members share their own organic funnels (ManyChat,
webinars, boot camps) — those are NOT Alex.

## Key claims

- He has "zero desire to become famous for the sake of being famous"; what he wants is
  business owners as an audience, not attention. Privacy is "the cost of Fame."
- 90-day test: wide content (relationships, fitness, food, advice-to-younger-self) got
  more overall views but throughput to high-quality applications and book sales dropped;
  so he's shifting back to almost exclusively business content.
- Two "content world" theories he says the data contradicts: (1) that shorts viewers
  become long-form viewers, and (2) that going wide brings the right people. People have
  consumption preferences by platform; shorts viewers keep watching shorts, longs viewers
  watch longs. Cross-pollination only happens if someone finds you in shorts on one
  platform but is already a long viewer on another.
- Give/ask ratio: once you run ads, the ads do the "asking," so organic should become
  almost entirely "gives." He runs zero CTAs in shorts; CTAs for *free* stuff he counts
  as a give, not a take.
- His #1 all-time YouTube video has a 14-minute CTA at the end — CTAs don't have to hurt
  if they're relevant and well integrated, and if they point to free resources.
- Purpose of organic content = free facial recognition at the cold edge, building
  "circles of awareness": recognize face → had a positive content experience → know his
  name → regular viewer. This drives higher CTR, conversion, and prices.
- Shorts have ROI mostly via retargeting ads, not via CTAs inside the short; he doesn't
  even mention his community in shorts (only description / first comment / profile),
  trading a few CTA seconds for ~10x the views.
- On Instagram: reels are for new-audience reach; a photo/carousel is "like an email" —
  guaranteed reach to existing followers but reaches no one new — so images are for
  nurture, reels for acquisition. He posts tweets-as-reels because they perform.
- Two big marketing laws he attributes to his team: "State the facts and tell the truth,"
  and "say what no one else can say and show what no one else can show."
- Proof hierarchy for edification (best → weaker): "I helped all these people do this" →
  "I did this thing you want to do" → "I did work you're not willing to do, here's the
  result." The action creates the proof; if you can't do the hard thing, you can observe
  and reverse-engineer how a big, credible person did it.
- Education content = "a deal on time": show huge ROI on time (and cost) — the more time
  and money you can show you spent, the more valuable the distilled lesson.
- "No longer doing branded content" is one of his six shifts from 35,000 pieces: if
  people don't know who you are, "Alex Hormozi's version of X" is meaningless — say what
  you've *done*, not who you are, and let that be the reference point.
- Growth insight: he grew most when he "treated everyone like they didn't know who I was
  and I had to earn every single second" — the dating-your-wife analogy for keeping an
  audience.
- Content system / repurposing: Twitter/X is his home base and only "direct" content;
  he tweets 5–7x/day, top tweets become IG reels → read to camera as clips → 20 clips
  strung into a YouTube long; captions are just other high-performing tweets. Four remix
  modes: cross-platform, different format, redoing the same thing, reposting an old clip.
- Platform "memory" for reposting: Instagram has the longest memory and lowest daily
  volume tolerance (~2–3 posts max); Twitter has the shortest memory / most forgiving;
  TikTok in between; YouTube you basically can't repost (Content ID / videos live forever).
- He built via agencies from day one (per the *$100M Leads* agencies chapter) with an
  explicit plan to bring it in-house, learning fast to skip newbie mistakes; capital
  wasn't the constraint.
- He has never used "content buckets"; buckets emerge from whatever topic he's obsessed
  with (LTV, then sales, etc.). Many "Alex Hormozi framework breakdowns" (e.g. "four
  pillars") online are false — he doesn't do them.
- "Too good to fail" strategy (from a roommate who now runs a $100M company): make it so
  good that even one viewer will share it, and eventually you win. For beginners: hold
  nothing back, give away the secrets — people still pay for implementation.
- Fame: biggest pro = can recruit anyone anywhere; biggest con = loss of privacy. Being
  recognized is irreversible ("like a tattoo, like having a kid"); it changes behavior
  because you assume you're always being recorded — "I just can't have bad days."
- He estimates his social presence is "probably worth about a billion dollars" via media
  volume × conversion value; taking Acquisition.com public would be the most elegant
  conversion of that audience.
