# You're the Reason Your Margins Look Good

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Gil Karakashian, whose single-location automotive window-tint / paint-protection / wrap business wants to copy-paste stores nationally. Alex warns that the current 50% margin is probably artificially high because the two owners are acting as unpaid general managers. Before opening a second location, the business needs the first location to run profitably without either owner for six months. His litmus test is owner absence: a general manager earns upside only if the owner's phone does not ring.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Gil Karakashian** - the coachee. His business model, revenue goal, margins, partner, and staffing situation are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Owner-operated margins can be artificial because the owners are doing GM work that has not been costed into the model.
- A common multi-location failure pattern: the owner opens location two, divides attention, the first store declines, the second store underperforms, fixed liabilities double, and profit stays flat or falls.
- The model should not be scaled until the first location can grow without the owners for six months.
- The first location needs a true general manager who is treated like an owner and can earn a share of profit.
- The GM bargain is simple: the GM gets upside, and the owners' phones do not ring.
- Owners need to document their hundred small tasks as observable behaviors before those tasks can be assigned to a manager and team.
- "Nail it before we scale it": perfect the first-location model before adding locations, because backtracking from a bad location or bad market can trap the business for years.
- If the first shop runs for six months without the owners and makes more profit, the business then has multiple options: open shops, buy and flip failing shops, license, or franchise.
