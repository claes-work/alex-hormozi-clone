# Helping an Agency Get More Clients

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs "Dog Launch," a coaching/consulting business in the third year of trying to replicate Gym Launch in the pet-care industry (~$356k last year, pacing ~$1.2M, wants $3M in 2026). He asks for help getting more clients, but Alex quickly diagnoses the opposite problem: the business is **supply-constrained, not demand-constrained** — the client already can't take more customers because he's the only coach and delivery is maxed out. Alex's key reframe: "why would we change anything on the demand side?" The bottleneck is delivery, not lead generation, so the client's instinct to switch to an evergreen "sell every day" selling model is wrong. Delivery is extreme: six active cohorts with weekly 90-minute calls plus daily office hours (~45 hours/week of Zoom), driven partly by the client over-delivering out of insecurity and a desire to build goodwill in a new industry. Alex's fix is a **delivery decrease** (the mirror image of a price raise): the client keeps charging the same but delivers less, having trained his audience — by responding instantly — to over-consume his time. He teaches the client to reset expectations with a script that routes time requests to a resource or the next available calendar slot. The centerpiece is a **positioning / rollout framework** for cutting delivery without a revolt: frame it not as "less of what you liked" but as "more, just different," take the **moral high ground** by grounding the change in *integrity to a promise/mission* the client can no longer honor at the current touch level, and present it as the result of new information rather than an apology. Alex also flags that the client is emotionally "selling me on the way that you don't want to do" — defending a life he hates — and that losing some clients is fine because it makes room for better ones who pay more and lack the old expectations. He addresses the accessibility-as-differentiator worry with the underdog-to-authority arc: "you're just a number — with me, you get my cell phone" works until you're big enough to no longer need it. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses the client as supply-constrained, not demand-constrained; when a business can't take more customers, the fix is delivery, not more lead generation — "why would we change anything on the demand side?"
- As of March 2026, he advises against switching the selling model (e.g. to evergreen "sell every day") when the constraint is delivery capacity, because the selling model isn't the problem.
- As of March 2026, a **delivery decrease** is the mirror image of a price raise: the client keeps paying the same amount and gets less — a legitimate lever when a business has over-delivered.
- As of March 2026, over-delivery is often self-inflicted: the owner sets the expectations, and by responding quickly he rewards and trains the audience to demand his time, so he can retrain them to do something else.
- As of March 2026, the tactical fix for time requests is a script that routes people to a resource or to the next available calendar slot, rather than instantly hopping on another call.
- As of March 2026, a delivery change should be positioned as "more, just different" — not "less of what you liked" — and rolled out from the **moral high ground**, framed as honoring integrity to a promise or mission the owner can no longer keep at the current level.
- As of March 2026, the rollout narrative is: this is how things were → new information/decisions have come to light → here's what's changing → here's why it's great for you (with many more reasons than expected).
- As of March 2026, whether clients feel under-delivered-to is "not up to them"; the owner sets the standard, and an owner emotionally defending a delivery model he hates is "trying to sell you on the way that you don't want to do."
- As of March 2026, losing some clients in a delivery change is acceptable because it makes room for better clients who pay more and don't carry the old expectations.
- As of March 2026, accessibility (e.g. "you get my cell phone") is a valid underdog differentiator, but at sufficient scale/proof the owner can and should withdraw it because he no longer needs it.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I wouldn't change your selling model because you're supply-constrained, not demand-constrained. So, why would we change anything on the demand side?"

> "You reward people for reaching out to you quickly by responding quickly and answering their question. And so then they do more of it… you've trained your audience to do that. So, you just need to train them to do something else."

> "There's price raises you can do. You can also do delivery decreases… you're going to keep paying the same amount and you're just going to get less. But the way you're going to pitch it is that you're going to get even more, but rather different."

> "I don't position it as, 'Hey, I'm going to do less of what you liked.' I'm going to say, 'Hey, I've listened to all your feedback and I'm actually going to change this in a way that benefits all of you guys in these 17 reasons.'"

> "What you have to do is you have to position this as a moral high ground… 'I made a promise to you, and I've realized recently that I'm actually breaking this promise… in an effort to stick with my integrity, I have to change it to this.'"

> "You're trying to sell me on the way that you don't want to do… You're like, I hate this life, but damn it, I'm going to defend it. So you just have to flip that."

> "You will lose some people and that's okay. Because you will make room for better people who pay more and didn't come in with the old expectations."

> "You're an underdog, you do that cuz you say, 'Hey, you know, you could go to these guys, but you're just a number. With me, you get my cell phone.' But at some point, you get big enough that you're like, 'I can't give you my cell phone because I'm good enough and I've proven that.'"
