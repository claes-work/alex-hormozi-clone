# How to Know When to Push and When to Pivot

## Summary

Alex treats "push or pivot" as an eternal dichotomy to manage, not a problem with one
universal answer. Good failure is an iteration that teaches what to do next. Bad failure
means a "need-to-believe" assumption about the world is false, so the path should pivot. If
the core assumptions still hold and the only issue is skill deficiency, keep pushing and
pay down iterations. He again defines commitment as eliminating alternatives.

## Key claims

- Alex says push-vs-pivot, risk tolerance, and consume-vs-invest are enduring dichotomies,
  not fully solvable problems.
- He distinguishes good failure from bad failure.
- Good failure: the attempt fails but teaches what to do next, so the operator iterates.
- Bad failure: a core assumption required for the business to work is false, so the
  operator should pivot.
- If the "need to believe" conditions still hold and the problem is skill deficiency, he
  says to push.
- He says speed of progress depends on learning rate and feedback quality.
- He defines commitment as eliminating alternatives, comparing business commitment to
  marriage.
- His ten-year thought experiment: if doing the thing for 10 years while improving makes
  success likely, keep paying down iterations.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "They're more dichotomies to be managed than problems to solve."

> "If... one of my three or four assumptions that underpin this being successful long-term is not true, then... we need to pivot."

> "If it's a deficiency in skill, then that is where I would push."

> "I define commitment by the elimination of alternatives."

> "I'd rather be two months in on this one."
