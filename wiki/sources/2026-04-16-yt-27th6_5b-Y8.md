# How to Stack Skills

## Summary

Alex endorses skill stacking but stresses that the skills should be complementary. The
more skills someone has, the more opportunities they can see. For business, he distinguishes
product-specific skills from the general game of business: marketing, sales, HR, legal,
finance, and the other functions. Early founders should usually start with promotion and
selling because those feed the first dollar.

## Key claims

- On 2026-04-16, Alex says he is a hardcore advocate of skill stacking and attributes the
  idea in the conversation to Scott Adams.
- He says more skills create more visible opportunities and through-lines.
- He says skill stacks become valuable when complementary, e.g. bookkeeping, accounting,
  insurance, tax, and M&A around financial/business work.
- He warns against overreading random skill anecdotes like Steve Jobs's calligraphy; the
  more relevant skills were computers and hardware, and in a business context perhaps
  supply chain.
- He distinguishes the game of business from the product itself; the product differs, but
  functions like marketing, sales, HR, legal, and finance recur.
- He says founders can learn business outside-in through promotion and sales, or inside-out
  through product, but must eventually cover product, marketing, and sales to get the
  first dollar.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The more skills you have the more opportunities you can see."

> "You want to learn skills that are complimentary."

> "If you literally only know how to promote and learn how to sell, you will be able to feed yourself for the rest of your life."

## Synthesis candidate

Synthesis candidate: complementary skill stacking; business game vs product-specific skill;
promotion and sales as early founder sequence.
