# How to Force Yourself to Be Consistent and Do Hard Things

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that business is unbelievably hard because growth
requires accepting, repeatedly, that you are not good enough — and that the pain of
plateaus is a signal you haven't changed. He reframes business as an "infinite game" that
has run since ancient Egypt: there are no permanent winners, only players and quitters,
and all players get swapped out within a hundred years. He then reads his "many-sided die"
parable (from his Leads book) to make the case that mental toughness — frustration
tolerance, fortitude, resiliency, adaptability — is what keeps you rolling: the more you
roll the better you get, and if you quit you lose.

## Key claims

- High-level business owners earn outsized compensation because they made it through the
  brutal early period; they "earn the right" to earn more.
- Growth comes from the deficiency between your current state and your desired/required
  state; that stretch hurts because you're inadequate — hence "growing pains."
- Wishing to grow fast is wishing for a large amount of pain over an extended period.
- Much of business pain isn't physical — it's not knowing what you're doing and feeling
  like an idiot.
- When you feel behind, there are only two paths: blame something outside your control, or
  take the ego hit and admit you're just not that good yet.
- You have to accept you're not good, repeatedly — every time you think you're good, the
  world proves you aren't as good as you think.
- Plateaus (one, two, three years flat) mean you haven't changed; same behavior yields
  same outputs, and the required change is painful — sometimes it means taking two steps
  back and admitting you built it wrong / hired the wrong leader / have the wrong culture.
- Refusing to confront that inconvenient change leaves you perpetually under your
  potential.
- Believing our pain/love is uniquely deep robs us of the ability to learn from those who
  came before.
- He has "yet to see someone really start figuring out what's going on in less than 5
  years" — 5 years all-in, not dabbling on weekends.
- Business is hard to teach in school because the game changes even though the principles
  stay the same.
- Comparison is valuable for spotting discrepancies ("what is he doing that I'm not?") but
  becomes destructive when you label yourself worthless as a result.
- Business is an infinite game running since the dawn of time (merchants in ancient Egypt
  ~5,000 years ago); no one remembers who was "number one," and all players on the board
  get wiped within ~100 years.
- Chasing "number one" at net worth ends in trust-fund heirs who never learned the skills
  of the game; the third generation destroys what was built.
- The "many-sided die" parable: everyone gets a die with an unknown number of sides, one
  green; rolling green turns a red side green and lets you keep rolling; quitting = losing.
  You can't tell if another player is on their hundredth roll or hundred-thousandth.
- Two guarantees of the game: (1) the more times you roll, the better you get; (2) if you
  quit, you lose.
- Mental toughness skills — frustration tolerance, fortitude, resiliency, adaptability —
  are what let you stay in the game and keep rolling even after 20 reds in a row.
- In infinite games there are no winners and losers, only players and quitters; beginners
  are just early with more reds left to roll.
- The one constraint: don't lose so badly that you "die" — as long as you don't die, you
  can keep playing.
