# Don't Start a New Office Cold

## Summary

Alex recommends seeding a new insurance office with a proven leader from the existing
operation, then hiring the replacement into the established office. This isolates the
location variable, carries culture into the expansion, and gives top performers a career
path with small ownership participation.

## Key claims

- The owner self-reports $4 million in revenue, a goal of $10 million, two capacity-
  constrained offices, and a six-month sales cycle; these figures are unverified.
- As of April 2026, Alex recommends moving one of the five proven people into the new
  location and backfilling that role inside the cash-flowing, culture-rich original office.
- Starting with both a new hire and a new market makes person, market, and culture change
  simultaneously, leaving failure difficult to diagnose.
- Seeding the office with an incumbent leader reduces the expansion test primarily to
  the new location and transfers operating norms through behavior.
- A small ownership stake and the chance to run an office under the parent company's
  systems, infrastructure, and leads can create a career path for a top salesperson.
- He favors patient culture seeding over an arbitrary launch deadline when the stated
  goal is a durable company over 20 years.
- Alex self-reports owning insurance companies; no ownership or performance detail is
  independently verified in this source.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Don't start a new office cold."

> "You only want to add one new variable at a time."

> "Culture is something that takes a long time to drip down."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: seed expansion with proven culture, then backfill at the mothership; office leadership as a career-path and retention mechanism.
