# How To Make 2025 The Best Year Of Your Life

## Summary

A short solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi argues that where you spend your time and
money — your calendar and your credit card — is the honest predictor of who you become,
not your stated resolutions. He frames goal achievement as an identity/priorities
alignment problem ("we vote with our dollars"), then applies "commitment = the
elimination of alternatives" to relationships, spending most of the video on why
ambitious people must cut mediocre friends and get comfortable being alone.

## Key claims

- Attributes to his father a maxim: "rich people buy time, poor people buy stuff,
  ambitious people buy skills, lazy people buy distraction."
- Your calendar and spending reveal your true priorities; people are surprised by a low
  bank account or unmet goals, but those outcomes are "obvious" from how they spent.
- If something is worth doing for "future you," it's worth doing for "current you" —
  and current you is the only version that ever gets to enjoy life.
- Identity precedes action: decide the type of person you want to become, then align
  calendar and budget to the actions that person would take (the Sephora anecdote about
  girls being told to "budget" for makeup as they step into a new identity).
- Wanting an outcome without investing (time and money) is like wishing you owned Apple
  without ever buying the stock.
- It has never been easier to be successful, and never easier to be distracted.
- Defines commitment as "the elimination of alternatives" (marriage, exclusive business
  partnership); to get someone to commit, ask them to eliminate everything else.
- "Bad friends" are the first thing to cut; better friends are busier and seen less.
- Offers a 5-question test for keeping/cutting a friend (see quotes) and a 2-part filter
  he credits using on Leila: same *scale* of goals, and same level of dedication to them.
- Being exceptional means being the exception; it's normal for normal people to find you
  abnormal, and ambitious people cycle through lonely periods as they outpace peer groups.
