# How to Say No to the Wrong Opportunities

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a regional dietitian consulting and wellness company with 10 locations, 30 dietitians, $3.5M in 2025 revenue, and strong provider-referral growth. The owner is pulled between the existing clinic model, virtual scale, licensing, franchising, gyms, and adjacent ideas. Alex narrows the decision to risk tolerance and order of operations: evaluate upside, downside, likelihood, and whether the downside is survivable; improve the existing model's margins and repeatable opening team before adding licensing or other expansion paths.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A useful decision filter is: what is the upside, what is the downside, how likely is it, and can the owner live with the downside?
- If a downside is not livable, the opportunity should be rejected regardless of upside.
- When competitors with money and talent have not made a model profitable, assume they may not be foolish; the model may be structurally hard.
- For an insurance-reimbursed business where price is constrained, the controllable lever is efficiency rather than top-down pricing.
- Franchising is primarily a capital tool; if the business is not capital-intensive, franchising may be unnecessarily expensive capital.
- Licensing can become reasonable only after the owned model can open locations without the founder's direct involvement.
- At higher scale, most businesses converge into managing the same seven-to-ten functional leaders; the vehicle matters less than the leadership layer and economics.
