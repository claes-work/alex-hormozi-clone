# Your Business Is NOT What You Think It Is

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that most businesses plateau because the
entrepreneur is working on the wrong problem — they don't actually know what business
they're really in. He walks through his own misdiagnoses (gyms = sales/marketing, not
results; supplements = brand/media/distribution, not product; software = product, not
sales) plus client stories, then lays out how he decides whether to *push* or *pivot*:
push when your first-principles assumptions still hold ("good hard"), pivot only when an
underlying assumption is proven false ("bad hard"). He frames every hard problem as a
concrete wall worth a known amount of enterprise value, hit with a sledgehammer of
unknown thickness.

## Key claims

- The skill set that took you from Z to $1M (or $1M to $10M) is not what gets you from
  $10M to $30M — a harder, different problem sits in front of you. (paraphrase)
- The biggest opportunity is the one already in front of you; the "woman in the red
  dress" (shiny object) only looks better because it flatters your *current* skill set.
  (paraphrase)
- Every business has a "big hairy problem" — the real constraint — and it's usually not
  the one you think. Entrepreneurs get paid, and paid well, to solve it. (paraphrase)
- What business you're *really* in, per Hormozi's own misdiagnoses (paraphrase):
  - **Gyms** — not results/workouts; it's sales and marketing. The biggest fitness
    businesses are "marketing sales machines." Even $10/mo gyms (Planet Fitness, Crunch)
    run ~5–6% monthly churn; they just sign up a ton of people too.
  - **Supplements (Prestige Labs)** — not product/ingredients; it's brand, media and
    distribution. Especially for capsules (no flavor). He hired Dr. Cashey (PhD in
    biochemistry at 20, worked on Olympic-team stacks) chasing efficacy, but that wasn't
    the business. Even AG1 gets rebought because it tastes good and forms a habit.
  - **Software (Allan)** — not sales/marketing; it's product. Software automates work,
    so "good/faster/cheaper/better" is easy to sell — they hit $1.7M/mo run rate in six
    months — but the outsourced dev shop owned the product and couldn't deliver on the
    promise. Sold it in an all-stock deal to a strategic buyer who could rewrite the code.
- Warning on outsourced dev: as soon as the shop sees your revenue, your fees go up; with
  no in-house W2/equity coder to QA them, you're at their mercy. (paraphrase)
- Client story — gym owner turned Airbnb cleaner: had insane LTV and ~$6 CAC but was
  stuck, because a cleaning business is not a sales/marketing business — it's a
  **recruiting and training (talent) business**. There are fitness enthusiasts who'll
  train for free, but no "cleaning enthusiasts." Reframing his customer funnel (leads →
  sales → onboard → retain) into a *talent* funnel (applications → interview → onboard →
  train → ascend) took him from ~$30–40K/mo to over $150K/mo in ~12 months. (paraphrase)
- Coaching/consulting/info businesses: the scaled version (McKinsey, Bain, BCG, EY,
  KPMG, big law) is a **recruiting machine**. The arbitrage in an expertise business is
  the spread between what you pay a person and what you can charge for their expertise;
  they don't scale because one guru hires weaker "minions" and dilutes service. At scale
  these become partnerships/LLPs so trained talent has an ownership path and doesn't
  leave with the clients. (paraphrase)
- Investing: he thought it was doing lots of deals at good prices; it's really learning
  to say **no** at high velocity — "you only need one Facebook." Of 22 deals over ~2.5
  years, 4 drove ~90% of returns (power law). (paraphrase)
- Scale premium: growing a $100M portfolio company from $100M→$150M is the same or
  easier work than $0→$1M or $1M→$10M, and you get a premium on the EBITDA — the reverse
  of buying economies of scale. (paraphrase)
- Almost any business can reach ~$1B in value with a long enough time horizon (roofing,
  restaurants via franchise/chains, dry cleaning e.g. Zips) — it just takes a long time
  and beating a new "boss" at every level. (paraphrase)
- **Good hard vs. bad hard** (push vs. pivot) (paraphrase):
  - *Good hard* = iterative problems downstream of first-principles assumptions you still
    believe (e.g., accountants are on TikTok + you can reach them profitably → keep
    iterating clicks → optins → targeting → messaging → lead magnet). Ad losses aren't
    "losing money," they're investing in a new acquisition channel that raises enterprise
    value.
  - *Bad hard* / pivot = only when an underlying assumption is proven false by data
    (e.g., TikTok bans accountants). Then it's learning, not losing — the "ignorant tax"
    of business.
- A second, diversified customer-acquisition channel raises enterprise value twice: more
  profit *and* lower acquisition risk for an acquirer (bigger exit multiple), even before
  extra EBITDA. (paraphrase)
- Reframe losses as investment: "Will you invest $100K and wait six months for a $10M
  return?" — obviously yes, but people get "butt hurt" when ads don't immediately ROI.
  (paraphrase)
- Canned-cocktail client (UK): premium 5% ABV cocktails in ~1,000 stores but only
  ~$1.5M/yr — low sell-through (fronted inventory). She lacked the money for a big-brand
  celebrity play and had never run direct-response, so DTC/media wasn't her skill; RTD
  shipping costs kill DTC. She couldn't answer the core CPG metric — what % of ideal
  customers *rebuy*. Advice: shrink from 1,000 outlets to regional, go store-to-store
  sampling for 10–15 years — but only if the product is good enough to earn repeat
  purchase. Hail Mary: sell into transportation (airlines/trains/cruise lines) where the
  small can size fits and there are only 2–3 competitors; if that contract fails, shut it
  down. (paraphrase)
- The same cocktail lady made all her real money in **property development** ("runs
  itself," a few hours/week) yet called it "not scalable." Hormozi: property development
  is one of the most scalable businesses ever (add zeros, bigger properties, thousands of
  years old). Her real cross-business constraint was that she didn't know how to manage
  and train people. (paraphrase)
- Reframe circumstance into control: "this business isn't scalable" → "I don't know how
  to scale it"; "no salespeople sell like me" → "I don't know how to get them to sell
  like me." Turns a universe-problem into a solvable problem. (paraphrase)
- CPG has no LTV without unprompted repeat purchase — no amount of marketing/branding
  saves a mediocre product; you'll just tell everyone once that it's mediocre.
  (paraphrase)
- Missionary vs. mercenary: a 10–15-year grind only works if the founder has a
  "missionary heart" (wants the thing to exist in the world), not just mercenary
  arbitrage-chasing. (paraphrase)
