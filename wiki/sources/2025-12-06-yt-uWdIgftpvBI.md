# If I Started A Business in 2026, I'd Do This

## Summary

Solo Hormozi video arguing that a new business should pick one end of the price
spectrum — extremely expensive to a select few, or super cheap to everyone — and
that starting out you should sell your time one-on-one at a high price, even though
it's unscalable. He reframes common objections to selling time, walks through the
profit math of an expensive tier stacked on a cheap tier, and gives three "frames"
for building a premium offer plus a tactical value-creation model built on
avatar/pain, perceived likelihood of achievement, speed/latency, and ease.

## Key claims

- Every business is a "core economic arbitrage" between the cost of getting customers
  and what you make from them; a business allocates resources for superior throughput.
- Sell extremely expensive to a select few OR super cheap to everyone — "the middle
  is where people die."
- When starting, it is significantly easier to sell expensive to a few, because you
  need enough money before you can serve the masses. Tesla is the case study: Roadster
  ($250k) → Model S → Model 3, starting high and working down.
- The simplest way to create an expensive offer is to sell your time one-on-one, even
  if unscalable.
- Personal story: at his Huntington Beach gym (a group/semi-private training gym), one
  referred client did 5 days/week of 90-minute sessions at ~$125/hour (~$180/day),
  netting him roughly $45,000/month in cash — which funded reinvesting the business's
  own money into faster growth.
- Reasons one-on-one is superior early: you learn more from fewer high-value clients;
  high-value clients shift your beliefs about the market; more delivery flexibility and
  fast iteration (no systems/staff/code to change); you can cap the time; 100% margin
  because you keep all the money.
- Everyone earns money per hour whether they denote it that way or not — take last
  year's income divided by 2,000 (40 hrs/week) to get your hourly rate. Even investors
  like Warren Buffett trade time (analysis, research, due diligence on deals they pass on).
- "When you have demand, cut supply." Contracting supply (1-on-1) forces you to raise
  price — as long as you drop limiting beliefs.
- The acquisition.com logo encodes the two most powerful concepts in business: leverage
  (a fulcrum) and the supply/demand curve.
- Objection "it's not worth my time": fix the price so it IS worth your time.
- A high-ticket 1-on-1 offer lifts the whole brand via anchoring, narrative, and
  association — value transfers to the cheaper scalable product even if no one buys
  the premium tier.
- Action step: list the price where you sell; if not listed, say it when you offer. You
  must "confront the high price" out loud for the anchor to work, then shut up and let
  them talk.
- Downsell move: if they balk, pull out a couple components and offer a ~90%-of-value,
  more-scalable version.
- Profit math example: $100 offer (40% margin) vs $1,000 offer; if 90 of 100 buy the
  $100 thing and 10 buy the $1,000 thing, the expensive tier is 100% margin and yields
  ~3x the profit of the cheap tier — ~three-quarters of profit comes from the expensive
  thing. "Lots of zeros still add up."
- Three frames to build the premium offer: (1) what would you include if it cost 10x/100x
  more? cross out hard-cost items, keep the rest; (2) design as if growth came only from
  word-of-mouth via one customer; (3) strip everything unscalable but make it worth 10x.
- Premium clients also give you better marketing ("my private clients"), better case
  studies, and become friends who shift your worldview.
- Value creation tactics: pick the right avatar (has money, feels pain, easy to reach —
  NOT the current $100 buyer); describe their pain better than they can. AI hack: pull
  reviews/quotes from books people buy in the niche to extract specific pain language.
- "Pain and persuasion only exist in the specific, never the vague." Articulating a
  problem better than the customer makes them believe you can solve it — sometimes a
  stronger motivator than a bigger promise.
- People don't want your time, they want an outcome; one-on-one raises the perceived
  likelihood of achievement, ease, and (via reputation) acts as an implied guarantee.
- Speed/latency is the single biggest persuasion lever: "latency beats magnitude 7 days
  a week and twice on Sunday." Sell the wealthy on time saved, not money saved.
- Tactics for speed: cut current delivery time in half or two-thirds; premium tiers get
  priority ("first in line," pull someone off a job). Reverse-engineer ease by mapping
  the customer experience and deleting friction point by friction point.
- Charging 10x lets you pay vendors/partners more to prioritize your customers, creating
  your own "priority ring" to out-compete rivals.
- Bootstrap strategy: spend 5-10% of your time on a high-price 1-on-1 tier, cash-flow
  all growth from it, and go on the offense with the rest of the money.
