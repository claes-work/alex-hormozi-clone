# I OWN 6 multimillion dollar companies...this is how I do it

Source id: `yt-V7oUcAfof34` · Published 2021-07-09 · Channel [[../entities/channel-alexhormozi]] · Speaker: Alex Hormozi (sole speaker)

## Summary

Hormozi describes his portfolio operating model — an early Acquisition.com-precursor —
where he treats himself as an owner rather than a CEO across a set of companies (three
owned outright, five held as minority positions), engaging each the same way: quarterly
meetings, "nose in, hands out," and no operational to-dos. He argues that structuring a
business so it *could* be sold — independent of any intent to sell — is one of the most
valuable things an entrepreneur can do because it forces true owner-independence and buys
freedom.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- (2021) His portfolio does just under $100M/year in revenue; he owns three companies
  outright and holds minority positions in five others.
- (2021) Structuring a company so it *can* be sold — even if you never sell — is one of
  the most valuable things he's done; it creates optionality and freedom.
- (2021) The hardest transition he made was CEO → owner, and it took him over a decade.
- (2021) His mistake early on: being CEO of two companies at once. CEO is a full-time
  role; a company that still needs you is not sellable (or sells for far less).
- (2021) Reframes the guru cliché: it's not that "a business that can't run without you
  isn't a real business" — rather, you are not an *owner* if it can't run without you.
- (2021) Warren Buffett as the model: he *owns* See's Candies, Geico, Ben Bridge — he
  doesn't *run* them.
- (2021) Operating principle **"Nose in, hands out" (NIHO)**, from a mentor: he advises
  strategically, bounces ideas, but leaves meetings with no to-dos/homework.
- (2021) He treats all portfolio companies the same regardless of ownership stake —
  quarterly meetings plus as-needed touchpoints.
- (2021) His litmus test of ownership: put someone else in charge and, over a six-month
  period, the company should **grow** (top line and profit), not just maintain, without
  his involvement.
- (2021) Downside of a personality-based brand vs. a faceless brand: harder to remove
  yourself (See's/Geico aren't about Buffett — Geico has a gecko mascot).
- (2021) A key lever for removing yourself: get off the company's central comms (Slack /
  Asana). The portfolio uses one separate Slack containing only the executives of each
  company; their teams report to them, not to him.
- (2021) The goal is ownership, not CEO — acting as owner yields better long-term
  decisions, less short-term stress, and ultimately more freedom.
