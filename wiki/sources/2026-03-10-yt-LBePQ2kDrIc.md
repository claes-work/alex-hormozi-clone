# Morning routine obsessed people make less money

## Summary
Solo clip. Alex's anti-morning-routine argument: people obsessed with their morning routines make less money than people obsessed with making money. His causal story is that people work, sacrifice, and get rich; *then* they fill their new free time with "weird routines," forget how they actually got rich (whatever it took, not a 3-hour routine), and wrongly credit the routines. He illustrates with a DM from a kid whose business coach had him doing a 3-hour morning routine (grounded walking, gratitude journal, daily list, ice plunge) that left him with no energy to work; Alex told him to cut the routine and replace it with 3 hours of work, and a week later the kid said it changed his life. Alex's operating question is "what actions do I have to take to get what I want?" — anything else is a distraction. He is careful to say he is not against doing things, only against doing things for their own sake: a 5-minute habit that measurably raises output 20% is fine; the test is measurable output, not ritual. He then generalizes to a broader thinking error — humans make mistaken correlations between things that are merely proximate ("tall people play basketball, so I'll play basketball to get tall"). The fix: study what a successful person did on the way up, in the grind, and mirror *that*, not what they do now at the top of the mountain. He mocks the correlation trap with Warren Buffett drinking Coke (Alex drinks ~five 12-oz Cokes a day) and rejects the interview premise that his hard childhood caused his success — the *work* caused it, and plenty of people with bad childhoods aren't successful (and plenty without them are). His conclusion: spend less effort chasing why something happened (intention and other amorphous, unmeasurable things) and only look at what actions produced a result. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
