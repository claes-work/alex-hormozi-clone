# How to Communicate Clearly

## Summary
Short solo clip on how to explain things so people actually understand and retain them, spanning both content creation and internal training. Alex's core moves: **explain via analogy** — analogies are "absolutely imprecise" but far more effective at getting comprehension than technical accuracy, so skip the science ("no one cares") and reach for the analogy. His content principle is that **good content is declarative, not procedural**: talk about *stuff* (what is possible, what the problems with an existing thing are) rather than *how to do stuff*; the moment you get granular ("we do two pills a day…"), "you lose everyone," so keep content conceptual. He extends the same logic to training staff: instead of explaining how something works, **teach pattern matching**, which is significantly faster to train and just as effective. In roughly 95% of cases a pattern always matches and the trainee "can just run the playbook"; for the 5% that don't, they escalate to you, you create a new pattern, and train them on it. His illustration: you don't need to explain how mitochondria work — "they present with this, this is the protocol." Ends with the standard $100M scaling roadmap / acquisition.com promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, when explaining anything, Alex recommends explaining via analogy — analogies are imprecise but "way more effective for getting people to understand," and technical/scientific detail should be dropped because the audience doesn't care.
- As of March 2026, good content is "declarative not procedural" — it talks about *stuff* (what is possible, what problems exist with an existing thing) rather than *how to do* stuff.
- As of March 2026, granularity kills comprehension: "as soon as you get granular, you lose everyone," so content should stay conceptual.
- As of March 2026, for training staff, teaching pattern matching beats explaining how something works — it is significantly faster to train and just as effective.
- As of March 2026, his 95/5 training model: in ~95% of cases patterns always match and the trainee runs the playbook; in the ~5% that don't, they escalate, you create a new pattern, and train them on it ("they present with this, this is the protocol").
