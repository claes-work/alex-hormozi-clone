# You Need to Take Action

## Summary

Alex Hormozi distinguishes declarative knowledge (knowing about something) from procedural
knowledge (knowing how to do it). He argues that business, sales, and content are learned
through repetitions with feedback, not through reading alone. For education content, he
frames the creator's job as changing behavior by selling time: compressing the creator's
experience into usable lessons for the audience.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Declarative knowledge is knowing about something; procedural knowledge is knowing how to
  do something.
- Someone can understand private equity or sales conceptually without knowing how to do a
  deal or sell.
- The first 100 sales calls teach more procedural selling knowledge than 100 sales books.
- Rapid learning requires doing many repetitions quickly, then examining the least-bad
  outcomes and improving the next batch.
- Entertainment's goal is maintaining attention; education's goal is changing behavior.
- Education sells time by packaging hard-won experience so the buyer does not have to pay
  the same time cost.
- Confidence in any endeavor comes from enough volume that continued incompetence becomes
  unreasonable.
- Repetitions need feedback loops; doing 100 outreaches without studying what worked is
  incomplete.
- A practitioner knows why rules exist and when they can bend or break them; a parrot only
  repeats someone else's rules.
- Alex's content team derived its proof/promise/plan/picture/pain video-opening checklist
  by reviewing outliers and adding observed variables over time.
