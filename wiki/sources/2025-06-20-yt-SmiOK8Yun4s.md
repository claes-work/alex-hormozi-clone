# I Blew Up A Secret Business To Prove It's Not Luck

## Summary

Alex Hormozi delivers a solo teardown of "Ashley," a fashion stylist doing ~$309K/year (130K profit, 42% margins) selling personal styling to wealthy clients. Despite the clickbait title, this is a business-diagnosis walkthrough, not an account of Alex building a business from scratch. He identifies and fixes three problems: (1) a leaky funnel (adds a lead magnet, a VSSL, harder qualification, and rewritten headlines), (2) a massively under-used affiliate channel (six partners drove a third of revenue from 40 emails), and (3) pricing inefficiencies (adds a price anchor, makes continuity the default, pushes price). Throughout he generalizes each fix into a teachable principle for the viewer's own business.

## Key claims (Alex's advice/teaching only)

- Ads split into **interruption-based** (social, TV, radio, banners — you weren't looking for it) and **intent-based** (search, referrals — you initiated it); you must track metrics separately because they are two different people needing two different processes and sometimes two different products.
- A common founder mistake is **blending cold and high-intent metrics**, which makes people wrongly think their offer is bad.
- A good lead magnet does **not** solve the whole problem — it solves one narrow problem completely, which reveals the next problem you sell the solution to. Adding free value before the money ask can roughly **triple** sales (his illustration: 1/100 buy cold vs. 10 take the free thing → 3 of 10 buy).
- Content is, in effect, "hundreds and thousands of tiny little lead magnets" given free without an opt-in — a pre-lead-magnet to the lead magnet.
- Traits of a great lead magnet: **fast, easy, narrow, solves one complete problem**, interesting + clear name, easy to consume, and actually good. (Cites *$100M Leads* p.45.)
- Don't put your "trash" as your lead magnet: "99% of people are never going to buy anything from you. You want the marketplace to judge you on your worst thing." His books are his business's lead magnet — ~2 years each.
- A lead magnet need not be automated or an ebook/video/book — **a service can be a lead magnet**; you're allowed to restrict who qualifies for it.
- Qualify on **BANT — Budget, Authority, Need, Timing** (invented by IBM, not him); he adds an income question on the intake form. Qualifying harder converts fence-sitters ("these guys serve people only like me") and is a form of positioning within a niche.
- A **VSSL** (video sales letter) is your best pitch on film; length varies with the "three Ps" — **Product, Prospect, Process**. High-intent traffic often needs only a 3–7 minute VSSL.
- Pre-selling shortens sales cycles: cutting two calls to one **doubles sales with the same team**; four calls can often become one or two. "It only takes a moment to make a decision. What takes prior to that decision is the information required to make the decision."
- Bring the most salient elements of all your calls to the front so the actual conversation is just the purchase decision — can also cut 60–90 min calls to 15–20 min.
- On the VSSL: **don't obsess — "it being made is it being posted."** Ship v1, then add answers to real objections over time. He's "pretty sure" no active VSSL in any company he owns is still its first version.
- If a prospect didn't watch the VSSL, **maintain the frame** — hand them the video on the call ("I'll go grab a coffee, back in 7 minutes") rather than re-scripting; keep the process uniform.
- **Speed** and **qualification** are two things that "age like wine" — he values them more the longer he's in business.
- **Headlines**: 80% of the advertising dollar goes to the first words seen; the headline is the lens through which all other copy is consumed. Headline formula: "how to [yay] without [boo/insecurity]." Example rewrite: *Free lookbook to dress for your tone using your existing closet.*
- Fix the funnel (stop the leak) **before** cranking ad spend, so you don't waste money on traffic that won't convert.
- Businesses behave in patterns across industry and size; Ashley is "textbook stage four" on the *$100M* scaling roadmap (a.com/roadmap) — double down on top 20% of customers, make better free stuff, add qualification/friction so extra volume self-selects, and track KPIs.
- **More > Better > New**: obvious "gold mines" are hidden in plain sight; founders chase new opportunities instead of doubling down on what already works, usually because they're distracted running everything at once.
- **Affiliates must be treated like customers** — "no one's going to do that work for you." It's a farming (relationship-manager) sale, not a hunting sale; treat them as recurring customer generators / nodes; provide value, buy dinners, host events to stay top of mind.
- Affiliate leads are pre-qualified warm referrals with high close likelihood. Affiliate build steps: find ideal affiliates → make them an offer → qualify them → figure out compensation → launch the partnership → integrate for ongoing monthly flow. (Chapter in *$100M Leads*, starts p.227.)
- **Staying focused enables partnerships**: if you try to do all things for all people you can't partner with anyone; a narrow avatar lets you partner with adjacent (non-competitive) businesses that sell to the same customer.
- **Congruence** is the one word for a sales process: high-end ad, lead magnet, qualification, price, and product must all match, or the frame breaks and conversion plummets.
- **Price anchor**: adding a top tier ~10x the core price can effectively double revenue even if only ~10% take it, and makes the former top price feel like a deal. Pro tip: if you don't plan to sell much of the anchor, make it nearly identical to the core offer, changing only one or two things most people don't care about.
- **Default bias / opt-out**: humans follow herd/default behavior to lower risk (the organ-donor opt-out vs. opt-in example — one country got 70%+ vs. others under 10%). Make continuity the default "next natural step" after onboarding with an assumed close.
- Two upsells that "only always work": **more of that thing they just bought**, or **more help with that thing they just bought**.
- **Veblen good**: raising price can raise perceived value, especially in luxury; luxury buyers don't want the cheapest option and will default to the lowest tier if you give no premium path.
- "The easiest way to make more money in any business is charge more for the exact same thing" (given similar take rate). **The point of maximum profit is after the point of maximum conversion** — keep pushing price until you'd make less money overall.
