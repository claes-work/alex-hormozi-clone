# He Owns 40% of His Market and It's Still Not Enough

## Summary

In this live coaching exchange, a business owner (Tom) running a veteran-compensation-claims firm in Australia asks Alex Hormozi how to reach a ~$1B enterprise value. Tom's firm does $55M revenue (contracted to $100M in two years), already captures ~40% of its market, sells one product to one avatar on a no-win-no-fee model (~7% of an average $350K government payout, ~$20K LTV). He fears that even doubling market share to 60% only adds ~$50M revenue and won't hit the goal, but he's also afraid that adding new products or avatars will dilute the singular focus that got him here.

Alex reframes the two growth levers: take more market share (acquire competitors) or go adjacent. On acquisition, his core move is to **buy the front end, not the back end** — purchase competitors for their marketing and sales teams (the thing they beat you on) and plug them into your superior delivery, since Tom says his back end scales easily. He argues for a portfolio-of-brands approach (like news companies under one parent), keeping acquired front ends targeting their sub-segments while running on shared delivery.

On the adjacency question, Alex identifies the structural constraint of Tom's model: a single payer (the government) and a fixed, capped supply of veterans. The right adjacent vertical would share the same structure — a government payback where clients "just have to file papers the right way." His decision rule is to **optimize for the path of least change**: if a new avatar is just a swap in the ads running through the existing system, that beats onboarding a whole company. He also notes owning 60% of a market creates a near-monopoly that is itself very hard to compete against, and that the $1B figure is arbitrary. He closes by telling Tom not to run both plays at once — pick one.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) The offer Tom describes is a "great slam offer" — a client gets ~$350K over two years while doing nothing; it maxes every component of the value equation, including speed (his service beats a normal 5-year wait). _(Alex, reacting to caller's model)_
- (2026-07-19) When acquiring competitors, don't buy their back end — buy their front end (marketing and sales team). Buy the thing they're beating you on, then plug it into your own delivery rails.
- (2026-07-19) This works specifically because Tom says his back-end service delivery is "not very" hard to scale — so acquired demand can be routed into existing delivery cheaply.
- (2026-07-19) Structurally: functionally buy the marketing/sales team by buying the company, keep their front end running as-is, and connect it to your standard of care and delivery.
- (2026-07-19) There's underrated value in owning multiple brands under one parent (like many news companies), each serving a slightly different sub-segment; don't force acquired brands to change if they appeal to a different avatar.
- (2026-07-19) Short term, roll an acquired brand into a "powered by" arrangement; long term it can be swallowed into the parent brand.
- (2026-07-19) The structural weakness of Tom's business is that it has one payer (the government) and a fixed, capped number of veterans generated each year.
- (2026-07-19) An adjacent vertical worth pursuing would share the same structural components: a government payback to someone who just has to file the paperwork correctly.
- (2026-07-19) The two paths are: take over market share (adjacent expansion aside) or go adjacent — "you're either going to go adjacent or you take over market share."
- (2026-07-19) Going from 40% to 60% market share is hard, but owning 60% also makes it very hard for anyone to compete against you — it becomes a dominant, monopoly-like position.
- (2026-07-19) The $1B enterprise-value target is "100% arbitrary."
- (2026-07-19) Decision rule for choosing a growth path: optimize for the path of least change. If a new avatar is just swapping the avatar in the ads inside the existing delivery system, that beats onboarding a company.
- (2026-07-19) If no second ~$350K high-fee product exists, the path of least change might instead be a lower-fee/"student workbook" style adjacent offer — but a similar high-fee product is preferable if available.
- (2026-07-19) Don't run both plays (acquire share and build the adjacent vertical) at the same time — pick one.
- (2026-07-19) The firm sells to one avatar, one product, one time; average lifetime value ~$20,000. _(attribution: audience — caller Tom)_
- (2026-07-19) The firm does $55M revenue now, contracted to $100M in two years, and captures ~40% of the Australian market for what it does; even 60% capture would only reach ~$150M revenue. _(attribution: audience — caller Tom)_
