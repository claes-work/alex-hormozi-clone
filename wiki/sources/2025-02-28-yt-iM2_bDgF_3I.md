# How to Become a Hero by using your Pain

## Summary

In this short clip, Alex Hormozi lays out his "pain-as-fuel" framework: heroes and villains share the same backstory (pain) and differ only in what they do with it — heroes use pain, villains are used by it. He argues most high performers are driven not by passion or purpose but by insufficiency, resentment, and a chip on their shoulder, and that the move is to deliberately "twist the knife" — get specific about the painful truth and use it as activation energy rather than numbing it. He concedes the fuel is potent for roughly a decade but becomes toxic if scaled across a whole lifetime.

## Key claims (Alex's content only)

- Heroes and villains have the **same backstory — pain**; the difference is the choice: the villain hurts the world back, the hero refuses to let it hurt anyone else. **Heroes use pain; villains are used by it.** (Alex frames this as a permutation of Donald Miller's writing, with the "using pain / used by pain" half being his own addition.)
- People stuck where they don't want to be usually have **abundant pain** but waste it chasing passion/purpose ("looking for cheese") while ignoring the "cats" (their pain) chasing them.
- Staying in a bad situation (bad boss, bad job, wrong relationship) means you are **being *used by* the pain**; the alternative is to use it as something to run away from.
- The move is **"twisting the knife"**: instead of numbing pain with "painkillers," soberly confront it and commit to doing something about it — an **"alchemy"** that shortcuts years of growth into a short period.
- Passion, purpose, meaning, and joy are commonly *presumed* to be what gets people going, but the **vast majority of high performers are driven by insufficiency, resentment, bad parents/upbringings, or a chip on their shoulder** about bullies.
- This pain can be converted into **activation energy** — it **lowers your action threshold** and raises how many points you feel you have to prove.
- Proving-the-doubters-wrong is **"potent fuel"** — stronger than the mild goal of "I want to live a better life."
- Scaled over a **lifetime the fuel is toxic**, but it will **fuel you for about a decade** effectively.
- **Being specific about your pain** (and about the "cheese") is more useful than vague statements; operationalize *what exactly* twisting the knife means rather than "I hate my life."
- The things that genuinely bother us contain **an element — or almost entirely — of truth** we don't want to look at; things we have evidence against (e.g. accusations of being poor or fat when you aren't) don't sting.
- Ultimately, **twisting the knife = looking in the mirror and asking "what if they're right."**

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

> "Heroes and villains always have the same backstory: pain. The difference is what they choose to do about it. Villain says the world hurt me, I'll hurt it back. The hero says the world hurt me, I'm not going to let it hurt anybody else. Heroes use pain, villains are used by it."

> "It's the cat and the cheese. It's like we're looking for cheese, but we have all these cats behind us, and all we have to do is look and remember that they're behind us and chasing us."

> "Rather than not looking at the knife or trying to take painkillers to not feel the pain, it's like completely sobering up, taking the knife and twisting it in your own heart and being like, I'm going to do something about this."

> "Of all of the high performers that I've spoken to, the vast, vast, vast majority of them are driven by insufficiency and resentment and terrible parents or terrible upbringings or a chip on their shoulder about bullies in school. Pick your poison."

> "You can lower your action threshold and increase how many points you have to prove. 'I want to live a better life,' yeah, that sounds good. It's like, 'I want to prove the bullies that said I was a worthless piece of [expletive] in school wrong' — that's some [expletive] potent fuel."

> "I do believe that scaled over a long enough time it's toxic... but it'll fuel you for a decade pretty well."

> "I think being really specific about your pain is helpful. What is twisting the knife? How do you operationalize twisting the knife? Instead of being like, I hate my life, it's, I hate the way Andrew makes me feel when he says that I'm a piece of [expletive]."

> "The things that bother us are the ones that have an element of truth, or sometimes not an element — are comprised almost entirely of truth, and we just don't want to look at it."

> "The twisting the knife is looking in the mirror and saying, what if they're right."
