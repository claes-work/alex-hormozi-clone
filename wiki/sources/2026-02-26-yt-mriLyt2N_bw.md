# You Need to Do a This Time Study

## Summary

In this short solo clip, Hormozi describes a one-week "time study" exercise for entrepreneurs: log what you did every 15 minutes for a full week, then review the log to find which chunks of your time can be delegated or systematized. He frames the entrepreneur as a "many-hatted fractionalist" who keeps pulling tasks onto their own plate until each one grows large enough to hand off as a full-time role. Underneath the tactic he restates his foundational view that entrepreneurship is the continuous process of trading your time up for higher-value work — buying back time so profit converts to owner distributions. He closes with the "unicorn" analogy (you won't find one person who does everything you do, but you can hire three who together do it better) and a plug for his free acquisition.com 10-stage roadmap.

## Key claims

_All claims paraphrased from Alex Hormozi, dated 2026-02-26, unless quoted below._

- **The time-study mechanic (2026-02-26):** Build a simple Google sheet with time slots from ~5:00 a.m. to midnight (however long you work) in 15-minute increments. Set a kitchen/cooking timer for every 15 minutes; when it dings, write down what you did in one or two words. Do it for one full week.
- He claims it will be "the most productive week of your life" because it makes you aware of how you actually spend your time; he admits that every time he does it he thinks he should do it all the time — but doesn't.
- **Step two — the review (2026-02-26):** After a week, analyze the log to see where time concentrates (e.g. realizing 40% of your time goes to one thing), and evaluate hiring a role to take that block if the freed time would generate more value than the hire's cost.
- Then examine the next largest blocks (e.g. ~15%) and ask, per task: can I push this down / delegate it, can I build a process to correct it, or do I need to keep "eating" it until it's large enough to justify a full-time role?
- **Entrepreneur as "many-hatted fractionalist" (2026-02-26):** You keep pulling tasks onto your plate until you accumulate enough of one type to ship it out to someone full-time; the core skill is being aware of how you allocate your one scarce resource — time — for the highest return.
- **Trading time for dollars (2026-02-26):** At the most foundational level, entrepreneurship is trading your time for dollars and continuously trading up what you trade your time for.
- **The ownership hypothetical (2026-02-26):** If your business doesn't strictly need you, imagine hiring someone to spend the same hours doing your tasks; you pay them out of profit, and everything left over becomes owner distributions. A mission-driven entrepreneur then re-stacks their plate with new things and repeats the cycle.
- **The "unicorn" analogy / handling the "no one can do what I do" objection (2026-02-26):** He concedes you're right that no single hire replicates you — but you won't find a mythical unicorn; you find a white horse, a rhinoceros for the horn, and fireflies for the sparkle. You may not hire one person with everything, but you can hire three who together do it better because it's all they do with all their time. Breaking your role into partitionable pieces makes delegation more emotionally manageable.
- **Buying back time (2026-02-26):** Once you buy back the time, you can take the next step in the business.
- **Promo (2026-02-26):** Plugs a free 10-stage roadmap from $0 to $100M+ that "less than 1% of companies finish," broken down across eight business functions (with the constraint's symptoms and the steps taken to graduate), applied across software, physical products, services, and brick-and-mortar. Available at acquisition.com/roadmap.
