# How to Know If Someone Is Lying to You

## Summary

Short interview clip in which an interviewer asks Alex Hormozi how he detects when
people are fake, fraudulent, or lying. Alex says he doesn't rely on a "BS meter" but
on **asking many questions** — his ability to judge someone is proportional to his own
skill in the domain they claim expertise in. He argues people have far more practice
deceiving than others have detecting deception, so he assumes he *might* be being
deceived and only proceeds inside guardrails he'd accept even if deceived. He shares a
few personal red-flag heuristics (posed photos with expensive cars, head-to-toe
designer, not understanding how someone makes money after they explain it) while noting
they're vibe-based and industry-dependent. On business claims specifically (marketing,
sales, pricing) he trusts his judgment enough to flatly say "that doesn't make sense."
He concedes he still makes wrong picks on hires and people "all the time." On
"too good to be true," he says the saying exists for a reason and he's yet to see an
exception — citing ChatGPT as the one thing that seemed too good to be true and then
actually was real. The clip ends with a promo for his free acquisition.com 10-stage
zero-to-$100M roadmap.

## Key claims (2026-02-19, paraphrased unless quoted)

- Alex says he doesn't think he has a "BS meter"; his main tool for detecting fakes is
  **asking a lot of questions**. If he asks ~10 follow-up questions and ends up knowing
  more about the claimed topic than the claimant, he has a much better read. (paraphrase)
- ★ **Domain-relative judgment heuristic** (L3-candidate): His ability to detect
  deception is proportional to his own skill in the domain the person claims expertise
  in. On something like training a sales team, he probes ("How do you think about
  this?"); bad answers reveal they don't actually know how. Judgment of character is
  therefore **domain-specific** for him. (paraphrase)
- ★ **"Assume you're being deceived" operating stance** (L3-candidate): People have far
  more experience deceiving others than others have at protecting against deception, so
  there will always be people more skilled at deception than others are at detecting it.
  Alex therefore operates from the assumption that he doesn't know and is *probably*
  being deceived — and only proceeds if he'd still do the deal anyway within guardrails
  that protect him even if deceived. (paraphrase)
- Personal red-flag heuristics he uses (vibe-based, industry-dependent): posed photos in
  front of expensive cars; head-to-toe designer / diamond-covered watches ("bad vibes");
  and when someone describes what they do and you *still* don't understand how they make
  money even after they explain it — which has previously turned out to be someone
  siphoning money, the whole thing "smoke and mirrors." (paraphrase)
- On business-domain claims specifically (how they market, sell, price), Alex feels
  confident enough in his skill to immediately call out "no, that doesn't make sense."
  (paraphrase)
- He admits he still makes wrong picks "all the time" — on team, hires, and people — and
  frames it as part of the game rather than something he's immune to. (paraphrase)
- On "too good to be true": the saying exists for a reason; he's yet to see something
  too good to be true that held up — with **ChatGPT** as the exception that seemed too
  good to be true and then actually was real (and became a "gazillion dollar company").
  (paraphrase)
