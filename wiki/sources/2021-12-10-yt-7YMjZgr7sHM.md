# $100M CEO: "How to make the HARD decisions that change your life"

## Summary
In this solo video, Hormozi shares the decision-making framework he used during a stretch in which several decisions carried nine-figure consequences for his personal net worth. His central insight is that the problem is rarely the decision process or the data — it is the emotional and physiological *environment* in which the decision is made. He argues humans are 100% emotional decision-makers and that the goal is not to eliminate emotion but to limit how much it drives a choice, guarding especially against confirmation bias and conviction bias. His practical method is a four-step sequence — rest, eat, separate, and operate from a place of needing nothing — designed to lower the emotional influence enough to make a truly rational call. He frames this as reserved for the biggest, irreversible decisions with downstream consequences (who to marry, where to live, what to work on, major strategic business changes), and closes by inverting the framework (à la Charlie Munger) to describe how one would guarantee a terrible decision.

## Key claims
- We are "one decision away from changing our life forever" — decisions are the wellspring of life and the track-splits that determine where you end up.
- This intensive process is for the most important, irreversible decisions with downstream consequences — not for every decision.
- The bug he noticed in himself: unchanging data and a sound process still produced different outcomes on different days, which he treats as a leading indicator of a bad decision (i.e., emotion, not information, was driving it).
- Humans are 100% emotional decision-makers; the aim is to control the *extent* to which emotions control the decision, not to pretend they are absent — even "rational" decisions are emotional.
- "Mistakes love a rush decision" — emotion decreases decision speed/quality; he names this as a credo he and Leila live by.
- Two biases to fight: confirmation bias (the emotional "soup" decides what feels safe, then the logical brain hunts for supporting data) and conviction bias (the more you want the positive outcome, the more you blind yourself to protect your ego, without changing the real probability).
- You can be rational about others but not yourself — you watch other people make terrible decisions not realizing you are that person to onlookers.
- Environment step 1 — Rest: only make big decisions when extremely well-rested ("slept like a cherub").
- Environment step 2 — Eat: being well-fed raises serotonin/dopamine; the body reads physical and emotional stress the same way (a cortisol response), so eating more makes you more stress-tolerant (why people stress-eat).
- Environment step 3 — Separate: get physical space from your day-to-day; familiar rooms/desks carry emotional "anchors" from past decisions that should be removed.
- Environment step 4 — Need nothing: abundance is having everything you need; when you have enough, you don't need the decision to get you anything, so you stop caring about the outcome and can weigh it appropriately.
- The four frames stacked together disinhibit emotional influence enough to "peek our heads above our emotional cloud"; emotions still act, but secondarily.
- Inversion (attributed to Charlie Munger): to make a terrible decision, be unrested, hungry, rushed, anchored around stressors, and operating from a place of having to have the thing — so reverse all of those.
- Self-reported: he owns acquisition.com, a portfolio of companies doing about $85 million a year (dated 2021).
- He says he will explain what the actual nine-figure decision was in a future video.
