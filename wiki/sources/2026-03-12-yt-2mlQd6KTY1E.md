# How I Learn New Skills

## Summary
Interview clip (a host asks the questions; Alex answers — only Alex's words are voice data). Asked how he decides whether a new business/skill is worth his time, Alex explains that he approaches every new thing with the **consultant's perspective**, the method he learned starting his career as a management consultant. He grounds it in a story: at 22, freshly security-cleared (top-secret) and "no beard," he did classified work for the defense department focused on space, cyber, and intelligence — projects like optimizing a "mix of assets to kill the most bad guys the most efficiently." The core problem: how does a 22-year-old say something impressive and worth a **$4 million, 4-month project** to two-star and four-star generals who've done this their whole careers? The method: the project sponsor names the ~five smartest people around the problem; you interview all five, and ask each of them for *their* five smartest people; you snowball that referral loop over and over until the **same names keep recurring** ("already talked to John, already talked to Steve") — at which point you've "mapped the neural network of expertise." Then you **consolidate the notes into buckets, re-code them, distill them down, and make a pretty slideshow.** That, he says, is the TLDR of consulting, and he was the 22-year-old doing all the calls and consolidation himself. He now applies this same expert-mapping method to every new endeavor. On deciding what to learn: it depends on his first pass — some industries look "not as bad as I thought," others "way worse" — plus what problems arise and how equipped he is to solve them. His worked example is **insurance**: interesting because its biggest problem is cost of acquisition (a demand-constrained business, not capital-constrained), it has extremely high retention (people need it but historically dislike it), it's regulated (though less than banks), and it has "pretty amazing tax advantages." He buckets it as a "not now or later" opportunity where the magic comes from finding one nugget over time — "we can win on this." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
