# Helping an E-Commerce Company Doing $3M/Year Fix Their Profit

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a baby-health products company (~$3M/yr, sold wholesale to pharmacy and baby stores across Australia/New Zealand, plus Amazon Australia and D2C) is scaling the team to get out of the weeds, but profit margin has compressed to ~7% and they want to reach $5M in one to two years. Alex diagnoses it as a **money problem** and forces the owner to separate the two channels: wholesale (the majority, recurring orders from ~1,000 stockists) versus D2C (advertising-heavy). The owner keeps blaming D2C ad costs, but Alex repeatedly redirects: if wholesale is the primary revenue driver, that is where the margin is being lost, so the lever is **"prices up or costs down — or a combination of both."** Because wholesale distributors charge back ~10% of revenue into advertising and the pricing has too many "layers of the cake," Alex zeroes in on **price**: RRP has moved from $79 to $109 over ~7 years (serious since 2019, 12 years total). His core math: a **10% price increase "would more than double your profit,"** so even a worst-case Armageddon scenario — raise price 10% and lose half the business — still leaves the company **making more money** because the profit-per-unit more than doubles. He addresses the owner's real fear — in Australia you **can't enforce MSRP** (recommended retail only), so rogue distributors and pharmacies undercut the $110 price down to $79 at 2% margin, destroying brand price integrity. Alex reframes that as an argument *for* raising price: higher prices force the channel to raise price, and the stockists who drop you because they can no longer undercut solve the price-integrity problem for you — **"kill two birds with one stone."** The owner reveals they've just (last three weeks) pulled out of the main Australian pharmacy distributor to regain pricing control, walking away from thousands of units but reclaiming control of RRP so they can then raise D2C prices. Alex's closing counsel: **"just be bold either way"** — either path can be made to work and narrated as the right call, so commit. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a squeezed-margin e-commerce business (~7% profit) as fundamentally a **money problem** (of the "Mozi 6"), not a customer-acquisition problem.
- As of March 2026, Alex insists on **separating wholesale from D2C** before diagnosing: since wholesale is the majority of revenue, that channel — not D2C ad spend — is where the profitability is being lost, so it must be fixed first.
- As of March 2026, the two margin levers are **"prices up or costs down, or a combination of both"**; for this business the primary lever is raising price.
- As of March 2026, Alex's core math: raising price ~10% **"would more than double your profit"** because the increase drops almost entirely to the bottom line on a thin-margin product.
- As of March 2026 (worst-case reasoning): even if a 10% price raise caused you to **lose half the business**, you would **still make more money**, because per-unit profit more than doubled — and you would also recapture margin on the D2C side.
- As of March 2026, because Australian law allows only **recommended retail price (RRP/MSRP), not enforceable pricing**, handing product to a distributor sacrifices brand price control — thousands of stockists can undercut the intended price (e.g. selling a $110 product at $79 for ~2% margin).
- As of March 2026, raising price is also a **brand-control move**: higher prices force the channel to raise prices, and stockists who drop the product rather than stop undercutting solve the price-integrity problem — **"kill two birds with one stone."**
- As of March 2026, Alex's meta-advice on a hard channel decision (stay with vs. exit a distributor): **be bold either way** — either choice can be made to work and later told as the right call, so the decisiveness matters more than the branch chosen.
