# Industry Averages Are Dumb. Here's Why

## Summary
Alex Hormozi argues that benchmarking a business against industry averages is a recipe for mediocrity, since the "average" of any category (like the average American) is unremarkable. He advocates ignoring averages entirely and instead playing to win, treating physics as the only real constraint on what outcomes are possible. He illustrates the point with a friend whose complex-machinery business runs net 70% margins, and frames a business fundamentally as a "black box" that transforms cheap raw inputs into higher-value outputs.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims industry averages are a poor benchmark because the average of anything is mediocre; comparing yourself to the average is "a great way to be average."
- As of March 2026, he argues quoting an average to say something is "good enough" means you have accepted you will stop trying to get better — which he wholeheartedly rejects.
- As of March 2026, he claims category winners do not look at the industry average because they don't care about it.
- As of March 2026, he asserts the only rule that ultimately matters is physics: if physics allows an outcome to exist, there is no reason a business cannot achieve it.
- As of March 2026, he cites a friend in complex machinery running net 70% margins by selling automation machines for ~$400,000 that cost ~$17,000 to build, driven by knowhow — offered as proof that manufacturing margins far above 10% are possible.
- As of March 2026, he defines a business as a "black box" that transforms raw inputs into an output whose value exceeds the inputs, and frames this value-creation as how civilization advances.
- This clip presents part of what he calls his "12 rules of thumb" / ratios used as guideposts in business.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Industry averages are dumb."

> "If the average American is in debt, divorced twice, overweight, and just mid, right? Why would I want to have that be my bar to compare myself against?"

> "You want to measure yourself by the same stick that your competition measures themselves. Well, it's a great way to be average."

> "I would highly encourage you to just ignore averages altogether and play to win."

> "When you quote an average to say this is good enough, you've accepted that you are no longer going to try to get better. And I just wholeheartedly reject that."

> "The winner of every category is not the industry average. And I can almost promise you that they don't look at the industry average, because why would they care?"

> "There is only like one rule that matters, which is physics. As long as the rules of physics allow it to exist, there's no reason that we cannot get this outcome that we desire. Period."

> "He will charge $400,000 and a machine will cost him 17 grand because he has knowhow."

> "A business is functionally a black box that transforms raw materials into an output where the value is higher than the inputs. That's it. That's all a business does."
