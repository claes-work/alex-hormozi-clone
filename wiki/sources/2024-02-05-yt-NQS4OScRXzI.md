# Alex Hormozi on Staying Motivated Without Anger

## Summary

In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi describes how he stopped being driven by a
"chip on his shoulder" toward his father and by the desire to out-earn him. He
recounts a phone call from his father — who apologized "for everything" while Alex
was taking home about $1.5M/month in his late 20s — and how Alex felt "nothing" in
the moment. He frames his growth as realizing he had been trying to win his father's
money-based game rather than defining and playing his own game, which he presents as
the more mature but higher-responsibility path than motivation fueled by resentment.

## Key claims

- Motivation-without-anger / evolution from revenge: Alex says anger and resentment
  ("alchemizing a toxic thing into something useful") can fuel you, but seeking
  external approval is a moving target — "the approval that I sought was always going
  to be moved." (attribution: Alex; paraphrase)
- His goals escalated in stages tied to his father: first "make as much as my dad,"
  then "make more than my dad," then "make more than my dad had ever made." He realized
  each was still measuring himself by his father's yardstick. (attribution: Alex)
- Turning point: a father's phone call (Alex was ~27, taking home ~$1.5M/month after
  Gym Launch was "printing money"). His dad said "you're going to want to sit down for
  this... I'm sorry... everything." Alex felt nothing, found that curious, and instead
  of accepting the olive branch, told his dad he wouldn't thank him at award shows
  "cuz you weren't [there] and you didn't believe in me." (attribution: Alex; self-reported)
- Cultural framing: Alex notes it is "fairly common in most foreign families to be
  very money driven"; his dad would introduce people by immediately stating how much
  they made, tying worth and name together. (attribution: Alex; self-reported)
- The core reframe: dropping blame isn't enough — you can stop blaming someone yet
  still be "playing his game" and "winning someone else's" game rather than your own.
  The next level is defining your own game, its rules, and what matters most, which
  carries more personal responsibility. (attribution: Alex)
- Relationship status update: "he's a good guy... we're fine now," distinguishing the
  present relationship from how he felt growing up. (attribution: Alex)

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

> "First the goal was make as much as my dad, then it was make more than my dad, then it was make more than my dad had ever made — and I realized that the approval that I sought was always going to be moved."

> "He says, hey, you're going to want to sit down for this... he's like, I'm sorry. I was like, about what? He was like, everything."

> "I remember in the moment actually feeling nothing, and thinking that was curious."

> "I probably should have just accepted it for the olive branch that he was probably trying to lean out to me, but here's what I said instead."

> "You know how people get up on stage when they win the awards and they're like, I just want to thank my mom and dad for always being there, always believing in me? I was like, I'm not going to say that — cuz you weren't, and you didn't believe in me."

> "Everything that I'd done to that point was to try and beat him at his game."

> "I don't blame my dad anymore, but I'm still playing his game, and so I'm winning — not my game — I'm winning someone else's."

> "Then I have to define the game and the meaning of the game that I want to play. I have more responsibility now, because I have to define the rules of what matters most to me."
