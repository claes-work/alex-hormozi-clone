# Helping a Law Firm Hire More Talent

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a ~$15M/year law firm (31 lawyers; real estate and business litigation, with a niche representing homeowners against HOAs) says his only real constraint is **talent acquisition** — he has more demand than he can serve and can only add about two attorneys a quarter. Alex reframes the whole problem as arithmetic. He walks the numbers live: each attorney generates ~$600k gross profit on a ~$200k salary; the firm needs ~20 more lawyers to saturate demand; that hire costs ~$1.2M in acquisition and would add ~$12M revenue / ~$4M profit; at an ~8x multiple that's ~$32M in "tax-free enterprise value." His conclusion: this is "a pure resource issue," not an economics problem, so the answer is simply **volume** — triple (or 10x) the recruiting effort. Concretely: don't run two or three recruiters, spin up ~10; go **up-market** on the recruiting firms you engage (recruiters exist "all the way up to million-dollar-a-year recruiters"); and multiply outbound reach-outs 10-20x rather than only reaching out when you happen to stumble on a candidate. He diagnoses the first bottleneck as a **metrics gap** — the client tracks no outbound recruiting numbers. As a proof case he cites an Acquisition.com portfolio company that needed to stand up a 50-person sales team and **hired 90 people in 12 weeks** by contracting **15 recruiting firms** at once and doing bulk wholesale deals ("give us five people, discounted rate") with replacement guarantees if a hire didn't work out. He also deploys a motivational reframe (imagine you must hire 20 elite attorneys in 30 days or lose something you love) to expose how much more aggressively the owner would act if forced. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex treats a hiring bottleneck in a profitable, demand-rich business as a **pure resource/volume problem**, not an economics problem: "spin up three times the recruiters" and multiply outreach.
- As of March 2026, he frames hiring decisions by unit economics: an attorney worth ~$600k gross profit per year on ~$200k salary, at ~30% cost-of-talent-acquisition (~$60k per hire), makes each hire an obvious ROI — ~$1.2M to hire 20 lawyers to generate ~$12M revenue / ~$4M added profit, worth ~$32M in enterprise value at an ~8x multiple.
- As of March 2026, he argues you should decide how many people you need by working backward from **saturation** (the headcount that fully meets current demand), then measure how many years behind your current hire rate leaves you.
- As of March 2026, the first thing he looks for is **recruiting metrics** — how much outbound outreach you do — and a business that only reaches out "when we find the right candidate" has no metric and therefore no lever to pull.
- As of March 2026, his recruiting-scale playbook: run far more recruiters than feels normal (10, not 2-3), go **up-market** to higher-caliber recruiting firms for high-touch talent, and increase outbound reach-outs 10-20x.
- As of March 2026, he recommends contracting **many recruiting firms in parallel** and cutting **bulk wholesale deals** (buy five placements at a discount) with **replacement guarantees** if a hire underperforms within an extended window — the tactic a portfolio company used to hire **90 people in 12 weeks** for a 50-seat sales team.
- As of March 2026, he holds that idle new hires are expensive: in that portfolio-company push they spent "a few hundred thousand a day" on traffic, so any day recruits weren't productive lost "multiple hundreds of thousands a day" — meaning you must maintain a quality bar even while hiring at volume.
- As of March 2026, he distinguishes hard cases from easy ones: if the firm had cash-flow problems he'd first fix the model and pricing ("four other steps"), but with $4M in profit already, the only move is to buy more recruiting capacity.
