# The Choice | How To Improve Decision Making Skills (2021-02-25)

## Summary

Prompted by a Clubhouse discussion, Hormozi argues that the hardest part of
entrepreneurship (and life) is the disconnect between the right decision and the right
outcome: you can make a good decision and still get a bad result. He treats the
decision-making process itself — your "picker" — as an entrepreneur's most valuable
asset, and warns against revising a sound decision framework based on a single
unlucky outcome.

## Key claims

- (2021) Your decision-making process is your most valuable asset — it's your "picker"
  that chooses your partner, business, pricing, mentors, and what to learn first. A
  billionaire stripped of money (e.g. Elon Musk) can rebuild it because the
  decision-making remains.
- (2021) The core method: judge a decision on its quality independent of its outcome.
  A good decision can produce a bad outcome, and vice versa — separate the two.
- (2021) Consistency test for changing your framework: if you'd change a past decision
  to capture one winner, you must apply that same altered framework to every other case
  — and the net effect (all the losers it would also have produced) would likely make
  you lose overall. He cites the Bitcoin example: passing on it and watching it rise
  doesn't prove the decision was wrong.
- (2021) You can't use information you have today to re-judge a past decision, because
  then it's a "different person" deciding with data they didn't have access to;
  claiming the past decision was wrong on that basis is "poisonous."
- (2021) The legitimate signal to revise your decision calculus: repeating the *same*
  mistake from *poor calculations* over and over — not a single bad outcome.
- (2021) Baseball analogy: even the best batter (a ~.350 average) strikes out roughly
  two of three at-bats; a great swing still fails most of the time. Don't reset your
  baseline swing after every strikeout — apply the same swing across enough at-bats and
  the home runs on the right decisions outweigh the wrongs.
- (2021) Risk caveat: never "bet the farm" / put everything on black when you can't
  afford to lose, even while keeping a consistent framework.
- Attribution note: Hormozi credits Ray Dalio's *Principles* as where he first heard the
  idea of passing on an opportunity, seeing it win, and needing to apply the same
  framework consistently; and cites Warren Buffett's reasoning that Bitcoin is
  speculative (value only from resale, no intrinsic value/production). These are cited
  by Alex; the persona-relevant claims are his own framing above.
