# I Run Soccer Tournaments. How Do I Scale?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The founder runs a national 5v5 soccer-tournament events business: ~$1.5M revenue, ~30% margins (~$500k profit), growing 50%/year for five years, with a goal of $15M in four years. He hired an operator early and is now out of the day-to-day, feeling under-utilized at the "leadership level" and unsure what to do. The model: they partner with a **local soccer club** (which provides fields and helps market) to set up a tournament, then **teams** register and pay — so *clubs are the first input*, teams the second. Operations could already run 1,000 tournaments; the true bottleneck is elsewhere. Alex walks his **constraint-diagnosis** routine: the business already has the metrics, the market, the model, sufficient money (30% margins), and sufficient leads ("super qualified people all day every day," ads just started). What it lacks is **conversion capacity** — the president is also the sole salesperson and can only take ~five sales calls a day, so lead flow exceeds selling capacity. Therefore the single constraint is **manpower on sales**, and the founder's highest-leverage job is to build an **acquisition channel for salespeople**: recruiting, career path, compensation, onboarding, and management — a repeatable "sales team" machine. Alex frames it playfully in the client's own language ("5v5… you got five here and five there and you can compete… I hold tryouts"). His bottom line for a bored founder: spend all your time doing outreach to recruit salespeople so they can sell more of your stuff — if you had 5x the salespeople and could deliver, the business would grow ~5x as fast. Ends with the standard $100M scaling-roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses scaling by checking the pillars in sequence — metrics, market, model, money, leads, conversions, manpower — and isolating the one that is actually binding rather than fixing everything at once.
- As of March 2026, this business's binding constraint is conversion capacity, not leads: lead flow already exceeds selling capacity because a single seller (the president) can only run ~five sales calls a day.
- As of March 2026, when a founder is out of the day-to-day and "bored," his highest-leverage work is building the acquisition channel for the constrained role — here, recruiting salespeople — not sticking his hand back into operations.
- As of March 2026, "models work for a reason": if the model already produces 50%/year growth at 30% margins, you scale by relieving the constraint, not by changing the model.
- As of March 2026, building a sales team is itself a system with defined parts — an acquisition/outreach channel for reps plus career path, compensation, onboarding, and management.
- As of March 2026 (this business's mechanics): the primary input is partner clubs (they supply fields and marketing), teams are recruited from those clubs and are who pays — so clubs must be secured first.
- As of March 2026, Alex's rough sizing logic: if you had 5x the salespeople and operations could deliver, the business would grow roughly 5x as fast — making seller headcount the direct growth lever.
- As of March 2026, whether the president should stop selling or keep selling is a real choice: he is great at it (which is why he does it), but his selling is also the constraint, so the fix is adding sellers around him, not necessarily removing him.
