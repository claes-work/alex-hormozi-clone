# Alex Hormozi Describes the Biggest Difference Between the Rich and The Poor

/ **id:** yt-XclY0Yl8u9M · **published:** 2024-03-05 · **channel:** [[../entities/acqofficial|@ACQofficial]]

## Summary

In this clip Alex Hormozi argues that the single greatest differentiator across wealth classes is how people perceive time. He frames money as "a condensed unit of time," so mastering time is equivalent to mastering money, and claims you can gauge someone's wealth by the time horizons they speak in (today vs. this week vs. this decade). He connects this to intention — building a company because it should exist rather than to make money this week extends the builder's time horizon — and to the discipline of choosing global benefit over local benefit (delayed vs. instant gratification). Along the way he cites the marshmallow test and names three traits he associates with the ultra successful. The conversation is with a podcast host (identified in-clip via "when I launched School of Greatness 10 years ago," i.e. Lewis Howes); only Alex-attributed material below is persona data.

## Key claims

- The single greatest differentiator between the poor, middle class, rich, and truly wealthy is **how they see time**. (Alex)
- Money is "a condensed unit of time" — time and money are almost equivalent units, tradeable for each other; those who master their time master their money. (Alex)
- **Time horizon** is the perspective from which you see what you want to achieve; building from "this problem is worth solving" produces a different company than building from "I need to make money this week." (Alex)
- You can estimate how wealthy someone is by the time horizons they speak in — today vs. this week/month/quarter/year/decade. (Alex)
- Extending your time horizon happens either by shifting the intention you build from (service / "this needs to exist") or by being "unbelievably self-disciplined" — the former is easier. (Alex)
- Ultra-successful entrepreneurs have a "graveyard" of ~10 failed business ideas; stop waiting for the perfect idea and just start notching off the bad ones. (Alex)
- Most people fail because they **sacrifice global benefit for local benefit** — instant gratification (eating the cake) over delayed gratification (the six-pack); the same local-vs-global tradeoff plays out inside organizations (e.g. salespeople skipping CRM notes that other departments need). (Alex)
- Three common factors of the most successful: an inflated sense of self (they believe they deserve/can achieve big things), a sense of inferiority (never good enough), and impulse control. (Alex)
- Self-reported: when launching, Alex sold a prior company, had ~two years of runway, lived frugally, slept on couches to save, and only started paying for hotel rooms about four or five years ago. (Alex, self-reported)
- The host's account of launching "School of Greatness" 10 years ago with a one-year, no-money-intention mindset is **not Alex** — attributed to the interviewer (Lewis Howes).
