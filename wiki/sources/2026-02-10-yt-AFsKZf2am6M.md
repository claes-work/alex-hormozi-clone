# How Small Brands Should Think About Content Strategy

## Summary
In a short live Q&A on @MoreMozi, a creator with a "super small" brand asks whether they should prioritize more volume or put more work into each individual piece of content. Alex answers that the volume/quality tradeoff is not a fixed choice but an "accordion" that oscillates over time: a creator making many pieces eventually notices that one piece drove ~80% of the output, cuts down to fewer higher-quality pieces (keeping total output roughly constant), gets better, expands the count back up at that new quality bar, spots an even better outlier, and cuts back down again — repeating the compress/expand cycle. He warns that maximal high volume is the domain of news channels, whose content is made for them but has near-zero long tail because it goes irrelevant once the day or week passes; for educators, quality matters more than almost anything else. His key point for beginners is that you cannot know what "quality" is without first doing enough volume to build a dataset — once you have, say, 100 videos where 10 outperformed the other 90, you can ask what was different and actually learn. Therefore he prescribes volume first, because reps generate the data you iterate on. The clip ends with a plug for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-10) The asker describes their brand as "super small" and asks whether to do more volume versus putting more work into each piece of content. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-10) The volume-versus-quality decision is an "accordion" — it expands and compresses over time rather than being a one-time choice.
- (2026-02-10) The cycle: after making a lot of content, a creator realizes one piece out of several was responsible for ~80% of the output/views; they then cut down to fewer, better pieces while keeping total output roughly the same; they improve; they expand the count back up at the new quality level; they spot an even-better outlier; and they cut back down again — repeating.
- (2026-02-10) Maximal high-volume publishing (e.g., a post every hour) is essentially the news-channel model; those channels do maximize on volume, but the content's long tail is near zero because it becomes irrelevant once the day/week is over.
- (2026-02-10) News channels have a structural advantage because "the content is made for them" — i.e., events supply their material.
- (2026-02-10) For educators, quality matters "almost more than anything," in contrast to the news/volume model.
- (2026-02-10) A creator can't actually know what quality is until they've done enough volume and repetitions to see what works and what doesn't.
- (2026-02-10) Alex prescribes volume as the first thing to do because it builds a large dataset you can act on.
- (2026-02-10) Concrete illustration: with 100 videos where 10 did better than the other 90, you can ask "what was different?" and learn from it; without that data it's very hard to learn anything.
- (2026-02-10) Hence his standard advice to beginners is simply to do more in the beginning.
