# Helping an Electrical Contractor Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. An electrical contractor doing ~$1.6M/year (keeping ~$650k) wants to reach ~$5M, but names his constraint as himself plus hiring quality candidates: he can handle current volume only when his whole crew is present — "as soon as one guy is gone, I'm back in the van." Alex diagnoses this as a **multi-step (second-/third-order) problem**, not a demand problem: the contractor has enough business, so the fix is to **raise price to fund redundancy in the team**. The chain of causation Alex lays out: bump price → generate cash flow → hire and backfill extra techs → build two or three backups per role → owner stays *above* the business instead of dropping back into the van. To justify a 20–40% price increase when he's already slightly above a cheaper market, Alex routes it through **offer improvement plus a guarantee**: make the service faster, more reliable, or easier, then guarantee it — "if we don't meet any of these qualifications, I'll give you all my profit back." Because almost no competitor offers a real guarantee (they won't put "teeth in the agreement" since they're not confident they'll be on time and on budget), the guarantee both differentiates and lets him charge the premium while the downside is merely reverting to today's price. On customer pushback about cost, Alex is dismissive: the contractor's HVAC clients have complained about price for six years yet keep buying — "I care more about what people do than what they say"; customers will *always* want it cheaper, faster, and guaranteed, so don't let complaints move your pricing. Second diagnosis: the contractor has **no marketing function** and is dangerously dependent on six or seven heating-and-cooling companies who feed him electrical work — they hold all the leverage (his "whales"), so when they call with a job tomorrow he drops everything. Alex's fix: start outreach to *other* HVAC companies ("let me quote some of these jobs for you… keep them honest"), which wins new business and gives him negotiating leverage against existing partners. Ends with the roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a stuck owner-operator ("as soon as one guy is gone, I'm back in the van") as a **redundancy problem**: you need two or three backups for every role so the owner can keep doing his own job, and that requires the cash flow to sustain multiple techs.
- As of March 2026, when demand is not the constraint, the lever is price: bump price → create cash flow → hire the extra person → build team redundancy → owner stays above the business.
- As of March 2026, most real business problems are **second- or third-order** — "if it were one thing, you probably would have already seen it" — so you reverse-engineer back from a downstream fix (price) to the core problem (owner trapped in delivery).
- As of March 2026, to justify a higher price, improve the offer first: make the thing faster, more reliable, or easier, then guarantee it — and if so, raise prices by 20–40%.
- As of March 2026, his guarantee structure: "if we don't meet these qualifications, I'll give you all my profit back" — a raised price where a miss reverts you to your current price, but on every job you fulfill you "get all the juice."
- As of March 2026, guarantees are a durable differentiator because almost no competitor offers one — rivals won't put "teeth in the agreement" since they aren't confident they'll be on time and on budget; a promise with no accountability is worthless.
- As of March 2026, Alex discounts customer price complaints: his HVAC clients complained about cost for six years but kept buying — "I care more about what people do than what they say." Customers will always want it cheaper, faster, and guaranteed; that's constant and shouldn't move your pricing.
- As of March 2026, having no marketing function and depending on six or seven partner companies is a **leverage risk** — the partners hold all the leverage, and the owner drops everything the moment a "whale" calls.
- As of March 2026, his fix for partner concentration: proactively do outreach to other HVAC companies ("let me quote some of these jobs… keep them honest"), which brings in independent business and creates negotiating leverage with existing partners. To get more whales, you must first tweak price to fund the manpower.
