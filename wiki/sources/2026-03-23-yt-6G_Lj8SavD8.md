# The 3 Core Roles Every Company Needs

## Summary
Responding to a business owner's question about the three key people every company needs beyond the CEO/founder, Alex Hormozi reframes the question from *people* to *functions* that must occur in any business. He names three: promotion/sales (getting customers), product/service (building the thing well), and operations (running the day-to-day, leading and recruiting the team). He then argues that which role to hire for depends on whether a company is supply- or demand-constrained, and that businesses should alternate focus between the two constraints rather than address both at once.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes "the three key people" as three business *functions* that must happen whether one person or twenty does them: (1) promotion — advertising and conversion, i.e. getting customers; (2) product/service — building and keeping the offering exceptional; (3) operations — running day-to-day, leading, building culture, recruiting, and setting standards.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that one person can rarely do all three functions well; it is easier to find three (sometimes up to six) people, each with varying overlap across the buckets.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes his own split as strongest at product and advertising, with operations his weakest, while Leila's primary strength is operations (also strong at advertising, with millions of followers, and able to do product).
- As of March 2026, Alex advises deciding which role to hire by finding the deficiency in each company and checking whether that deficiency ties to the business's current constraint; a missing advertising skill only matters if the company is demand-constrained.
- As of March 2026, Alex asserts a business is, for the most part, either supply- or demand-constrained at any time; you fix the active constraint and ignore the other side until the constraint flips, alternating between the two in a rhythm.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "It's not like what are the three key people, so I could just be like I'll reject the premise. It's more so what are the functions? What things must occur in a business — and whether one person does them or 20 people do them, these things have to happen."
> "At the most foundational level, you have to have somebody who can promote — somebody who can advertise, let people know about the stuff. Now that also technically includes conversion. So that one person who goes and gets customers."
> "The other person is the person who builds the thing — who's the one who's at head of product or the service, making sure that it continues to be exceptional."
> "And then the third kind of function is who's running the day-to-day? Who's operating? Who's leading the team? Who's continuing to basically build the culture? Who's the one who's recruiting the people in, setting the standards for how the company runs."
> "We still always start with: are we supply or demand constrained? And once we know which one we are — cuz you really can for the most part just be one of the two — we basically ignore everything else on the other side."
> "Leila and I joke, which is like one of us is always killing ourselves. Never both of us at the same time."
