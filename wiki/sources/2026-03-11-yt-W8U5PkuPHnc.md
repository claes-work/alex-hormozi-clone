# Did My Childhood Make Me the Way I Am?

## Summary
Short clip. An off-camera interviewer opens by asking whether Alex's childhood shaped him into who he is, observing that he seems both very compassionate and very tough (on himself and sometimes others). Alex's answer is a pointed rejection of the standard childhood-trauma-to-success narrative. He notes he's fielded this question in various forms across several podcasts — "the struggle growing up… and how that creates success now" — and argues the causal story doesn't hold: plenty (in fact "way more") people have terrible childhoods and do nothing with them, so pointing back to a hard childhood to explain present success is unreliable. He's careful to say he did **not** have a terrible childhood — just "tough times." He claims the "why" of anyone's behavior is genuinely unanswerable ("I don't think anyone can answer the question"). Instead he offers a behaviorist self-model: he does what he does because he's been **reinforced** for it. The only "rule or law of humanity" he sees is reward/punishment — you avoid what you've been punished for and keep doing what you've been rewarded for. He explains his relentless work ethic this way: his **minimum standard keeps rising**, and the higher he raises that minimum, the better his outcomes get, which reinforces working even harder — a self-reinforcing loop rather than a childhood wound.
