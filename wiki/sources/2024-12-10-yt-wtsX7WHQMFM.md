# How the Top 1% ACTUALLY Make Their Money

## Summary

Solo whiteboard-style talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out a three-layer model of how the top 0.1% create wealth. Layer 1 (global): countries get richer by reinvesting in **education** and **technology**, both of which increase output per unit of time. Layer 2 (individual): the skills that generate production are **Build, Sell, Lead (BSL)**, all multiplied by **Risk**. Layer 3 (environmental): the money BSL produces is either wasted, stored, or multiplied — the top 0.1% multiply it by **buying** assets or **lending**, with the highest-return investment being oneself (buying skills).

## Key claims

- The top 0.1% wealth story has three layers: Global (education + technology), Individual (Build/Sell/Lead × Risk), and Environmental (what you do with the money — waste, store, or multiply).
- Country wealth is measured by GDP per capita; increases can be tied almost directly to reinvestment in education and technology (cites Luxembourg ~$151k, Singapore ~$148k as highest per-capita examples).
- Singapore treats teachers as prestigious, well-paid positions because attracting good teachers keeps the country competitive long-term.
- Every person/country has the same base inputs — time and bodies (24 hours each); technology lets you do more of what you do, do it better, and lowers the skill requirement to do anything.
- The three individual production skills are Build (mechanical), Sell (verbal), Lead (social) — "the tripod of business." You must have all three; how far you develop each dictates how much money you make.
- Each skill creates leverage: build once and many use it, learn to sell and sell many at once, learn to lead and get many people acting on your behalf.
- Building and selling usually come first; leadership develops over time (in the beginning you have almost no one to lead).
- Zuckerberg example: started mostly with build skills, added promotion baked into the product, then developed leadership over time.
- Build tactics referenced from *$100M Offers*: trimming and stacking, the "delivery cube," and solving problems psychologically not just logically (Rory Sutherland's slow-elevator/mirrors example).
- The problem-solution cycle (from the *Leads* book): solving one problem creates the next (pretzel → thirsty → dessert stomach); good builders develop an ecosystem over time (Apple's closed end-to-end ecosystem).
- Selling covers everything that moves someone from unaware to paying — advertising, marketing, sales, branding, promotion, conversion. The "call out" (getting attention) is the most important part.
- Cites David Ogilvy: "after you've written your headline you've spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar." Framing/first impression shifts perception most.
- Call outs range from hyper-specific (one person's name) to broad (dropped tray); make them specific enough to get the right people, broad enough to get as many as possible. Start narrow, expand over time (Facebook: college → high school → adults).
- To learn what customers want to hear: ask them, and read competitor user reviews for pain points.
- Leadership is a meta-skill — it grants access to everyone else's skills; the best leader could recruit the best builder and seller. "Steve Jobs didn't build the iPhone, he built the team that built the iPhone."
- Every new hire is a guaranteed cost but not a guaranteed return; unless they build, sell, or support building/selling, they are waste.
- All three skills are multiplied by Risk — the risk you're willing to take on (equity/ownership). Selling shares reduces risk and upside: Jobs' original 15% of Apple would be worth ~$465B today; Bill Gates' ~49% of Microsoft would make him worth ~$1.5 trillion had he not diversified.
- Leadership tactic: eliminate wasted communication. Reframe conversations to "what behavior do you want us to start or stop?" — if communication doesn't change behavior, it was wasted.
- "Just because you can measure it doesn't mean it changes anything" — research/understanding only pays off if it changes what you do.
- Seek clarity and truth rather than agreement; if you don't understand someone, assume no one knows what's going on rather than assuming you're dumb.
- Alex started with selling; it's a chicken-and-egg — you need something to sell, then you must sell it, then you recruit and learn leadership. His first business: no CRM, no website, just his email and phone number sending personalized nutrition and workout plans.
- Business growth is limited by product quality/offerings, ability to sell (not enough leads), or both; identify the constraint to know where to reinvest time.
- Technology interweaves with all three: build faster/at scale (a developer's app vs. a table maker), sell one-to-many (personal brand, ads), lead via project management and predictive analytics. Co-pilot lets coders write code ~10x faster.
- Alex admits he has personally been more hesitant to use technology and thinks it has limited his success; he's now spending more time incorporating it into products, sales, and leadership/recruiting.
- Skills/education can never be confiscated, stolen, or seized — unlike assets. Tied to his family's Iranian revolution experience: assets were seized, but relatives with skills rebuilt while those with only assets struggled.
- Layer 3: money is wasted, stored (gold — a store of value that produces nothing; Buffett's ball-of-gold vs. all US farmland + ~13 ExxonMobils example), or multiplied by buying (owning a % of others' BSL) or lending (selling money for more money later via interest).
- This is why he built Acquisition.com the way he did: companies generate money that reinvests into the company or buys another money-generating company, compounding.
- Investment levels: buy a piece of a company (higher risk), hire people into a company (less risk), or invest in yourself (lowest risk) — and investing in yourself also builds the skills to do everything else better.
- "You will always be the best investment." Always spend as much as you can buying the most valuable skill currently constraining your growth. Buying skills = paying someone to give feedback on an activity until you can replicate their outcome without them.
- References his leverage video as the top video on his channel / a follow-up.
