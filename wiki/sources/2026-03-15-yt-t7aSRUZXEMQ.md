# Helping a Dentist Hire Better Talent

## Summary
Live workshop clip. A dentist running four dental offices (fifth opening next month) at $15M/year, ~$4.5M profit, wants to triple to ~$50M in about two years. His stated constraint is **manpower** — he needs trained doctors (specifically orthodontists) who fit the culture, and leads run parallel. Alex reframes the whole problem with his signature move: **treat hiring as an acquisition-math problem identical to customer acquisition.** He argues that all businesses are ultimately *demand constrained* — even a supply-constrained one is "demand constrained on the talent side" — so the customer LTV:CAC ratio has a mirror on the talent side: **lifetime gross profit per employee ÷ cost to acquire that employee.** He walks the dentist through the numbers: an orthodontist generates ~$400k gross profit/year and sticks ~6 years (≈$2.4M lifetime), yet the dentist currently pays $0 to acquire one (relying on referrals) — which, Alex says, is exactly why he's stuck. He pushes the willing acquisition spend up live ($50k → $100k → "let's do 200"), showing that even $50k–$100k to land a $400k/year asset is an 8:1 return before compounding. Because the client wants five orthodontists across new locations *at the same time*, Alex's prescription is to spend aggressively and in volume on recruiting channels — headhunters, agencies, orthodontist forums/communities (pay the community owner to let you post) — and to accept a temporarily high CAC that pays back a year later. He reframes the trade two ways: "you either pay in profit or in growth rate" (want it slower? pay less), and the enterprise-value angle — a dental group trading at ~10x means a $250k recruiting spend that adds ~$1M EBITDA can create ~$10M of enterprise value, "the cash is just gravy." His canonical proof story: a portfolio company that scaled from $2M/year to $10M/month in ~18 months brought sales in-house in 90 days, hiring 80 and keeping 50, by paying 10 recruiting agencies $10k/head (~$500k total) to stand up a team that added $5M/month — "great trade." Core mindset line for supply-constrained owners: recruiting is the constraint, so "let's bomb the hell out of it." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (excluded from claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues all businesses are ultimately **demand constrained** — a supply-constrained business is simply "demand constrained on the talent side."
- As of March 2026, he frames a talent metric mirroring customer LTV:CAC: **lifetime (or annual) gross profit per employee ÷ cost of acquiring that employee** — hiring should be run on the same acquisition math as customers.
- As of March 2026, he holds you should be willing to pay far more than $0 to acquire high-value talent: an orthodontist producing ~$400k gross profit/year (≈$2.4M over a ~6-year tenure) justifies paying $50k–$200k to acquire, an 8:1 or better return.
- As of March 2026, he says relying on free referral hiring ("it costs me nothing") is precisely *why* owners stay stuck — paying $0 to recruit caps growth.
- As of March 2026, his recruiting-as-advertising stance: when talent is the constraint, attack it with volume and spend across channels — headhunters, agencies, industry forums/communities (pay the community owner for the right to post/recruit).
- As of March 2026, the core trade-off: "you either pay in profit or in growth rate" — accept a temporarily high CAC (payback ~a year later) to pull growth forward, or pay less and grow slower.
- As of March 2026, "you can't sell doctor time you don't have" — solve the supply/talent constraint before adding leads.
- As of March 2026, the enterprise-value multiplier: for a group trading at ~10x, a ~$250k recruiting investment adding ~$1M EBITDA can create ~$10M of enterprise value — "the cash is just gravy."
- As of March 2026 (proof example): a portfolio company grew from $2M/year to $10M/month in ~18 months, then insourced sales in 90 days — hiring 80, keeping 50 — by paying 10 recruiting agencies ~$10k/head (~$500k) to add $5M/month.
