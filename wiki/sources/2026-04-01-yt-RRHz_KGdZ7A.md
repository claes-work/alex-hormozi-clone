# The #1 Habit That Fixed My Relationship With Money

## Summary

Alex recommends checking one's bank balance before social media, especially when doing
so produces anxiety. He presents measurement as the first behavioral intervention: regular
exposure creates awareness, changes small decisions, and can later shift to longer review
intervals when daily asset movements become uninformative.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says he once avoided reviewing his money because it produced
  anxiety, then adopted a daily bank-account check after a friend told him to look.
- He applies the attributed Peter Drucker maxim that what gets measured gets managed and
  claims measurement alone can begin improving a behavior by increasing awareness.
- His examples are daily weighing before changing diet or exercise and daily balance
  review before changing spending; no supporting research is cited in the source.
- He recommends confronting an uncomfortable metric rather than using discomfort as a
  reason to avoid it, because awareness influences conscious and subconscious choices.
- Alex self-reports checking daily from balances in the hundreds or thousands of dollars
  until he had about $20 million, when daily market fluctuations obscured useful progress.
- At that point, he moved to monthly or quarterly review, extending the gratification
  horizon from daily saving to longer-term asset growth.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Check your bank account before you check social media first thing in the morning."

> "Look at it. Confront reality, and then reality will start to improve."

> "The first thing that you can do to improve any metric for any skill is to actually measure it."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: exposure to an avoided metric as behavior design; measurement cadence should lengthen when daily variance stops being informative; self-reported $20 million threshold.
