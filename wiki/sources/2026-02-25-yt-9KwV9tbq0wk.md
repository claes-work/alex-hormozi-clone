# "'How Do I Sell Training Programs to Athletes?'"

## Summary

Short Q&A clip in which Alex Hormozi answers an audience question (username "iPhone 5") about a partnership opportunity with an athlete who has ~170,000 Instagram/Facebook followers. The questioner is weighing whether to niche down to amateur athletes (who allegedly lack disposable income for a high monthly subscription) versus going wide to target people seeking fitness/inspiration through sports. Hormozi pushes back on the premise that amateur athletes have no money, points to parents already paying for skills coaching, and argues the real question is willingness-to-pay for a remote offering. He suggests an under-tapped third market: recreational adult-league players who want to get better. All claims are Alex's opinion/advice; the questioner's follower and income assumptions are self-reported/audience framing.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) The premise that amateur/up-and-coming athletes lack disposable income is not fully convincing to Hormozi; there is more spending in the space than assumed.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Parents routinely pay for skills coaching at even the high-school level — he cites figures like $300–$500/month for pitching, jumping, or hockey coaches — because they attach status and confidence to their kid being good, and see it as an investment toward a potential college scholarship / free tuition.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) The real decision isn't niche vs. wide; it's whether the specific remote service being offered is something the target is actually willing to pay for.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Example of monetizing a narrow athletic niche cheaply at scale: a YouTube creator (referred to as "Jacob Hillier," attribution uncertain — likely caption garble) with a jump-training focus who reportedly sold a ~$67 ebook and did roughly $4M/year from just that product. [self-reported by Alex about a third party; figures unverified]
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) For a big-name athlete building a community (he references advising an unnamed high-profile UFC fighter), Hormozi's own approach would be to build content around the "championship mindset" — how a champion approaches things — plus game/fight breakdowns where the athlete pauses real footage to explain their in-the-moment thinking and why they moved a certain way, giving insight into playing the game better.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) An under-tapped "third market" is recreational adult-league players (softball, basketball, football, etc.), a booming segment in the US, who want to improve simply to not look lame in front of friends and to feel cooler — a market he would target if advising this person.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "So I'm not entirely sold on the fact that they don't have money, but the question is just is the services that you're going to be offering remotely what they're willing to pay for."

> "There's plenty of kids who pay $300, $500 a month for pitching coaches and jumping coaches and hockey coaches because the parents... one have status associated with their kid being good, confidence, skills, whatever, but also there's a decent likelihood that they can get a full ride to university."

> "He had like a $67 ebook and from that $67 ebook he was doing like four million a year. And that's all he did."

> "I would basically have it from like the championship mindset of like how I approach things as a champion... and be like, 'Hey, pause. This is what I was thinking about here. This is why I moved this way on the court.'"

> "There's a huge market for people who still want to get better just because they don't want to look lame in front of their friends and just kind of be cooler. And so I think that might be a third market that is probably undertapped."
