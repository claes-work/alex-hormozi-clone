# The 2 Types of Employees

## Summary

Short interview clip (@MoreMozi) in which an interviewer asks Alex Hormozi how founders
should navigate the classic hiring dilemma: hire naïve/scrappy young talent (cheaper,
disruptive) vs. hire experience (expensive, conventional, safe). Alex answers with what
he calls a **barbell strategy** — deliberately staffing the two ends of the experience
spectrum and keeping very little in the middle. The conversation then branches into how
naïveté drives innovation (via first-principles thinking) and, as an aside, his
**four Rs** customer-success framework. Two speakers: an unnamed interviewer and Alex.
Only Alex-attributed material below.

★ **L3-candidate** — the "barbell strategy" for hiring is a concrete, named model worth
promoting into a hiring-and-recruiting / management topic page.

## Key claims (2026-02-22, paraphrased unless quoted)

- **Barbell hiring strategy.** Alex says he has strong opinions on the naïveté-vs-experience
  hiring dilemma: staff both ends of the spectrum and keep very little in the middle
  (paraphrase). One end = young, hungry people who are there for growth, want to learn,
  and will work long hours / rack up reps. Other end = people in the "back half" of their
  career who have already made enough money, could get a job anywhere, and are there
  because they genuinely love the work — not to be done a favor.
- **The middle is the problem.** People in the middle of the spectrum "tend to be the pain
  in the ass for everybody"; the vast majority of hires who don't work out fall in that
  bucket (paraphrase).
- **It's a perspective, not an age, thing.** Alex explicitly says the barbell is not about
  age — it's about whether you're there for growth or there to share/protect what you
  already know. He dislikes "careerists" who are obsessed with title, career path, and
  promotion plan, and who bring a "tit-for-tat" transactional relationship.
- **Complementary contributions.** Young people over-compensate with hours and reps and
  are learning; late-career people bring experience and create leverage through strategy
  (paraphrase).
- **Naïveté enables disruption.** You can fundamentally disrupt any industry if you don't
  know how it's "supposed" to go, because not knowing the rules lets you stumble onto new
  answers (paraphrase, prompted by the interviewer).
- **First-principles method.** Build from the few things you know to be true and forget
  everything else; from those assumptions you find many different "paths up the mountain,"
  some untrodden. Sometimes you re-derive a solution that already works — which gives you
  more confidence because now you understand *why* it works, not just that others did it.
- **Worked example of first principles.** Known truth: customers who love a product are
  more likely to tell others than customers who hate it. From there, break down what must
  occur to make someone love the product — timing of messages, moments of high posting
  likelihood, reducing friction to refer friends, and incentivizing referral. Same logic
  applies to service, physical-product, and software businesses.
- **The four Rs (customer-success framework).** For each customer Alex targets four
  milestones: **retain** (immediately), get them to **review** the product, get them to
  **refer** someone, and then **resell** them. All customer-success work reduces to
  reverse-engineering which activities increase the likelihood of each R.
- **Promo/CTA.** He plugs a free "10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+" that fewer than 1%
  of companies finish, broken down across eight business functions (constraint symptoms +
  steps to graduate), available at acquisition.com (roadmap link in the video description).
