# Helping a Private Equity Firm Doing $12M/Year Get to $90M

## Summary
In a live Q&A, a business owner who runs a buy-and-hold group of four software companies (~$12M/year revenue, aiming for $90M in five years) asks Alex Hormozi how to balance growing existing portfolio companies with pursuing new acquisitions. Hormozi argues the real constraint is talent quality, not process: the founders of small businesses are usually the bottleneck, and the fix is to buy bigger businesses run by more ambitious operators ("dogs") rather than babysitting mediocre founders. He draws on his own 2022–2023 acquisition spree to illustrate why small acquisitions rarely pay off.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says his own venture/portfolio book is run entirely by a separate team — he no longer even approves the checks, and is only informed after the fact.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the vast majority of his time is spent generating demand, which he considers his single highest-leverage activity because a strong brand delivers talent, deal flow, and everything else downstream.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames his job as: find the one thing that, if accomplished, achieves all other objectives — do only that, and hire people for everything else.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the acquisition side is straightforward (outbound, trade shows, conferences to generate deal flow); the hard part is organic growth of acquired companies.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues very small businesses are hard because they have "a hundred problems" at once and the founder who capped the business at that size is usually himself the problem, dominating the business.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims that in 2022 and 2023 he did 24 acquisitions and sold or gave back his shares in 19 of them, because the founders "just weren't winners." (Self-reported.)
- As of March 2026, Alex says winners either die or win — they won't tolerate staying flat three years running — so he now spends a greater share of his time looking for winners.
- As of March 2026, Alex says a reliable telltale sign of a good operator in buy-and-hold is simply size: a founder who reaches ~$20M ARR is probably a better entrepreneur than one who caps at $3M and sells.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues founders who only ever needed to make $20–30M and already have it are hard to motivate — their mindset (protect what they built, take care of their people) is not the mindset of someone going from $3M to $30M.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes the classic PE failure: a good business tanks after the founder is removed because PE doesn't know how to run it; you need a rare, genuinely good operator either found inside the business or installed.
- As of March 2026, Alex says every investor (himself included) reinvents the wheel — the appeal of cheaply flipping a $5–10M business to $100M collapses once you realize you must rebuild the entire business, which is why experienced buyers "just buy bigger."
- Caller claims (unverified): his group runs four software businesses (three serving property management, one serving event management), does just under $12M revenue, buys 100% of founders (buy-and-hold, founders stay), has capital available and only needs deal flow, and the companies are backed by a much larger cash-rich parent group.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "My highest leverage — the vast majority of my time is spent generating demand. Cuz that's the highest leverage thing I can do. So if I build the brand, I will be able to get all the talent I need. I'll be able to get all the deal flow that I need."

> "If I could only accomplish one thing, and that one thing on its own would accomplish all of our other objectives, then I will just do that. And then I will hire people to do everything else."

> "The founder who got to that level is at that level for a reason. And so usually it is the founder that's the problem. At least that's been my observation."

> "Winners aren't going to come and say, 'Yep, we stayed the same for the third year in a row.' Like they will die or they will win. And I'm just trying to find those people."

> "The entrepreneur who can get to 20 million in ARR is probably a better entrepreneur than somebody who caps at three and then sells at that point."

> "It's very difficult to motivate someone who only needed to make 20-30 million dollars in their life and then they made it."

> "It's probably the founders of the companies you bought aren't dogs. And you probably need dogs. So just buy bigger businesses with the right talent."

> "You realize why you get paid so much to do this is because it absolutely sucks. And you actually have to build the entire business. And then you're like, 'If I had to build this thing from scratch, I wouldn't have picked this business.'"
