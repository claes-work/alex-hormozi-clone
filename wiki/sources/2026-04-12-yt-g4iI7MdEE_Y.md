# How to Build a Business That Keeps Customers

## Summary

Alex argues that businesses become larger, more valuable, and easier to operate when
they retain customers rather than repeatedly refill a leaking bucket. He credits John
Paul DeJoria for the distinction between selling and reselling, sets 80% annual B2B
retention as a target, and explains how stronger retention multiplies LTV and acquisition
power.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex wants to acquire customers once and have product quality first,
  then brand, drive repeat purchases.
- He credits John Paul DeJoria for saying businesses should be in reselling rather than
  selling.
- Alex recommends solving retention and churn before scaling acquisition.
- His B2B rule of thumb is at least 80% annual logo retention measured on the original
  cohort, not total customers after new sales.
- He uses revenue divided by annual churn as back-of-envelope LTV math: 50% retention
  implies roughly two annual turns, while 80% implies roughly five.
- Because industry CAC tends to commoditize, superior LTV lets a business outspend
  competitors and creates acquisition arbitrage.
- Scaling before retention works merely scales the underlying problem.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I want to only have to acquire customers once."

> "You don't want to be in the sales business, you want to be in the reselling business."

> "When you scale problems, they just get meaner and uglier."
