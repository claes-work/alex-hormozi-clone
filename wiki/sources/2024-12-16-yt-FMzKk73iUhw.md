# How To Get Customers So Fast It Feels ILLEGAL

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi monologue laying out his playbook for fast customer acquisition. He
covers eight-ish levers: give away for free what others charge for; keep marketing teams
tiny and staffed with the highest-leverage people; ask "what would it take to be number
one"; sell in a vacuum (where no one sells, or what no one sells); invest in pre-production
over post; minimize customer "need-to-believes"; split-test relentlessly (especially
offers and third-party integrations); raise LTV so you can outspend competitors; build
affiliate deals so generous they hurt; and make truth (including damaging admissions) your
advertising ally.

## Key claims (his fast-customer-acquisition playbook)

- **Give away for free what others charge for.** Process: look at what others charge for →
  look at your hard cost to deliver it → give that away free → measure leads and
  conversion. Make the free thing better than what competitors sell. This moves prospects
  from "needs more information" to "needs less information" — which he says is what
  branding is at scale.
- **Fewer, better people in marketing.** Marketing has outsized returns on talent (like
  developers with technical career levels): one skilled person with an iPhone can get a
  100M-view video, so an A-player can do 5-25x+ a B-player. Back investment in talent with
  finances. Founders must stay close to the marketing, not delegate it away — the biggest
  asset the business has is the founder/operator.
- At Gym Launch he cut the marketing team from 17 to 5 (after removing the leader and
  moving in himself) and output tripled.
- Every founder must learn the core business, how to get customers, and how to deliver to
  customers; almost everything else can be outsourced.
- **"What would it take to be number one?"** (also "what else would have to be true"). This
  circumvents incremental thinking, assumes the goal outcome, and reverse-engineers it into
  the present. Often you already have the resources — so ask why you aren't pointing them
  all at the number-one goal. Credits the question's power to Stephen Schwarzman's book
  *What It Takes* — building big is about as hard as building small ("hard is a constant"),
  so go after the bigger upside. It's about resourcefulness, not resources.
- He applied this to himself: to be the best investor he needed proprietary/inbound deal
  flow → needed a big business brand → needed to make the best content and write a book →
  so he allocated all resources to content and books.
- **Sell in a vacuum** so you don't compete on price or terms: either sell where no one
  else sells (find the empty pond) or sell what no one else sells (unique feature/tech
  advantage, "what Elon does"), or provide value at scale for inbound demand so buyers
  choose "you or nothing."
- **Pre > post** ("an ounce of pre is worth a pound of post"). Pre-production has high
  leverage and low cost but takes more mental work; post has low leverage and high cost. A
  strong video idea/hook, even poorly executed, gets an order of magnitude more views. He
  now spends more time on pre (reviewing past winning ads/hooks, props) and strips back
  post. Cites the Indian channel "Physics Wallah" (huge views from just a guy at a
  whiteboard) as what shifted his thinking. In education, post-production should only exist
  to enhance comprehension.
- **Minimize "need-to-believes"** (term from marketer Perry Belcher). Fewer beliefs
  required = faster sales. Bad example: Prestige Labs required two beliefs (gym owners
  should sell supplements + should use Prestige specifically) — too much education, hard
  sale. Good examples: real estate investing (few beliefs needed), Amazon and Uber (they
  removed nearly every reason someone would need to believe — "barely a half-belief").
  Reposition/repackage so there's zero or one belief required.
- **Split-test everything** for permanent, one-time-effort lifts. Ranked priority: (1)
  offers, (2) packaging (headlines/subheadlines), (3) images, (4) third-party integrations.
  He says #1 and #4 have produced his biggest winners; third-party integration swaps (e.g.
  switching which software captures a step) have "exploded" companies four times in his
  career even when aesthetics were identical — almost no third-party provider reports
  conversion-rate data, so testing it yourself is a big unlock. Stacking 10-30% boosts
  across 3-4 funnel points can double/triple output, and profit rises disproportionately to
  revenue.
- **Ability to spend more = LTV; LTV is the arms race of advertising.** "Leads cost too
  much" is rarely the real problem — usually the business doesn't make enough per customer.
  Cost to acquire is roughly similar across competitors (1-2x); LTV is where the rich get
  richer (Starbucks ~$14,000 LTV from 40-50 years of optimizing repeat purchase).
  Advertising only gets more expensive over time, so LTV must concurrently rise. Raising
  prices is one of the strongest LTV levers and most owners are too afraid to pull it. The
  first ad in a new market/offer always performs best — don't chase that first high like a
  heroin addict; build the business to accommodate stabilization.
- **Affiliate models that work: make it so good it hurts.** Aggregate many smaller
  businesses/nodes rather than chasing super-affiliates. Don't offer 20% commission (no one
  bothers). Instead "peel a corner off" your product and give the affiliate 100% of that
  piece. Worked example: a masseuse lets a partner business sell three massages for whatever
  they want (min ~$300) and keep all the money; hard cost ~$90 (3 x $30 labor) buys a
  pre-paid, highly qualified lead with no ad/media/landing-page work — if you convert 1 in 3
  into a $2,000 offer, customer cost is $270 for a $2,000 sale. Discounts need to be 50%+ to
  change behavior; 10% coupons don't move the needle.
- **Truth sells; make damaging admissions a weapon.** "Nothing sells like the truth."
  Authenticity = just say what's true. State your negatives (small capacity → scarcity +
  personalization; small business → "buy local" appeal). Formula: **"[bad] but [good]"** —
  "but" is an amplifier that diminishes the negative and amplifies the relevant positive
  (bad thing should be relevant to the good claim). Claiming your own flaws first (the
  "8 Mile / Eminem" move) removes others' ability to hold them against you and builds
  trust. The hardest thing in marketing is getting someone to believe you; truth is the
  only long-term strategy.

- "This is about resourcefulness not resources."

- "An ounce of pre is worth a pound of post."
