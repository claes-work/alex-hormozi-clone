# Ways Creators Can Monetize in 2026

## Summary

In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi lays out a full **creator-monetization spectrum** for the creators interviewing him. He frames all monetization options as a single continuum ordered by **risk, capital, and effort** — low on one end, high on the other — and walks the creators from the lowest-commitment path (sponsorships, which they already do) up to the highest (building an entire product business "soup to nuts," MrBeast/Feastables style). Between the extremes sit affiliate deals, drop-ship/white-label (own the brand, outsource operations), and equity stakes in an existing business paired with distributions/royalties. He advocates a specific "best" structure — the model he used for his own Skool investment — where the creator writes a check, buys a meaningful stake, AND promotes. His governing decision rule: prefer deals that change little or nothing about what you already do, because you keep making content anyway, so slotting in a product you own a piece of adds long-term upside without changing behavior. He also states his core partnership test: a true partner must be able to succeed without you. The clip closes with an acquisition.com/roadmap CTA (ad read).

## Key claims (2026-02-19, paraphrased unless quoted)

- **Creator monetization is a single continuum** ordered by risk / capital / effort — low on the left, high on the right. Upside (enterprise value of what you promote) is higher on the high-risk side and lower on the low-risk side, and so are the risk and difficulty.
- **The spectrum, low → high commitment:**
  1. **Sponsorships** — lowest risk/capital/effort; "you make money from doing the same thing" (what the interviewing creators already do).
  2. **Affiliate relationship** — one degree right; e.g. Adobe gives 20% of all referrals for life. You promote, they pay a cut.
  3. **Drop-ship / white-label** — a middle ground where someone else manages operations while you own the brand and just promote it (Hormozi notes the creator had done this "with a coffee" before).
  4. **Equity stake in an existing business** — negotiate a percentage of equity, ideally paired with **distributions and/or royalties** (profit share or a royalty on topline). A minority deal, best done with a company/product you genuinely like.
  5. **Write a check (invest)** — the "best" version in his view: putting cash in removes expectations and aligns you to grow it for a return. Usually a minority stake plus **performance tranches** that kick in based on how well you promote.
  6. **Build the whole business yourself, soup to nuts** — the "final frontier"; you're starting a business (his example: MrBeast with Feastables — sourcing cocoa nibs in Africa, doing everything). Highest upside, highest risk and difficulty.
- **Royalties/distributions matter because most companies never sell** — "the vast majority don't sell" — so a creator needs cash flow (profit share or topline royalty) coming back, not just an illiquid equity bet, especially since the creator carries their own marketing costs.
- ★ **His preferred structure = the Skool-deal model** (L3-candidate, dated 2026-02-19): put cash in, buy a big stake, AND promote. "The best deals is kind of like the Skool deal where you put cash in, you buy a big stake, you also promote."
- **Decision rule — minimize behavior change:** "I would like to do deals that change as little or nothing about what I do." If you're going to make content anyway and already send buyers toward a category, slotting in a product you own a piece of and get a kickback on doesn't change what you do but adds long-term upside. This is "high leverage" because you just point your audience in a vetted direction (with a free trial to de-risk it for them).
- **Partnership test:** for a true partnership to work, the partner "has to be able to be successful without you." If they're already growing/successful (his Skool example — "Skool's going to be successful either way, I just want to pull the future towards us faster"), you're adding acceleration, not rescue. Ideal partner is as good as or better than you at their thing while you're great at yours — the magic is in the sum of the parts.
- **Advice to the interviewing creators specifically:** since they're great at making content and their sponsorship revenue keeps growing, they can layer on deals further right of center "as long as it changes nothing about what we do" — enter those conversations with the frame "I'm already sponsoring, so slotting your product in (that I also own a piece of and get a kickback on) doesn't change anything about what we do, but gives me long-term upside."
