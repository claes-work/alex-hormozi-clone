# Master Branding: How to grow your Brand for Your Success — Alex Hormozi

## Summary

A Q&A/whiteboard session (Skool School) in which Alex Hormozi lays out his refined
definition of a brand — "an association between two things" — and its three measurable
elements: strength of influence, directionality, and reach. He then answers audience
questions on the algorithm, shorts vs. long-form, content volume, how he manufactures
topics, his "framework for making frameworks," controversy/cancellation, and how he
hires and beats content agencies. Alex is the sole main speaker; audience members only
pose short questions.

## Key claims

- A brand is fundamentally **an association between two things** — between things people
  know and things they don't. Branding is a form of teaching: repeated pairing (e.g. a
  hat worn in every video) makes the audience associate the two, until the value
  transfers even when the cue is removed.
- A brand has **three elements**: (1) *strength of influence* — what percentage of
  people change their behavior when they see you/your logo; (2) *directionality* —
  whether that behavior moves toward or away from you (can be strong in both directions,
  as with Donald Trump); (3) *reach* — how many people in general hold the association.
- Etymology: branding began with **branding a cow** — the mark changed how people
  treated the animal. "Brands change behavior at scale — that's why they're valuable."
- The hypothetical maximum of a brand: as many people as possible moving toward you and
  changing behavior at once when they see your thing (cites Taylor Swift as close to
  this).
- Rejects "all publicity is good publicity" — replaces it with **"all publicity is
  publicity"**: it increases *reach* but not necessarily influence or directionality.
- Because a brand is built through association, be careful who you appear next to. Alex
  says he **no longer speaks on stages and has become strict about podcasts/appearances**
  to avoid being associated with other speakers. "You are always going to be the purest
  version of your brand… you are your brand."
- **Align your objective with the platform's.** Hacks (e.g. hashtags) fail because
  platforms close the loop; every platform's long-term objective is to keep people on
  the platform, so it rewards content that lots of people click, watch all the way
  through, and then watch something else from you. Align with those three and you never
  have an issue. This is why the hook matters.
- **Long-form has only three formats: lists, steps, and stories** — and they can be
  intermixed (a list with steps, with a story per step). Early on, think of a long video
  as several shorts strung together (e.g. seven shorts).
- **Volume**: Alex's team puts out ~350 pieces a week (~50/day across platforms). Start
  with what you can commit to, then ramp. Key leverage is finding where you get
  "double/triple/quadruple/quintuple dips" on content.
- His **highest-leverage activity is making tweets** — they are the "life source" of
  everything. A tweet captures an idea in seconds; the team later expands tweets into
  recordings, Instagram posts, reels, and spoken-word videos. This replaced an old habit
  (from Gym Launch days) of emailing lessons/failures to a single email thread to himself.
- **Four topic buckets**: (1) real-time (tweet/notes dumps), (2) recent past (review your
  calendar/meetings from the week), (3) far past (big lessons that shifted your beliefs),
  (4) manufactured (challenge-style, e.g. "I lived on $100 for a month" — closer to
  entertainment; he rarely does these).
- **The framework for making frameworks**: if you must do something repeatedly, make a
  template so you don't re-solve the same problems. "Drink in a ton of data," list
  everything, then find the commonalities and delete the rest. Example: he watched the
  intros of 35 of his YouTube videos, listed what each did, and reduced to the three
  things they all shared. Naming: make items share a letter (promise/path/plan) or an
  acronym to make it memorable.
- Origin of the **Five Horsemen of retention**: at Gym Launch he polled his customer base
  for gyms with <3% monthly churn for six straight months (~20 out of thousands),
  interviewed all 20; each did 20+ things but only **five things in common** — those
  became the framework.
- **On cancellation**: "I actually don't think you can be cancelled." You can only be
  cancelled if all means of communicating with an audience are removed AND you stop
  making content. Cites R. Kelly (stopped making content) vs. Kanye (kept making content,
  kept followers). "You become uncancellable as long as you don't accept that you can be
  cancelled."
- **On controversy**: controversy behaves like a trending topic — it drives reach/CTR but
  not necessarily influence or directionality. Alex personally doesn't engage with
  controversies ("I don't care enough") and wouldn't want to associate his brand with them.
- **Hiring for content**: hire one smart person and overpay them; have them hire ~10.
  Product-market-fit customers come from niche content even if it gets few views.
- **Agencies**: Alex used agencies first (a podcast agency and a YouTube agency posted for
  him), then brought Caleb in to manage them and hire an in-house team to replace them —
  running both in parallel ("you pay twice") until the in-house team beats the agency,
  then the agency goes away. To pick an agency/creative director: talk to ~10, listen to
  all, and you'll quickly see who's competent; go off referrals. "The best agencies don't
  need to run ads."
