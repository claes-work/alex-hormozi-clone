# How to Run a Sales Team

## Summary
Room Q&A / whiteboard teaching session. Alex walks a group of business owners (attendees ask questions via `>>` turns — a home-services seller, a veterinary-clinic owner with vet techs, etc.) through Acquisition.com's actual sales-team operating system, the one packaged in their "closer handbook" handed to every new hire. The spine is a **training cadence**: daily 30-minute team training (blocked on everyone's calendar, scheduled at the deadest sales hour — 10:00–10:30 a.m. PST — and untouchable except when you're live on a closing call), a weekly one-on-one, and a weekly team meeting. Daily training opens with **kudos** (manager-to-rep recognition that reinforces whatever behavior you want more of) and **peer shout-outs**, then **role-play** that rotates through the four script sections on a fixed weekly calendar — intro (Mon), discovery (Tue), pitch (Wed), close (Thu), and a Friday "hot topic" on whatever the team is struggling with — because you have to "sharpen the whole sword" or reps just break the section you skip. The manager models each rep first, locks it in, then reps break into pairs. The weekly one-on-one has three parts: personal (how are you actually doing — because a rep whose life is falling apart can't focus on objection handling), short-term (review three pre-submitted struggle calls, tutor the specific weakness), and long-term/career (only earned once someone's hitting quota). Alex then teaches a **bimodal career ladder** — junior/senior setter, junior/senior closer, then manager/portco-manager/advisor — giving six-plus rungs so good people don't get headhunted away; each rung is earned on **gross output** (e.g., cumulative sales/kills), never tenure, so hustle compresses timelines, and every promotion pairs a pay bump with a title and a concrete perk (no weekends, best parking). He hammers that any output KPI must be **paired with a quality metric** (sales volume AND close-rate above X; sessions AND five-star reviews) so reps don't burn leads — "speed and quality" as antagonistic metrics forming a North Star. The team meeting mirrors the training but reviews **game footage** (recorded real calls) on a trending/seasonal objection. New hires get a structured two-week on-ramp: two-a-days with the manager, ride-alongs, and self-directed script memorization (the black-marker method — black out one word at a time; a 400-word script gets repeated 400 times), gated by three milestones (script memorization → recaps/labeling the buyer's problem → close & objection handling), then a half-calendar of leads, then a full calendar after five days of hitting KPI, with one remedial cycle before "they're out." He closes on his signature theme: most companies "hire salespeople and hope for the best"; repetition volume (80 reps of a presentation before someone's good) is the moat, and an untrained team working leads is far more expensive than the 30 minutes a day training costs.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex puts training above everything else on the sales floor: 30 minutes daily, blocked on every rep's calendar, scheduled at the deadest sales hour (10:00–10:30 a.m. PST for his team), and the only permitted excuse for arriving late is being live on a closing call.
- As of March 2026, he opens daily training with kudos (manager-to-rep recognition, used deliberately to reinforce whatever behavior you want more of — e.g., shout out reviews first if you want more reviews) followed by peer-to-peer shout-outs, framing culture as the manager's job: in any department "the leader is always at fault."
- As of March 2026, his role-play follows a fixed weekly calendar mapped to the four script sections — intro (Mon), discovery (Tue), pitch (Wed), close (Thu), and a Friday "hot topic" — because drilling only one section makes reps fail the others; you must "sharpen the whole sword every week."
- As of March 2026, the role-play method is model-first: the manager demonstrates, does it with one rep until it's locked in, then reps break into pairs to drill each other; Zoom works as well as in-office.
- As of March 2026, the weekly one-on-one has three parts — personal ("how are you doing," fully dedicated to life issues if needed), short-term (rep submits three struggle calls beforehand; manager tutors the specific weakness and re-scripts it), and long-term/career (only reached if the rep is hitting quota and stable).
- As of March 2026, he advocates a bimodal/two-pronged career ladder for any repeated, high-headcount role (sales, editors, customer success): junior setter → setter → senior setter → junior closer → closer → senior closer, then onward to manager / portco manager / advisor — creating 6+ rungs so strong people aren't headhunted away.
- As of March 2026, a first promotion should come within the first 30 days for just clearing the basics ("something to call mom and dad home about"), and each subsequent rung pairs a pay bump with a title change plus a tangible perk (senior setter = no weekends + qualified — not entitled — for the next closer opening).
- As of March 2026, advancement is measured on gross/cumulative output (e.g., "first 10 sales," "first 50 sales," 100/200 jobs), never tenure, so a rep can compress the timeline by working nights/weekends ("pulling the future forward") — ~200 sales in 2 months if crushing it, 3–4 months if not.
- As of March 2026, every output KPI must be paired with an antagonistic quality metric to prevent lead-burn: sales volume paired with a minimum average close percentage; a clinician's number of sessions paired with five-star reviews / zero complaints / zero refunds — "usually it's speed and quality."
- As of March 2026, he cites a nail-salon owner as the model: a skill-gated progression that sped up onboarding (teach the one basic skill first, add skills only after they stick) and raised billables, taking techs without a high-school diploma to $75k/year (twice the industry average) and retaining them for years.
- As of March 2026, the weekly team meeting mirrors daily training but reviews recorded "game footage" of real calls, focused on a trending or seasonal objection the whole team needs to work.
- As of March 2026, new-hire on-ramp for talking-based roles is two weeks: week one is two-a-days with the manager plus alternating ride-alongs and script memorization, gated by three milestones — (1) memorize the core script, (2) "recaps" (labeling/summarizing a buyer's stated pain into the problem bucket you sell to), (3) close & overcome objections — each with a manager-run test you can't pass without nailing the script.
- As of March 2026, his script-memorization method: read the script aloud, black out one word, repeat; a mistake sends you back to the clean version — so a 400-word script is repeated ~400 times, after which the reps run automatically ("they don't think about the words"); reps do this on their own time.
- As of March 2026, the on-ramp gate: after passing all milestones a rep gets a half-calendar of leads ("the most expensive thing I can do is waste leads"), earns a full calendar after hitting KPI five days in a row, and if they miss they get one remedial half-ramp on their specific issue — pass and "they're in," fail and "they're out."
- As of March 2026, he estimates 98% of companies just "hire salespeople and hope for the best," and claims sales training is what he is personally best known for.
- As of March 2026, mastery comes from raw repetition: he had a new advisor run the value-orders presentation ~80 times before it clicked (roughly the 10th rep to start understanding, 40th to see the overarching flow, 40–80 to add the micro-bridges and jokes); even his money-model launch pitch was 1,700 scripted slides in two hours with every word on-screen, rehearsed physically (unboxing books, weighting pages with duct tape) not just verbally.
- As of March 2026, his cost argument: an untrained sales team working leads is far more expensive than losing 30 minutes a day to training, and training compounds into a culture because "they see it work"; he cites close rates that jumped once training started ("close beats used to be four to five hundred people").

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "We put training above everything else."

> "In every organization or department the leader is always at fault. So if the sales team isn't doing what you want them to do, it's because the sales manager isn't doing what they need to be doing."

> "If you just do objection handling, you get way too many objections cuz they [screw] up the intro… And so we have to sharpen the whole sword every week, and that's what keeps everything top of mind."

> "I don't want it to be on tenure, because I don't want people to just think, like, as if I just stay here I'm going to advance. I want somebody to be incentivized to say, well, if I can close a hundred deals in two months rather than six months, I can move up the ladder faster."

> "Whenever you do any kind of KPI it has to be paired. So if we do sales volume, we also have to make sure they're doing that volume with an average close percentage of above X. I don't want you to burn through my leads."

> "The most expensive thing I can do is waste leads."

> "The amount of times they repeat the script is the amount of words that are in the script. So you have 400 words, they're going to say it 400 times. And by the time they do that, they don't think about the words."

> "Most people just hire sales people and hope for the best."

> "I can't waste time training my sales team. It's like, can you waste time having the sales team not get trained?"

> "Quantity has a quality all of its own… at a certain point it's just like, doing so much volume, you just learn how to do things better. You just can't help it. The boredom forces innovation and improvement."
