# The Difference Between an Owner and a CEO

## Summary

Alex says entrepreneurs often confuse owning many things with being the CEO who must
make one thing work. He calls focus an antidote to arrogance: doing three things is not
likely to beat a competitor doing one. He gives his own example of splitting attention
across Gym Launch, gyms, a chiropractor agency, a dental agency, and later an
e-commerce bolt-on, and says his biggest entrepreneurial mistakes came from splitting
attention.

## Key claims (Alex's self-report and advice)

- Entrepreneurship is more a war of the heart than of the mind because people often know
  what to do but do not do it.
- Any of several opportunities can work, but none will work unless the founder picks one.
- Alex calls it arrogant to assume divided attention can beat a competitor with full
  focus.
- Founders should distinguish being an owner from being the CEO responsible for
  execution.
- Modeling someone else's portfolio too early confuses order; the portfolio came after
  concentrated execution.
- Alex says his biggest entrepreneurial mistakes came from splitting attention.
- Focus means leaving money on the table in one place to pursue the larger compounding
  return elsewhere.
- People compare year zero of a new thing to year zero of the current thing instead of
  comparing year zero to year four.
- Crazy goals become sane when the timeline extends.
- Entrepreneurs may need to unlearn the lesson of quitting one thing to start another,
  because the next phase requires staying with one thing for a long time.
