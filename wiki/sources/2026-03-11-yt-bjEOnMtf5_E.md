# How to Build a Good Product

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks the questions; all substantive content is Alex's). The prompt: what should someone do when they suspect their product or service isn't good enough? Alex's answer reframes the anxiety into a **feedback-and-iteration loop**. First, a baseline of promotion is non-negotiable — "if no one knows about your stuff, you will make nothing ever" — but you only *scale* promotion once you know the thing is good; early on you must accept it won't be great yet. His preferred de-risking move is **giving free samples**, which lowers the stakes on your personal reputation. The free-sample test is itself diagnostic: if no one will take your thing even for free, that's strong feedback the market won't pay for it. If people *do* take it, there are three ways a free recipient makes you money: they convert to a paying customer (e.g. once demand outstrips your free capacity and you add a paywall), they leave a **review/testimonial** you can use to win others, or they **refer** you another customer. So if your product "sucks," the prescription is: give it away free, ask for honest feedback, ask what it would take to "suck less," and iterate. Positive feedback becomes a public review; negative feedback stays a private review. Alex then widens the lens to a mindset point: you won't get it right the first time. Quoting The Matrix ("everyone falls the first time," on Neo's jump), he argues nearly every entrepreneur has a graveyard of "failed" attempts that were really lessons, each business building the only asset that ultimately matters — **you**. The friction from the marketplace serves to build who you become; he frames this against mortality (we all die and are forgotten) as the reason to seek reward in the *actions* rather than only in money manifesting immediately. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo (the $100M roadmap quiz across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance).
