# How to See Reality More Clearly Than 99% of People

> Source page for MoreMozi YouTube video `yt-O4xXOK4MQ24`. Interview format
> (Alex + an off-camera interviewer). Only Alex-attributed statements below feed
> the persona; the interviewer only prompts.

## Summary

Alex lays out the "meta concept" he operates from: define every important-but-amorphous
word strictly in terms of **observable behavior in the observable universe** — i.e. by
answering "what would someone have to *do* for me to say [X] has occurred?" He walks a
chain of behavioral-science definitions (learning, intelligence, patience, resilience,
trauma, courage, authenticity, confidence, purpose) and argues that most people
communicate badly, can't manage relationships, and can't change themselves because they
trade in "bundled terms" that nobody has defined — so both parties say words neither
understands. Operationalizing the terms turns vague traits into **recipes** ("if you want
to be perceived as patient, figure out something to do in the meantime"), which he says
made persuasion, training, and self-change dramatically easier and let him make
higher-quality decisions. He closes on behavior change mechanics: behaviors repeat
because of what happened *after* them (reinforcement), not what "triggered" them — using
his own anger habit with Leila as a worked example. Frames this as the core of an
upcoming book. Ends with an acquisition.com/roadmap plug.

## Key claims

_All dated 2026-02-14, paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed._

- ★ **Define every important term inside the observable universe.** His central method:
  for any amorphous word, the question to answer is "what would someone have to do for me
  to say [that term] has occurred?" Definitions must point to things everyone can observe
  "with their own two eyes," so all parties agree on and can discuss the same referent.
  (This is the fresh, unifying framing of the video — the systematic "operationalize the
  term by its observable behavioral test" recipe.)
- **Learning = same condition, new behavior.** From behavioral science: a person in
  condition A who, on re-entering condition A, behaves differently has learned. Changing
  to the *wrong* new behavior is still learning (the wrong thing); no change = no
  learning.
- **Intelligence = rate of learning.** If someone needs 10 reps to change behavior and
  another needs 5 in the same context, the second is more intelligent *in that context*.
  He likes this because it means people have direct influence over their own intelligence
  — learn/change faster and you are, in a real sense, more intelligent.
- **Bundled terms are useless as instructions.** Telling a kid/employee "be patient," "be
  confident," "be authentic" transmits nothing because the term isn't broken into
  observable component behaviors.
- **Operationalized definitions (his examples):**
  - Patience = figuring out what to do in the meantime.
  - Resilience = returning to a behavioral baseline; how resilient = how fast you return.
  - Trauma = a permanent change of behavior caused by an aversive/negative stimulus
    (and by that definition not always bad — a baby learning not to touch a hot stove).
  - Excuse = a statement to avoid punishment.
  - Courage = the interval of time a potentially bad outcome affects whether you act
    (shorter interval between perceiving risk and acting = more courageous).
  - Authenticity = how you behave when there's no risk of punishment (behaving the same
    alone as in public).
  - Confidence = an aggregate of observable behaviors (eye contact, nodding while
    listening, repeating back what was said, taking action despite downside) that
    together get a person labeled "confident."
- **Traits are dials, not binaries.** Not "authentic or not" but *how* authentic, and in
  *which setting*. He cites B.F. Skinner — "if many variables exist, many variables must
  be studied" — against wanting reality in a "neat box with clean lines."
- **100% authenticity may not even be desirable.** Society's rules/laws constrain behavior;
  the only fully authentic person behaves identically alone and in public. Defining the
  term first lets you discuss the tradeoff calmly instead of it becoming a charged accusation.
- **Most people can't communicate because neither party's words are defined.** Both say
  words the other doesn't understand; they want the other to *guess* which behavior
  upset them. He ties this to why many are dissatisfied and can't manage relationships.
- **Fix people by naming the micro-behaviors, not the label.** Worked example: a
  "star performer" everyone called "a dick." Telling him "stop being a dick" failed;
  naming 2–3 specific behaviors (interrupting in meetings, telling people how to do their
  jobs) and asking him to stop those made him "night and day." Same with calling someone
  "lazy" → translate to "respond in under 5 minutes; send meeting notes ahead of time."
- **A book of recipes, not a how-to-live.** His book won't say "here's how to live"
  (that assumes he knows and that they should); it says "if you want these traits, here
  are the recipes" — explained simply enough for a child who doesn't know the words.
- **Behavior repeats because of what happened *after*, not what "triggered" it.** People
  misdiagnose their behavior by asking "what triggered that?" The real driver is the
  reinforcement that followed the last time they did it.
- **His anger was reinforced (worked example).** When Leila cried, he'd comfort briefly,
  then get angry; his anger scared her, she stopped crying — so anger got reinforced as
  the thing that "worked." Long-term it erodes the relationship, so he's actively working
  on it. To change it, Leila now reinforces the *new* behavior (thanking him for staying
  calm and present); the more the new behavior is reinforced, the weaker the old
  reinforcer becomes.
- **Anger's cost to business = lost information flow.** Being sharp/cold even once or twice
  (even not directed at someone — people learn by modeling) makes people afraid to bring
  him information. He offsets this operationally: Leila (CEO) gathers all the information
  and deliberately has no direct reports, but he still wants to improve personally.
- **Self-change is downstream of changing what you do.** Change behavior → it ladders up
  to "who you are"; the lever is building the fastest-possible reward cycle around the new
  behavior. He frames all of this as descriptive, not logical — hard-won "from a lot of
  suffering and pain" because he previously had no words for how to change.
