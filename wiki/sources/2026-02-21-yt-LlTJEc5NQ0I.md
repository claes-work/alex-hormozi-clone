# Helping a Business Owner Build a Culture

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A (`@MoreMozi`). The questioner (audience — NOT Alex) owns a
residential real-estate brokerage in Orange County (self-reported ~$6.5M revenue,
~80 agents, ~12 at the "home base" adjacent to a new market) and wants to open a
second office in the Riverside market, scaling that location from ~12 to ~50 agents
while continuing to grow the main office. He asks Alex how to duplicate sales
process and culture across locations he won't physically be in.

Alex reframes the problem: sales process is likely already duplicated, so the real
risk is cultural. He argues culture fails to transpose because it is a large body of
uncodified rules, and prescribes codifying them explicitly. He then applies his
"nail it then scale it" / "scale to zero" principle: don't start the second location
until the first runs and grows without the owner's time. He closes with guidance on
leadership: hire people better than you, be worthy of admiration, and build fast
reinforcement/feedback loops (leadership role-plays) to train the new leader inside
the existing office before sending them out.

Extends Alex's existing **"What Does Culture Mean?"** framework (culture = the spoken
and unspoken rules that govern reinforcement; values = chunked-up rules). This video
adds the *operational* half: how to codify those rules by walking the conditions in
which behavior occurs, and how to transpose codified culture to a new location. See
also the "nail it then scale it" / scale-to-zero scaling framework.

## Key claims (2026-02-21, paraphrased unless quoted; Alex-attributed only)

- Culture is the rules — spoken and unspoken — that govern reinforcement in an
  organization. A rule is a conditional if/then statement about consequences: if you
  do X you get rewarded, if you do Y nothing happens, if you do Z you get punished.
- Culture is hard to transpose from one location to the next because there are 100+
  rules governing behavior, and people never codify them — hence the vague advice to
  "just be around and soak it in," which really means observing many
  reinforcement conditions over time to infer how the rules work.
- Two paths to culture: (a) Alex's preferred — codify *all* the rules of behavior
  (what's rewarded, what's punished), then chunk them up into ~3 values/statements
  that, when unpacked, imply the underlying set of behaviors; (b) the fast-and-dirty
  way — start with 3 values and just describe them as best you can.
- Values are "chunked-up rules." A value is a compressed label for a set of
  behaviors; unpack the value and you should recover the behaviors it implies.
- Practical method to surface rules: think about the *conditions* where behavior
  occurs (e.g. "within a meeting, what are all the rules of a meeting?") — it is
  easier to enumerate rules by walking the contexts of interaction than to list them
  abstractly. Example rule at acquisition.com: if you're not talking in a meeting,
  you're muted; talking (side-conversation) while not addressing everyone is near
  grounds for termination because it signals you're not paying attention.
- Codify rules → map them to the conditions/contexts where culture occurs → chunk up
  to ~3 statements. That codified set becomes the "litmus test" you transpose to the
  new location.
- To transpose culture: make sure the new leader actually adheres to those rules, and
  ideally move your **biggest culture champion** to the new location — but only if the
  home culture can still reinforce itself without that person.
- Scaling principle — "nail it then scale it," restated as **scale to zero**: get the
  current business to *zero of your (the owner's) time* before replicating it, and run
  the replication economics based on the business at zero owner-time, NOT at you
  maxed out.
- Preferred sequencing: don't launch the second location while still running the first
  full-time. Start the second only when the first is "going and growing without you
  and you're bored out of your mind." Otherwise the new site needs attention (the new
  leader isn't as good as you), you take your eye off the first, and both suffer —
  which is what happens "the vast majority of the time."
- Train the new leader *inside* the main office first (eat some margin by having them
  do the role there): lower risk and faster feedback loops mean they're already
  "fixed" and know how to do it before running the next location. Put "training
  wheels on that as fast as possible."
- What's missing for this owner: faster **reinforcement loops for the leader** —
  ways to check in, monitor performance, and give real-time feedback, the same way a
  leader gives a salesperson real-time feedback.
- Role-play *leadership* conversations, not just sales conversations. A leadership
  conversation is itself a sales conversation — there are just more of them and each
  is more unique — so it's less scripted and more question-based frameworks.
- Hiring/leadership razor: to grow beyond yourself you must hire people better than
  you. If you're always the best person in the org, it will never grow beyond you.
  You get to "hire your mentors" (attributed to Bezos — caption reads "Basos"). Every
  leader in Alex's business does something better than him and he learns from them.
- Leadership requires mutual admiration: a leader must admire some component of who
  you are (you must be "worthy of admiration"), and you should admire portions of
  them — otherwise they shouldn't be a leader.
- On the objection "how do you get someone better than you to work for you?" —
  delivered as a joke — "They just don't know they're better."
- Self-reported by the questioner (audience, treat as context, not verified):
  ~$6.5M revenue, ~80 agents (~75), ceiling ~200 agents; new market target ~40–50
  agents; managing the new location as a sales manager would take ~20% of his time;
  ~60% of his time is content (proprietary/inbound deal flow is his real constraint).

## Notable verbatim quotes (Alex)

> "So I define culture as the rules spoken and unspoken that govern reinforcement in an organization... A rule is a conditional statement of if this then that. So if you do this then you will get rewarded. If you do this then nothing will happen. If you do this then you will get punished."

> "I see values as chunked up rules." (context: chunking ~200 behavioral rules into 3 statements that, unpacked, imply the behaviors)

> "You have to nail it then scale it. And so I use that moniker of scale zero. It's like how do I get your current business to zero? And once it's at zero, then I can replicate it. And we have to use the economics of it at zero, not it at you maxed out."

> "Most of the time my preference is that the first location is already going and growing without you and you're bored out of your mind and that's when you start the second."

> "If you're always the best person in the org... it'll never grow beyond you." / "Because how do you get someone better than you to work for you? They just don't know they're better. That's how... That's my secret."

> "You need to roleplay leadership conversations rather than sales conversations. Even though a leadership conversation, by the way, is a sales conversation. They're just more of them and more unique."
