# How Learning to Try One Thing Beats 99% of People

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says "learning how to try" is a transferable skill. When someone puts all
discretionary effort, time, money, and attention into one domain for an extended period,
they learn how few things can be done well at once and how easy it is to beat people who
never apply that degree of effort. He maps the same skill across saxophone, tennis,
content, and repeated business actions.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Really trying means concentrating discretionary effort, time, money, and resources on one
  thing every day for an extended period.
- Once someone learns what real effort requires in one domain, they realize how few things
  they can do well at once.
- The same experience also reveals how little most people actually try.
- "Learning how to try" generalizes across domains because skill acquisition requires many
  repetitions with feedback.
- Content creation can be treated like athletic practice: many reps, feedback, persistence,
  and no stopping.
- People are the result of actions, not aspirations.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Learning how to try becomes a generalizable skill across domains."

> "We are the result of our actions, not our aspirations."
