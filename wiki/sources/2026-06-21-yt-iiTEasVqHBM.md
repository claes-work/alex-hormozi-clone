# Door-to-Door Sales Secrets That Print Cash

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a window-replacement company whose door-to-door setter role has heavy first-week attrition. Alex frames churn as partly inherent to door-to-door sales, but says the business can improve by selecting better recruiting avatars, training under realistic conditions, setting truthful expectations, and gamifying early leading indicators. He recommends freestanding doors, repeated knocking-and-script drills, hostile-customer roleplay, early leaderboards, and a career path from seller to trainer to team recruiter.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - window-replacement business owner. His revenue, margin, compensation, model, and first-week retention problem are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- High churn is a feature of door-to-door sales, not necessarily evidence that the business model is broken.
- The company should analyze successful reps by demographics, past experience, and application-process behaviors.
- Training should approximate the real sales environment as closely as possible.
- Even seemingly small differences, like not physically knocking on a door, can reduce transfer from training to the field.
- Reps should practice with freestanding doors, transitions between doors, script repetition, and hostile-customer simulations.
- Early recognition should reward leading indicators such as doors knocked, doors opened, and difficult rejections, not only closes.
- Recruiting copy should tell the truth about the difficulty while making the opportunity compelling to the right person.
- A door-to-door team should create career progression: learn to sell, teach one person to sell, then recruit and lead a team.
