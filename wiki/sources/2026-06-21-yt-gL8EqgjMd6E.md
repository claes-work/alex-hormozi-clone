# My CAC Is Up 65%. How Do I Fix It?

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a $14 million Mexico-based skincare ecommerce brand whose CAC has risen while affiliate conversion has fallen. Alex says tactical fixes like better attribution, upsells, creative, and offers would only delay the problem because the current model is essentially media arbitrage. He recommends shifting the economics from customer-level CAC to influencer-level CAC, building a more durable affiliate distribution base, and deliberately turning the product into a premium brand so affiliates gain status by association rather than behaving like mercenaries.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - skincare ecommerce founder. His revenue, margins, Mexican market, product, paid-ad strategy, affiliate history, and CAC trend are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The business is currently too close to a media-arbitrage model: buying clicks and selling clicks for more than they cost.
- Media arbitrage is easier at small numbers and gets harder as the business scales.
- Tactical marketing fixes may buy 6 to 12 months but will not solve the durable strategic issue.
- The company needs a vehicle that drives CAC down as it scales.
- One path is stronger affiliate distribution, measured as LTV:CAC per influencer rather than per end customer.
- Affiliate strategy should be paired with brand building, especially for a product that can be copied by other manufacturers.
- The long-term goal is for affiliates to gain status from promoting the brand.
- Without brand, affiliates are mercenary and may leave for a small commission improvement from a competitor.
- The skincare market is large enough that a $14 million brand should not treat market saturation as the problem.
- Alex says higher-level companies often need strategic resource-allocation advice more than small tactical fixes.
