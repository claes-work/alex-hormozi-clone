# I'm a Dentist. How Do I Buy and Sell Other Dental Offices?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A dentist with three general-dentistry offices doing ~$20M/year (roughly $6-7M each) wants to build "the Gym Launch of dentistry": use social-media content to source warm leads among other dentists, take **minority investment** positions in their practices, help them grow, then flip — "get in, get out" — without buying the whole thing. His stated reasons for a minority/invest model over full ownership are speed and dental **licensing** limits across states. Alex pushes back hard on the entire premise. His core argument: a minority-stake growth-consulting roll-up will "not be fun" because you'll want control but won't have it, the operators "are not you" and won't execute like you, so you'll end up paying money in just to make them go away — which is exactly why "everyone who gets into it buys **majority control**." He reframes the opportunity: the dentist is at the point where the business is "about to get fun" (three offices, team in place, finally above water), so the move is to **go hard, not go different** — open two more locations this year to hit the $35M goal, which builds a "significantly more valuable enterprise" than selling one-offs. On the flip-and-sell-back fantasy he's blunt: operators won't buy their practice back for more than you paid in "cuz they'll hate you." On licensing, he probes the excuse — you can hire a state-licensed dentist, and while a lone dentist can hold a small practice hostage ("mutiny"), it's much harder to be held hostage when a location has three or four dentists. His alternative playbook: (1) keep expanding **locally** and own the whole market — "a very clean exit"; or buy out retiring local dentists' practices "for nothing"; (2) failing that, expand into an **adjacent market** to test execution chops. Both routes, he argues, are no harder than the invest-and-flip model, carry a higher probability of success, and deliver far better returns. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues a minority-investment growth model in someone else's business "will not be fun": you'll want control, the operators won't execute, and you'll end up paying to make them go away — which is why serious acquirers "buy majority control."
- As of March 2026, his stated principle: the operators "are not you" — "if they were you, they would be you, and they're not you, so they're not going to magically become you," so you can't expect a minority stake plus coaching to make them perform like you would.
- As of March 2026, when a business "gets easy" (team in place, finally profitable/above water) the right move is to **go hard, not go different** — double down on the working model rather than start an entirely new business.
- As of March 2026, for the dentist specifically: opening two more locations to reach ~$35M builds a "significantly more valuable enterprise" than a portfolio of grown-and-flipped one-off minority stakes.
- As of March 2026, the sell-it-back-to-the-operator exit is unrealistic: operators won't repurchase their own practice for more than the investor put in "cuz they'll hate you" (resentment from having needed the money and being unable to repay).
- As of March 2026, owning your entire local market via continued local expansion is "a very clean exit" — a great, high-value outcome for a multi-location practice.
- As of March 2026, buying out **retiring local dentists** whose practices aren't profitable enough for a younger buyer and aren't automated enough for corporate/DSO interest can be done cheaply ("buy their practices for nothing").
- As of March 2026, the state-**licensing** barrier is a weak objection: you can hire a dentist licensed in the target state, and although a single dentist can stage a "mutiny" and hold a one-doctor practice hostage, that leverage largely disappears when a location has three or four dentists.
- As of March 2026, expanding into an **adjacent market** is a valid way to test execution ability, is no harder than the invest-and-flip model, and yields a higher likelihood of success with better returns.
