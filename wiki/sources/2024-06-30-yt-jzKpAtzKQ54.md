# Business Owners: You NEED to Know This Number

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi unpacks a single ratio he calls the "fundamental economic unit" of any business: LTV to CAC — more precisely **Lifetime Gross Profit (LTGP) to Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**. He argues this one number predicts how far a business can scale, how profitable it will be, and how much it can outspend competitors, and that most owners fail to make money because they never learn this "language of business." He walks through how to calculate both sides, illustrates with Starbucks, a Facebook-ads agency, a hair-extensions coach, and glass/pool businesses, and closes with the two levers to move the ratio: raise LTGP or lower CAC.

## Key claims

- **The number is a ratio: LTGP ÷ CAC.** Alex insists that "LTV" in common speech should really be **Lifetime Gross Profit** — you must subtract the cost of delivering the good/service, not just multiply price by duration.
- **Gross profit excludes fixed costs.** Rent and non-delivery staff are not subtracted; each additional unit's gross profit is what the owner reinvests or pockets.
- **CAC calculation:** add up marketing payroll + ad spend + sales commissions over a period, divide by number of customers. He recommends measuring over a full year to remove volatility. Example: $10K ads + $10K payroll = $20K ÷ 20 customers = $1,000 CAC.
- **LTGP calculation:** price − cost of goods sold, times number of purchases (or price ÷ churn %). Example: $2,000/mo at 20% churn = $10,000 LTGP; equivalently $2,000 × 5 months.
- **A high ratio = "license to print money."** At 100:1 with a reliable customer source, you can be a tenth as efficient on advertising and still get 10:1 returns; business can create disproportionate wealth because "you can put $1 in and get $100 back tomorrow."
- **The agency counter-example:** an agency bragging about 5:1 ROAS was losing money — after subtracting ad spend ($1K) and delivery cost ($1,100 per account from reps at $5K/mo handling 5 accounts) from $2,000 revenue, they had turned $2,000 into zero. Lesson: they mistook revenue for gross profit.
- **Starbucks example:** ~38,000 mostly corporate-owned locations grown without heavy franchising because per-customer LTV is ~$14,199 (stated in the video) while local-food CAC is tiny; Alex cites a personal cookie-company campaign where leads cost under $1 and 1-in-10 walked in = ~$10 CAC, a 100:1 return. (See note on figure.)
- **A high ratio buys a legal/ethical "monopoly":** since ad markets are attention auctions, whoever can afford the highest CAC outbids 100% of competitors and takes their customers.
- **Stacking LTV via a back end:** the hair-extensions coach had a 34:1 front end ($2,000 course, ~$67 CAC) and a 7:1 coaching offer ($15,000). Advice: stop advertising the expensive one, make the cheap high-ratio offer the front end, ascend 20% into the $15K back end (CAC on back end ≈ zero). 20% × $15K = $3,000 added to the $2,000 front → $5,000 LTV, taking the ratio from ~30:1 to ~75:1.
- **Choose the offer with the best LTV:CAC to lead with**, then stack everything else behind it — unless TAM is tiny (most owners are nowhere near market saturation).
- **Operational efficiency is baked into LTV:** productizable offers (shower doors vs. custom glass; modular pools vs. resort pools) can have a *higher* LTV:CAC even at a lower ticket because delivery is cheap, fast, repeatable, and the TAM is bigger. Pick the "shower door" in your business.
- **Business at the top is all the same** — books, pools, shower doors, or agency services all end with the same org (sales, marketing, legal, finance, ops); so start with the offer most likely to get you there rather than "following your passion."
- **Gym Launch's first year ran at 100:1** — Alex says he spent $100,000 and made $10 million back.
- **Only ~1 in 250 businesses reaches $10M/year;** getting there is mostly knowing "I make $10 for every $1 put in" and running the machine as often as possible.
- **Two levers / eight ways to improve LTV:** raise price, decrease cost of goods, get more purchases, cross-sell, upsell (better), sell more quantity (more), down-sell (worse/smaller to turn a no into a yes), sell fewer/cheaper versions.
- **Improve CAC by efficiency down the funnel;** the closest thing to a "silver bullet" is nailing the offer.
- **Directionality trick:** build **LTV back-to-front** (start from the most expensive thing, stack gross profit backward) but optimize **CAC front-to-back** (early funnel steps affect the largest % of people; you can lift a 1% CTR to 3%, but you can't take a show rate from 60% to 180%). Exception: chase a clear outlier metric (e.g., a 5% show rate vs. 60% industry average = a 12x fix).
- **CAC is fairly constant across players in a market; LTV is the variable** — the durable competitive advantage of the biggest businesses is how much LTV they've stacked, not cheaper acquisition.
- **LTV:CAC generalizes to investing** — cost to buy a company vs. lifetime profit from it; "the product and the customer itself is really the business."
