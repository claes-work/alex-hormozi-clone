# My business has stopped growing..what should I do? (2021-10-01)

## Summary

Hormozi walks through the back-of-the-napkin diagnostic he uses when evaluating a
potential acquisition.com portfolio company, using a real example of a business doing
100 new sales/month and $400K revenue with 380 clients at $1,000/month. He shows how
five to six variables (new sales, current revenue, price, churn, LTV, gross profit)
reveal a business's "hypothetical max" — the asymptote its current model will cap out
at — and thereby locate its binding constraint. This is an early, worked application
of his [[../topics/business/business-diagnostics]] method, grounded explicitly in the
Theory of Constraints.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- (2021) An entrepreneur should be able to run these business calculations in their
  head, back-of-napkin, without waiting on finance for reports — relying on systems to
  make directional decisions makes you weak as an operator.
- (2021) The diagnostic uses five core variables — number of new sales/month, current
  revenue, price, churn, and lifetime value — plus gross profit as a sixth (bonus)
  number. Most people know sales, revenue, and price but not churn or LTV.
- (2021) **Churn** is derived from cancellations: ~13 cancels/week ≈ 50/month; 50 ÷ 380
  active clients ≈ **13% monthly churn**.
- (2021) **LTV** ≈ price ÷ churn: $1,000 ÷ 0.13 ≈ **$7,700 per customer**.
- (2021) **Hypothetical max (asymptote)** ≈ new sales × LTV, or price × active clients
  cap; here the business caps near ~$770K/month (~$9M/year) despite currently doing
  $400K — growth slows as churn eats a bigger share of new business. *(proto "growth
  ceiling / asymptote" framework)*
- (2021) **Lifetime gross profit** = LTV × gross margin: at >90% margins (fulfillment
  <$100 on a $1,000 customer), ~$7,000 — so any CAC below that is profitable.
- (2021) There are only two ways to grow a business: get more customers, or make each
  customer worth more. Period.
- (2021) Cutting churn is often the highest-leverage move: dropping churn 13% → 3%
  raises LTV to ~$33,000 and the cap to ~$3.3M/month with almost all of it margin —
  far easier than 10x-ing acquisition.
- (2021) Buyers/PE value three things multiplied together: growth, stickiness
  (retention), and profit; PE typically wants ~80% annual retention, whereas 13%
  monthly churn keeps <20% of customers by year-end.
- (2021) If you don't know your hypothetical max under your current numbers, you're
  fooling yourself — you can't see where you'll cap out or where the weak points are.
- (2021) **Theory of Constraints**: a business grows up to its nearest constraint; the
  smartest work is doing the fewest things with the most return — find the biggest
  constraint and remove it. *(explicit early statement of his constraint-based
  scaling method)*

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You can only grow a business by getting more customers or making them worth more.
> Period. That's all we have to do."

> "I'm a big believer in the theory of constraints, which is a business will grow up
> to its nearest constraint. And so all I try and do is simply remove the constraints
> of the business to allow it to grow."

> "I see business as organic beings. They want to grow and they are simply being
> constrained by something. And our goal is to find what the biggest constraint is and
> remove it."

> "If you're a business owner and you don't know what your hypothetical max is with
> your current model and your current numbers, you are fooling yourself."

> "Which of these do you think is easier to do? 10x-ing the acquisition or simply
> dropping your churn from 13 to 3%?"

> "The smartest people in the world try and find how to do the fewest things that get
> them the most returns. And removing the biggest constraint is the biggest one of
> those."
