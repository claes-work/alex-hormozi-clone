# Behind The Scenes At Gym Launch Inner Circle Live

## Summary
A recorded Gym Launch coaching call in which a panel of coaches works through the last three months of numbers for a client named Fritz, who owns a ~1,500 sq ft boutique small-group gym in Snellville (North Atlanta) doing roughly 40 members and ~$12K/month. The coaches audit his June stats (─ $1,440 ad spend, 236 leads at under $5/lead, 111 CAC, ~38% conversion, ~30% real churn, 21% net margin) and prescribe a full model overhaul: kill semi-private, cut low-attendance classes, run three large-group classes per day at a 20-person cap, and fill those before adding any capacity. They then drill lead nurture (the 916 rule, five-minute call, boiler room), a shift to in-person small-group sales, an onboarding "nutrition meeting" whose purpose is to "wow" and re-sell supplements, and a "wedding cake" scaling model that layers semi-private and subs on top of large group to reach ~$1M/year out of the facility. The overriding message is focus: put on "blinders" and drive to 150 members before doing anything else. Multiple coaches speak; individual speakers are not labeled in the captions.

## Key claims
> ⚠️ Speaker attribution is uncertain throughout — see Notes. Claims below are attributed to "the coaches" (the Gym Launch panel) unless a stronger signal points to the lead coach.
- With 36 large-group members spread across 16 classes/week, classes are mathematically almost impossible to fill; consolidating to fewer, fuller classes creates better energy and frees the owner's attention.
- Gym-math rule of thumb: with no barbells, budget ~50 sq ft per person; ~1,200 usable sq ft therefore fits ~24 per class.
- Recommendation: kill semi-private, cut the dead 7pm and single-person 5pm slots, and run three large-group classes/day at a 20-person cap — a hypothetical max of ~120 across three days a week that two people (owner + wife) can run.
- Don't add a new class until you are at ~70–75% of hypothetical max (roughly 100–115 members); fill and create culture in existing classes first.
- Semi-private should be a status/VIP layer added later; keeping a near-empty semi-private next to full large-group is demoralizing and hurts perceived value.
- CAC benchmark: under $140–150 is fantastic; under $250 is fine for a six-week challenge; under $150 means "scale to the moon." Fritz's $111 CAC and sub-$5 leads are strong.
- Churn math: if signups roughly equal or trail losses while total count drops, real churn is far higher than reported (Fritz's real churn ~30%, not the 7–13% shown); numbers must reconcile.
- Average client value (EFT revenue ÷ EFT members) had fallen three months running to ~$146 in June — a warning sign that legacy/discounted members and mispriced conversions are dragging value.
- Optimal challenge-to-membership conversion is ~70%; Fritz averaged ~38%.
- Lead nurture: implement the "916 rule" — clear the lead inbox at 9, 1, and 6 every day — plus a five-minute call on every new lead; owner should join the "boiler room" daily.
- "There's no such thing as a bad prospect"; getting the prospect in is the owner's obligation.
- Switch from phone appointments to in-person small-group sales: book ~6 seats per block so a 40–50% show rate still seats two to three people, who often close each other; isolate people with questions and close the rest first.
- If a prospect won't come in to meet, that is a signal they won't join — meeting in person is a genuineness filter.
- Record every sales appointment (in person via a laptop/Zoom screen recording, faces not required) for training review; aim for 50%+ close across the board.
- Onboarding "nutrition meeting" is mandatory before any workout; its one-word purpose is "wow" — make the client feel they finally found their home — and it is also the moment to (re-)sell supplements.
- Selling supplements is framed as an ethical obligation: product buyers are more committed, get better results, and stay longer, so declining to sell them harms the client.
- Focus discipline ("blinders" / Kentucky Derby analogy): the client already has the "jockey" (the coaching team); he must add blinders and go "EFT or bust" — head down, no price changes, no new plays, until 150 members.
- Work backward from take-home, not revenue: at a 21% net margin, a $20K/month take-home requires ~$95K/month top-line; track net margin monthly and reverse-engineer the revenue target from it.
- "Wedding cake" scaling model: Layer 1 large group (150 EFTs at $49/wk ≈ $31.6K/mo + ongoing challenge sales) → Layer 2 semi-private via a "golden ticket" upsell (~30 people in 8 weeks ≈ $75K, plus straight $3K/12-week sign-ups) → subs commission on top, compounding toward ~$1M/year from a 1,600 sq ft facility.
- Bump ad spend to ~$100/day given the strong lead economics to accelerate reaching 150 members.
