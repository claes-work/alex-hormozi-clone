# Helping an Online Service Business Fix Their Offer

## Summary
Live consulting clip. The client runs an online coaching business for **live poker players** ("we help live poker players win more money who are already winning"), doing ~$2M/year and wanting to reach $10M. They have two front-end offers that both convert well to the back end but overlap heavily: a high-ticket $7,500 / 28-day program (~12 sold/month, ~$100k/mo) and a cheaper $2,800 / 7-day program (~16–32 sold/month, ~$50–70k/mo). They're **demand-constrained** on the high-ticket and **supply-constrained** on the cheaper one (it needs more coaches). Their question: differentiate the two, collapse to a single ascension offer, or drop the front end entirely? Alex's counsel is **sequencing over cutting**: don't cut either offer yet, because the cheaper one is their only scalable demand-gen right now — cut it and "you'll just lose money" since they can't acquire customers on demand. His fix order: (1) **fix ops first** — reconfigure delivery so they get 3–5x operational capacity; (2) **turn on paid ads** to bring in enough volume (their warm inbound is growing but slow); (3) only *then* make the split/cut call, because by then they'll have enough $7,500 sales to triple that line and drop the other. On pricing, Alex resists an off-the-cuff hike and prescribes a **pricing deep-dive / price analysis** first. He tells a portfolio story of an acquired business so underpriced they doubled the price (it took nine calls to convince the founder), after which **conversion went up** because the old price was so cheap buyers didn't believe it. Because this client sells to **gamblers**, Alex flags a possible **Veblen good** dynamic — the more expensive it is, the more they'll believe it — but insists on research before a pricing recommendation for that specific avatar. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex advises **not cutting either offer** while a business lacks on-demand customer acquisition: the cheaper front-end offer is often the only scalable demand-gen, so cutting it just loses money until paid acquisition is working.
- As of March 2026, Alex's fix sequence for this situation is **ops first, then paid ads, then the split/cut decision** — reconfigure delivery to unlock 3–5x operational capacity before pouring in paid traffic.
- As of March 2026, when two products are very similar and overlap so much they don't upsell into each other, Alex leans toward eventually consolidating — but only after capacity and demand are solved, at which point you can triple the preferred (higher-ticket) line and drop the other.
- As of March 2026, Alex warns that relying on organic content for leads means "you get what you get"; paid ads are "the thing that's going to unlock" scale once ops can absorb the volume.
- As of March 2026, Alex won't give an off-the-cuff pricing recommendation for a specific avatar — he prescribes a **pricing deep-dive / price analysis** first ("we do price analysis all the time").
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that a price can be so low it *hurts* conversion because buyers don't believe it; in a portfolio case they doubled an acquired company's price and **conversion rate went up**.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes some markets — especially selling to **gamblers** — may treat the product as a **Veblen good**, where a higher price increases perceived value and belief, so raising price can raise demand.
