# Life Changing "Dad Advice" for Father's Day

## Summary

For Father's Day, Alex Hormozi shares the three pieces of advice his own father gave him, then reacts to ~20 of the most powerful and universal "dad advice" responses distilled from 600+ replies to a Twitter poll he ran to his ~300,000 followers. The three from his father — "whatever you do, be the best," "they can never take away your education," and "God gave you the power to ignore, use it" — are framed against his father's biography as an Iranian refugee. The rest of the video is Alex commenting on crowdsourced maxims about accountability, prioritization, discomfort, risk, decision-making, and long-term thinking. Alex is the sole speaker throughout.

## Key claims

- His father's three core pieces of advice were: "be the best," "they can never take away your education," and "God gave you the power to ignore, use it."
- The "be the best" frame made him someone who always wanted to win, but also forced constant comparison to others — a frame he says was hard to break and made him "a little bit resentful," and which he has spent much of his life trying to redefine (redefining what "winning" means).
- "Our best is usually significantly further than what we call our best" — there is far more effort available in the tank than people default to, especially when they genuinely want to achieve something.
- The "power to ignore" lesson taught him that "no one cares, so you shouldn't either"; he treats ignoring menial complaints as a muscle that strengthens with age.
- Fault and responsibility are not the same: something may not be your fault but is still your responsibility — accountability gets people out of victimhood.
- Prioritization requires accepting forks in the road you won't take; most people sit at the fork for decades instead of choosing. Trying to please everyone / be everywhere means you're nowhere you need to be.
- You choose your discomfort by how you perceive it; modern "mental masturbation culture" around suffering exists because lives are so easy (why Tough Mudders proliferate).
- "Leave things better than you found them" / anything worth doing is worth doing well — going above and beyond makes people associate your identity with that behavior.
- You can learn something from everyone; asking "what are they better than me at?" makes every conversation additive and makes you interesting.
- Success requires that people who knew your flawed earlier self deny your growth ("you've changed") — "if the people that you grew up with are not denying aspects of what you have accomplished then it means you're not successful enough yet."
- By the time you have all the information, the opportunity is closed — you must decide with imperfect information; risk is unavoidable because no one can predict the future.
- "Just because it's a good opportunity, it doesn't mean it's your good opportunity" — distinguishing a good opportunity from your good opportunity is among the most important decisions in life.
- Judge people by their actions, not their words or intentions.
- Whoever has the longer time horizon always wins; a long time horizon frees you from the day-to-day comparison game.
- Avoiding mistakes (per Buffett/Munger) beats chasing great decisions: "a string of extraordinary numbers followed by zero still equals zero" — humans are better at spotting threats than opportunities, so eliminating threats navigates you to success by default.
- "Strong beliefs, loosely held" — successful founders commit to an idea but don't attach to it until a better one appears; "a sign of intelligence is being able to hate an idea, love a person at the same time."
- The opposite of love is not hate but apathy; you can flip love to hate instantly, but it takes a long time to move from neutral to either.
