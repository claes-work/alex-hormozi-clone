# Are You Even Trying...

Short @MoreMozi clip. An interviewer asks Alex what holds people back from
excelling beyond average; Alex answers. Solo Alex content (the interviewer only
prompts — nothing here is attributed to the interviewer for persona training).

## Summary

Alex argues the thing separating high performers from average is simply the
**number of actions taken per unit of time** — not talent or belief. People "do
a lot of talking about doing and not a lot of doing," and they wildly
underestimate the volume required. He illustrates with the flyer story from his
first gym: acting on a mentor's advice, he put out 300 flyers total over two
weeks, got one call (a stranger saying he'd dinged their Mercedes), and was ready
to quit — until the mentor revealed his own operation tested 5,000 flyers per
batch and, once a winner was found, ran 5,000 flyers a day (~150,000/month). Alex
generalizes: people think a top performer is doing 2–3× their work, when it's
usually 1,000× or 10,000× more. He anchors it in his own content output (~450
pieces of content a week; Twitter/X as home base) and in sales — a rep taking 5–6
live calls a day over 90 days has ~400 calls, versus a top closer who has closed
4,000 deals (~12,000 calls at 30% close), i.e. ~30× the work. On how to compete,
his answer is: **just start.** Much of the delay when people begin is the time
they waste searching for an easier way before finally accepting there isn't one —
"the hard way is the only way." The clip closes with a promo for his free
acquisition.com/roadmap (10-stage zero-to-$100M+ roadmap).

## Key claims

All dated 2026-02-20, paraphrased unless quoted.

- What holds people back from beyond-average performance is not talent or belief
  but that they simply don't do very much — it comes down to number of actions
  taken per unit of time. (paraphrase)
- Most people dramatically underestimate the volume of activity required to
  succeed. (paraphrase)
- Flyer story (first gym): acting on a mentor's advice he put out 300 flyers in
  total over two weeks and got a single call; the mentor tested 5,000 flyers per
  batch and, after finding a winner, ran 5,000 flyers a day — ~150,000 a month
  versus Alex's 300. (paraphrase; biographical anecdote)
- Alex's team makes ~450 pieces of content a week across many platforms, with
  Twitter/X as the "home base" from which everything is generated. (paraphrase)
- Doing one post every day for 90 days (90 posts) already puts someone in roughly
  the top 1% because most people give up — but Alex produces that same 90 posts'
  worth in about two days. (paraphrase)
- People assume a leader is doing 2–3× their work; more often the leader has done
  1,000× or 10,000× the work. (paraphrase)
- Sales illustration: a rep taking 5–6 live calls a day for 90 days has ~400
  calls; a top closer ("John") has closed 4,000 deals — at ~30% close that's
  ~12,000 calls, i.e. ~30× (Alex first overstates as "300 times") the work. That
  volume, not innate ability, is why he's better. (paraphrase)
- To compete with someone that far ahead: just start. (paraphrase)
- A large share of the delay in getting started is the time people spend trying
  to find an easier way; they only begin once they give up looking and accept the
  hard way is the only way. (paraphrase) ★ L3-candidate — the "delay = time spent
  searching for an easier way" framing is a fresher angle on the well-worn
  effort/volume theme.
- Promo: acquisition.com/roadmap — a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+
  that less than 1% of companies finish, broken down by eight business functions
  (constraint, symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate), applied across
  software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar businesses.
  (paraphrase; promotional)
