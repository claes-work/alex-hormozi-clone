# He Makes $600,000,000/Yr Selling Bacon

## Summary

A long-form case-study interview in which Alex Hormozi interviews Mike Salguero, founder and
CEO of ButcherBox, a grass-fed-meat subscription box doing ~$550M/yr, bootstrapped and
profitable since day one. Salguero walks through his prior failure (custommade.com, a
VC-funded marketplace that foreclosed after 8 years) and the founding of ButcherBox: a
Kickstarter pre-sale, influencer/affiliate arbitrage, the accidental "free bacon" offer, and
obsessive per-box unit economics. Throughout, Alex overlays his own frameworks — pre-selling
over raising, "solving for zero," who-not-how, offer stacking, money-back guarantees, and
content as top-of-funnel — making this primarily a vehicle for Alex's business commentary.

## Key claims (Alex's business lessons)

- **Pre-selling beats raising capital.** After his first gym (funded from savings), Alex ran
  multi-month pre-sales — pre-bought memberships and weight-loss challenges — that financed
  each subsequent gym entirely. Pre-sales are also a product-market-fit test: if nobody buys
  the pre-sale, you're grateful you didn't raise money for it.
- **"Solve for zero."** When Alex tries to *build a business* it fails; when he asks "how do I
  make money that doesn't take a ton of my time?" he builds a good one. Solving for no-time +
  profit also makes the thing scale more easily, because it started low-risk and profitable.
- **Who, not how/what.** Early-career entrepreneurs are tactic-obsessed; past a point business
  is about *who*. The leverage is finding the person who already lived the 15–20 years in an
  industry (e.g. the ex-Omaha Steaks operator) instead of reliving it yourself.
- **Deal in the currency an influencer cares about — often fame, not money.** ButcherBox's
  "Trojan horse" (asking influencers to contribute a recipe for the box) worked because it
  paid them in exposure; Alex frames this as matching the counterparty's preferred value.
- **Reverse-engineer a competitor's affiliate/customer list to find high-value targets (HVTs).**
  Alex's own Gym Launch origin: a VA scraped every CrossFit affiliate's contact info; a
  ~1,600-name list was enough to seed a Facebook lookalike audience that "built Gym Launch."
- **Offer stacking and "for-life" offers.** Alex praises free-bacon-for-life and stacked
  for-life offers (ground beef, etc.) as retention flywheels — customers with six or seven
  for-life offers never leave. He has a book coming out on this offer structure.
- **Money-back guarantees: the fear is misplaced.** Refund rates are tiny, the conversion lift
  is worth it, and the fix for the fear is "make your product better." Track "dollar per box"
  and also "delight per box."
- **Content = "show, don't sell."** Alex's content model is demonstration; selling follows.
  He keeps all his courses free with no login, which beat hiding them behind an opt-in wall —
  because most people never log in, and the brain can't tell whether content was behind a
  paywall. Free content spins the referral machine and drives top-of-funnel.
- **Rush is where mistakes happen** — you grow fast *without intending to*, over-hire (the
  bloated "middle"), and lose the pirate culture (Alex ties this to the "$500M trap").
- **No rip cord.** Success partly comes from having no mental option to quit — like a marriage
  where divorce is off the table you actually solve problems: "I either die or we keep going."

> and profit? But when you solve that problem, it also scales a lot easier, because you already
> started with the premise of how do I have it be no time, not be as risky, and be profitable."

> "In the beginning it's always about how... you're very tactic obsessed. Beyond a certain
> point it just becomes a lot about who, because the leverage is in not having to live the 15 or
> 20 years or however long this guy had been in the steak business."

> most. And a lot of influencers care more about fame than they do about money."

> "Fix your product. Make your product better. Spend time obsessing over your product and you'll
> do very well with money back."
