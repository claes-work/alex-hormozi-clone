# The SEASON OF NO (What it Takes to Win)

## Summary

Hormozi makes the case for entering a "season of no" — a temporary (three months to
three years) period of extreme focus in which you say no to almost everything to protect
the one goal that matters most. He frames every yes as a withdrawal from a personal energy
account and every no as a deposit into a goal account, and walks through the categories of
things worth refusing: opportunistic side deals, work below your pay grade, family
obligations, and business "quick calls."

## Key claims

- Growth comes in a "season of no": a temporary, not permanent, period of extreme focus
  where you say no to almost everything to protect the single most important goal.
- Most people fail not because they lack effort but because they don't do the thing that
  matters most enough times, don't do it long enough, or are doing the wrong thing.
- Task-switching is costly — he cites research that switching between two tasks makes them
  take ~4x longer than doing them sequentially; interruptions therefore "4x" your time.
- Family "obligations" are actually family choices/trade-offs; you must let other people's
  version of you die so your own goals can live.
- To change a behavior, change the environment rather than trying to change yourself —
  leave no space for anything but the work, and productivity becomes the default.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "A season of no isn't forever but it's just for now — and sometimes seasons can be three
  months, sometimes seasons can be three years."
- "You have to kind of trick your brain into thinking that the no is actually a yes to the
  thing that matters most."
- "I like to think of yeses that I give to other people as withdrawals from my personal
  energy account, and I think of no's as deposits into my goal account."
- "You have to let other people's dream of you die so that your dream can live, because
  there's only so much of you to go around."
- "It's not that they're doing things you aren't — it's that you're doing things they
  aren't. They're choosing to say no to more activities than you are."
- "When I need to change a behavior I try and change the environment rather than changing
  myself, because it's way easier to change everything else than to change you."
