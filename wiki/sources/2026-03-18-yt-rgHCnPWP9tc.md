# The Single Greatest Habit That Helped Me Grow Acquisition.com

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex names the single greatest habit behind Acquisition.com's growth: a **memo system** — every high-stakes decision is first written down as a memo, defined as "crystallized, written down documentation of a thinking process of how we're thinking through a higher stakes decision." Everything at the company begins as a memo: the content cadence, the book launch, Scale Advisory, the playbooks, even ACQ.ai feature sets. The purpose is to **memorialize the thinking process so it can be disseminated throughout the organization** — because the hardest thing to translate as a company grows is *shared thinking*. He argues that when leadership sits two, three, or four levels away from ownership, people "go rogue" and start mini-cultures and mini-contexts; poorly rolled-out changes almost always trace back to leaders failing to provide adequate context. Memos operationalize that context: they force everyone to slow down, think through costs and benefits, and be willing to **defend the memo to other intelligent people**, which means anticipating naysayers and pre-solving their objections inside the document. Doing this repeatedly clarifies and sharpens thinking. Alex layers an AI step onto his own process: he writes a memo, then pairs it with a reusable **memo prompt** plus a data source and his best all-time memo, instructing the model to "make this look like that" and surface blind spots he hasn't considered. Of ~20 questions it returns, maybe 10 are irrelevant and 5 obvious, but ~5 are good and get woven back in to make a robust memo. His founder goal is **"one-hit-KO" memos** — every possible objection pre-answered — which he frames as a real-world sales letter that also details execution and sells the team on the idea. Memos become durable **artifacts**: new hires read them first to absorb every decision, its context, and the company's direction and execution quality. He notes a side effect: adopting memos quickly reveals that many employees "aren't that smart" — if a team member can't articulate why they'd make a decision, you shouldn't want them making decisions. He credits **Jeff Bezos / Amazon** as the origin; the one difference is that Amazon reads memos at the start of the meeting, whereas Acquisition.com sends them ahead of time, collects comments, then handles the comments live — feasible because they don't have so many big memos that people skip the pre-reading. Ends with the free 10-stage zero-to-$100M+ roadmap promo.
