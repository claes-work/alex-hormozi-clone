# How I See the World More Clearly

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host asks questions; every substantive answer is Alex's, so all quotes below are his). Alex lays out the mental "operating system" he credits with changing his life — a **behavioral lens** he attributes to his relationship with "Dr. Cash" (a behavior-analysis mentor) and develops with his collaborator Trevor. The core claim: **everything comes down to behavior — "what do you do?"** Most conversation, he argues, is "face noise": people trade big amorphous words (energy, vibe, confidence, courage, authenticity, manifestation, identity) that neither party can actually define, so "nothing occurs when people talk." His fix is to **deconstruct every abstract adjective back into the observable behaviors that made you apply the label** — the test being "what would occur in the world or in the environment in front of us for us to know that this adjective had happened?" He works two running examples: (1) instead of telling "Emily" to "stop being lazy" (a label she can't act on), name the specific behaviors, prescribe the replacement behavior, then reinforce it when it appears (find each person's most positive reinforcer — public praise, a free lunch, etc.) to build "reward cycles"; (2) becoming a good speaker isn't copying "confidence," it's minutely observing what a good speaker *does* with feet, hands, and timing, then reproducing those observables. He anchors this in empiricism: humans have only the five senses, "that is what you are limited to, period" — there are no other channels, so "you have observables," not mystical energy. The back half applies the lens to persuasion and sales: you *can* move someone against their supposed identity by controlling variables, chiefly **deprivation** (motivation is "the equal opposite of deprivation" — make the behavior you want the path to the thing they're deprived of); marketing should be **"clear, not clever"**; and buyers are more logical the bigger the purchase (citing Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*), while **advanced buyers translate features into benefits themselves** and prefer specificity, so you can sell them on features (the McLaren carburetor / gym-equipment strength curve), whereas beginners need the "vroom vroom." He also mentions wanting to write a "behavior book" — an operating system for how to see the world — but only after he's achieved enough to publish it from. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's core view of the world is that **everything comes down to behavior — "what do you do?"** — and the skill that matters most is observing the tiny nuances and details of what people actually do.
- As of March 2026, most human communication is "face noise": people exchange words they can't define and act on loose, non-operational meanings, so "nothing occurs when people talk."
- As of March 2026, big amorphous words (energy, vibe, confidence, manifestation, courage, authenticity) are just **shorthand for bundles of underlying behaviors**; they feel meaningful but are ill-defined, and stacking several of them together is why people "try to learn" and it doesn't work.
- As of March 2026, the way to think through any abstract term via a behavioral lens is to ask: **"what would occur in the world / in the environment in front of us for us to know that this adjective had happened?"**
- As of March 2026, labels like "Emily is lazy" should be traced back to the specific observed behaviors that produced them; effective feedback names the concrete behaviors, prescribes the replacement, then **reinforces the desired behavior** with whatever reward is most positive for that person, building "reward cycles."
- As of March 2026, skill acquisition (e.g. public speaking) works by **closely observing what a skilled performer does** — foot placement, when hands go in/out of pockets, timing — and reproducing those observables, rather than chasing undefined traits like "confidence."
- As of March 2026, humans perceive the world only through the five senses; "that is what you are limited to, period" — there is no evidence of any other sense, so apparent "good energy" is really a set of **observables** that can be copied to someone with "bad energy."
- As of March 2026, Alex says he hasn't thought about his own "energy levels" in a very long time — he just looks at outputs: "if I'm a little tired I'll take a stimulant, if I'm a lot of tired I'll sleep, and if I'm no tired I'll work."
- As of March 2026, you *can* get someone to act against their stated "identity"; it comes down to **how many variables you can control**, chiefly deprivation — **"motivation is the equal opposite of deprivation."** To motivate a behavior, deprive the person of something good and make doing the desired thing the way to get it.
- As of March 2026, over time Alex has dropped "the fanciness of marketing and sales" for **"clear, not clever"** — the behavioral lens makes his language more specific, which makes instructions clearer and raises the odds people do the desired behavior.
- As of March 2026, buyers are **more logical the bigger the purchase** and less logical the smaller it is (citing Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*), so whether to sell on logic depends on the purchase.
- As of March 2026, **the more advanced the buyer, the more they translate features into benefits themselves** and prefer specificity — so with advanced buyers you can sell on features (a car expert wants the carburetor/engine specs; Alex wants a gym machine's strength curve), while beginners need emotional/benefit framing ("vroom vroom," "the girls are going to love him").
- As of March 2026, Alex intends to write a "behavior book" with Trevor — an operating system for how to see the world — which he predicts will outsell all his other books combined, but only after he's achieved enough to publish it credibly.
