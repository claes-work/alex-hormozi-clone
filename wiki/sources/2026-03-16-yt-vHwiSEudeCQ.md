# Helping a Brick-and-Mortar Service Business Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client owns a single brick-and-mortar racquet-sports facility in Chatham, New Jersey — 45,000 square feet, ~$3M revenue, ~22% margins, three years old — mostly tennis lessons and group clinics (pickleball just fills dead time and runs poor margins; open court time is "dead"). His stated constraint is **supply**: he can't find enough huge buildings to satisfy demand, and he wants to build "America's next tennis facilities." Alex reframes the problem. First he separates the two strategic paths: (a) keep tweaking the existing model — price, offer, sales process — which is the immediate "double for profit" sitting right there (roughly $600k → $1M+ in profit, three to four million top line), versus (b) actually building a multi-location chain, which requires nailing a replicable model first. He flags that ~$3M revenue sounds low for 45,000 sq ft, so his diagnostic is **revenue per square foot by service category** — find which offering (lessons vs. clinics vs. court time) actually earns per foot. He illustrates with his friend Rick Mayo (Alloy personal training): one 8,000 sq ft store did $3–4M with a juice bar, PT, semi-private, and group all mixed together; when Rick decided to franchise, he examined every service category, found the one with the **highest revenue per square foot and lowest CAC-to-LTV** (semi-private — a three-person model on ~1,200 sq ft doing ~$500–600k top line at ~50% net margins), extracted that "core economic engine," and replicated only that. Alex's counsel: the client likely has a hidden micro-model inside his big facility; instead of hunting for rare 45,000 sq ft buildings, find the smaller footprint (8,000–20,000 sq ft) that's easier to source across more states, nail that unit, then scale it. Order of operations: capture the immediate pricing/offer/sales-process win first, then do the deeper unit-economics dive to pull out the core engine and duplicate it. The client recognizes it live — his margins were ~45% back when he ran three courts (a micro facility) — and concludes he should build micro facilities. Ends with the roadmap promo.
