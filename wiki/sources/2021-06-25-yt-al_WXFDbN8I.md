# How gurus lie about how much MONEY they make...I'm calling it out

## Summary

In this solo teaching video Hormozi enumerates seven ways people measure business value
and shows how "gurus" on the internet exploit the ambiguity of financial terms to appear
richer than they are. He walks each metric, rates it "survey says yes/no" as a trust
signal, and gives viewers the exact questions to ask to see through inflated claims. The
piece doubles as transparency positioning: he repeatedly discloses his own numbers and
admits to using a compelling headline figure himself, framing honesty about the underlying
math as the point of difference.

## The seven value metrics (as presented, 2021-06-25)

1. **Contract value extrapolated** — taking one month (or one event) of contract sales and
   projecting it to a year/eternity. Rated **NO**.
2. **Lifetime revenue** — total revenue ever collected. Rated **NO**.
3. **Business valuation** — market value if sold. Rated **YES, with caveats**.
4. **Yearly revenue** — top-line per year. Rated **partial** (only meaningful next to profit).
5. **Profit / EBITDA** — bottom line per year. Rated **partial/closer to reality**.
6. **Net free cash flow (NFCF)** — owner earnings extractable after reinvestment. Rated **YES**.
7. **Net worth** — what's left "after all the chips have fallen." Rated **YES**.

## The specific deceptions he names (paraphrase, 2021-06-25)

- **Extrapolating a single event's contract value into monthly recurring revenue.** Example:
  sell 100 people a $30k/yr contract at one event (= $250k collected, one-time) and claim
  "we're doing $3 million a month." The contracts are largely unenforceable and the event
  won't recur. Ask: how much cash was **actually collected**, and how reliable are the
  contracts?
- **Quoting lifetime revenue as if it were annual.** "How I built a $50 million business"
  usually means total revenue collected over the life of the business. A McDonald's employee
  collects ~$1M in income over a working lifetime — so a big lifetime number is nearly
  meaningless without the time span.
- **Conflating revenue with cash collected.** Newer entrepreneurs mix contract value (revenue)
  with cash actually processed and received; only the latter is reality.
- **Inventing a valuation with a fantasy multiple.** Claiming "I could get 15× top line"
  when the market pays what the market pays. Reality: sub-$5M/yr businesses typically fetch
  2–3× EBITDA; $10–100M mid-market ~6–8×; $100M+ can reach 12–15×, almost always on EBITDA,
  very rarely on top-line revenue. A headline "100× EBITDA" usually means the business made
  almost no profit (e.g. $10k profit on a $5M sale = 500× EBITDA — impressive-sounding, hollow).
- **Posturing with top-line revenue while hiding thin profit.** He cuts through it by asking
  "what are we doing bottom line per year?"
- **Hiding net worth while flaunting revenue.** People with "half a million dollars to their
  name" tout "$10 million in revenue" (lifetime) as authority; a $1M-lifetime-revenue business
  over 10 years at 50% margins is a ~$50k/yr owner income — the U.S. median — so take the
  authority claim "with a grain of salt."

## Transparency positioning (self-reported, 2021)

- Admits he runs an ad headlined **"$122 million in revenue"** and that he uses the big
  number because "the headline is more compelling than saying we've done mid-30s for the
  last three or four years... I'll play the game too" — but then tries to disclose revenue
  **and** profit so viewers "at least know what to look for."
- States a **ClickFunnels "Two Comma Club X"–style award** for passing **$100M in total
  revenue in ~3.5 years** since starting with ClickFunnels — but stresses it means cash
  collected lifetime, not per year; says 2021 is a "breakout year" and he expects to come
  close to (but not cross) $100M in the year.
- Advocates **extracting dividends while the business grows** to de-risk, contrasting himself
  with the Silicon Valley "bet everything on yourself" ethos: "there are so many more stories
  of guys who bet everything on themselves at the casino and walked away empty-handed."
