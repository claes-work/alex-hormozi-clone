# Helping a $2.2M/Year Cold Email Agency Scale to $10M

## Summary
Alex Hormozi advises the founder of a high-volume cold-email agency (roughly $2.2M/year on ~$10K/month retainers, serving B2B SaaS and high-LTV B2B service clients, sending up to 10 million AI-written one-to-one emails per client per month) who wants to switch to a performance-based pricing model to reach $10M. Alex's central warning is that performance pricing lives or dies on getting paid: it works only when the provider controls the money flow or has fully transparent tracking, and the caller's mostly-SMB client base makes non-payment the core risk. He also recommends restructuring pricing around a high one-time "find message-market-fit" fee before any ongoing/performance tier.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues performance-based pricing is "always the best model on paper, not always the best model in practice," and that its viability is "strongly predicated on the quality of the prospect."
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that enterprise customers with assets and obligations adhere to contracts, whereas small and even medium business owners do so far less; he adds that "US contracts mean basically nothing," stacking risk against the provider.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises that with performance models you must either control the flow of payments (get paid first, then remit the client's share — as he did with Allan/gym model) or have absolute transparency in tracking (e.g. Shopify email agencies using Klaviyo, where tracking is clear and no one starts a second store to cut you out).
- As of March 2026, Alex frames incentive design as decisive: a model where the client self-reports results "sets up an incentive system that incentivizes them to cheat you," and "if they have to cheat them or cheat you, they will cheat you."
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends a two-part pricing structure: a significantly higher one-time up-front fee (zero recurring) to solve the single problem of finding message-market-fit, then introducing a separate ongoing/performance tier to actually run the volume.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims a one-time offer is far easier to sell than an ongoing one — e.g. moving from a $1,500/month membership to a $5,000 one-time up-front can "double or triple close rates," later self-correcting to "9X" close rates at triple the price.
- As of March 2026, Alex prefers scaling price against a known-ROI "north star metric" (e.g. a rate per million emails) over per-deal custom pricing, though he concedes custom up-front pricing may fit a business with fewer, higher-value customers.
- Alex reasons from the caller's numbers that ~$2M at $10K/month implies ~20 customers who are likely SMBs (not even mid-market), which undermines confidence in payment; he says he'd have "high confidence" in the performance model if the agency only served clients doing over $10M ARR and venture-backed.
- Caller claims (unverified): the agency does $2.2M/year, believes it can reach $10M within about a year, has churn around 30% annually, is billing some clients $75K–$100K/month on early performance deals, and that clients leave mainly due to failure to find offer/message-market-fit rather than poor performance.
