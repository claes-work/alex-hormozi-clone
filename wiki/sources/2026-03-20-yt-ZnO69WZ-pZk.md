# Fixing Pricing in a Service Business

## Summary
In a short coaching exchange, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a San Diego restaurant exhaust-hood cleaning business (six months in, ~$6K/month, ~$100K yearly recurring book) who feels stuck competing on price for a seemingly commoditized service. Hormozi argues the owner must first stop believing the service is a commodity, then engineer differentiation by removing everything that "sucks" about competitors. He also gives concrete pricing/offer mechanics: down-sell from a higher anchor, optimize service cadence for gross margin, and stack cash up front.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames quality as "the absence of hard": good service means removing friction so all that is left is the outcome; you cannot make something easy, you can only make it "not hard."
- As of March 2026, Alex says the operator must believe before anyone else that the service is not commoditized; the way to de-commoditize is to list every reason bad providers suck and fix all of them.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises anchoring high and down-selling (e.g., "start at four [cleanings], down-sell to two") and playing with payment terms to stack more cash up front rather than getting paid quarterly.
- As of March 2026, Alex suggests possibly cleaning less frequently but charging more per visit, raising gross margin while the customer pays less over the year; and later increasing route density for efficiency and margin.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he puts all his effort into answering "why is this different?" before thinking about pricing at all, and must be able to articulate the difference simply to the buyer.
- Caller claim (unverified): the business is a subscription service with a legally required cleaning cadence (at least twice a year), currently sold on 1-year agreements, with door-to-door knocking and cold email as its only outreach.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "There's good, fast, and cheap. Pick two."

> "You have to believe before anybody else that it's not a commoditized service."

> "Good is basically the absence of hard."

> "You can't make something easy. You can only make it not hard. When something's easy, all the hard vanishes and all that's left is the outcome."

> "If you're the same thing to the customer, these two things are about the same, I'll buy the cheaper one. It's the worst comparison. It's not the fight you want to fight."

> "I don't even think about pricing. I don't think about anything until I answer that question."

> "Here's the five things that happened that suck. Here's how we don't have any of those five. And for that in exchange, instead of paying this, you pay this. But this is why it's worth it."

> "Start at four, down-sell to two, if you can, and then probably play with payment terms. Which is like, can I stack more of that cash up front."
