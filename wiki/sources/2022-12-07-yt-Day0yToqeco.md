# Why Looking Poor is actually Smart

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out the deliberately frugal, "stealth-wealth"
lifestyle he ran in his early entrepreneurial years — living on ~$1,500/month or less
while taking home ~$20,000/month — so he could stockpile capital and stay risk-tolerant
enough to "go on the offensive." He breaks spending into three cost centers (food,
transportation, housing), argues that living cheap raises your risk tolerance and makes
you "hard to stop," and pivots into a set of habits around geography, relationship-building,
and daily bank-account tracking. He frames it all as maximizing both sides of the game:
spend as little as humanly possible while making as much as possible.

## Key claims

Alex-attributed (spending / stealth-wealth view):

- He lived on ~$1,500/month while taking home ~$15,000–$20,000/month; he lived that way
  because he is "incredibly risk-averse" AND because he wanted cash to go on the offensive
  later. Making $20k/month never felt like "making it" — he wanted to shoot much bigger.
- The three big cost centers are **food, transportation, and living (housing)**.
- **Food:** he ate out every day at chain restaurants ("Mosy Meals") rather than cooking,
  because his time was worth more spent making money. Rotation: Chipotle (twice a day),
  Panda Express (protein-hacked with extra chicken), and Chick-fil-A. Cost ~$15/day then
  (~$20/day now) → roughly $600–$700/month.
- Grocery shopping + meal prep actually cost him ~$150/week (~$600/month) as a single guy
  and ate 8 hours of a Sunday — so eating out was cheaper on a time-adjusted basis.
- Absolute-cheapest food route: loaves of bread + peanut butter + a multivitamin (add
  lactaid milk as a third staple). He ate peanut-butter sandwiches to bulk, on top of
  normal calories, but didn't want to live on them.
- **Transportation ("Mosymobiles"):** buy a used 4-door car, 5–10 years old, in **cash**
  for ~$10,000 — no car payments. Cheaper car = cheaper insurance (~$100/month). Never
  take a loan for the car.
- **Housing ("mansions"):** he first slept on the floor of his gym (rent was $5,000/month
  as his business rent), then stayed at a gym member's place for free, then split a single
  bedroom in a 7-bedroom house with a roommate — $400/month for his half, mattress off
  Craigslist on the floor, fan by his face. House had multiple dogs and was filthy.
- Move to "where shit's happening" even for $200–$400/month — being around people doing
  things with their lives raises your standard of excellence. Every early-career move he
  made was to be around people.
- Wealth signal: judge how wealthy someone will become by the **increments they measure
  time and money in** — paychecks/hundreds vs. decades/$100k investments.
- "Handshakes over hacks" — the **who** opened more doors than any **what**; his big deals
  all trace one or two degrees from people he met.
- Relationship rules: don't turn down the first invite; do 30 min of prep research before
  meeting someone; over-deliver value ("blow them away," make them feel awkward) so favors
  actually land; the ultimate leverage is not needing anything back — "dig the well before
  you're thirsty."
- **Plan B was always: strip at night, drive Uber during the day.** If you make under
  $60–70k/year, a paid-off qualifying car (4 doors, ≤15 years, no visible damage) lets you
  drive Uber — flexible, immediate income, low risk.
- Living cheap raises risk tolerance: "it's very hard to fight someone who has nothing to
  lose / can live on nothing." He never tracked an income-to-spending ratio — he just
  maximized both sides.
- Dave Ramsey hack he endorses: pull the whole month's cash, put it in your wallet, don't
  spend past it. Don't obsess over credit-card points ("wrong percentage") — just be
  disciplined.
- **Daily bank-account tracking:** first thing every morning he checked his bank balance
  (not Instagram/texts) and logged it in a Google Sheet. Gamify money; attention improves
  what you measure (like a daily scale for weight loss). "Money sticks to the person who
  pays it the most attention."
