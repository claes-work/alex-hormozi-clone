# Scaling a Sales Team Agency from $14M to $100M

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A in which **Alex Hormozi** advises the owner of a done-for-you sales-team agency (builds and manages sales teams/processes for online, service, and coaching businesses; ~$14M revenue, wants $100M in 3 years) whose margins are compressing as it scales. Alex diagnoses the problem as a bottom-up unit-economics/ICP issue rather than a broken business model: the fix is to profile customers by profitability, find the most profitable avatar (ICP), and template/automate service around that avatar so margins hold or expand as the company scales, while pruning fulfillment activities that don't correlate with retention. He closes with his standard acquisition.com/roadmap call-to-action. The owner supplies situational context (not persona data); only Alex's advice is captured below.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **All service-based businesses have a version that is profitable enough to attract and pay high-level talent** — the agency's model (charging a % of client top line) is not fundamentally wrong; you should be able to make plenty of money on 25% of someone else's top line.
- **Service businesses, especially B2B, have prices that inevitably rise over time.** As you build reputation you get more demand than supply (a good service business is always supply-constrained), so you raise prices, which lets you afford more A-players, which raises reputation and demand further — "the virtuous cycle of services." Talent tiers climb from ~$100K to ~$250K to multi-million-dollar-per-year people.
- **Margin compression while scaling is usually the cost of building an asset.** Previously fat margins often reflected unpaid/underpaid founders doing the work of many people; once you pay a real leadership/fulfillment team, margins compress. The key question is whether they compress and then stop, or keep compressing — and what specifically keeps eating the margin.
- **The pricing fix is bottom-up, not top-down.** Rather than raising the take-rate (e.g. 25%→30%), pursue larger clients so each rep generates more revenue — "more zeros on their price tag." You'd make little selling $100 gym memberships and a lot selling $100M buildings.
- **Profile customers by profitability to define the ICP, then re-target messaging around it.** Compute profitability per customer, identify the most profitable avatar (it may be "left of center" from your current broad targeting), and rebuild sales/marketing to attract that avatar.
- **Serving a single specific avatar unlocks operational efficiency** — templated service levels, automation of portions of the work — which lets margins expand or at least hold as the company scales.
- **Audit fulfillment activities against retention.** Some rep activities correlate with retention, some don't, and some may actively hurt it; identify and prune the ones that don't drive retention. This is a people-ops problem.
- **Don't just add margin by cutting service.** Fulfillment teams were forced to manage fewer clients because churn was too high / success rate too low — so the real lever is finding the profitable avatar and operationalizing service, not simply raising prices to a churn-inducing level.
