# Wealth Principle #1: Get Paid First

## Summary

Alex Hormozi lays out what he calls the "money ladder" — a hierarchy of who gets
paid, and when, in any exchange of work for money. He argues that watching the
flow of money (who fronts time and effort, who splits payment, who gets paid in
full up front, and who gets paid first if things go bad) reveals who holds control
and captures wealth. The ladder runs from employees at the bottom up through
self-employed contractors, "paid-first" professionals, banks, insurance companies,
and — half-jokingly — God/franchisors at the top.

He frames "get paid first" as one of the wealth beliefs that changed his life and
made him wealthier. Leila (his wife) is credited as context only, for suggesting
the topic; the framework and all claims are Alex's.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- (2021) The "money ladder" is the order in which people get paid; how you view
  the flow of money is one of the biggest beliefs that changed as (and before) he
  got wealthy, and helped cause that wealth.
- (2021) To locate power in any transaction, watch who gets paid when: who fronts
  time/effort and waits, who splits payment, who is paid in full up front, and who
  gets paid first if things go bad.
- (2021) **Bottom — Employee:** works first, then gets paid two-to-four weeks
  later; fronts time and effort, absorbs the delay. (He notes nothing is "wrong"
  with this; he was an employee too.)
- (2021) **Self-employed / contractors / gig:** flexible position on the ladder;
  worst case they mirror employees (work first, paid later), better case they move
  to 50% up front / 50% on delivery, and as they gain independence they get paid
  up front or as they work.
- (2021) **Paid-first professionals:** like a doctor who is paid before performing
  surgery — getting paid before delivering (and paid in full, not just partial) is
  a higher level of wealth, control, and power; driven by demand and skill.
- (2021) **Banks:** hold a "capital stack" as preferred creditors — when you buy a
  house the bank effectively buys it and you repay them; if things go bad the bank
  gets paid first and you get the scraps.
- (2021) **Insurance:** ranks above banks — paid for years (potentially decades)
  before ever having to fulfill anything, and may never have to pay out. It is the
  reverse of a depreciating asset: instead of paying capital up front and watching
  it depreciate, insurers collect money up front and let it *appreciate* over time.
  He notes insurers are among the world's oldest companies (180–300+ years) and
  that the model predates the tax code.
- (2021) **Top — "God and franchisors":** God gets 10% off the top for life (a
  tithing reference, told partly as a joke).
- (2021) Internalizing where money flows changed how he structured agreements and
  contracts and made him wealthier.
