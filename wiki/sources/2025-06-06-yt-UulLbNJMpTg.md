# give me 14 minutes and you'll learn how to be absurdly happy with your life

## Summary

Solo morning monologue in which Alex Hormozi lays out his theory of dissatisfaction:
we become unhappy because we want everything while having limited resources (time,
energy, life) to get it, and we refuse to accept the trades that any real choice
requires. Prompted by a friend agonizing over what kind of wife to marry, he argues
that all viable paths carry trades, that we overrate the unchosen path's benefits and
underrate its hidden costs, and that the antidote is high commitment plus low
expectations. He frames three life skills — deciding, deciding well, and being happy
with your decision — and offers his personal refrain, "you are the problem," for
catching self-imposed dissatisfaction.

## Key claims (his happiness view)

- Dissatisfaction comes from wanting things you don't have and being unwilling to
  accept the downsides of the trades you already made — wanting to "retrade" what you
  gave up while keeping what you got (paraphrase).
- A core formula for misery: wanting everything while having limited resources
  (time, energy, effort, life) to get it (paraphrase).
- We regret the lives we have because of paths not taken, without accounting for the
  trades we made at the time (paraphrase).
- Any unchosen path has both perceived benefits (probably weaker than imagined) and
  unknown costs (probably worse than imagined); "playing it out" corrects the bias
  (paraphrase).
- Modern dissatisfaction isn't that people/partners are worse, it's that expectations
  are higher — we expect one spouse to be ~10 people at once (he credits this idea to
  Esther Perel; see Notes).
- Three stages of the individual: (1) blame everyone else for what you lack, (2) learn
  to get things but then crave the other path's benefits without its costs, (3) —
  implied — be satisfied with the trade (paraphrase).
- Even deathbed regret reduces to wanting another life's benefits while keeping your
  own; what you're really trading for is novelty, which doesn't actually inform good
  decisions (paraphrase).
- We keep the goalpost moving: change what we want as soon as we get it, so we
  effectively choose to stay dissatisfied (paraphrase).
- The commonality of lasting marriages (and big life choices generally):
  **high commitment, low expectations**. Commitment = the elimination of alternatives;
  low expectations mean reality tends to exceed them, so you live "above expectations"
  for the rest of your life (paraphrase).
- Big life doors are largely irreversible; a prized trait is the ability to both make
  decisions and make good ones, plus the underrated third skill of being happy with
  the decision you made (paraphrase).
- Circumstance doesn't fix it: wives of rich men and poor men are both "not always"
  happy — "humans will human," we'll be dissatisfied independent of circumstance;
  therefore "we are the problem" (paraphrase).
- His self-report: he'd probably be about as happy or unhappy in three different
  lifetimes/countries/upbringings; realizing this dramatically decreased his anxiety
  (self-reported).
