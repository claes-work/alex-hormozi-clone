# "Should I Fire the Customer That's 45% of My Revenue?"

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A. The questioner (introduces herself as **Julie**) sells custom
screen printing and embroidered merch to clubs, groups, and organizations through
e-commerce, with all production in-house. Her stated numbers: **$17.5M revenue**,
long-term goal $100M with $50M as "a good first stop." One big **contract customer**
takes up ~80% of production capacity but only ~45% of revenue — they get paid on the
printing only — while their own direct business (~20% of capacity) is **about three
times more profitable**. She frames the temptation: if they flipped that contract
capacity to their own customers, "we would be a $50M company today." Her actual
bottleneck, by her own admission: "we really suck at getting people to come to our
website." Web and outside sales currently bring in about equal money; she wants to
scale the web side because it sells 24/7 nationally.

Alex answers in three moves. **(1) Don't kill the flow.** Despite the video's title
premise, the first thing he rules out is dropping the big customer: "we never stop a
flow until we create another flow. Ideally, we just don't stop flows." He then checks
capacity — she still has ~25% free, which is enough headroom to double the (3x more
profitable) direct side without touching the contract at all, so the whale-vs-own-
customers dilemma is a false one for now. **(2) Constraint diagnosis, one link at a
time.** He walks the chain with her: what stops more web revenue? → more ads → the
manpower to make them. He explicitly rules out spend and ad-account problems, landing
on **creative volume** as the single constraint: she spends ~$25k/month (~$1k/day) but
runs only ~15 ads a month. His benchmark: get to **~50 pieces of creative per week**,
which he calls "not hard" — the founder can film the raw material "in like an hour and
a half" and then "you just have a couple editors." He sizes the investment against her
profit (from her "not 20%" margin he infers ~$3–4M profit): **three editors at ~$200k
total** won't change her life, "but your business could double. That feels worth it.
You going to do that?" **(3) Hire organic editors, run an organic→paid loop.** When
hiring, look for **organic-content people, not paid-ads specialists**, "because
organic and paid have more or less merged." The proven best organic pieces are the
ones you put money behind anyway; the only paid-specific change is a lower-third
banner with a CTA on an existing winning creative, or a CTA sliced in at the end with
a freebie/lead magnet. Net motion: editors ramp organic → organic gets leads → the
proven organic becomes the paid ads. The video ends with the standard
acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift CTA (10 stages, functions across product, marketing,
sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Never stop a revenue flow until another flow replaces it — and preferably don't
  stop flows at all.** Asked (implicitly, via the title premise) whether to drop a
  contract customer worth ~45% of revenue, Alex's first move is to take "kill the big
  customer" off the table: you don't shut off a working flow to make room for a
  hypothetical better one.
- **Check capacity before framing it as an either/or.** The whale takes 80% of
  capacity, but ~25% is still free — enough to double the direct business (which is 3x
  more profitable) without touching the contract. The dramatic flip-the-whale decision
  is deferred, not taken.
- **Diagnose the constraint one link at a time.** "Let's solve one at a time": more
  web revenue → requires more ads → requires manpower → requires more creative. He
  explicitly clears spend ("it's not a spend issue") and the ad account, isolating
  **creative volume** as the bottleneck — at which point "this feels really solvable."
- **Benchmark: ~50 pieces of ad creative per week.** Against her ~15 ads/month on
  ~$25k/month spend, Alex's prescription is roughly 50 creatives weekly; the founder's
  raw filming input is about an hour and a half, with a couple of editors doing the
  rest.
- **Size the hire against profit, not against payroll fear.** On ~$3–4M profit, three
  editors at ~$200k total is life-neutral for the owner but could double the business
  — a trade he frames as obviously worth taking, closing with a direct commitment ask
  ("You going to do that?").
- **Hire organic editors, not paid-ads specialists — organic and paid have merged.**
  The best proven organic pieces become the paid ads anyway; the only paid-specific
  modification is a lower-third CTA banner on an existing winning creative, or a CTA
  spliced onto the end with a freebie/lead magnet.
- **The organic→paid flywheel for e-com creative:** editors ramp up organic → organic
  generates leads → take all the proven organic and run it as paid.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at
  acquisition.com/roadmap: ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies distilled into
  10 growth stages with a quiz that locates a business and prescribes actions per
  function (product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance);
  thank-you page books a call, with an in-person Vegas session if they can help.
