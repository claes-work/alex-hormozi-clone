# If You Don't Understand Margin, You Don't Understand Business

## Summary
In this clip (part of a live "rules of thumb" teaching session), Alex Hormozi explains gross margin as the difference between what you charge and what it costs to deliver, and argues it is the master variable that dictates everything else in a business. He works through a live pricing example (a $100 cost priced at 70% vs. 80% vs. 90% gross margins) to show how small-looking margin differences produce materially different per-customer profit, and states his personal minimum of 80% gross margins for service businesses. He then adds "rule of thumb #8" — 30-day cash collected — and closes by pitching his book and free 10-stage roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames gross margin as a relationship between two numbers — what you charge minus what it costs to deliver — and distinguishes it from net profit margin.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues net margin can never exceed gross margin; a business with 50% gross margin could reach 50% net only with zero acquisition, overhead, admin, or non-delivery staff cost — which he calls basically impossible.
- As of March 2026, Alex says small business owners chronically undercharge because they "sell out of their own wallet" — either because they lack money themselves, or because they undervalue work that comes easily to them.
- As of March 2026, Alex teaches pricing off the value of the customer's solved problem, not off the ease of delivery.
- As of March 2026, Alex's worked example: at a $100 cost, an 80% gross margin implies a $500 price; 70% implies ~$333 (making ~$233 gross profit per customer); 90% implies ~$1,000. He stresses margins that "look similar" (60% vs 80%) are in "a different stratosphere."
- As of March 2026, Alex states his hard rule: he will not enter a business with less than 80% gross margins, and cites fixing gym pricing to at least 80% as an example that service businesses can hit it.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the way to earn high margins is to de-commoditize the offer via branding (his salt / pink Himalayan example) and a functioning sales process, plugging his book $100M Offers.
- As of March 2026, Alex's "rule of thumb #8" — 30-day cash collected: the gross profit collected in the first 30 days should exceed cost of goods sold plus cost of acquiring the customer, so each customer's cash pays for the next customer's acquisition and delivery.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes manufacturing carries different (COGS-driven) margins, but he still prefers 80% gross margins and holds 80%+ as his rule of thumb for human services.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you are a business owner, you have to learn the language of business. There's not a huge amount of words you have to know. You might need to know like 100 terms. And almost all of them are relationships between two things."

> "Your net margins cannot exceed your gross margins."

> "To quote the Joker, my father always told me, when you're good at something, never do it for free."

> "To a customer, we have to sell off the value of what their life would be like if they didn't have this problem solved. That is what we have to charge off of."

> "When people are like, 'Oh, well, my margins are at 60%, so I'm close to 80.' It's like, bro, you're in a different stratosphere."

> "I will not get into a business with less than 80% gross margins. I won't do it. Because I know that I then have to run everything else off of this 80."

> "If a customer can look at your thing and somebody down the street's thing and say these are about the same, I'll buy the cheaper one, you sell a commoditized service, just like you can sell a commoditized product."

> "If you're trying to figure out what's wrong with your business, it's usually because your margins are off. You're mispriced."

> "We want the gross profit of the cash we collect in that first 30 days to be greater than [cost of goods sold] plus [cost of getting the customer]. Once that occurs, that customer pays you back all of that cost, and then what can you do? Go get you another customer."
