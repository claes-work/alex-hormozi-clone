# Don't Invest in the S&P 500

## Summary
Solo monologue on Alex's personal investing philosophy — framed provocatively as "don't invest in the S&P 500," but the actual argument is narrower and conditional: **when you are early and still building earning capacity, the highest-return use of your capital is investing in yourself and your own skills, not a ~10% index return.** He coins the tag "invest in the SME 500 — invest in you," arguing your personal growth will beat a 10% stock-market return. His core mechanism is that **education/skill acquisition has the highest return on invested capital because it multiplies earning power**, and he illustrates with a retold sales anecdote (a speaker asks a woman earning $50k what she "pays reality" every year for not knowing how to make a million — $950,000, the gap between what she makes and could make). He runs explicit toy math: $10,000 in the S&P 500 becomes ~$10,900 in a year, whereas spending that same $10,000 on skills that triple earning capacity (e.g. $50k → $100k/yr) yields a far larger return — then compounds it forward ($50k → 10x $10k or 2x $25k programs → $100k → $250k earning power). He reframes buying skills as **"buying the future at a discount"** — the closest thing to buying time, pulling years of earning power forward. His "very controversial advice": spend all your savings on education, every year, for as long as you can, until you literally can't spend it fast enough because you're earning too much. He stresses he has never paid for education and not gotten more than he paid, credits a student's mindset ("I will be your biggest success story"; you can learn as much from bad teachers as good, since getting good is "the removal of bad"), and favors practitioners who run real private businesses over tenured academics. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo. **This is his personal opinion/philosophy on capital allocation for early-stage operators, not financial advice, and not a blanket claim that index funds are bad for everyone.**

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that early in your journey the highest-return investment is yourself/your own skills, not the ~10% return of the S&P 500 — "invest in the SME 500, invest in you" (his opinion, conditional on still building earning capacity; not general financial advice).
- As of March 2026, he claims anyone can get rich fast **provided** they are willing to make no money for a long time while getting good enough to get rich fast.
- As of March 2026, he frames the cost of not having a skill as the gap between current and potential income — retelling an anecdote where someone earning $50k "pays reality" $950,000/year for not knowing how to make a million.
- As of March 2026, he claims education is by far the highest return: you can learn to make ~$1M/yr for roughly $200,000/yr, typically spent on conferences and seminars in $2,000–$5,000 (sometimes $30,000) chunks.
- As of March 2026, his toy math: $10,000 in the S&P 500 → ~$10,900 in a year, versus spending the same $10,000 on skills that triple earning capacity ($50k → $100k/yr), which nets far more than the $900 index gain even though the $10k is "spent" (he reframes it as invested in an asset you control).
- As of March 2026, he argues for compounding this forward — reinvesting new income into multiple education "bets" (e.g. $50k into 10x $10k or 2x $25k programs) to lift earning power from $100k to $250k, rather than parking it for a 10% index return.
- As of March 2026, he defines buying skills as "buying the future at a discount" — the closest approximation to buying time, pulling years of earning power forward (his "20 to 25" analogy).
- As of March 2026, his "very controversial advice": spend all your money on education for as long as possible, until you can't spend it fast enough because you're earning too much.
- As of March 2026, he claims he has never paid for education without getting back more than he paid, though he concedes some things he bought weren't worth the money to other people.
- As of March 2026, he credits a specific student mindset: commit to being the vendor's/program's "biggest success story," and choose to succeed rather than to be right that the teacher was bad — you can learn as much from bad teachers as good, because "getting good is the removal of bad."
- As of March 2026, he prefers learning from practitioners running real private businesses over tenured, government-funded academics who "haven't been in reality for 20 years."
- As of March 2026, he defines becoming an expert as doing more repetitions (and thus failing more) than anyone else in a narrow field.
