# How To Know When to Switch Business Models

## Summary

In a live coaching exchange, a 27-year-old founder explains that his company sells "digital business cards" (a Linktree/CRM hybrid) primarily to real estate agents, that he did $150k last year, wants to reach $500k, and is the sole salesperson. Alex advises against scaling the current offer, arguing the founder's real asset is his sales skill and that he should redeploy it onto a better "vehicle." Alex's central heuristic: sell products that customers don't cancel, to an avatar you already know, in a demand-constrained industry with little delivery burden — rather than one where you must create the problem, solve it, and explain the mechanism all in a single pitch.

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, Alex argues the founder would make far more money selling something other than digital business cards, because the offer is a "hard pitch" that forces the seller to create a problem, solve it, and explain an unfamiliar mechanism all within one presentation.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the founder's core asset as the transferable skill of sales, and recommends applying it to a "better opportunity vehicle" rather than a weak product.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises looking for products that customers "don't cancel," ideally serving an avatar similar to the one the founder already sells to (for speed to money).
- As of March 2026, Alex criticizes college entrepreneurship classes for being "invention heavy" (Shark-Tank-style), arguing students would earn more by copying an existing proven business than by inventing something new.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims a person with strong closing skills could reach $2-3M/year with a door-to-door lawn care business, or roughly $800k/year in personal income selling solar. (Illustrative figures, not verified.)
- As of March 2026, Alex describes a path to a full business: learn a new industry by selling within it, then solve lead gen; once you have lead gen and sales, the only remaining piece is delivery.
- As of March 2026, Alex cites insurance sales as "incredibly profitable" because there is essentially no delivery — the seller sells the product while large carriers absorb the risk.
- The founder's business claims (senior design project turned company, $150k year one, ~90% of sales from in-person presentations, sole salesperson) are self-reported and unverified.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Fundamentally you can sell — like you obviously have the skill of sales, and so I'd rather you just like use that on a better opportunity vehicle."

> "The big thing that I hate about those classes is that they try and make seniors invent something. It's like it's very invention heavy. Like everyone thinks it's Shark Tank."

> "You could sell solar and make, you know, 800 grand a year on your own income."

> "Now I look for products that people don't cancel. I would look for products that people don't cancel — probably the people that are similar to who you already sell to."

> "Basically for your current pitch to work, you have to create a problem and then solve a problem all within one presentation. And also explain some stuff that they don't understand how it works. So it's kind of like three things. I'd rather just talk to somebody who immediately already knows the problem that they have and I'd be like, I can solve that."

> "Fundamentally every entrepreneur is a salesman at heart. We're the promoters."

> "A good way to learn a new industry is to sell within that industry, kind of learn the ropes, and then try to basically just figure out lead gen for it. Once you have that, then you'll have lead gen and sales, and then the only thing that you'd really need would be delivery."

> "There's so many businesses that are basically demand constrained where people stick. You just want to find one of those that you like that caters to the existing avatar you currently sell to."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: The "three-burden hard pitch" test — a weak vehicle forces the seller to create the problem, solve it, AND explain an unfamiliar mechanism in one presentation; a strong vehicle sells to a customer who already knows the problem, in a demand-constrained, low-cancellation, low-delivery industry serving an avatar you already know. Sharp phrasing worth promoting for the business/sales "which vehicle to sell" decision.
