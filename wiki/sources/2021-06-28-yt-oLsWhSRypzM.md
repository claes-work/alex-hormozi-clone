# Never start a business until you hear this (3 THINGS THAT MAY...)

## Summary

In this early (2021) advice video, Hormozi answers a question he says he gets
most frequently — "when should I quit my job and start my own business?" He
frames the decision around three things: your mindset and level of
dissatisfaction, the reality that entrepreneurship is hard and lonely, and
realistic financial expectations backed by data. He recounts quitting his
management-consulting job at Booz Allen at 22, selling his Baltimore condo, and
driving to California, calling it the single hardest decision of his life. His
overall stance is cautionary: most people should not become entrepreneurs, and
there are several lower-risk alternatives to try first.

## The 3 things

1. **Mindset / dissatisfaction** — Ask whether you are so deeply sad or
   dissatisfied with your life that you would be willing to uproot everything and
   change. Quitting was the hardest decision of his life; he only made the jump
   because he was deeply depressed and felt empty, not because he had a
   "traditional entrepreneur" story.
2. **Entrepreneurship is not easy (and is lonely)** — You must acquire every
   skill to run a business (generate demand, generate leads, work leads, sell,
   fulfill, manage/recruit/hire/interview, communicate). It is one of the
   loneliest paths; if you need motivational quotes to do it, it isn't for you.
3. **Set realistic financial expectations (data)** — Per SBA figures he cites,
   the median small-business-owner income is $49,000/year and the average is
   $73,000/year. Expect a 3–5 year decrease in income after quitting. Know where
   you sit on the income hierarchy before jumping.

## Key claims

Claims below are paraphrase unless in the verbatim section; all dated 2021-06-28.

- (2021) The most frequently asked question he gets is "when should I quit and
  start my own business?" — his answer: it depends on who you are, your
  self-awareness, skill set, and how much pain you're in.
- (2021) At 22 he was a management consultant at a Booz Allen subcontractor doing
  space/cyber/intelligence work for the military; he had graduated Vanderbilt in
  three years magna cum laude, saved ~$50,000, and owned an appreciating condo in
  Baltimore — yet felt empty and deeply depressed, experiencing passive suicidal
  ideation for about two years.
- (2021) Quitting took him six months of deliberation; he listened to Arnold
  Schwarzenegger's "rules of success" speech every morning because he was so
  status-driven and afraid of being seen as a failure.
- (2021) He describes himself as very risk-averse and says that despite having no
  wife, no kids, zero dependents, and living on nothing, it was still one of the
  hardest decisions of his life.
- (2021) His parents thought quitting was stupid and that he was throwing his
  education away to start a gym — a poor return on investment.
- (2021) For roughly the first four years after quitting he essentially broke
  even and made less money than he did as a consultant.
- (2021) SBA data (as cited): median small-business-owner income is $49,000/year
  (the most likely, most-repeated outcome, roughly US median household income);
  the average is $73,000/year, skewed upward by top earners.
- (2021) He lists lower-risk alternatives to quitting, in order: (1) switch
  companies if you dislike your employer; (2) switch what you do and get paid to
  learn a new skill; (3) if you like the work and company, ask "how can I provide
  more value?" (never "I'd like to get paid more") to earn more in a low-risk
  environment; (4) start the business as a side hustle in your extra time and only
  jump once it exceeds your current income.
- (2021) He admits his own path was "candidly very stupid" and that he got
  extraordinarily lucky — he spent his entire $50,000 savings within the first
  three months on mentorship, coaching, and reinvesting in a gym he says he
  shouldn't have opened the way he did.
- (2021) He advocates living cheap and keeping expectations cheap so that if
  income drops you aren't losing money, while continuously reinvesting in yourself
  as the valuable asset.
- (2021) He cites a survey stat: the average high-school senior believes they'll
  be a millionaire by 25 — i.e., in the top 1% within seven years of graduating —
  which he calls unreasonable.
- (2021) The reason he runs the channel: most people are broke, and he wants to
  make sure viewers aren't among them.
