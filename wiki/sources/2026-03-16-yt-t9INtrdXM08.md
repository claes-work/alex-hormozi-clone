# Helping a $3M/Year E-Commerce Brand Build a Team

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A UK owner selling autographed sports memorabilia does ~$3M/year with ~$400k margin (~12%) and has been "stuck at 3 million for the last couple of years" — the plateau Alex calls **"the swamp."** He wants to reach $6.5M and thinks he needs "an A player." Alex diagnoses by **decomposing the business into its core functions** and asking the owner which he's actually good at. The owner's real skill is **product/sourcing** — arbitrage buying memorabilia at ~$200 and selling at ~$2,000 ("you're a middleman"). Alex points out that for an e-commerce brand with supply nailed, "there's not much else into the business" except **media buying** and, later, **brand-building**. Crucially, Alex reads the *hesitation*: because the owner did not immediately say "I'm really good at e-commerce and media buying," Alex infers that media buying is the missing skill — and therefore the missing hire. His frameworks here: (1) hire against your **deficiency**, not a generic "A player"; the right hire is the person who owns the function you can't; (2) e-commerce scales fast once the media function is filled ("three to six in like 60 days… three to 10, 12" with the right person), with cash flow the next constraint; (3) great media buyers earn "multiple hundreds of thousands a year," so to attract a "star or a stud" you may need to **give up a few points of equity** rather than the whole margin — and you can pay across **different levers** (cash and stock/inventory), not just salary. He also reframes the owner's stated bottleneck: the owner blames leads and a 1% conversion rate, but Alex argues the fix for conversion is usually **better traffic/targeting**, not obsessing over CRO — "the easiest way to [improve] your conversion is get better people" onto the site. Sequence: get the media hire → drive revenue → then learn branding to become a "household name" of sports memorabilia. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses a stuck owner by decomposing the business into its core functions (for e-commerce: product/sourcing, media buying, brand) and identifying which function the owner personally cannot do — that gap is the hire.
- As of March 2026, the right first hire is defined by your **deficiency**, not a vague "A player": the owner was good at sourcing (arbitrage) but not media buying, so a media buyer is the person he needs.
- As of March 2026, Alex reads hesitation as signal: because the owner didn't immediately claim to be good at e-commerce/media buying, Alex inferred that is the missing skill and hire.
- As of March 2026, an e-commerce business with supply/sourcing solved is "just media buying for the most part and then building the brand" — so the plateau usually traces to a missing media-buying capability.
- As of March 2026, with the right media hire an e-commerce brand can scale fast — "three to six in like 60 days," even "three to 10, 12" — because e-commerce "scales very easily"; the next constraint that appears is cash flow.
- As of March 2026, to attract a genuinely good media buyer (who can earn "multiple hundreds of thousands a year") an owner may need to give up "a few points of equity" rather than the entire margin, and can compensate across multiple levers — cash and stock/inventory — not salary alone.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames two "genres" of e-commerce owners — product/brand-focused vs. hardcore media buyers; owners who are neither (like this client) must hire or learn the media function.
- As of March 2026, a low conversion rate (here 1%) is often a traffic/targeting problem, not a CRO problem — "the easiest way to prove [improve] your conversion is get better people" onto the site rather than banging your head on CRO.
- As of March 2026, Alex's sequence for this client: fill the media hire and drive revenue first, then learn branding later to become a "household name" in the category.
