# A Framework for Dealing With Life's Problems

★ L3-candidate — a compact, self-contained multi-frame system for emotional regulation (cosmic zoom-out, historical distance, the "veteran" habituation frame, expectation-adjustment, refusing to define problems as problems, and short-term-problem/long-term-benefit) that could seed a mindset topic page on reframing/acceptance.

## Summary
This is a short mindset monologue by Alex Hormozi (with brief interjections from an unnamed co-host, marked as guest below) laying out several stacked "frames" he uses to defuse everyday upset, anxiety, anger, and sadness. His core argument: negative emotions arise because our expectations go unmet, and we have two options — demand that "an uncaring universe" change to meet us, or change our own expectations. He walks through six-plus reframes: (1) zoom out cosmically until Earth itself is invisible; (2) the 20-year test — what bothered you 20 years ago you can't even recall; (3) the "ancient Americans" frame — in 5,000 years none of today's strife will be remembered; (4) the "veteran" frame (credited to a "Dr.") — imagine the bad thing happening once an hour every day for a year, and by the 2,000th time you'd simply accept it as how life is; (5) surrender to reality through acceptance rather than fighting a universe that will win; and (6) his favorite — stop defining the thing as a problem at all, because most short-term problems are actually long-term benefits (illustrated with the parable of the boy and the horse and the weather/drought/wedding metaphor). He concludes that getting used to not getting his way has been one of his biggest anxiety- and anger-reducing psychological hacks. The clip ends with a promo for his free acquisition.com 10-stage scaling roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-09. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-09) Zooming out far enough — until you can't even see the Earth — shrinks a present annoyance (like bad Wi-Fi) into something that "maybe isn't as big of a deal."
- (2026-02-09) The 20-year test: he can't recall what really bothered him 20 years ago, so whatever bothers him today he'll likely feel the same way (indifferent) about 20 years from now — and if it won't matter then, it shouldn't matter now.
- (2026-02-09) The "ancient Americans" frame: just as we barely remember the Romans, Babylonians, and Sumerians beyond a few leaders' names, in 5,000 years people will look back on us the same way — the day-to-day strife won't be remembered, so it shouldn't matter now.
- (2026-02-09) The "veteran" frame, which he credits to a "Dr.": imagine the bad circumstance happening once an hour every day for a year (~12x/day x 365); by the ~2,000th time you'd simply conclude "this is just how life is" and stop being upset, because you'd have adjusted your expectations.
- (2026-02-09) If he can change his expectations by the 2,000th time his maids put the spoons in the wrong drawer, he can readjust from the start; the example is tongue-in-cheek and swappable (someone double-parking you, a boss being short with you).
- (2026-02-09) Angst, anxiety, sadness, and anger come from the fact that our expectations are unmet.
- (2026-02-09) We have two options: demand that an uncaring universe change to meet us, or change our own expectations.
- (2026-02-09) In "you versus the universe" — contrary to much "Americana propaganda" — the universe will win; the move is to surrender to reality through acceptance, saying "this is, period," neither good nor bad.
- (2026-02-09) One of his favorite ways to solve a problem is to stop defining it as a problem in the first place — the least effortful way to solve something is to ask "is this really a problem? What if this was good?"
- (2026-02-09) Most short-term problems are actually long-term benefits.
- (2026-02-09) The parable of the boy and the horse illustrates that an event labeled good or bad reverses over time (gets a horse = good; falls off and breaks his foot = bad; can't be recruited to war because of the broken foot = good); over a long enough horizon we don't know whether a thing is good or bad.
- (2026-02-09) Since we don't know whether something will turn out good or bad, there's no point ascribing a label to it — the only thing we actually know is that it happened.
- (2026-02-09) Good/bad labels are situational, like weather: in a drought a rainy day "comes from heaven" and a sunny day "comes from hell"; before a wedding, the opposite. [note: the drought half is Alex; the wedding-flip continuation appears to be voiced by the co-host — see attribution flag below]
- (2026-02-09) [guest-attributed] Two people can pray for the exact same thing (weather) at the same time; one will be upset and the other pleased. Early in his dealings with Christianity, praying for good weather for an outdoor event, he realized a farmer was praying for rain — suggesting the construct of "my way is therefore good" may be flawed (not that praying in general is flawed).
- (2026-02-09) [guest-attributed] Getting used to not getting his way, and saying that's okay, has been one of the big psychological hacks that decreased his anxiety and anger around responding to stimuli — because he might not know what he actually wants; "maybe today's rainy day is tomorrow's sunny day," and maybe the sun is neither good nor bad, it just is.
