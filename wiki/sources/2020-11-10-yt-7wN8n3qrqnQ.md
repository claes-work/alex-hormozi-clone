# Why Coaches Get So Much Hate

## Summary

Alex explains why coaches and mentors attract resentment despite evidence that
mentorship dramatically improves outcomes. Drawing on a Cialdini book, he argues that
the perceived value of a favor (or of information) peaks immediately after it is
received and then decays over time, while the giver's sense of its importance grows —
a mismatch that leaves clients feeling "I'd have figured it out anyway." He turns this
into tactical advice for coaches: capture testimonials immediately, and keep delivering
new value continuously.

## Key claims

- (2020) Alex cites (secondhand, via "Dr. Cashi") a meta-analysis of 40,000+ studies
  claiming that people with mentors/coaches experienced roughly four times greater
  success than the same person going it alone, after controlling for income and IQ.
  *(Unverified statistic he relays, not a source he read directly.)*
- (2020) The perceived value of a favor peaks immediately after it is received (when the
  recipient is just relieved of pain) and declines steadily over time — a claim he
  attributes to a Cialdini co-authored book he calls roughly "50 Scientific Ways to
  Persuade."
- (2020) Meanwhile the favor-giver's perception of the favor's effort and impact rises
  over time, creating a chronic mismatch between coach and client.
- (2020) Information behaves like a favor: it is extremely valuable at the moment of
  consumption but rapidly loses perceived value afterward.
- (2020) Tactical takeaway: the moment to ask for a favor (or a testimonial) in return
  is immediately after delivering the result, when perceived value is highest — not
  months later.
- (2020) Coaches must continuously deliver new value ("telephone poles" of goodwill)
  because delivered value has an expiration date; every solved problem exposes the next
  gap, forcing the coach to keep leveling up.
- (2020) He self-reports that only in the "last year or two" has he become better at
  giving credit and respect to people who helped him earlier in his career.
- (2020) Reframe for students (attributed to Tony Robbins): don't only blame parents/
  mentors for insecurities — also credit them for the resilience and perseverance you
  developed in reaction. He notes his book's acknowledgments include both positive and
  "negative" mentors.
