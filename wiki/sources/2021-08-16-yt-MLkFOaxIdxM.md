# The Biggest Mistake Everyone is Making (2021-08-16)

## Summary

In this solo vlog, Hormozi argues that the single biggest mistake he sees
entrepreneurs make (and made himself) is chasing "easy money" side-opportunities that
distract from the one business that produces most of their revenue. He frames strategy
as knowing what to say *no* to, introduces his "woman in the red dress" metaphor for
seductive distractions, and lays out the "simple scales, fancy fails" principle plus an
early definition of the transition from CEO to owner.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- The number-one mistake entrepreneurs make is starting new businesses / product lines
  instead of doubling down on the one that already drives ~80% of revenue (Pareto).
  Proto-version of his **focus / one-thing thesis** — see [[2021-09-03-yt-s59jbVBprn0]].
- "Easy money sitting on the table" is precisely the money that distracts you from the
  main game.
- **Strategy = learning when to say no**, not what to say yes to. Strength comes from
  knowing what you *don't* do and who you *don't* serve.
- Entrepreneurs jump to a "new ladder" when their current one gets hard because they've
  hit a limit in their beliefs, skills, or character traits — but the new ladder is
  capped by the same personal limit. Proto-version of the **"business grows to your
  level of competence" / constraint-is-you idea**, expanded in [[2021-09-03-yt-s59jbVBprn0]].
- **"Simple scales, fancy fails"** — the richest people run simple businesses; complexity
  is a warning sign. (Stated as a core Mozi Nation tenet.)
- The higher you climb, the more seductive the distraction ($1k/mo is easy to refuse;
  $50–100k/mo is tempting) — the temptation scales with success.
- Use patience/time as an asset, not a liability: repeat successful actions, let
  compounding work for you ("aggressive patience," "macro perspective").
- Uncommon success is hard not because it's *complicated* but because it's *simple* and
  few have the discipline to do the boring work.
- **CEO vs. owner definition** (proto-systems framework): you've moved from CEO to owner
  only when the business grows without you spending more than ~5 hours/week on it, for
  six straight months, and you never have to jump back in. If you're not in day-to-day
  fulfillment but the business still needs you driving it, you're still CEO — you still
  have a job.
- Self-reported: he believes he started his second business (Prestige Labs, the
  supplement company) too early, which "basically flatlined" growth for ~two years while
  he developed the character traits to run two businesses.
- Owners "assemble a team to pursue an opportunity" and acquire businesses; CEOs "start
  a business." The language you use reveals which you are.
- Reframe for himself now: with a multi-company portfolio, "the portfolio is the one
  thing" — the one-thing rule still holds at a higher altitude.
