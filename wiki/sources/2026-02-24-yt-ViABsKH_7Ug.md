# How to Scale a Photography Business

## Summary

A short, casual lunch-table clip in which Alex Hormozi answers how to scale a wedding
photography business. He opens by restating his advertising framework — the "Core Four"
ways to get customers (outreach to customers, paid ads, content, and outreach to
affiliates), which he frames here as a simplified version of the eight methods in his
leads book (paraphrase). He then reasons through the specific economics of wedding
photography: because it is a one-time purchase, buyers rely heavily on local referrals
rather than ads. His recommended play is to go outbound to wedding planners and offer to
shoot one or two weddings at whatever price the planner can sell it for, letting the
planner keep 100% of the money as an incentive to push him. Once he has proven his
quality, he negotiates an ongoing deal, turning each wedding planner into a "node of
distribution" that repeatedly feeds him customers. The clip is informal (shared as a meal
between Alex and a companion) and does not go deep beyond this single tactic.

## Key claims (2026-02-24, paraphrased)

- There are four fundamental ways to advertise / get customers ("Core Four"): outreach to
  customers, paid ads, content, and outreach to affiliates — presented as the four most
  likely-relevant methods distilled from the eight in his leads book. (paraphrase)
- For a wedding photography business specifically, the purchase is one-time, so customers
  typically find providers by asking local people who they used — i.e. referrals dominate
  over ads. (paraphrase)
- His recommended acquisition play: go outbound to wedding planners rather than chase ads
  directly. (paraphrase)
- Offer to do the photography for whatever price the wedding planner can sell it for, and
  let the planner keep 100% of that money for the first one or two customers, so they have
  a strong incentive to push you. (paraphrase)
- Use those initial jobs to prove your quality to the planner, then negotiate an ongoing
  deal. (paraphrase)
- Each wedding planner (and their helpers) then becomes a recurring "node of distribution"
  that feeds you customers; repeat the process across many planners to scale. (paraphrase)
