# How The Top 1% Actually Think About Trust

## Summary
In this solo video — flagged by Alex himself as "a departure from my normal business content" — **Alex Hormozi** builds a behavioral definition of trust: to trust someone is to make yourself *punishable* by them. He decomposes trust into four types along two variables (who is at risk, and who does the punishing — the other person or "the environment"/reality), covering both trusting someone's actions/word and trusting their advice. He gives a two-question test for whether to extend trust (track record of not burning you, plus whether betraying you is a bad deal for them), and argues that trust is uniquely fragile because it rests on *zero punishment* — a single betrayal retroactively cancels all prior trust-building. He closes with his standard acquisition.com/roadmap call-to-action.

## Key claims
- **Trust = making yourself punishable.** "If I trust someone, it means that I make myself punishable by them." Sharing a secret hands the other person something they *could* punish you with; trusting is betting they won't.
- **There are four types of trust, mapped on two variables: who is at risk, and who does the punishing.** The punisher is either the other person or "the environment" (reality). You can be the one at risk, or you can be the trustworthy one holding the other person's risk.
- **Two forms of the trusted thing:** (1) secrets/information someone can use to hurt you, or (2) your given word to do something (make it happen, or that they should do it). In the "word" cases the environment usually does the punishing rather than the person.
- **Trust also applies to advice, not just action.** Trusting someone's advice means following it; if it goes badly you trust them less ("she gives terrible advice").
- **Two-question test for extending trust:** (1) Do they have a track record of protecting what they've been given (has a knife been at your back before and not used)? (2) Does burning you cost them more than protecting you — i.e., is betraying you a bad deal for them? If yes to both, proceed.
- **Why trust at all, given the risk:** the ceiling of a relationship's potential is set by the shared context both people have about each other. Every disclosure gives the other person context to serve you better — but also more ability to hurt you.
- **The core difficulty:** humans have short-term incentives that run adverse to their long-term goals; being trustworthy means choosing the relationship over the short-term win (e.g. a spouse not weaponizing a disclosure to win an argument).
- **Trust is based on zero punishment.** You disclose incrementally; each time nothing bad happens, you disclose more — until the incentive to wrong you gets high enough that some people do. One betrayal undoes all prior reinforcement ("I trusted him for 20 years... then boom, that one thing, and that was it").
- **To be trustworthy requires zero punishment at two moments:** (1) when someone discloses (reward/reinforce it — "your secret's safe with me"), and (2) never using it against them later, including via third parties ("loose lips sink ships").
- **Breaking your word for a short-term benefit doesn't merely harm the relationship — it likely ends it,** just not immediately: "like cutting a tree off the vine," visibly standing but dead inside, nutrients (truth) cut off.
- **Payoffs of being trustworthy:** more influence (people trust you with more), higher follow-through on your advice, and reputation for keeping your word.
- **Rebuttal to "this is just transactional":** everyone makes this plus/minus appraisal on some level; he is only defining it clearly so he can name *why* he distrusts someone.
