# Helping an Interior Design Company Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. Sarah runs a bespoke interior-design and wellness-advisory firm selling to ultra-high-net-worth families and family offices (~$1.6M revenue, targeting $10M by end of next year, $250M in 10 years). Her question is about a three-tier "ladder": tier one at $80/sq ft (design + construction selections + wellness layers, the offer she's done for 20 years), tier two at $100/sq ft (a 7–10 year portfolio commitment with locked rates), and tier three (whole ecosystem — yacht, plane, offices, curated annual experiences) at $100/sq ft plus a $200k annual stewardship retainer. Alex's core answer: **don't use a ladder for a bespoke business.** Because the service is custom, she should simply **price each job** to whatever size and asset it covers ("sell whatever you can get away with"). Fractal/tiered pricing only works when each tier is roughly 5x the last; her tiers stepped up only ~20%, too undifferentiated, and the long 10–15 year commitments cut against what the ultra-rich actually want (flexibility, speed, options, no rework). His prescription: (1) make the annual retainer **de minimis** — a rounding error positioned as *insurance/continuity* ("I'll come by once a year to make sure everything's working"), which he draws from home-services maintenance plans; its real purpose is to guarantee an annual meeting so she's the established, top-of-mind vendor with a standing excuse to sell more. (2) Accept that **"small jobs get the big jobs"** — off-year bathroom/kitchen refreshes keep the relationship warm until the next house/yacht/jet purchase; they're still profitable and keep her in the acquisition pipeline. (3) Price by **asset type** (house pricing, yacht pricing, jet pricing) since cost already scales with the thing. On messaging: the $80-or-$100/sq ft figure "means nothing" to a family-office buyer — they'll just do the math — and buyers at this level aren't price-shopping apples-to-apples anyway because she's **decommoditized** via wellness-advisory positioning. Better to set expectations with a floor ("we only work on $20M-plus estates minimum") so they anchor well above six figures. Sarah lands on $100/sq ft plus a bundled continuity plan. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex would not use a tiered "ladder" for a bespoke/custom business — since every job is bespoke, you price each job individually to what the client will pay rather than forcing predefined tiers.
- As of March 2026, fractal/tiered pricing only works when each tier is roughly 5x the price of the one below; a ~20% step-up between tiers is too undifferentiated to justify separate tiers.
- As of March 2026, long multi-year commitments (10–15 years) work against ultra-high-net-worth buyers, who value flexibility, options, and speed and don't want to redo work they paid for.
- As of March 2026, the annual retainer should be **de minimis** — a rounding error on the project, positioned as *insurance/continuity* rather than a profit center (his home-services analogy: a $500/year maintenance plan on a $100k job).
- As of March 2026, the real function of that annual plan is access: it guarantees a yearly visit that keeps you the established, top-of-mind vendor, and once you're inside a rich person's home they'll always have more work for you.
- As of March 2026, "the small jobs get the big jobs" — taking modest off-year projects (a bathroom or kitchen refresh) keeps the relationship and pipeline warm until the client's next major purchase; smaller jobs aren't necessarily less profitable.
- As of March 2026, rich clients buy houses, yachts, and planes continually, so staying top-of-mind means an off year still produces revenue and preserves the relationship for the next acquisition.
- As of March 2026, price by asset type — house pricing, yacht pricing, jet pricing — because cost already scales with the size of the thing; this is cleaner than artificial tiers.
- As of March 2026, a per-square-foot number ($80 or $100/sq ft) "means nothing" to a family-office buyer — they'll just compute the total — so it can be kept internal; better to set expectations with a minimum estate value ($20M-plus) that anchors the client above six figures.
- As of March 2026, at this level buyers choose on being more buttoned-up, professional, and better-looking (best finishes/aesthetic), not on saving money — they want the best.
- As of March 2026, decommoditizing (positioning as a wellness advisory, effectively one-of-a-kind) means clients can't price you apples-to-apples against other designers.
- As of March 2026, a round, simple number ($100/sq ft) is preferable because it's easy for the client to do the math in their head.
