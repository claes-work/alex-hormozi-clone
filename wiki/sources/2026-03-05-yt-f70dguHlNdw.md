# Lessons from My First Business

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera interviewer asks the questions; every substantive answer is Alex's). Alex walks through the origin arc of his first business — the pre-gym "half step" between quitting corporate consulting and opening a gym. During his **last year of consulting** he ran what he called the **"Free Training Project"**: he'd "charge" $500–$1,000 for online fitness coaching but require clients to donate that amount to the charity of their choice instead of paying him — building goodwill, letting clients feel good and get a write-off, and (per the deal) collecting testimonials/before-and-after photos he could use later. After ~a year he had ~10–12 strong before/afters, then transitioned those clients to paying him directly ("charity of Alex, cuz Alex can't eat either"). His whole "business infrastructure" was an **LLC and a PayPal account** — no website at first. His recurring startup advice: you only need a bank account and a way to process money; make the first ~10 free/charitable to dodge the awkwardness of selling; then transition free users to paid — those who won't pay tell you either "you suck or they suck." In **2013** he made a public post announcing he'd built a website and was "open for business"; from there he got ~20 clients at $200/month = **$4,000/month**, delivered in **4 hours every Saturday** (updating training programs, macros, meal plans, texting from his personal cell). He *hated* the repetitive one-on-one fulfillment despite loving fitness, which pushed him toward owning a gym so he'd be a "business owner" rather than "self-employed." He explains he was choosing between three businesses — a **test-prep company** (which he did heavy prep on, planned to partner with a university professor, but abandoned after the professor allegedly took/used his materials, leaving "a very sour taste"), a **frozen-yogurt business** (killed by a ~$250k startup cost he couldn't afford), and **fitness** (which won by elimination + existing skill + existing demand). To learn the gym business he cold-contacted **40 gym owners** offering to apprentice for free; **one** responded, ran a mastermind Alex joined for **$10,000** (before he even owned a gym), and later hired Alex as an employee at roughly minimum wage (~$2,800/month) — Alex took it "to learn, not to earn," backed by ~$50k in savings, living on ~$400/month rent (splitting a bedroom) and ~$100/week food. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says his first real income came from an online fitness-coaching business he bootstrapped from a charity-based free program, not from the gym — the gym came later.
- As of March 2026, he describes the "Free Training Project" (run during his last year of consulting): clients "paid" $500–$1,000 by donating it to a charity of their choice rather than paying him directly, which he says built goodwill, gave clients a write-off, and let him collect testimonials as part of the deal.
- As of March 2026, he says the general lesson is that things people already ask you to do for free (because you're good at them) signal what you can charge for later.
- As of March 2026, he claims you only need two things to start a business — a bank account and payment processing — citing "30 million businesses in the United States" as proof others have figured it out, so you can too.
- As of March 2026, his transition tactic: make the first ~10 clients free/charitable to remove selling awkwardness, then convert them to paid; those who refuse to pay reveal that "either you suck or they suck," and the percentage who stay gives you income plus referrals.
- As of March 2026, he says he acquired ~20 clients at $200/month ($4,000/month) with only ~4 hours of fulfillment every Saturday (updating programs, macros, meal plans, texting clients), and that he hated the repetitive delivery despite loving fitness.
- As of March 2026, he says he chose the gym over simply scaling the online coaching because owning a gym felt like being a real "business owner" rather than "self-employed" — a leap he now calls low-inference and not intelligent.
- As of March 2026, he says he was deciding among three businesses: test prep, frozen yogurt, and fitness. Yogurt was ruled out by a ~$250k startup cost; test prep collapsed over a professor partnership that went sour; fitness won by elimination plus existing skill and demand.
- As of March 2026, he says online coaching wasn't seen as a "legitimate business" back then ("this is 10 years ago"), which is part of why the gym felt like the "legit" move.
- As of March 2026, he says he cold-contacted 40 gym owners to apprentice for free; one responded, he paid $10,000 to join that owner's mastermind before owning a gym, and was later hired as an employee — taking it "to learn," not to earn, funded by ~$50k savings.
