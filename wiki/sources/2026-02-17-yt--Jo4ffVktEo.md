# YOU Are the Biggest Constraint in Your Business

## Summary

A short clip (Alex voiceover intro + a clipped conversation) on the founder-as-bottleneck
theme: the entrepreneur who is the best at everything becomes the business's **keyman
risk**. Alex frames this as a double-edged sword — the more you invest in your own skill,
the more you become a liability as much as an asset — and argues the only way to "pay down"
keyman risk is to break your own role into component parts that ordinary hires can cover,
accepting a margin hit in exchange for scale. He works through the **unicorn analogy**
(you can't hire another you, so you assemble an approximation), a numeric **scale-zero**
illustration, and a concrete operational tactic: run a **time study**, feed it to GPT, and
ask for the smallest set of roles + job descriptions that would replace 100% of your
activities. Overlaps heavily with the existing keyman-risk / scale-zero / time-study thread
already documented in `wiki/topics/business/scaling-and-enterprise-value.md`,
`hiring-and-recruiting.md`, and `ai-in-business.md`; captured here mostly as reinforcement,
with one fresher operational detail (★).

## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased unless quoted)

- A common limiting belief is "no one can do it as good as I can" — but that belief creates
  **keyman risk**: any business that relies on a single person (founder or otherwise) has
  that person as a keyman liability.
- Keyman risk is a **double-edged sword**: the better you get at something, the more keyman
  (and the more of a liability) you become — investing in yourself makes you as much a
  liability to the business as an asset. The only way to pay it down is to break the role
  into parts.
- Founders learned every function themselves (marketing, sales, product, hiring), then
  expect to hire one person who has "lived our entire lives" for a fraction of their own
  pay — an unrealistic single-hire fantasy. The fix is to **break the role into parts**.
- **Unicorn analogy:** you are a unicorn in your business; hunting for another unicorn is
  unproductive. Instead build an *approximation* — bolt a rhinoceros horn onto a white horse
  and add fireflies (pixie dust). Not as good as you, but "pretty good." If the business
  *requires* someone as good as you to run it, the model doesn't work; if it can run on
  someone **half as good as you**, you scale — but always sacrifice some margin to do it.
- **Scale zero (numeric illustration):** hypothetically a $20M/yr business at 50% margins
  with the founder heavily involved. Hire all the people you truly need and margin might
  drop from ~$10M to ~$4M — but now the company requires *zero* of you and can grow $20M →
  $40M, so you make ~$8M on $40M with none of your time. Trading margin now for a bigger,
  founder-independent business later.
- Ego is often tied to the profit number ("if it's not this number, you suck") — you have
  to be willing to **give up some profit** to break your current activities into component
  parts that others can run.
- ★ **Time-study → GPT role decomposition (operational tactic):** log what you actually do
  in a current week (time study), plug the entire study into GPT, and ask: "what is the
  smallest number of roles that would replace 100% of these activities?" plus job
  descriptions and names for each. Typically resolves into a few roles (some HR, some
  marketing, some sales work) → hire one of each. (Fresher operational detail than the
  time-study material already on file, which stops at logging/grouping tasks.)
- **Hiring is iterative and failure-heavy:** the first time you hire the new roles, ~two of
  three will "suck" because you lack pattern recognition; one will be okay. You re-hire, it
  takes ~6 months to figure out — but once you get there you reach the next revenue stop
  ($500K or $1M/month), then hit the next "I don't know how to hire this person" wall, fail
  for a while, and get it again. The rule is you can't stop trying.
- Strip the "mysticism" out of what the founder does (visionary / energy / etc.). In
  practice you are mostly **making decisions or doing individual-contributor work** — break
  the day-to-day into its constituent buckets and hand them off.
- Most small businesses **don't need more ideas — they need more execution.**

> in yourself, in some ways, you become a liability for the business as much as you are an
