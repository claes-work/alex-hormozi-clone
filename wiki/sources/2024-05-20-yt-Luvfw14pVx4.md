# Business Owners: You're (Probably) Not Good Enough

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi attacks the "victim mentality" — the habit of
blaming a bias (age, gender, race, appearance, location) for not winning. His core move:
even if a bias is real and provable, proving it changes nothing; the only lever you
control is being so good you're "better than the bias." He argues biases are inevitable
because they are just how humans learn from repeated experience, and that the honest but
ego-bruising alternative explanation is usually "you're just not good enough yet."

## Key claims (the skill/competence argument)

- When you don't get the deal/promotion, there are two possible realities: a bias against
  you, OR you're simply not good enough. People prefer the bias explanation because it
  protects the ego; the competence explanation hurts but is more actionable.
- Biases will always exist because bias is how humans learn — repeated experiences create
  judgments/expectations (the waitress learning which "faces" tip). Wishing bias away is
  wishing humans wouldn't learn.
- Proving a bias exists is "good for society, bad for the individual." Life is a
  single-player game; you must proceed as if nothing will change and ask what you'll do
  anyway.
- Accepting the excuse hurts you more than the bias does — it gives you a warm reason not
  to succeed and removes the pressure to improve.
- The only response that works: overcompensate. Be so good you're "better than the bias" —
  don't tip 20%, tip 100%. Stand out precisely because you break the expectation.
- Breaking a stereotype makes you *more* compelling, not less: the more disadvantages you
  overcome, the more powerful and unrepeatable your story (cites Oprah as first Black
  female billionaire building a media empire in the '80s).
- Operating question for both employees and business owners: "What would it take?" — to get
  on the team, get the promotion, close the deal. Then go do that objectively.
- "Volume negates luck" — the fix for lacking skill is doing far more than everyone else.
- Even if the stereotype about you is *true* (immature, inconsistent, sounds like a child),
  the answer is the same: it's a skill you haven't learned yet, so go learn it.
