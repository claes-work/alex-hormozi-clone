# There Are Only 2 Types of Games in Life

## Summary
Solo monologue (music-interlude breaks only; all speech is Alex). Alex frames life's pursuits through **finite vs. infinite games** — a distinction he says took him too long to learn. A *finite frame* has known players, agreed-upon rules, and a way to win or lose, after which the game ends. An *infinite frame* has known and unknown players, no agreed-upon rules, and a single point: keep the game going. He argues the greatest games — marriage, health, business — are all infinite: you don't win at marriage, you stay married; you don't win at health, you stay in shape; you don't win at business, you stay in business. The consequence is a redefinition of winning: **if you don't give up, you win by default.** The way to win long-term is therefore to define winning as "leaving everything on the field," because the external win then happens on its own — and if you truly commit to that perspective, the external win won't even mean much to you. He warns this is why athletes who win gold or a championship sometimes fall into depression or suicide: they were "playing the wrong game," measuring themselves on the outcome rather than the play. He cites Kobe Bryant, who shortly before he died rejected both "afraid of losing" and "loves winning" and said (Alex paraphrasing) he just loves playing to the best of his ability — the world's choice to measure him by wins "is the world's problem, not ours." Alex reframes this as declaring yourself **game master, not player**: making your own rules for winning rather than accepting the world's. He invokes Naval Ravikant ("what looks like work to other people should feel like play for you") and the painter whose point is to paint, not to finish. The practical lever: the **broader you generalize what you commit to, the easier it is to stick with** — don't commit to "making calls" while hating CRM entry; chunk up to "I prefer to do hard work because of what that work does to me," so every hard task (CRM, editing, hard conversations) contributes to the person you want to become. Since the only asset you keep is your **character**, make character the daily win — "did I or did I not contribute to the character of the person I want to become?" If that's the win every day, you can never lose. Closes with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
