# Close High-Ticket Sales By Saying 'NO' (2021-04-22)

## Summary

Hormozi breaks down the phrasing of sales questions, distinguishing mining
questions (extract information) from agreement questions (get commitment). He
focuses on how to phrase agreement/closing questions along a spectrum — yes-based,
neutral, and negatively-inclined ("no-based") — arguing that no-based questions
are the easiest way to get agreement because saying "no" makes prospects feel
safe and in control. This is an early articulation of the questioning/closing
mechanics later systematized in his CLOSER framework.

## Key claims

- (2021) In a sales process, questions are either **mining questions** (extract
  information from the prospect) or **agreement questions** (put out a
  proposition and ask for agreement); stacking enough agreements makes moving
  forward feel natural. **Proto-CLOSER framing.**
- (2021) Agreement questions sit on a spectrum: **yes-based** ("Sounds great,
  right?"), **neutral** ("Does that sound fair / reasonable?"), and
  **no-based / negatively-inclined** ("Would you be opposed to...?"). He credits
  the yes/neutral/no framing to Chris Voss (*Never Split the Difference*).
- (2021) Neutral, reciprocal "fair enough" yeses are easier to get than
  high-bar yeses like "Is this the most amazing thing you've ever heard?",
  because a concession triggers reciprocity. He credits the "fair enough" close
  to Jordan Belfort and notes it is deeply wired into his own scripts from
  heavy repetition.
- (2021) **No-based questions** are the most powerful: people feel safer saying
  no — they retain autonomy, feel less vulnerable, and it dodges decision
  fatigue at the end of a call. Phrase questions so a "no" *is* the yes:
  "Would you be opposed to moving forward today?" → "No, I'm not opposed" → close.
- (2021) He cites a tactic (credited to Richard Schwartz) of getting people to
  say yes by saying no — "You don't want anything else, do you?" "No, I don't" —
  which reportedly got 95% of people to take an upsell.
- (2021) The precedents that lead into no-based questions: "Would you be opposed
  to...", "Would you be against...", "Would it be unreasonable...". Illustrated
  with a personal example of getting his wife to agree to dinner: "Would you be
  against going to Cheesecake Factory?" beats "Do you want to go?"
- (2021) You don't need every question to be no-based — rephrase a couple of the
  key milestone/closing questions, or lean no-based when a prospect is close to
  the edge or fatigued. Not for mining questions. He notes he is shifting some
  of his own sales scripts from "fair enough" closes toward no-based phrasing.
- (2021) Aside: nearly everyone he knows who teaches sales was a natural-born
  salesman, but anyone can become great with more reps — naturals just needed
  fewer.
