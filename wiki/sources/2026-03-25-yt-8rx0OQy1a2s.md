# Helping an Online Coaching Business Get to $50M/Year

## Summary
In this workshop clip, Alex Hormozi advises a business owner who sells paid-advertising
training to business owners (doing ~$5-6M/year at a reported 2.5 EBITDA and paying no
employee more than $100,000) and wants to reach $50M/year. Hormozi reframes the owner's
capacity constraint as a "talent debt" problem: the biggest arbitrage in business today
is still talent, and reinvesting a slice of profit into far more expensive, elite hires
compounds returns. He argues that underpaying staff is not saving money but paying with
something you value less, and closes with a promotion for the acquisition.com scaling
roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says the biggest arbitrage opportunities in business are still in talent.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames underinvesting in people as incurring "talent debt" — one of several debt types a business can take on (financial, technical, management), and the founder chooses which.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that once you have real profit, you must think in terms of return on invested capital: e.g., spending $200K to drop taxable profit from $2.5M to $2.3M can be worth it if that hire generates an extra $1M.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims talent quality steps up dramatically at each pay tier ($100K → $250K → $500K → $1M), and that his best people cost significantly more than $250K.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that a service business is capped by its culture, which is set by the founder and proliferated by the people they attract.
- As of March 2026, Alex's hiring litmus test: only hire when you feel a "desperation in your core" ("I have to have this person"); never settle for the "least bad" candidate, and keep a role open for weeks or months until you find the right person.
- As of March 2026, Alex credits a maxim from a colleague named Shirong: "Your best talent is always in the future," using it to argue founders should shrink the gap to that future team as fast as possible.
- Caller claim (unverified): the owner reports ~$5-6M revenue, an EBITDA figure stated as "2.5," pays no one more than $100,000, and cites his own capacity as the main constraint on reaching $50M.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I still believe that the biggest arbitrage opportunities that exist right now in business are still in talent."

> "Your best talent is always in the future."

> "By making more money you're increasing your debt for humans."

> "Your business will be capped for sure by the culture that you keep, which is going to be on you, and the people that proliferate that culture is the people you attract."

> "When you pay someone 500,000 a year you're like, 'Oh my god, this is a totally different level talent.' Then you pay someone a million dollars a year and you're like, 'Holy — I didn't know people could... they made people like this.'"

> "The litmus test that I have is that you feel this like desperation in your core when you talk to them. You're like, 'I have to have this person.'"

> "We will keep a role open for weeks or months if we do not find the person. And so we are always actively recruiting and we have a lot of flow."

> "You're always paying. It's just what you're paying — you pay with the thing you care the least about."

## Notes
Synthesis candidate: The "talent debt" framing — treating underpaid teams as a form of business debt alongside financial/technical/management debt, priced against return on invested capital — and the "your best talent is always in the future" maxim (attributed to Shirong) are sharp, quotable articulations worth promoting to a hiring/talent topic page and to beliefs.
