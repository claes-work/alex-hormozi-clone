# What to Do When People Ask for Equity

## Summary
Short coaching clip on how a business owner should respond when an employee (especially a sales leader) asks for equity. Alex's core move is to **reframe the request**: people ask for "equity" because they don't know how else to ask, but literal equity is a bad deal for them — you can't *give* equity (it's not an inheritance/gift; recipients either write a check to buy it or pay income tax on its value if handed over for free), and it forces a company valuation. Instead he decomposes equity into **four things it actually provides**: (1) risk, (2) decision-making power/control, (3) profit sharing, and (4) equity/sale value. He then walks the asker through which of the four they actually want — almost always they do *not* want downside risk ("if we lose money, do you want to lose some?") and the owner will not hand over control, so what's left is participation in cash flow and a piece of the upside on a sale. He solves for exactly that with a legal structure instead of equity: for an LLC, a **profits interest** — which he describes as functioning like a **sale bonus** (a payout equal to your vested percentage of the company at the point of sale), and which can optionally include ongoing profit share equal to that percentage. His example vesting: 0.25% per year up to a 1% cap, after which the incentive is to grow the company so the 1% is worth more. Key advantages: sales reps feel they "have equity" (a huge psychological win), they don't have to write a check, and if they leave or are let go the interest **returns to the pool** rather than walking out the door. The two downsides he names honestly: at sale it's taxed as **ordinary income (~37%)** rather than capital gains (~20%), and if you let the person go they lose it. A client raises the fear of being fired right before a deal closes to dodge the payout; Alex says a smart negotiator would ask for a **tail** on the agreement, but the fear is unrealistic because firing all your sales leaders right before a sale is a terrible move that would spook a buyer. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes equity requests: people ask for "equity" mostly because they don't know how to ask for what they actually want, so the owner should translate the request into its components.
- As of March 2026, he decomposes equity into four things: risk, decision-making power/control, profit sharing, and equity (sale) value — and walks the asker through which of the four they truly want.
- As of March 2026, most people asking for equity do not actually want the downside risk ("if we lose money, do you want to lose some?") and the owner will not give up control, leaving cash-flow participation and a share of sale proceeds as what they really want.
- As of March 2026, you cannot simply *give* someone equity: it's not treated like an inheritance or gift; they either have to buy it (write a check) or pay income tax on its value if it's handed to them for free — and issuing it forces a company valuation.
- As of March 2026, his preferred alternative for an LLC is a **profits interest**, which is not a profit share but an analog for equity value, structured as a **sale bonus** — a payout equal to your vested percentage of the company at the point of sale.
- As of March 2026, an example vesting schedule: 0.25% per year up to a 1% cap; once capped, the incentive becomes growing the company so that the fixed 1% is worth more.
- As of March 2026, a profits interest can optionally also grant ongoing **profit share** equal to the profits-interest percentage (e.g. 1% of profits on top).
- As of March 2026, the structure's advantages: sales reps feel they "have equity" (a major motivator), they don't have to write a check, and if they leave or are let go the interest returns to the pool instead of walking with them.
- As of March 2026, the two honest downsides: at sale the payout is taxed as ordinary income (~37%) rather than capital gains (~20%), and if the person is let go they lose the interest entirely.
- As of March 2026, a savvy recipient should negotiate a **tail** on the agreement to protect against being fired right before a deal closes — but Alex says that fear is unrealistic, since firing all your sales leaders right before a sale is a poor move that would alarm a buyer.
