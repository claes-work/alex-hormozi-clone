# 54.1% of People Who Try This Achieve Their 1st Dollar Online

## Summary

Alex Hormozi (co-owner of Skool) explains the Skool Games — a 30-day competition
he and Sam Ovens designed to help aspiring entrepreneurs make their first dollar
online, in which the top 10 finishers get flown to Vegas. He shares first-cohort
completion stats, describes the free-community mechanic behind the Games ("look at
what everyone else charges for, make it better, make it free"), and lays out the
Skool business model plus a case for why hosting a community on Skool lets a
beginner "borrow the platform's credibility" the way an Amazon listing outconverts
a standalone Shopify store.

## Key claims

- **The Skool Games** — a 30-day competition Hormozi built with Sam Ovens to make
  the Games "fun" rather than "just try this out." Structured as **14 days free
  inside a 30-day competition** so people who feel it isn't working can dip out at
  two weeks; those who finish and rank in the **top 10 get flown to Vegas**, where
  the team helps them grow their communities and gathers feedback to improve the
  product.
- **First-dollar stat (self-reported, see Notes for discrepancy):** of people who
  finished the full 30 days of the first Games, a share made their first dollar or
  more online — Hormozi frames it verbally as "just under one out of three." The
  video title states 54.1%; the auto-caption reads "3.44%." (Contradiction flagged
  below.)
- **The give-it-away playbook:** his go-to customer-acquisition move is to look at
  what everyone else charges for, make it better, and make it free. Applied to the
  Games, they gave away for free what the industry normally charges for — coaching
  calls, courses, community, and tools ("the C's": coaching, courses, community,
  code/tools) — plus support from others doing the same thing, which builds
  **conviction**.
- **First dollar is psychological, not tactical:** "so little of it has to do with
  making their first dollar and so much of it has to do with getting out of your
  own head" — which is why many aspiring entrepreneurs start with personal
  development to get past fear of rejection.
- **Transparency thesis:** because Skool owns the platform, all member revenue
  numbers are public and visible — "no one's fronting" — bringing honesty to an
  industry Hormozi says is full of misrepresentation and deception.
- **Skool business model:** a flat **$99/month** per group; platform revenue =
  number of groups × $99. He states it's deliberately kept simple (no tiers) to
  maximize student and teacher count during **growth mode**, not profit; the
  platform has been **losing money** by aggressively reinvesting in engineering and
  design. He says he wrote "a big check into it." Long-term monetization is not the
  $99 tier. Framed against a "$10 trillion global education" opportunity.
- **Borrow-the-brand / Amazon-vs-Shopify analogy:** a standalone store (Shopify)
  gives control but no inherent trust and is technically hard; a marketplace
  (Amazon) forces a template but confers platform trust. **Self-reported conversion
  data:** his book's Shopify page converts at ~2–3% (standard e-commerce) while the
  **same book on Amazon converts at ~30% — ~10x** — because buyers trust the
  platform. His bet: as trust in Skool compounds, Skool pages will outconvert
  anything a beginner builds themselves.
- **The beginner's business model on Skool:** get eyeballs/traffic (posting, ads,
  outreach, or partnering with an existing audience) → drive them to a Skool page →
  convert into a paid community charging between **$1 and $9,999/month** →
  recurring revenue. No inventory, no third-party integrations, no technical
  knowledge; money is processed and delivery happens on-platform.
- **Skool page design convergence:** Skool pages copy the layout that Zillow,
  Airbnb, and Amazon converged on — video, image carousel, reviews, a buy button,
  and a handful of product bullets — because those platforms have more traffic and
  conversion data than anyone on Earth and "built it that way for a reason."
- **Low-risk front end / ascension:** a low-priced community lets a customer come
  in, get value, and then choose to ascend to higher-priced offers — versus asking
  a stranger for $50,000 upfront, which is a big risk.
