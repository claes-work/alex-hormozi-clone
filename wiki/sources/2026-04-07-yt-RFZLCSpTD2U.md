# Stop Wasting Time Asking Why

## Summary

Alex says he largely ignores hidden causal explanations and focuses on observable
behavior. The only general cause he is willing to propose is a reinforcement history:
behavior previously produced reward or avoided punishment. Experts' verbal accounts of
their success can be rehearsed responses rather than accurate descriptions, so learning
should compare what successful people actually did.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex avoids claiming why people behave as they do beyond past
  reinforcement or punishment.
- People often cannot consciously explain automatic behavior and may produce an answer
  simply because answering questions has been reinforced.
- Repeatedly praised success stories can become polished explanations without accurately
  describing the behavior that caused the result.
- Alex credits a line about preferring understood failure to unexplained success to
  "Professor Bergman from Stanford"; the attribution is not independently verified here.
- He tries to identify what higher performers did differently rather than label their
  result lucky.
- Alex acknowledges chance exists but says calling another person lucky does not improve
  his own skill and may hide an observational deficiency.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Ignore the black box."

> "The only thing I'll commit to saying why."

> "Me calling someone else lucky makes me better in zero ways."

## Notes

Reinforces observable behavior, reinforcement history, and anti-luck framing.
