# Should Business Come Before Marriage?

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks the questions; all substantive content is Alex's). The host frames it around Alex and Leila's history: early on, business was prioritized over the marriage, and around **December 2019** they deliberately shifted to marriage-over-business. Asked whether those ~three business-first years were *necessary* — or whether he'd have found more success by prioritizing the marriage from the start — Alex says he has "no idea" and "can't replay the game," treating December 2019 as one of his periodic "recalibration moments": he told Leila on their balcony that he did not like the way they were living, she agreed, and they simply changed it. He credits their durability to always having been "on the same page," and to living so mutually entwined that they're exposed to the same stimuli and "suffer at the same time." He quantifies their closeness: from the day they met they've spent only **two weekends apart** (excluding a ~3-week launch stretch), and because they work in the same building and were remote for years, he estimates they've logged the time-together equivalent of a **70-year marriage**. On maintaining the relationship, he reframes the real thing to manage as **closeness** — citing Esther Perel's *Mating in Captivity* (which he hasn't read; Leila told him the premise) — arguing modern couples who both work from home spend too much time together in the wrong ways and become "siblings" or "roommates," losing mystery and chemistry. His counter-practice: deliberately create physical space and "permission" to take alone time ("me time," a walk, a workout, an audiobook) so absence draws them back together, explicitly distinguishing *wanting to be alone* from *not wanting to be with someone*. Finally, through his behaviorist lens, he says a spouse — the person you esteem most and who holds the most reinforcers — shapes your behavior; Leila has made him more patient, less angry, kinder, and more considerate, and he's made her more aggressive, direct, and less patient. He likens picking a spouse to "buying all of the behaviors that you'd like to have in the future." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says he can't know whether the ~three business-first early years were necessary — "can't replay the game" — and won't claim prioritizing marriage sooner would have produced more success.
- As of March 2026, he frames December 2019 as a deliberate "recalibration moment": a single balcony conversation where both he and Leila agreed they disliked how they were living and chose to change it.
- As of March 2026, he attributes the marriage's durability largely to always being "on the same page," which he calls exceptional luck; if Leila had wanted to keep things as they were, he says, that would have been a problem.
- As of March 2026, he claims a benefit of living mutually entwined is shared exposure to the same stimuli, so they tend to "suffer at the same time" and each senses when the other is off.
- As of March 2026, he states that from the day they met he and Leila have spent only two weekends apart (excluding a ~3-week launch trip), estimating their time together equals roughly a 70-year marriage versus the ~4–5 quality hours/week he says the average couple gets.
- As of March 2026, he argues the key thing to manage in a long relationship is closeness, not distance — couples who both work from home spend too much time together in the wrong ways and become roommates/siblings, losing mystery and chemistry.
- As of March 2026, his practice is to deliberately create physical space and explicit permission for alone time, distinguishing "wanting to be alone" from "not wanting to be with someone," and calls making it okay to not hang out all the time one of the biggest things that has helped them.
- As of March 2026, via his behaviorist frame, he claims your spouse — the person you hold in highest esteem and who holds the most reinforcers — has the biggest influence on who you become, and that he and Leila have exercised reciprocal, deliberate "shaping" of each other.
- As of March 2026, he says Leila has made him more patient, less angry, kinder, and more considerate, while he's made her more aggressive, more direct, and less patient — their "yin and yang."
- As of March 2026, he reframes choosing a spouse as "buying all of the behaviors that you'd like to have in the future from somebody else," expecting rough edges to smooth out over time together.
