# I Lost $100 Million... Here's What I Learned

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video in which he narrates eleven career mistakes that, he says,
collectively cost him roughly $100 million (mixing real cash losses, opportunity cost,
and lost sale-multiple value). The losses run from small dollar amounts (a $50,000 Ohio
rental house) to his self-described "biggest Kahuna" — a 2019 pricing/offer change at Gym
Launch that he estimates permanently cost him ~$5M/year in profit, amplified at exit
multiple. Each mistake is paired with a transferable lesson so viewers "get the lesson
without the scar."

## Key claims

The $100M figure is a career-total aggregate across eleven distinct mistakes, not a
single event. Ordered roughly smallest-to-largest as he presents them:

1. **Crypto (self-reported ~$500K loss).** In 2017 he set up an account to buy ethereum
   at ~$100, put $1M in, but did not click purchase when it dipped to ~$93; it later ~30x'd.
   A few years later, with Bitcoin at $60,000, he and a different friend each put in $1M
   and lost $500K in weeks. Lesson: stay inside your "sphere of confidence" (marketing,
   sales, customer acquisition, pricing, product); the further from his core, the more he
   loses. Beware FOMO — "when everyone thinks something is a good idea it's usually a bad idea."
2. **Real estate — Ohio house (~$50K loss).** His first-ever real-estate investment. A
   seller flipped him a $50,000 house he'd actually bought for $19,000 and put nothing
   into; it was a 2-bed sold as 3-bed, had no legal electricity (run illegally from a
   neighbor), an unusable driveway. He had to house the tenants in a hotel, then dumped it
   for ~$20,000. Lessons: if it's too good to be true it usually is; ask how long someone
   has been in a deal (won't consider < 2–3 years / short hold period); invest alongside
   the operator ("I'll buy a property that you buy").
3. **Partnerships — bad structure (nine failed partnerships).** Early partners were
   ethical people but the deals were badly structured. Lessons: a partner must bring
   something you don't (time, money, or skills) or one of you isn't needed; you lose the
   most equity on day one; equity need not be equal (a 95/5 split is fine); if you work in
   the business, pay yourself a salary separate from ownership. "Not making a profit is not
   reinvesting everything in the business — it means your business sucks."
4. **Getting fancy (ROIC).** His first Huntington Beach gym cost $37,000 to open and made
   $25,000/month profit within 6 months. His second location cost $250,000 (glass lobby,
   bigger space) and made the same money. Lesson: return on invested capital — every
   expense must be tied to a return; "don't be fancy, do the basics." Cites a PE-CEO mentor:
   "don't be cute — backyard football... two fat guys in the middle to the right."
5. **Bad partner (the traumatizing one).** After selling five of six gyms, he partnered
   with a gym operator to launch and own gyms. The partner had him personally guarantee
   leases, front all costs, do all the work and sales; then one morning the partner drained
   the entire bank account in a single withdrawal, claiming his "half," sent the money to
   his girlfriend in Sweden, and filed bankruptcy so he couldn't be sued. The partner had
   previously been **indicted for fraud**, which Alex knew. Emotionally the worst: he went
   from multi-location gym owner to "losing everything I had spent years building in 6
   months." Lesson: "if someone shows you who they are, believe them the first time."
6. **Push vs. pivot (post-Vanderbilt consulting job).** At a management-consulting firm
   doing space/cyber/defense work, after ~6–9 months he stopped learning and neither pushed
   (sought challenge) nor pivoted (changed path) — he coasted and read books all day, was a
   "bad employee." Lesson: doing nothing is the real mistake; cost him time and self-confidence.
7. **Food business (~$500K "made" but heavy opportunity cost, part of the $100M).** He
   started a meal company off his gym/supplement distribution. First month did $600,000
   (60,000 meals at $10) but cost was $9/meal → ~7% margins; 80% of support complaints came
   from meals while 90% of profit came from supplements. Estimates this split-focus mistake
   cost ~$10–25M in lost/foregone value because the distraction reduced Gym Launch profit at
   a sale multiple. Lesson: "more better," don't split focus; understand the margins of a
   business before entering.
8. **Two "where the bodies are buried" mistakes (~$1.2M+).** (a) An HR director in
   California unilaterally ordered a cost-of-living study and gave company-wide raises
   (including a large one to herself) via a blast email — cost $600,000/year in profit for
   zero benefit; she was removed. (b) He delegated legal compliance; CFO Suzanne found a
   $120,000/month legal bill from lawyers reviewing every piece of licensee marketing —
   notes nobody read; cost ~$1.2M. Lesson: stay close enough to know what problems each
   department has; sign the checks.
9. **Software — ALAN (~$1.5M+).** Built an AI lead-nurture/messaging bot for gyms
   (front-desk staff booked 10% of leads; the bot booked 19%). Mistake: he had no technical
   co-founder and outsourced core development to a dev shop that undocumented the code and
   kept raising fees as it saw revenue. Sunk-cost fallacy pushed spend to ~$1.5M+. Lesson:
   a software company needs a technical co-founder; never outsource the core value engine.
10. **Vendors (~$15M real cash loss).** Paid a vendor $270,000 as "insurance" to handle a
    big launch's volume; the vendor lied about capacity to put his logo on their site, and
    ~75% of the launch traffic never made it through after 9 months of buildup. Lessons: get
    references *like you* / your use case; "referrals mean jack" — treat every referred
    vendor as a stranger and demand assurances; when someone shows you who they are, believe
    them the first time.
11. **The biggest Kahuna — 2019 pricing/offer error (~$5M/year, biggest at exit).** A
    competitor undercut him and poached top gyms. Reacting rather than anticipating, he
    added a huge, operationally complex 1-on-1 accountability layer AND lowered price from
    $1,000/week ($4K/month) to $800/week for everyone (grandfathered down). Losing 20% of
    topline on ~40% margins cut profit roughly in half; estimates ~$5M/year in profit,
    never recovered, and multiplied at the eventual sale of Gym Launch. Lessons: all price
    changes draw complaints (people dislike change); listen to customers, not competitors —
    cites Paul Graham: "every business problem can be solved by talking to your customers."
