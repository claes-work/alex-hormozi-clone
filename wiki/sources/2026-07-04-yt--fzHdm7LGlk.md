# $15 In, $550 Out. Why Aren't You Scaling This?

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Cassie, who runs an indoor children's play park and wants to grow a single location from roughly $1.1M toward $2M. She is considering shifting focus from play visits to birthday parties. Alex argues that both revenue paths appear economically close, so the real missing piece is not a new front-end but an in-person sales motion that converts play visitors into higher-LTV purchases such as birthdays, camps, field trips, and memberships. He emphasizes giving customers what they already want on the front end, then using sales and education to sell what they need next.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Cassie** - the coachee. Her play-park model, revenue, visit count, LTV estimates, and birthday-party context are background only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If a business has a front-end that already brings customers in cheaply, the founder should not try to make customers want something different.
- The hard part is finding the front-end offer that gets people in the door; once customers are in the door, the business can fix LTV through sales motion and education.
- For an in-person venue, the sales motion has to happen in person, not only through post-visit emails.
- Check-in should seed the next sale, for example by asking whether the visitor is there for a birthday party even when they are not.
- Check-out should ask about upcoming events and get the next purchase booked while the parent and child are still in the experience.
- Alex compares the situation to a teeth-whitening chain in which selling plans instead of waiting for repeat visits increased LTV by 4x.
- If the play-park business doubles LTV from the same traffic, Alex thinks the single location can reach roughly $2M.
