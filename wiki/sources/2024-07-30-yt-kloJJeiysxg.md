# 4 Steps to Unf*** Your Business

## Summary

A recorded group coaching / Q&A session (not a true solo monologue) in which Alex Hormozi walks entrepreneurs through diagnosing and fixing a stuck business using four growth levers — traffic, conversion, price, churn — and his "more, better, new / for who" prioritization loop. The second half turns to community retention (overwhelm as the main cause of churn, "consumption is the highest priority"), problem-definition through customer conversations, and a long treatment of hiring A-players, treating recruitment as an acquisition funnel, and learning "what good looks like" at each level. Multiple community members, plus team members referred to as Sam, Kirby and "goose," also speak; only Alex-attributed material is persona data.

## Key claims (the framework)

- **Four levers of growth.** Every business grows through exactly four levers: **traffic, conversion, price, churn.** To grow, one or more of these must change; if a task doesn't "ladder up" to one of the four, don't do it.
- **Find the biggest constraint, then fix it.** Scale until something breaks, fix the thing that broke, then go back to scaling. Pick the single lever that is the biggest current constraint (e.g. a very low landing-page conversion rate is high-leverage because you can double it with a day of work).
- **Prioritize in the order "more → better → new," then "who."** First ask why you can't just do *more* of what already works; if capped, make it *better* (tighter hooks, higher-quality content); only when you can't do more or better do something *new*. Then decide *who* owns it. Most small entrepreneurs stall because they find something that works, stop doing it, and get spread thin across six new things.
- **For retention/community: consumption is the highest priority.** Value only lands if it's consumed; overwhelm is the main cause of churn, so remove, simplify and shorten rather than keep adding. Diagnose churn by talking to customers who left vs. those who stayed and copying/removing the differences.
- **Defining the problem is the hard part.** The silver bullet is disguised — "if it were obvious you would have already done it." Iterate fast by showing drafts to real users (even an outsider who doesn't get it is the best signal) before launching into reality.
- **Hire like an acquisition funnel; brand counts double.** Recruiting mirrors the customer funnel (generate → nurture → interview[=sale] → onboard → retain/ascend). Scaling is where the inflow of talent matches the inflow of customers. Brand works once on the front end for customers and again on the back end for talent.
- **A-players need a vision big enough to fit their dreams; training is inversely proportional to skill.** The slowest, highest-value skill in entrepreneurship is learning "what good looks like" — in general and per role.
