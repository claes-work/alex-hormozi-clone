# Helping a $140M Roofing Company Scale

## Summary
Alex Hormozi advises a roofing entrepreneur (self-identified as Cody) who sold his ~$20M retail roofing business into a PE roll-up and is now the "platform" tasked with combining eight acquired brands (~$140M aggregate revenue). Cody wants an "irresistible" roof offer he can roll out across all brands, but worries about hard costs preventing a money-back guarantee. Hormozi reframes the priority — sequence the low-risk, high-certainty change (centralizing virtual sales) before touching the offer — and then proposes a "profit guarantee" structure suited to high-hard-cost businesses.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues the offer is the highest-leverage lever in a business but also the riskiest, because it touches every department (billing, sales, advertising) and can build or destroy a business overnight.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises introducing change in graded levels rather than changing ~five things at once; the greater the change, the greater the risk it fails to yield the wanted outcome.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames every change as a bet that the return from the change exceeds the cost of making it.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends centralizing sales FIRST (before altering the offer): virtual/remote centralized sales cuts road costs, widens the hiring pool for better cheaper closers, and raises sales utilization — potentially a ~25% EBITDA lift on its own by cutting the low-performing bottom third of the sales force.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes EBITDA is what such a roll-up is valued on (he estimates ~$30M EBITDA on ~$140M revenue implies ~$300–400M enterprise value), so cost/revenue changes that drive EBITDA disproportionately raise exit value — "do this massive change first before risking the biscuit."
- As of March 2026, Alex's guarantee formula (from his Offers book, ~page 125): "If you don't [X] in [Y] time, we will [Z]" — a guarantee must state terms, timeline, and remedy; saying "I guarantee it" alone means nothing.
- As of March 2026, Alex says a guarantee's purpose is to maximize NET conversions (increase sales more than refunds), not to have the craziest terms; if 10% or 25% back changes behavior as much as 100% back, give the smaller amount.
- As of March 2026, Alex's high-hard-cost workaround is a "profit guarantee": guarantee on-time and on-budget delivery or give back the profit (~20%), keeping the vendor above water while showing skin in the game.
- As of March 2026, Alex says customers don't want their money back — they want the roof; a guarantee just pays down the risk of not getting the wanted outcome. Guarantee around the customer's biggest fears (in home services: on time, on budget, and for roofs, disruption/displacement).
- Caller claims (unverified): his roofing business did ~$140M last year across the group, targeting ~$250M this year; ~40% gross margin, 10–15% net; retail (not insurance/storm-chasing) model; sold ~30 days ago into a PE firm with eight tuck-ins; his own pre-acquisition revenue was ~$20M; centralized full sales control was a condition of his deal.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "The offer is one of the highest leverage things you can change, but it also affects every department. It changes how you bill. It changes how you sell. Changes how you advertise. It changes everything. Which is why it can change a business overnight, but it can also destroy a business overnight."

> "Let's do this massive change first before risking the biscuit."

> "I would typically not try and change like five things at once."

> "The point of a guarantee is not to have the craziest guarantee. The point of a guarantee is to maximize net conversions."

> "People don't want their money back. They want the roof. And so they just want to know that you care enough to make sure the roof gets delivered."

> "I position it as a profit guarantee. So it's like, listen, you want your thing to be on time and on budget, probably. And so I guarantee that I will deliver it on time and on budget or I'll give you my profit, which is 20%. And that way you're not underwater and you still have 40% gross margins."

> "You want to list out what their biggest fears are and then crush those with the guarantee."
