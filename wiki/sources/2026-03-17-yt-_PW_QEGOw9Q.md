# Helping an E-Com Brand Make Better Content

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A welding-accessories e-commerce founder ($1.7M revenue, ~300k TikTok followers, Shopify store) wants to reach $10M and feels demand has plateaued. He's burned out after a year of daily original content and is weighing whether to double his TikTok audience (300k → 600k) or open a new channel like Amazon. Alex reframes the question — the founder isn't actually resource-constrained to an either/or choice — and delivers his core **content sustainability** framework: **reverse-engineer yourself**. Figure out what you actually enjoy making, because that's what you can keep making long-term. Your two options are to either genuinely like the content you produce, or to *capture yourself doing things you already like*; everything past that is "pain tolerance." Because the founder enjoys educational content but struggles with idea generation, Alex delivers his central creative principle: **"we need to be reminded more than we need to be taught,"** and it "goes double for your audience." Creators wrongly believe content must be as novel *for them* as it is for the audience — it doesn't. Dave Ramsey has answered "I'm broke" questions for 35 years ("stop spending the money you make"); each call is the same core answer wrapped in a slightly novel context (different lottery amount, different life circumstances). Alex adds that a visual business like welding is a huge content advantage versus teaching intangibles, and that audiences will happily watch "today's version" of a familiar thing (he'll watch the same gym-equipment reel 30-40 times). Prescription: find a format you enjoy, then optimize the algorithm mechanics *around* that format rather than the reverse. On follow-ups, Alex probes the real constraints — the founder admits he doesn't know his numbers, runs 50/50 organic/paid (Google + Meta), and his marketing team wants to "trickle up slower." Alex's blunt take: spend more. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes an "either/or" resource question (list on Amazon *or* make more content) as a false constraint — the founder shouldn't chase a random new channel "for no reason."
- As of March 2026, the long-term content play is figuring out how to make content you actually *enjoy* so you can keep making it; the two options are to like what you make, or to "capture yourself doing things you already like" — the rest is "pain tolerance."
- As of March 2026, there are "no rules and no content police"; creators should give themselves permission to make what they want, and a creator's content naturally shifts season to season (Alex includes himself) as their focus changes.
- As of March 2026, his central creative principle: "we need to be reminded more than we need to be taught," and this "goes double for your audience" — a familiar message in a slightly novel context is a service, not a disservice.
- As of March 2026, a big self-imposed burden creators carry is believing content must be as novel for *them* as it is for the audience — which is "just not true."
- As of March 2026 (example), Dave Ramsey has answered "I'm broke" questions for 35 years with essentially the same answer ("stop spending the money you make"); each call differs only by surface context (different lottery amounts, different personal circumstances) — and part of why he's sustained it 35 years is that he *enjoys* it.
- As of March 2026, a visual, tangible business (welding, "cool projects") is a huge content advantage versus a business built on intangibles — Alex notes demonstrating "client finance acquisition" is far less visually compelling than welding.
- As of March 2026, audiences under-appreciated appetite: Alex will watch reels of the same gym equipment 30-40 times; audiences are happy to watch "today's version of that thing," so just be okay with posting it.
- As of March 2026, prescription: start with yourself, find a format you like, *then* figure out all the algorithm optimization "around it" to make it clickable — reverse backwards from what you enjoy.
- As of March 2026, on the founder's other constraints: not knowing your numbers is a real problem; the business is ~50/50 organic (TikTok) vs. paid (Google + Meta), and Alex's advice on scaling ad spend is simply "spend more" (rejecting the marketing team's "trickle up slower" caution).
