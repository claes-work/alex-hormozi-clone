# How to Find A Players for Your Business

## Summary

A short solo teaching clip (Alex monologuing to camera; no second speaker) on the
**sourcing/finding** angle of A-player hiring. Alex's headline filter: you find A-players
by how densely they describe their own role in metrics — "I can measure someone's skill in
any endeavor based on the quality and the quantity of metrics they track." He illustrates
with a story about the first strong HR professional he hired, who out-interviewed *him* by
asking talent-side metrics he'd never tracked (cost of acquiring talent, time to fill,
90-day two-way fit), and generalizes to a preference for hiring people who can teach him
things he doesn't know. The second half is about **interview volume as the actual sourcing
mechanism**: interview far more people than feels reasonable, use group interviews for
frontline-through-manager roles, and understand that volume "tunes your picker" — pattern
recognition built across hundreds of conversations becomes one of the biggest assets in
building each next company faster. He closes on the reframe that building a business "feels
much more like assembling the people who then build the business."

## Key claims (2026-02-14, paraphrased unless quoted)

- You find A-players by **metrics density**: look for candidates who describe their role in
  metrics in as much detail as possible. Skill in any endeavor is measurable by the quality
  *and* quantity of metrics a person tracks. (Same signal as the existing 3-part rubric.)
- ★ Concrete **talent-side metrics an A-player tracks** (from the first strong HR pro he
  hired, a turnaround specialist): cost of acquiring talent, time to fill, and **two-way
  90-day fit** — the % of hires where, 90 days later, *both* the employee and the manager
  rate it a 10/10 fit. He'd tracked none of these; "not having lawsuits lately" was his
  low-bar talent equivalent of judging a product by a low refund rate (which he flags is
  *not* how you know a product is good).
- Prefer to **hire people who can teach you** — paying someone who knows more than you to
  do it inside your business is "the best thing in the world." Early on this is hard because
  such people don't want to work for you, you can't afford them, or both.
- ★ **Interview far more people than you think you should** — expensive, but it's the
  sourcing mechanism. Stack as many as you can; use **group interviews** for frontline
  through manager roles (saves time, lets you read vibes fast); keep director/executive
  interviews individual.
- ★ **Interview volume "tunes your picker."** Talking to 10 self-described sales managers
  teaches you more than talking to one; by the 50th you already know who has game. Pattern
  recognition (some call it wisdom) accumulated this way is one of the biggest assets in
  business and is why each successive company can be built faster.
- Figuring out what "good" looks like at each level has a **trial cost**: he had to hire
  ~five different sales managers and waste six months to a year on each before finding a good
  one. You repeat this level by level — frontline, first-line manager, director — until you
  know what "right" looks like across an entire org.
- Reframe: at scale **you're not building the business — you're assembling the people who
  then build it.**
