# How I Got 106M Impressions With Ads

## Summary
In this early solo teaching video — explicitly made partly to train his own marketing team — Hormozi walks through how he mines client-success data to manufacture compelling marketing hooks. His central device is a "three wheels" framework: any statistic can be spun by combining (1) the outcome/result, (2) the time duration, and (3) the percentage of clients who achieve it, and each wheel itself has many variables that can be inverted or reframed. He demonstrates with real Gym Launch numbers (e.g. "one in 5.5 gyms is a million-dollar-a-year gym," reframed as 18% or as "4.5 out of 5.5"), showing how the same underlying data can be sliced into dozens of different, more or less compelling claims. He argues his edge is that he actually tracks his data while most competitors do not, giving him substantiated, compliant, high-conviction claims. He stresses that the goal is to hunt the data for the slice that tells (or reinforces) the story the copy needs.

## Key claims
- The "three wheels" of any marketing statistic: (1) the outcome/result, (2) the time duration, and (3) the percentage of clients who achieve it — mix and match to generate near-unlimited hooks from one dataset.
- Wheel one (percentage of clients) can be expressed as a raw percentage, top X%, bottom X%, median, average, "one in N," or the fractional/inverted version (e.g. "one in five" vs. "four out of five").
- Their most successful campaign ever was a "one year later" campaign: took clients of 1+ year, measured stats before vs. a year later, and showed increases like $250K, $500K, or $1M in extra revenue (self-reported).
- Reframing example: "one in 5.5 gyms is a million-dollar gym" tests better than the mathematically identical "18% of gyms are seven-figure" — and can also be inverted to "4.5 out of 5.5 gyms hit this."
- He deliberately plays both on survivorship bias / lottery desire ("people think they'll be the one") and on averages/medians ("this is just what the normal person does"); after stating an average he adds "that also means half of people are above this," exploiting that everyone thinks they're above average.
- Longer durations are advantageous: 1-year / 2-year testimonials prove clients stay (social proof of continued value creation), demonstrate long-standingness in the space, and clients who stay that long usually have strong outcomes — especially in education-based businesses.
- Data can also be sliced by short windows: their average gym adds ~$15,500 in additional revenue in the first two weeks (self-reported; "half of people get better than that").
- Four ways to show an increase: percent increase, absolute increase (e.g. "+$15,500"), total number made (revenue plus increase), and a relative increase — his favorite — reframing the number as what it buys (e.g. "the average gym pays off nine months of rent in the first 30 days").
- The time-duration wheel can be flipped into a cadence hook: "one in every X days we achieve Y" — e.g. ~52 seven-figure gyms created in a year becomes "one in every 7.7 days we create another seven-figure gym."
- The same three wheels apply to any metric: revenue, profit, churn, growth percentage, etc.
- Numbers must be fully compliant and substantiated; his advantage is that he has tracked client-success data for years while most competitors do not track theirs (often because their numbers aren't good).
- Data is his central selling point because "there's only so many ways for me to say we make gyms more money" — and everyone promises that, so data makes a more unique, compelling, believable promise.
- Stats give him high conviction because "those are what they are" — take it or leave it.
