# Helping an Agency Owner Hire Better Talent

## Summary
Live coaching clip. An agency owner runs a done-for-you client-acquisition business for high-ticket agencies (he gets contracted by a marketing agency — Amazon, email, paid — and runs both their ads and their sales). He does ~$5M top line with 31 clients paying ~$150k/year each (~$12–15k/month, fairly even, one whale at $27k), and wants to scale to $1M/month toward a ~$30M-EBITDA exit. His self-diagnosed constraint: **talent, specifically sales directors** for his own team — each director can only manage ~3 clients (3 pods of 5–6 reps), and he can't find enough seven/eight-figure directors experienced in this space and price point. He recruits via organic, paid (Indeed), and LinkedIn at ~$15k/month spend; cost to acquire a sales director is ~$60k, and a director is worth ~$600k/year in gross profit — an arbitrage Alex confirms works ("60k in, 600k out"). His problem: TAM of qualified directors is "dwindling." **Alex reframes the whole constraint as a false one.** He argues the real issue is the *sales process*, not the talent pool: the owner's scripting is "probably a bit too consultative," which forces reliance on rare "unicorn" salespeople with specialized skill instead of a refined, repeatable "follow-the-script" process. Fix the script so the process (not the person) does the selling, and you no longer need a $60k hire — the arbitrage "gets really juicy" and the TAM of possible reps opens up massively (he points to the huge pool of door-to-door sellers who already sell harder things, for whom moving to selling from a computer at home is a cheap, teachable learning curve). On the director role specifically, Alex insists directors don't need to hop on phones or know delivery/media — their job is **culture and training**, so they arguably need *less* product knowledge than frontline reps because "teaching sales is teaching sales." He also flags a span-of-control point: managing ~15 reps per director is "a little high"; he'd start splitting teams around 10 (e.g. two managers with 7–8 each). His prescription: it's a pure sales-training problem — use the sales handbook/training process Acquisition.com provides. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes a "can't find enough talent" hiring bottleneck as a **sales-process problem, not a talent-supply problem** — the felt scarcity of qualified people is usually a symptom of an unrefined process.
- As of March 2026, a sales process that is "too consultative" forces reliance on rare "unicorn" salespeople with specialized skill; a tightened, repeatable script lets ordinary trained reps succeed, so you no longer need expensive hires.
- As of March 2026, the tactical scripting fix: when a prospect asks off-script or "weed-based" questions, loop them back to the straight line and keep them moving, rather than letting the rep improvise consultatively.
- As of March 2026, refining the script is an **arbitrage unlock** — it lets you stop paying ~$60k to acquire a specialist and "massively opens up the TAM of potential sales prospects" you can hire from.
- As of March 2026, the untapped talent pool for high-ticket phone sales is huge (e.g. door-to-door sellers who already sell harder products); moving them to selling from home on a computer is a cheap, teachable learning curve — mainly language and script, not delivery.
- As of March 2026, a sales director's job is **culture and training**, not selling — they don't hop on phones, and they need *less* product/delivery knowledge than frontline reps because "teaching sales is teaching sales."
- As of March 2026, reps don't need to know the delivery (the ad/media work) or more about the client's business than the client does — they just need to know how to sell "how we can help you"; Alex notes his own sales team averages ~23 years old.
- As of March 2026, span-of-control guidance: ~15 reps under one director is "a little high"; Alex prefers to start splitting teams around 10, favoring e.g. two managers with 7–8 reps each.
- As of March 2026, the recruiting economics can be sound on their own (~$60k to acquire a director worth ~$600k/year gross profit) — but scaling by "doing way more of that" fails when the qualified-talent TAM is dwindling, which is why fixing the process beats buying scarcer talent.
