# Why Self-Care Makes You Poor & Fragile

## Summary

A short, contrarian rant (expanded from a tweet that went viral that morning) arguing the
"self-care movement" is nonsense that traps people. Hormozi's core move: if you constantly
need to escape your life, the fix isn't more self-care — it's changing the life you're
trying to escape from.

## Key claims

- If you feel you constantly need self-care/escape, that's a signal your daily life is wrong
  — better to "flip the ratio" and change what you do than to schedule recovery from it.
  (paraphrase)
- Weekends are a recent human invention (he dates the first union-passed weekend to 1929 in
  England), evidence that the need for "time off" comes from hating what you do. (paraphrase
  — see accuracy flag in Notes)
- People who tell you you're "working too much" or "not balancing" are projecting their own
  self-hatred; they can't imagine someone genuinely liking their work every day. (paraphrase)
- Genuine physical needs (sleep, rest) are real and distinct from the "spa day" self-care
  narrative; the objection is to *prescribing* how others must relax. (paraphrase)
- Mortality as liberation: "you're going to die and that is the ultimate freedom" — most
  constraints are imagined chains that only exist in your mind. (paraphrase)

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "If you want to truly give self-care, then stop hating what you do every day — novel
  concept, I know — stop hanging out with people that suck."
- "It's my life. I don't need to live by anyone else's rules, and neither do you."
- "The reason these people try and project their own self-hatred for their own lives onto
  you is because they cannot imagine a world in which someone might actually like what they
  do every day."
- "You're going to die and that is the ultimate freedom."
- "You have been through harder [stuff] than this. You will survive. It will be okay."
