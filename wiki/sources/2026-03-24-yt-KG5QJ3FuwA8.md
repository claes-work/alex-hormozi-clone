# Helping a Home Services Business Get to $3M/Year

## Summary
In a live coaching exchange, a cleaning-services business owner (self-reported $1.2M in sales, targeting $3M in 18 months) asks Alex Hormozi whether to focus on residential homeowners or vacation-rental hosts. Hormozi walks through an LTV:CAC comparison and argues for eliminating the weaker, smaller, price-conscious avatar and concentrating entirely on the larger, stickier one. A second owner's case (a yoga studio owner who also bought a chocolate factory) is used to illustrate that businesses should not attack additional verticals or avatars until they have nearly maxed out their core addressable market.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex advises picking the avatar with the better LTV:CAC and the larger addressable market, and letting the weaker avatar "just die" rather than run two businesses in parallel. (Caller reports 9:1 LTV:CAC on recurring residential homeowners vs. 6:1 on vacation-rental hosts, with far more homeowners — unverified caller figures.)
- As of March 2026, Alex frames serving a second avatar with a different customer journey and process as effectively running "two businesses," which splits attention and makes both mediocre.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues focus plus operational/process improvements could push a 9:1 unit economics toward 12–14:1, but that gain is forfeited if attention is split.
- As of March 2026, Alex says attacking new verticals/avatars (via M&A or building) only makes sense once a company has nearly maxed out its addressable market — a stage he associates with much larger scale (he jokingly defers the caller's decision to $10M, $30M, $100M).
- As of March 2026, Alex characterizes adding a second service "because we needed the money and we said yes because YOLO" as poor business discipline versus a deliberate expansion decision.
- Alex describes businesses growing in step-changes ("all at once and then all at once"), not smoothly.
