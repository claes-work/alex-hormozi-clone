# I'm in E-Commerce. Should I Build a Software Product?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. An Australian hair-extension entrepreneur runs three related things — a salon (~$900k revenue, 50% margin), a wholesale/B2B and direct-to-consumer extensions business (~$2.6M, growing ~30% month-on-month, 30% margin), and both run without her. She has already spent $500k building a salon-booking SaaS with AI analytics, wants to move into software because she believes her service businesses aren't "sellable" at a high multiple, and asks Alex to bless the plan (she already has 150 "stockist" subscribers and imagines 2,000 converting to ~$2M/year). Alex declines to tell her what she wants to hear and instead argues **against building the second business**. His reasoning: (1) the SaaS is *harder*, less cash-flow-positive, and won't make money "for many years if ever" — "to win at software you have to commit to making no money for basically 7 years," while her competitors don't have two other businesses to run; (2) having a distribution base does not mean you should sell it every product it *could* buy; (3) the $500k already spent is **sunk cost** — "some of the best money you ever spend is deciding to stop spending bad money." He reframes her real constraint: the current business is *not* unsellable — extensions are a **reoccurring** product (customers rebuy every 8–16 weeks) and she simply needs more customers and better margins. His prescription for the existing business: raise prices, improve margins, and go get acquisition — make content, run ads to attract stylists, use offset funding to get them integrated, and run a hunter/farmer motion where you help salons make more money selling extensions (more than they make cutting and coloring) so they stick. He also distinguishes "reoccurring" from "recurring" (same value) and says what matters is demonstrating **revenue retention**. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, having an organic audience or distribution base does not mean you should sell it every product it can buy; spawning multiple mini-businesses that serve the same customer pool usually means you dodged the real constraint — growing the audience/customer count.
- As of March 2026, building a SaaS as a first-time software founder, self-funded, while running two other businesses is a bad move: it's harder, less cash-flow-positive, and won't make money "for many years if ever."
- As of March 2026, "to win at software you have to commit to making no money for basically 7 years," and your software competitors don't have two other businesses splitting their focus.
- As of March 2026, money already sunk into the software build is sunk-cost fallacy; deciding to stop spending bad money is itself some of the best money you can spend — said "only having done it myself."
- As of March 2026, a service/product business is not inherently unsellable — extensions are a **reoccurring** purchase (rebought every ~8–16 weeks), so the fix is more customers and better margins, not a pivot.
- As of March 2026, "reoccurring" and "recurring" revenue have the same value; what a business must demonstrate is **revenue retention**.
- As of March 2026, software is "probably the worst way" to achieve the goal of making passive money online while not working.
- As of March 2026 (prescription for the extensions business): raise pricing, improve margins, and get acquisition — make content, run ads to attract stylists, use offset funding to integrate them, and run a hunter/farmer play that helps salons make more money selling extensions than they make cutting/coloring so they keep selling them.
