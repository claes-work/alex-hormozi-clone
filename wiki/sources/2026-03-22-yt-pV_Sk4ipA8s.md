# You Can't Build Something Big If You Want Everything

## Summary
In a live workshop Q&A, Alex Hormozi coaches a serial-entrepreneur "visionary" (a publishing-house owner running multiple businesses) on gaining focus. He argues that building something truly big requires saying no to nearly every opportunity, trading novelty for loyalty to one business, and channeling creative/ADD energy into marketing and content rather than into disruptive internal changes. He introduces a "risk-adjusted" change-decision razor and a personal practice of parking ideas on a list instead of acting on them.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that the more talented you are, the more opportunities you could pursue — which just means more things you must say no to; you have to say no to nearly every opportunity to make one worth pursuing.
- As of March 2026, he frames the core trade-off: continue chasing novelty and pay the price in impact and business size, or build something big with teams and systems and give up the novelty of constantly doing new things.
- As of March 2026, he recounts staying stuck on multi-year plateaus because he wanted to pursue every single thing, and says you must "trade novelty for loyalty" — becoming loyal to one business (an analogy he extends to relationships and to business aging like fine wine).
- As of March 2026, he says he realized how much work greatness takes and felt deep sadness recognizing he has only two or three big entrepreneurial "swings" left in his life — and that acquisition.com is the swing he plans to ride until he is too old.
- As of March 2026, he advises channeling creative/ADD energy into things accretive to the business — specifically marketing, sales, content, and ads — rather than into disruptive internal changes; he says making content has made him a better entrepreneur because it distracts him from messing up his business.
- As of March 2026, he shares his change-decision razor: any change causes roughly a 20% immediate performance decrement (anecdotal, "Alex doing this"), and left alone, teams tend to improve on their own — so he only makes a change if he sees a risk-adjusted 20% improvement, defined as a 40% expected improvement with a 50/50 chance of happening.
- As of March 2026, he recommends keeping a private "big list of ideas" on your phone, writing ideas down instead of calling emergency team meetings, and only acting on one if it still preoccupies you weeks later and beats other pending high-return moves.
- As of March 2026, he says he has "grown to accept that some [things stay broken]," because every attempted fix causes several other things to drop, and the cost of change usually is not worth it.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You basically have to say no to every opportunity to be able to make one opportunity worth pursuing."

> "You have to trade novelty for loyalty. And so it's like you become loyal to the business."

> "Life will not give you what you want if you want everything. We have to make trades or we get nothing."

> "I only have like two maybe three entrepreneurial seasons left in me. It's like I only get two or three more big swings. That's it."

> "I put all of my ADD into content. I've become a significantly better entrepreneur by making content because it distracts me from [messing] up my business."

> "Whenever I decide to make a change in the business, I see about a 20% decrement or decrease in performance."

> "If I don't see a risk-adjusted 20% improvement... I need to see a 40% improvement that I think has a 50/50 shot at happening for me to make any change at all."

> "When you leave people alone, they tend to just get better at their jobs."

> "Instead of messaging the team and saying, 'Hey guys, emergency meeting'... I just write it down. And I just keep living my life."

> "If you're still thinking about that idea a month later, it probably has some legs."

> "I have grown to accept that some [things stay broken]."
