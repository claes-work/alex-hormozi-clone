# My Biggest Mistake in Business

## Summary
Interview clip (two speakers: an unmarked investor/host who asks the questions and contributes the `>>` turns, and Alex, who gives the substantive answers — only Alex's words are used as voice/belief data here). Asked whether, in hindsight, he'd have gone deeper on his winners or diversified more, Alex names his **biggest financial mistake**: alongside Gym Launch he built a software company called **ALAN** as a *lead-nurture* tool (decision-tree responses, pre-AI) instead of building a simple **CRM for gyms**. He believes that product choice capped the exit — self-reporting that the business sold for roughly **$46 million ("46 to be exact")** when a CRM "would have sold for half a billion dollars." On the broader focus-vs-diversification question he declines to declare a verdict, because he "can't play out the other side": had he never diversified he might have 500–2,000 gyms and possibly be richer, but he also might not be doing what he does now. He reframes his scattered path — driven by his self-described ADD (which is *why* he preaches focus so hard) — as the source of his current edge: he has founded/scaled a B2C online fitness business, a brick-and-mortar service business, a physical-products B2C business, B2B SaaS (ALAN), and prosumer B2C SaaS (Skool), plus an investing arm, and has exited via nearly every deal structure (to competitor, customer, partner, private equity, friends; seller-financed, cash, leveraged buyouts). Like **Steve Jobs' calligraphy class** feeding into the Macintosh, that breadth is what lets him "talk about business" with real depth today — so the Alex Hormozi brand might not exist without it. The clip then turns to an investing lesson (largely a back-and-forth with the host): the hard-won realization that **the best companies feel expensive yet still yield higher returns**. Alex confesses he's had to unlearn Buffett-style "cigar-butt" investing (a few last puffs from cheap, fair businesses) in favor of wonderful companies at fair-or-expensive prices, citing a decade-old Google Ventures study (up-priced rounds, even insider-led, tend to still be underpriced) and Thiel's PayPal point that a fast valuation jump is fair when a "material change" removes real risk. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex self-reports his biggest financial mistake: building **ALAN** as a lead-nurture software (decision-tree responses, before the AI era) instead of a simple **CRM for gyms** — he believes a CRM would have sold for "half a billion dollars" versus the ~$46 million the business actually sold for.
- As of March 2026, he declines to say whether focusing versus diversifying was the "right call," because the counterfactual is unknowable — had he never diversified he speculates he might now own 500 to ~2,000 gyms and *possibly* be richer, but might also not be doing what he does now.
- As of March 2026, he attributes his scattered path to his self-described ADD, which he says is precisely *why* he talks about focus so much.
- As of March 2026, he counts his range of experience as a moat: he has founded/scaled a B2C online fitness business (later turned in-person), a brick-and-mortar service business, an online service business, a physical-products B2C business (direct-to-consumer media buying plus wholesale to brick-and-mortar gyms), B2B SaaS (ALAN), and prosumer/B2C SaaS (Skool), plus an investing arm.
- As of March 2026, he has exited companies via nearly every structure — sold to a competitor, a customer, a partner, private equity, and friends; via seller-financed deals, cash deals, and leveraged buyouts.
- As of March 2026, he frames that breadth via Steve Jobs' calligraphy analogy: seemingly unrelated experiences later became "key" to his current work, making him "exceptionally good" at talking about business with depth — so the Alex Hormozi brand might not be what it is without them.
- As of March 2026 (investing), he holds that the best companies "feel expensive" yet still yield higher returns, and says he has had to learn this "the hard way" — unlearning Buffett-style cigar-butt investing (taking the last few puffs from cheap, fair businesses) in favor of wonderful companies at fair or even expensive prices.
- As of March 2026, he endorses the point (crediting Thiel/PayPal) that a large, fast valuation jump can be genuinely fair when a "material change" — e.g., a solved risk or an emergent network/virality effect — removes real risk that had justified the earlier discount, even though it feels unfair to have missed the lower price.
