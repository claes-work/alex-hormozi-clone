# How to Scale In-Person Events

> **Format note:** Q&A. A guest/questioner (runs a creative-AI Skool community doing
> in-person dinners) asks Alex Hormozi how to scale their in-person events. Alex
> answers. Questioner lines are attributed as such; everything else is Alex
> (paraphrase unless quoted).

## Summary

The questioner runs a ~30-person ticketed dinner series (~$150/ticket) for a creative-AI
Skool community, hosting events this month in London, Dubai, New York, and LA, and wants
to scale to ~250-person events using their existing organic reach. Alex diagnoses the
constraint: it is not demand or marketing, but the team's internal capacity to organize.
The team of six are all "content guys" with no events infrastructure. His prescription is
to hire a dedicated events coordinator who has run bigger events and many of them — someone
who will fully own the event end-to-end so the founder never has to touch the operational
stress. He also frames two structural event models (I-fly-to-you vs. you-fly-to-me) and
notes the you-come-to-me model is logistically far simpler and becomes available as price
point and pull increase.

## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased unless quoted)

- The rise of at-home creative-AI work is driving loneliness; that is a big reason Alex's
  team started their Skool community and is running a lot of in-person events with it.
- ★ **Diagnose the real limiter before scaling.** When asked how to scale, Alex first asks
  "What's the limiter to you scaling it?" The questioner's limiter is internal organizing
  capacity, not demand — they have plenty of organic reach and are only marketing to the
  Skool community. Scaling the wrong constraint (e.g. pushing more tickets) wouldn't help.
- ★ **Hire a dedicated events coordinator who owns it end-to-end.** The fix for an
  ops-capacity constraint is a person, not more effort from content-focused founders. Bring
  in someone who has done bigger events and a lot of them, so they "live and breathe events."
- ★ **Whether it's a full-time role depends on event frequency and size.** Alex declines to
  prescribe full-time vs. part-time in the abstract — it scales with how many events and how
  big they are.
- ★ **Delegate the stress, not just the tasks.** Alex says events stress him out because of
  the many moving pieces, even for a small one; if they do a bigger event he needs someone
  who owns it completely so he never hears about it. The point of the hire is to remove the
  founder from the operational load entirely.
- Reference model for what "someone who owns events" looks like: whoever runs Taylor
  Swift's events — enormous, happening every few days, pulled off reliably. Such
  specialists exist; go find one.
- ★ **Two event models.** (1) "I fly to you" and (2) "You fly to me." Which is viable
  depends on price point and how much pull/draw you have. Eventually you can move to the
  you-come-to-me model, which is logistically very straightforward by comparison.
- Event economics from the questioner (context, not Alex's claim): ~30-person ticketed
  dinners at ~$150/ticket; goal to scale to ~250 people.
