# Which Path Should You Take?

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host asks the questions; Alex answers). Alex reframes the "job vs. entrepreneurship / which path" question as a false binary. His first principle: at any given time you should be either **earning or learning** — ideally both. On the earning side, plenty of careers can get you to $100k–$1M/year inside a company; but if the goal is $20M/year, very few companies get you there and it takes a long time, so that goal pushes you toward more personal risk. On risk itself, he insists there is "no correct answer to a number of eternal questions" — how much risk you're willing to take is "a you question" no one can answer for you; you discover your tolerance empirically by starting to risk things (if you haven't slept in a week, maybe it's too much — or maybe you're just adapting). His core decision rule is to **reverse-engineer risk from the goal**: some paths let you all-but-guarantee a modest outcome ($1M via known, repeatable steps), and if you can guarantee success there's no reason to add risk; bigger goals ($100M) inherently require taking on more risk. The **worst-case scenario is a small goal paired with massive risk** — which he says happens a lot (the host adds the crypto/shitcoin example). On the job question specifically, Alex dissolves the "job or no job" frame entirely: jobs are among the best ways to learn skills, especially working under someone you admire, and often you get *paid* to learn what you'd otherwise pay money or time to acquire. He points to acquisition.com's heavy internal training and well-compensated staff as proof. The real questions aren't "job or not" but: what are my daily activities, how am I being paid for them, and how many skills am I learning in between — and that can be maximized either on your own or in a job. Ends with the standard $100 scaling roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, the "which path" question is a false binary; the real test at any moment is whether you are **earning or learning** (ideally both).
- As of March 2026, employment can realistically deliver $100k–$1M/year, and even $1M is achievable at a company; but ~$20M/year is available at very few companies and takes a long time, so outsized goals push you toward personal risk.
- As of March 2026, there is "no correct answer" to how much risk you should take — it's a personal question no one can answer for you, and you learn your tolerance only by actually starting to risk things.
- As of March 2026, his central rule is to **reverse-engineer risk from the goal**: if a modest outcome ($1M) can be near-guaranteed by known, repeatable steps, don't add risk; larger goals ($100M) inherently require more risk.
- As of March 2026, the worst-case combination is a **small goal with massive risk** — a common mistake (host's illustration: putting one's life savings into a shitcoin for a 2x upside).
- As of March 2026, jobs are among the best ways to learn skills — especially under someone you admire — and can pay you to acquire skills you'd otherwise buy with your own money or time.
- As of March 2026, he "wholeheartedly" rejects the job-or-no-job frame; the real questions are: what are my daily activities, how am I being remunerated for them, and how many skills am I learning — all of which can be maximized either self-employed or in a job.
- As of March 2026, he cites acquisition.com's extensive internal training and well-compensated staff as evidence that a job can be a high-growth, well-paid learning environment.
