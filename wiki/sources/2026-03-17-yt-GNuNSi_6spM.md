# Why Is It So Hard to Compound Skills?

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-screen host asks the hard-hitter questions with `>>` turns; only Alex-attributed speech below is voice/belief data). Alex works through why skill-compounding is hard and how it actually pays off. He defines compounding skills as **multiple skills used together producing an outsized outcome versus any one alone**, and says the core difficulty is that you *can't imagine the upside* of a skill you don't yet have — it's an "unknown unknown," a perspective shift you can't foresee (learn accounting, then e-commerce, and suddenly things connect). His canonical illustration is **Jay-Z**: base rhythm → making beats → rapping → promoting → recruiting artists → doing deals — each skill stacked on the last until the bundle becomes a record label, the next platform. Citing Steve Jobs, "you can only connect the dots in reverse." The common trait among the hyper-successful people he's around is **intense curiosity** — "bloodhounds for knowledge" who extract something useful from anyone, billionaire or the guy earning $80k fixing lights. The two barriers to compounding: **patience** and **not understanding the upside**. On mastery-vs-compounding, he says the distinction is just how narrowly you define the domain: "master of business" is itself a stack (marketing, sales, recruiting, finance), so compounding is part of mastery, versus going deep on one narrow thing like biotech. On marketing, he's candid: despite success he still feels he barely understands it — ~15 rules he follows, and the rest he's been wrong about so often he has "zero ego." He frames **prediction and control as equal opposites**: perfect prediction plus influence over the variables equals control. Marketing/sales both raise the *likelihood* of a purchase (one-to-many vs. one-to-one); with ~1,000 variables, the ~15 chunky known ones get you maybe 60–70% of the way and you "roll the dice" on the rest, with downside truncated — over time you "move up the median." On why people can't stick with one domain, he invokes **luck surface area**: intelligence = accuracy, determination = number of shots; given a choice he'd take unlimited shots over perfect aim, because staying in the game "tips luck in your favor" (cites Ogilvy: wasted half his ad dollars, just didn't know which half). Sticking around raises the odds of the rare lucky break that defines a career. Once you get a hit you internalize and scale it. He closes with the **rising-floor** idea: each stacked skill lifts the worst-case baseline (early-20s plan B was "drive Uber and strip" → after learning to sell, top car salesman at ~$400–500k → after learning to advertise, "multiple millions" as a rainmaker), and the **video-game levels** metaphor for why people quit: it feels good to win and losing sucks, so after getting stuck on "level four" people start a new character, breeze through 1–3, and stall at 4 again — chasing too many rabbits and catching none. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
