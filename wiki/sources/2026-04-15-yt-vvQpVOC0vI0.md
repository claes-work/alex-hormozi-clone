# Your Cash Flow Problem Isn't a Problem. It's an Expectation Problem.

## Summary

Alex coaches a commercial remodeling business doing about $2M/year that wants $100M in
roughly a year and says cash flow is the blocker. Alex says the real issue is the arbitrary
time expectation: the business may be fine if it aims for the goal over a longer horizon.
Payment terms and sales can improve cash flow, but growth rate must fit the model's cash
conversion cycle.

## Key claims

- On 2026-04-15, Alex says trying to go from $2M to $100M in about 13 months can cause a
  founder to shoot himself in the foot for a decade.
- He says the same $100M target over ten years may be achievable if the model works.
- He says commercial remodeling cash-flow pressure can often be solved with better payment
  terms, such as billing upfront or quarterly upfront in other service contexts.
- He frames payment terms as a sales issue: closing the sale and collecting the money are
  different skills.
- He notes that whale clients may force the seller onto their terms, so expectations and
  customer choice matter.
- He says a cash-flow-constrained business is limited by rate of growth if gross margins
  are sound.
- He calls the growth-rate/cash-flow plan an Excel-sheet problem, not necessarily a real
  business problem.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Sometimes you can shoot yourself in the foot for a decade because you're trying to go from two to 100 in 12 months."

> "It's another thing to actually collect the money."

> "Do you actually have a problem or do you have a problem based on the time limit that you've created for yourself?"

## Synthesis candidate

Synthesis candidate: expectation-created cash-flow problem; payment terms as sales issue;
cash-flow businesses limited by growth rate when margins work.
