# Watch This If You Keep Chasing Every Shiny Business Model

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Andrew Wade, who runs a regional dietitian / wellness services company and is tempted by franchising, licensing, virtual care, gyms, and other adjacent models. Alex frames the decision through upside, downside, and risk tolerance. He warns that competitor funding does not prove the business model works, that all mature businesses converge into the same leadership structure, and that the founder should first improve unit economics and build a team that can open locations without him before chasing licensing.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Andrew Wade** - the coachee. His dietitian business, referral sources, virtual-care context, VC-backed competitors, and licensing/franchise ideas are background only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The first question for a shiny model is upside, downside, and whether the founder can live with the downside; Alex attributes this framework to Keith Cunningham.
- The existing vehicle may be able to reach $100M, but it may take 10-15 years; the real question is whether the founder is okay with that timeline.
- A hard pivot into virtual care can make the existing business unprofitable while failing to solve the virtual model, especially if well-funded competitors have not yet figured it out.
- Franchising is usually used for capital; it is often the most expensive capital because the founder gives away most economics in exchange for a royalty.
- Licensing at 6-8% of top line may make sense only under the premise that the founder is unwilling to wait for fully owned growth and margins are thin enough to justify the trade.
- Mature businesses converge: whether selling widgets, services, or motorcycles, the owner eventually manages heads of sales, marketing, product/CS, IT, legal, and finance.
- The grass is greener on the other side because it is fertilized with hidden problems; competitor models have buried problems the founder cannot see.
- For insurance-reimbursed services where price is constrained, the founder wins from the bottom up by improving efficiency and COGS.
- Alex's litmus test before licensing: the business can open one new location per quarter with a team that does not involve the founder at all.
