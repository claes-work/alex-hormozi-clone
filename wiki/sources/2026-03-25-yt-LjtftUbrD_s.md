# Lessons from Losing Millions | Dave Ramsey

## Summary
In this clip, Alex Hormozi interviews Dave Ramsey, prompting him to share the biggest, most expensive mistake of his 35-year career. Dave (not Alex) recounts how Ramsey Solutions sank several million dollars into building a branded "Ramsey debit card" — an on-brand product with strong transaction-fee revenue potential — only to kill it after outside fraud bots exploited the partner bank's inadequate software, leaving Ramsey contractually liable for potentially infinite overdraft losses. Alex's own contributions are the framing questions (competence vs. intention, on-brand vs. off-brand, "what's the biggest goof-up") and observations that the idea was compelling on paper, plus a closing ad read for his scaling road map.

## Key claims
- (Alex, 2026-03-25) A good manager/employee can be evaluated on two axes — competence (are they good at their job) and intention (do they mean well) — which ladders up to whether an action is "on brand" or "off brand" for a company.
- (Dave Ramsey, 2026-03-25) Ramsey Solutions' most expensive mistake was investing several million dollars building a branded Ramsey debit card; it was on-brand, not off-brand, but they "didn't know what they didn't know" about banking regulations.
- (Dave Ramsey, 2026-03-25) The appeal was transaction-fee revenue plus a data play — tracking customers' expenses and furnishing reports back to them; the product reached ~10,000 beta users and customers loved it.
- (Dave Ramsey, 2026-03-25) The COVID pandemic shelved the project mid-development, slowing it down before it was restarted through alpha and beta.
- (Dave Ramsey, 2026-03-25) Outside fraud bots hit the card; the partner bank's fraud software failed to catch them, and Ramsey's contract made them responsible for the resulting overdrafts — exposing them to potentially "infinite" losses, so they killed the project to cap the loss at the sunk millions.
- (Dave Ramsey, 2026-03-25) He keeps one of the debit cards in his folder as a reminder; he frames the failure as ignorance of the risks, not incompetence — "we did everything correctly, but we were ignorant."
- (Dave Ramsey, 2026-03-25) The partner (click-and-mortar) bank later "went sideways" and blew up publicly; Dave credits divine protection for their early exit.
- (Alex, 2026-03-25) Many business owners hit the same trap: an idea that looks great on paper but doesn't work in practice.
