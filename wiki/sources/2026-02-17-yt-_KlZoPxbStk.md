# It took me 36 years to realize what I'll tell you in 26 minutes

## Summary

A solo, personal reflection (Hormozi states he is 36) reframing the advice
"follow your passion." He argues passion (Latin *passio* = suffering) means
finding something worth suffering *for*, not something you love doing every
moment. His central claim: suffering is a fixed cost on every life path, so you
cannot escape it — you can only choose what and whom you suffer for. He grounds
the argument in his own biography (gym-floor years at 22, a miserable post-exit
year off, his Macedonian immigrant grandfather) and lands on his stated life
purpose: helping men provide, protect, procreate.

## Key claims

- The word "passion" derives from Latin *passio* ("suffering"); its first usage
  was the Passion of Christ (the crucifixion). "Follow your passion" as "do what
  you love" is a bastardization.
- Passion exists "in the vague, not in the specific" — even in a business built
  around your passion, ~95% of daily work (if you're successful) is *not* the
  thing you love, but support work for it.
- Demand for your passion, once you're good, outstrips your supply of time; the
  passionate "what" also changes, so most of life is spent not doing it.
- The rarity of a loved thing is what makes it loved; do it constantly and you
  habituate and stop loving it (his pizza-place / eat-it-every-meal analogy).
- Suffering is a fixed cost on all paths — hate-path, moderate-goal path, or
  swing-for-the-fences path all cost "the same $10." Therefore aim big; the
  downside fears "are not true, they're just suffering."
- "The secret to getting what you want is doing lots of things that you don't
  want." Pick the path that pays better, since all paths suck equally.
- The goal is to reframe your perception so bad things read as good — change your
  frame, not your conditions ("your perceptions, not reality").
- Be passionate about your *why* and *how* (internal, persistent), not your
  *what* (external, uncontrollable — "those are treats… they can't be
  requirements").
- A why must be bigger than the self ("or you'll only be able to overcome
  obstacles smaller than you"); self-serving goals get satisfied quickly.
- Love, operationalized, is measured by what you're willing to give up to
  maintain it. Cites Viktor Frankl ("a big enough why… almost any how") and Rogan
  ("crawl through broken glass with a smile").
- Hedonic adaptation is real: after any major life change, subjective well-being
  returns to baseline. Money gives options but doesn't durably change well-being.
- The 10-stage "zero to $100M+" roadmap (broken down by 8 business functions)
  finished by <1% of companies — free plug at acquisition.com/roadmap.
