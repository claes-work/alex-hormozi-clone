# Helping a Marketing Agency Find Their Ideal Customer

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs an **experiential-marketing production** company (mobile tours, booth/live events — e.g. producing "eBay Live") doing ~$13M top line, aiming for $50M, with marketing named as the bottleneck. Their situation: ~20–25 customers a year, ~80% of them *new* each year (mostly one-time projects, high churn), demand-gen is "posting and praying" plus ~$13k/month paid search with no metrics tracked. Rather than fix the metrics first, Alex reframes the *unit of acquisition*. Instead of chasing individual episodic customers, he tells them to **"chunk up a level"** and acquire **agencies as a distribution layer** — because the client's real buyers are marketing agencies (and specifically the *producers*/account teams inside them) who bring projects to be produced or white-labeled. The framework: treat each onboarded agency as a **node of distribution** with a known expected yield (e.g. one agency ≈ one new customer/month, or 5–10/year), compute a **cost of acquiring a node**, and win nodes by showing agency owners *how much more they make when they bundle your service into their offering*. This converts a scary business (must re-sell ~80% of revenue every year just to stay flat) into a **stackable, compounding** one: land one new agency a month and by year-end you have 12 monthly-recurring customer flows, then 13, then 14 — the customer *logos* churn but the *distributor* stays. He extends the same logic to distributor economics: watch **distributor activation and distributor churn** exactly like customer churn (get ~80% activated and still referring next year). When the client objects that agencies are now remote/distributed post-COVID and the true relationship is with the individual *producer*, not the agency, Alex holds the frame: **"swap agency for producer, but same same."** The mechanics don't change — you're still acquiring repeatable nodes of distribution rather than one-off customers. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, when a business does episodic/one-time project work sold *through* intermediaries, Alex reframes the acquisition unit: stop acquiring individual end-customers and instead acquire the **intermediaries (agencies) as a distribution layer**, "chunking up a level."
- As of March 2026, each onboarded agency should be treated as a **node of distribution** with an expected yield (e.g. it "should net me 5, 10 customers a year"), so the relevant metric becomes the **cost of acquiring a node of distribution**, not cost per customer.
- As of March 2026, the recommended go-to-market is **outreach to agency owners**, pitched on *how much more money the agency makes when it bundles your service into its own offering* — turning them into a self-motivated distribution layer.
- As of March 2026, this converts a fragile business (having to re-sell ~80% of customers every year just to match last year) into a **compounding one**: land one new agency per month and monthly new-customer flow stacks (12 → 13 → 14...) as distributors accumulate.
- As of March 2026, distributors must be managed like customers: build the **customer journey / affiliate-distributor journey around making their lives easier**, and track **distributor activation** and **distributor churn** (target ~80% still activated and referring next year).
- As of March 2026, this makes an inherently one-time-work business **valuable** because the distribution relationships are stackable and recurring even though each individual job is not.
- As of March 2026, the framework is robust to who the "node" actually is: if the real relationship is with the *producer / account team* rather than the agency entity, you "just swap agency for producer" and the mechanics are identical ("same same").
- As of March 2026 (diagnostic), Alex's first instinct with a business that can't articulate its funnel is metrics — "why can't we just do what we're currently doing?" fails when the owner can't say what they're currently doing — but he judged the higher-leverage move here to be the distribution reframe rather than metrics first.
