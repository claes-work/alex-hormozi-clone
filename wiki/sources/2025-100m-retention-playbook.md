# $100M Retention Playbook (2025)

**What it is.** A ~36-page Acquisition.com playbook by Alex Hormozi, subtitled "How to Get Customers to Keep Buying" (`PDF p. 1`), that boils customer retention down to a "Churn Checklist" of nine concrete activities drawn from his Gym Launch and Skool data. Raw text: `raw/books/2025-100m-retention-playbook.md` (cited by `PDF p. N`).

## Thesis

Churn is "the invisible hand that suppresses your growth" — a monster too small to notice early and impossible to escape late; there is "no magic bullet," only "100 tiny things that all save a few people each" (`PDF p. 33`). Because it is far cheaper to sell existing customers than strangers, cutting churn is the highest-leverage growth lever: reducing churn from 10% to 3% multiplies each customer's lifetime value 3.3x, and referrals exceeding churn is "the only path to compounding growth" (`PDF pp. 13-14`). The mechanism: keep the value a customer gets above the price they pay, primarily by adding value (and helping them consume it), not by cutting price (`PDF p. 10`).

## Structure and content

- **Origin story — the leaky bucket (`PDF pp. 4-5`).** June 2019: 1,000 gym owners in the program got customers but lost them just as fast; at 15% monthly churn a gym loses 83% of members in a year. Frames the problem and the DoD-style "ask experts, find common factors" method.
- **The 5 Horsemen of Retention (`PDF pp. 6-8`).** Common factors from gym owners with <3% monthly churn: track attendance, reach out 2x/week, handwritten cards, member events, exit interviews. Includes the "shaking the three" first-month churn spike and results.
- **What churn is + price/value math (`PDF pp. 9-12`).** Definition and calculation of churn, the price-vs-churn-vs-LTV table, and "provide ongoing value" (overwhelm as the #1 churn cause).
- **Why reducing churn matters (`PDF pp. 13-15`).** LTV math, 5x-25x cost of acquisition vs. retention, HBR profit stat, structural churn note.
- **The Churn Checklist — 9 steps (`PDF pp. 16-30`).** Activation points, onboarding, incentivize activation, community linking, correct/fire bad customers, annual payment options, cancellation calls/videos, surveys, and the four-milestone customer journey — illustrated throughout with Skool.
- **Putting it all together + tear-off checklist (`PDF pp. 31-35`).** Summary of compounding upside and the one-page action-step checklist.

## Key claims

- (`PDF p. 4`, June 2019) At 15% month-over-month churn a gym owner loses 83% of members in a year; a 250-member gym would need 208 new clients over the year just to stay flat, and at 12.5% margins falling behind by two customers/month kills the gym.
- (`PDF p. 6`) Hormozi researched gym owners with <3% monthly churn for 6 months, interviewed each, and extracted five common factors — "The 5 Horsemen of Retention."
- (`PDF p. 7`) Attendance is the gym leading indicator: 3+ sessions/week and members stick; ≤2/week and they churn; intercept at the drop to two sessions to rescue them. Exit interviews save ~half of cancellations, cutting churn ~25% for a ~33% LTV increase.
- (`PDF p. 8`) After implementing all five horsemen, gyms saw churn rise ~50% in month 1 ("shaking the three"), then fall ~50% in month 2 and again in month 3 (10%→15%→7%→3%); a drop from 9% to 3% is a 3.3x LTV increase.
- (`PDF p. 8`) Hormozi now owns Skool, "which has millions of memberships," giving hard data on membership churn.
- (`PDF p. 9`) Churn = customers lost divided by the original pool at the start of the period; new signups during the period do not change it.
- (`PDF pp. 11-12`) Price affects both conversion rate and churn (higher price → higher churn on Skool), but not proportionally; the price/value table shows $20 beating $10 and $100 on total return; "test, test, test."
- (`PDF pp. 12-13`) Overwhelm is the #1 reason for churn; focus on 2-3 core things, ensure customers consume the value; Gym Launch is on its "10th version," each shorter and simpler than the last.
- (`PDF p. 13`) Cutting churn 10%→3% turns a 10-month customer into a 33-month customer (3.3x LTV).
- (`PDF pp. 13-14`) Acquiring a new customer costs 5x-25x more than retaining one; an HBR study found a 5% retention increase lifts profits 25%-95%.
- (`PDF pp. 17-19`) Activation points are leading indicators of retention (e.g., a Skool host retains longer with 3+ group members). Find them by studying the top 20% of churned-but-long-staying customers. At Gym Launch, honing onboarding to a "Fast Cash Play" (recoup investment in first 30 days) took churn from 8% to 3% within 6 months.
- (`PDF p. 18`) A Gym Launch competitor sold as many customers at similar CAC, but Hormozi made 70x more profit by targeting only likely-long-stayers — his customers were worth $42,000 in year one vs. the competitor's $5,000.
- (`PDF p. 22`) On Skool, most host cancellations come from the bottom two engagement tiers; the more a customer uses Skool, the longer they stay. "Usage churn" (still subscribed, no longer using) is a leading churn indicator.
- (`PDF p. 22`) A portfolio company moving from group to 1-on-1 onboarding got a 25% boost in ascensions, "one of the largest contributing factors to going from $2M per month to $2M per week."
- (`PDF pp. 26-27`) Annual options: ~10-20% take an annual plan priced "buy 10 months get 2 free"; the "big head, long tail" structure ($6,800 upfront setup + $199/mo) can lift LTV from $6,800 to $12,800.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I call this a leaky bucket business. And it's a scary way to live. If you get behind at all…your business dies." (`PDF p. 4`)

> "Churn refers to customers who leave over a specific period of time." (`PDF p. 9`)

> "Instead of asking 'How should I retain all my customers?', I ask 'What would make my customers leave?' Then, I do the exact opposite." (`PDF p. 10`)

> "Think value per second, not seconds of value. Less but better is better than more and decent. Overwhelm is the #1 reason for churn." (`PDF p. 12`)

> "It's easy to quit a membership, it's hard to leave a relationship." (`PDF p. 24`)

> "They come for the bikini. They stay for the community." (`PDF p. 24`)

> "Customers who pay for longer stays…stay longer. So, if customers want to stay longer, allow them to pay to stay longer." (`PDF p. 26`)

> "Churn is the invisible hand that suppresses your growth… There is no magic bullet for churn. It's 100 tiny things that all save a few people each. And those people stack up into mountains of cash over time." (`PDF p. 33`)

> "I have never not made more money by going through and applying this checklist. It works every time." (`PDF p. 31`)
