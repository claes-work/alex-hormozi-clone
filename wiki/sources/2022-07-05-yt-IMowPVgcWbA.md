# The SIMPLE Managerial Framework that changed my business

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi shares a three-part ("triangle") diagnostic
framework for why an employee is not doing what you want them to do. He extends
Andy Grove's two-reason model (from *High Output Management*: they don't know
how, or they aren't motivated) with a third reason he says he discovered
himself — they don't know that you want them to do it. He credits the managerial
sensibility largely to Leila, admits management is his personal weak spot, and
closes with conversational scripting for using the framework without attacking
the person.

## Key claims (the managerial framework)

- **Three reasons someone won't do something** (the "triangle"):
  1. **Communication** — they don't know you want them to do it.
  2. **Training** — they don't know how to do it.
  3. **Incentive / motivation** — they don't want to do it.
- Reasons 2 and 3 come from Andy Grove (founder/CEO of Intel, *High Output
  Management*), who framed the boss's job as to **motivate and to train**. Alex
  says he added the third reason (communication) after hitting a case Grove's
  two didn't cover.
- **Communicating expectations has three requirements**: communicate them,
  repeat the communication, and document them in writing (ideally in multiple
  places).
- **Communication cadence signals importance**: the more frequently you ask
  someone to report on a metric/task, the more important they judge it. Meeting
  twice a day about a metric reads as very important; a quarterly one-line item
  reads as unimportant; never mentioning it means it very likely won't get done.
- Put the desired activity into a **daily or weekly checklist / reporting**
  cadence so the person subconsciously registers it as a priority.
- **Training** = show them what the task looks like, do it together, record it
  for future reference, and specify how you want it reported back.
- **Motivation** has an internal and external side: align on *why* the task
  matters, and check the **compensation/bonus structure** — if they're paid for
  four other things and not this one, they won't be motivated to do the extra
  thing.
- **Diagnostic script**: lead with an ego-protecting compliment ("it's not like
  you to..."), attack the process/behavior not the person, then ask "what's
  going on?" through the three lenses so the person has clear "boxes" to answer
  in; isolate the issue and solve it.
- If all three boxes are addressed and the activity still doesn't happen, it's a
  genuine motivation/fit issue. Moving a chronic underperformer to another open
  role is usually the wrong call — "a track record of failure does not make a
  high likelihood candidate."
- People want to know **why**: tie the task to both the organizational benefit
  and a **personal benefit** to the individual. "Activity without purpose is
  what they do at prison camps."

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "There's only two reasons that an employee does not do what you want them to
  do, which is either they do not know how or they are not motivated. [...]
  therefore, the job of the boss is to motivate and to train." — Alex
  paraphrasing/quoting Andy Grove, not his own original claim.
- "This one is something that I have stolen ruthlessly from Leila because she's
  the one who really does more of the managing than I do."
- "You're not attacking the person, you're attacking a process, and it makes it
  much easier to talk about."
- "Being advanced is simply never not doing the basics. [...] being advanced is
  always doing the basics even while you have tremendous volume."
- "They stopped doing the basics at scale. It's all it is. And that's why
  businesses plateau."
- "It's not like you to be late for stuff. So, what's going on? [...]
  automatically we're actually protecting the ego of the person while still
  attacking the behavior."
- "A track record of failure does not make a high likelihood candidate."
- "Activity without purpose is what they do at prison camps."
- "The faster the communication cadence that you have around the item or task or
  activity or metric, the more important they will deem it."
