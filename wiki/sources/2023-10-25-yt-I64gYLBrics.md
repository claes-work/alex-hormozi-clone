# How to make SO much money it makes you sick

## Summary
A ~46-minute live workshop talk (to a room of mostly gym/business owners) in which Alex
Hormozi lays out his strategy stack for making the fewest, highest-leverage moves in a
business. He redefines strategy as prioritization ("allocating finite resources against
infinite options"), teaches Theory of Constraints (every business has one limiting
factor; if you're working hard and not growing, you're working on the wrong thing), and
gives three nested prioritization frameworks — the three objectives (more customers /
increased LTV / decreased risk = enterprise value), what-who-how, and more-better-new
ordered by impact-over-ease. He argues wealth comes from the asset you own (a
team-run, sellable business) not the income you personally earn, that the constraint is
usually the "big obvious thing" (a mediocre product / churn / low margins, i.e. "the
sandwich sucks"), and that leads reduce to four acquisition channels (warm/cold
outreach, paid ads, content) plus four types of "other people" (customers, affiliates,
employees, agencies) — quantified via his Rule of 100.

## Key claims
- **Strategy = prioritization.** "Strategy is just prioritizing." Layman's version:
  what gets the most bang for the buck. Fancy version: how you choose to allocate
  limited resources against unlimited options. The costliest thing in business is not
  the unknowns but the "unknown unknowns" — options you didn't know you could pick.
- **Volume x Leverage = Output.** The more times you do something and the more you get
  per time, the more you get. You can be lazy and still make a lot of money if you have
  a lot of leverage; hours are capped, so scaling big requires other people's hours (a
  form of leverage).
- **Case: niche-down + 10x price.** A bought company had ~85% of customers churn in 2-3
  months; the 15% who paid most stayed longest. He cut the 85%, re-messaged to the
  narrow avatar, 10x'd the price, and now sells more units than before — 10x'd the
  company. The owner went ~9 months near zero to get there ("play the long game").
- **Case: dentist-association rollup.** Advised bundling ~10 dentist practices a year
  and selling the bundle at ~$100M, taking 20%, giving 80% to owners — an annual exit —
  then exiting the rollup machine itself (doing $20-30M/yr profit) at ~10x for a
  ~$300M exit instead of a one-time $25M exit.
- **Theory of Constraints.** Every business is constrained by one thing limiting its
  growth. If you work all your waking hours and it isn't growing, you've mis-identified
  the constraint. Diagnostic question: "Why can't we 10x this business?"
- **Case: 40M+ sub YouTubers.** Monetizing only via AdSense ("very inefficient").
  They're "97/100 on media and 0/100 on something to sell"; going 0→20 on an offer would
  make ~10x more than optimizing media from 97→99. The constraint wasn't media.
- **Three objectives.** Every proposed activity must map to one of: (1) get more
  customers, (2) increase LTV (make them worth more), or (3) decrease risk that (1) and
  (2) stop happening — which is another way of saying increase enterprise value.
- **Wealth is the asset you own, not the income you do.** Two businesses, both $5M top /
  $2M bottom line: the owner-dependent one (dies if he dies) is worth far less than the
  team-run one, which can sell for ~10-20x. Going from $2M to $3M income on the
  team-run business adds ~$10M to net worth. "You become wealthy from the stuff you own,
  not the stuff you do." He says this took him "an absurdly long time to learn."
- **Hiring A-players is the difference between small and big businesses.** Big
  businesses just have more A-players; they keep looking (low tolerance for mediocrity)
  instead of settling for the one lucky good hire. The skill of recruiting/training/
  managing talent is worth getting no matter what.
- **The biggest lever is often a few hard conversations.** A mediocre employee eats ~25%
  of your bandwidth. Winners are separated by ability to confront discomfort. Prescription:
  say to the person's face what you say behind their back; run a 14-day performance
  improvement plan ("fix it or get out"). One in five turns around 180°; the other four
  you elegantly exit.
- **What-who-how.** A big list of "whats" — most default "who" is "me." Bigger owners
  ask how to get the team to do it. If the same who fails the same what 12 weeks
  running, the who is the problem. Credits Leila for building a team on Gym Launch
  (grew from ~$3M/yr to $37M) by taking things off his calendar and giving them to
  A-players ("six Sandras") instead of making it "the Alex show."
- **More-better-new.** Exhaust "more" (do more of the working thing) and "better"
  (improve it, e.g. call leads in <5 min instead of 30) before "new." Small owners
  default to chasing "new"/silver bullets. Big outcomes are "golden BBs" — many small
  improvements. Gym Launch grew 40% after Alex/Leila sold majority (they still own 30%+)
  via no single thing, just doing many small improvements; credits CEO Kale as
  exceptionally disciplined, "a better CEO than I am."
- **Impact/Ease ordering.** Number your task list by high-impact + easy first.
- **The constraint is usually the "big obvious thing."** Egos assume our business is
  excellent; if it were, referrals (exponential: 2→4→4→8) would remove the lead problem.
  "The sandwich sucks." Fix the product/retention before pouring in marketing gas, or it
  leaks out the other side.
- **$100M Offers book as compounding proof.** Wrote it over ~a long time, gave it away
  for $99 [sic — likely $0.99/free-plus-shipping], made one post at ~20k IG followers,
  and it now sells more each month than the last; "last month it sold 25,000 copies" —
  ~1,000/day with no marketing spend, ~14,000 5-star reviews. Product obsession creates
  compounding; every business needs a compounding vehicle or you promote it forever.
- **Delayed gratification is the single greatest skill.** Two years building something
  exceptional (customers then market it for you) beats two months building something you
  must promote for life. People have this discipline "with cookies but not with money."
- **LTV over everything.** "The guys who can spend the most money on marketing are the
  ones who make the most money from ads." Massive campaigns aren't better marketing —
  their LTV lets them spend more. "If you print cash on the back end you can get silly
  on the front end."
- **Intelligence = rate of learning; learning = same condition, new behavior.** If you
  come to an event and then do the same things, you learned nothing. Operationalizing
  words puts them under your control.
- **Emotion-to-diagnosis mapping.** Sadness/hopelessness = lack of options = ignorance
  (solvable: get informed / find a path). Anxiety/overwhelm = too many options, don't
  know which to pick = lack of strategy (solvable: prioritize).
- **Inputs→outputs obsession.** Most of his success is boiling things into an
  input-output equation. GMAT story: found research that number of problems solved
  correlated with score; did ~4 hours/day for 16 weeks and scored above a Harvard median.
- **Four ways to get customers + Rule of 100.** Only four channels: warm outreach, cold
  outreach, paid ads, content. Rule of 100: $100/day on ads, OR 100 cold outreaches, OR
  100 warm outreaches, OR 100 minutes of content — every day.
- **Four types of "other people" (leverage).** Customers (referrals), affiliates,
  employees, agencies — each does the four activities on your behalf. Getting them still
  starts with you doing the four activities to acquire them. Free/good giveaways are a
  strong way to get opt-ins.
- **Scaling local brands / franchising.** Know cost-to-open vs. profit-per-facility
  (e.g. $100k to open, $100k/yr = 100% cash-on-cash, better than the stock market). To
  scale, the best franchisors massively simplify (least square footage, least
  operational drag — "fill my bank account, not my ego"), then build "the machine that
  builds the location" (launch teams fill it before open, hand to management). Enduring
  businesses can't depend on one acquisition channel — all ad platforms eventually get
  too expensive and die.
- **Case: 20-location franchise, thin margins.** ~$1M to open, ~$150k profit/store,
  ~$1.3M topline / $150k bottom — "might as well invest in a stock." He'd spent 10 years
  trying to market the franchise when the fix was Ops/margins: cut a 500-item menu to
  the top 15, consolidate vendor buying. Prescription: find 1% more profit each month
  for 12 months → a sellable, scalable business.
- **Brand = associating the unknown with the known.** Consistently associating your
  intangible desired perceptions (clean, friendly, caring) with your tangible service.
