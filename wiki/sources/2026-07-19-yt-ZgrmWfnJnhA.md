# Do I Lower Prices to Grow Faster?

## Summary

Alex Hormozi coaches the owner of a designer resort-wear line for women (swimsuits,
cover-ups, beach dresses) doing roughly $4.5M/year split about 50/50 between wholesale
(B2B) and e-commerce (D2C). The owner is a shy designer who is not the face of the
brand and relies on models and UGC. She asks whether to keep her current premium line
and scale it as-is, or lower prices to reach a broader avatar, with a modest goal of
$100M over about ten years.

Alex advises against lowering price. He frames a premium brand as an asset everyone
wants, and after establishing that her bikinis retail at $200–$400 against a $30–$40
product cost, he notes the real cost center is customer acquisition (marketing) on the
e-com side, not the goods. His central recommendation is to stop thinking about price
and obsess over repeat purchase: moving her ~30% return rate toward ~65% is, in his
view, the single unlock for scaling the brand. He argues that lifting repeat purchase
does more than raise customer value — it drives referrals, lowers blended CAC, and lets
the business expand into progressively colder markets, the same mechanism that lets big
apparel brands (he cites Gymshark) keep scaling.

On how to build the brand without putting herself forward, Alex points to associating
with the right faces: not "generic hot chick number six," but influencers with genuine
followings and personality who signal the brand's values, and specifically luxury-coded
influencers ("Aspen" rather than "Carnival Cruise") to match and protect the premium
positioning and reduce complaints about the price point.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) A premium brand is what everyone wants; if the margins are good, she should not lower her price.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The business is a designer women's resort-wear line (swimsuits, cover-ups, beach dresses), ~$4.5M/year, roughly 50/50 B2B wholesale and B2C e-com, targeting ~$100M in about ten years.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ Net margin is about 20%; bikinis retail $200–$400; product cost is roughly $30–$40; ~30% of customers repurchase; average order value is about $350.
- (2026-07-19) At ~$30–$40 cost on a $200–$400 retail price the product is running very high gross margins (he cites ~9% as cost-of-goods share), so the majority of the cost sits in marketing / customer acquisition, not the product.
- (2026-07-19) To reach $100M she has to build a brand, and a brand is built on customers coming back.
- (2026-07-19) If he bought the company today, he would put all effort into moving repeat purchase from 30% to 65% — getting two of three customers to come back.
- (2026-07-19) Raising repeat purchase is not only worth more per customer; it also increases referrals (harder to attribute but felt in free sales) because the product is getting better, and that is what begins brand love.
- (2026-07-19) Repeat purchase from the existing base lowers overall blended CAC and is the force that lets a brand keep expanding into colder markets — the mechanism behind large apparel brands like Gymshark continuing to scale.
- (2026-07-19) Aside from supply-chain risk, hers is essentially a direct-marketing business; as demand for the brand rises, the B2B side gets easier to sell into because the brand drives foot traffic for wholesale partners.
- (2026-07-19) She does not have to be the face of the brand; a stronger brand comes from the people she chooses to associate with — five to ten influencer faces.
- (2026-07-19) Avoid generic attractive models; choose influencers with true followings and a personality behind the body, because they carry more loyal fans and their values transfer to the brand (customers know the influencer, not her).
- (2026-07-19) Match the influencers to the positioning: to keep the luxury buyer, associate with luxury ("Aspen") influencers rather than mass-market ("Carnival Cruise") ones, because a premium price reads as a complaint on a mass audience but as a bargain to a luxury one.

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION: Alex is widely associated with a "raise your prices" / charge-more
> stance. Here he tells the owner **not** to lower prices — which is directionally
> consistent (defend premium pricing) — but notably he does **not** push her to raise
> them either. Instead he redirects the growth lever away from price entirely toward
> repeat-purchase rate and brand. The nuance: price is treated as already correct for a
> premium brand, and the scaling constraint is retention/referral, not price.

## Notable verbatim quotes
_Alex Hormozi unless noted._

> "I don't think you should lower your price."

> "if you have a premium brand that's what everybody wants"

> "if you want to get to 100 million, you have to build a brand. And the way to build a brand is going to be on customers coming back."

> "if I were to buy the company today, I would put all my effort in taking that 30 and making it 65."

> "it's not just that those customers are worth more, it's that if you go from 30 to 65, also the amount of customers that are going to be bringing other people and referring other customers... you'll feel it in all the free sales that you'll get because the product's getting better. Fundamentally, that's what begins the brand love."

> "as you get into colder and colder market, you have to have an equally strong force that is fighting to keep your business alive. And that's where the brand comes in and repurchases from the base."

> "if it costs you more to get customers, but then those customers refer other customers, it lowers your overall blended CAC and allows you to mass expand to a much larger degree."

> "It's going to be the people you choose to associate with. So, it doesn't have to be you."

> "you don't want just generic hot chick number six to be the person who's representing the brand."

> "you want somebody who has a personality behind the body... because the customer's going to know them not you. And so, by them knowing that person, they associate all the values they describe with that person to the brand."

> "if you go on Carnival Cruise and put a $200 bikini, they're going to say that you should put it get put in jail. Right? But if you do it at Aspen, they'll be like, oh my god, it's half off."
