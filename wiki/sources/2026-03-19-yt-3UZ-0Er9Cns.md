# Hard work is the goal

## Summary
Podcast clip (Alex plus an interviewer). Alex traces his philosophy "hard work is the goal" to the year off he took during the sale of his business (when he had to do nothing and demonstrate the team could run it without him). His argument: any "work hard *so that* X" makes X the real goal and leaves you on the hedonic treadmill; but if working hard *is* the goal, the only durable output is "who we become along the way," which you can measure and win against every day in real time. He frames the growable thing as your *capacity* to work hard (the anti-hedonic-treadmill), keeping his "RPE" (rate of perceived exertion) at 8–9–10, because the best days of his life were ones where he had "nothing left in the tank" (crediting Jesse Itzler's "empty the tank / zero" idea he teaches his kids). Only you can judge your success because only you know how much was left in the tank; the gap between "how hard you could have worked" and "what was required to beat everyone else" is the opportunity. The interviewer presses on the tension with "direction over speed," and Alex reconciles: if the goal is *satisfaction/presence*, the outcome is irrelevant; if it's *achievement*, tie inputs as closely as possible to the outcome, then put blinders on and dig. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex dates the crystallization of "hard work is the goal" to the year-off during his business sale (he "basically just sat there for a year," couldn't change anything major, had to prove the team could run it).
- As of March 2026, he argues "work hard *so that* X" makes X the goal; instead "if the goal is to work as hard as you possibly can, then the only real output we have is who we become along the way."
- As of March 2026, he frames the anti-hedonic-treadmill: whatever you get you become accustomed to, but "my ability to work hard itself is growable" — that's the resilience piece.
- As of March 2026, he keeps his "RPE, my rate of perceived exertion, at eight or nine or 10," because "the days that I loved the most were days when I had nothing left in the tank."
- As of March 2026, he credits Jesse Itzler's "empty the tank / did you leave anything in the tank? / zero" concept (taught to Itzler's kids after races).
- As of March 2026: "the only person who can judge you on your success is you, because you're the only one who knows how much [was] left in the tank."
- As of March 2026, he diagnoses "it felt so empty" after external wins as people who "worked hard enough to beat everyone else" but not their hardest — that discrepancy is the opportunity that "work being the goal" unlocks.
- As of March 2026 (self-reported): "I work harder now than I did when I was poor… because I've learned to enjoy it."
- As of March 2026, he reconciles hard-work with "direction over speed": for *satisfaction/presence* the outcome is irrelevant (just empty the tank); for *achievement* you must "make sure that my inputs are tied as closely as humanly possible to the outcome," then "put your blinders on and you start digging."
