# How to get unlimited funding to build your business in 30 days (2021-10-11)

## Summary

In this short talk Alex Hormozi lays out his core self-funding thesis, which he
names **Client Financed Acquisition (CFA)**: get customers to pay for all of your
marketing and acquisition costs so that growth is funded from customer cash rather
than investors or outside capital. He frames it as the single equation behind
growing his portfolio companies to (self-reported, 2021) ~$85M/year on 100% equity
with no outside money, and gives the explicit inequality — 30-day cash must exceed
2× (cost to acquire + cost to fulfill). This is an early, explicit statement of the
mechanism later formalized as CFA in *$100M Offers* and *$100M Leads*.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- (2021, self-reported) He grew from $1,036 in the bank ~5 years earlier to a
  portfolio doing ~$85M/year, taking no outside capital or investors — 100% equity.
- (2021) CFA in plain terms: get your customers to pay for all of your marketing and
  acquisition costs, so you fund growth with "the universe's money," not your own.
- (2021) The equation/process: **30-day cash > 2 × (Cost to Acquire + Cost to Fulfill).**
  This is stated as the minimum requirement for CFA.
- (2021) "30-day cash" = net free cash flow the business can collect within the first
  30 days of a customer entering its world (excluding later upsells, downsells, and
  continuity). He notes it is his own term; he expects a fancier word exists.
- (2021) The 30-day window is chosen because a credit card gives ~30 days of
  interest-free financing — pay it off within 30 days and you can effectively recycle
  near-unlimited capital. He says this is literally how he started his second business.
- (2021) Worked example: if CAC = $100 and fulfillment = $100 (so $200 out), you must
  make more than $400 in 30-day cash. Net of the $200 spent, $200 is left — exactly
  enough to acquire and fulfill the next customer, who yields another $400, and the
  loop repeats → unlimited customer acquisition.
- (2021) When this holds, getting customers and making sales will never be the
  constraint on the company — only operations, hiring, and the owner will be.
- (2021) Twice the cost is only the minimum; 10× is better because the surplus cash
  can fund additional customers and other investments.
- (2021, self-reported growth stats using CFA): brick-and-mortar chain 0→6 locations
  in 3 years (opened at near-full capacity from day one after the first); replicated
  at 33 other locations; licensing business 0→$2.5M/month within ~12–14 months (0→$28M
  topline), then $4.4M/month 12 months later; second business 0→$1.7M/month in 4
  months; software business 0→$1.7M/month in 6 months.
- (2021) He rejects "it takes money to make money" as a limiting belief; profit is
  what lets a business weather storms in a capitalist society.

> "30-day cash must be greater than two times the cost of acquiring [a] customer and

## Topic notes

- **Proto-CFA flag.** This 2021 video is one of Alex's earliest explicit, named
  statements of Client Financed Acquisition. The mechanism — fund customer acquisition
  from the customer's own early cash rather than investors or debt — is stated here as
  a concrete inequality (**30-day cash > 2 × (CAC + fulfillment cost)**), predating the
  fuller treatment in *$100M Offers* / *$100M Leads*. Treat this as the dated origin
  point for the CFA thesis in [[../topics/business/acquisition-channels]].
- The core levers map directly to [[../topics/business/business-math]]: CAC, cost of
  fulfillment (COGS), and a 30-day gross-cash-collected metric. The "2× minimum, 10×
  better" ratio is an early version of his CAC:30-day-GP payback framing.
- Funding mechanism to preserve precisely: **credit-card float** — put the $200 (CAC +
  fulfillment) on a card, collect >$400 within the 30-day interest-free window, pay the
  card, keep the surplus, repeat. The 30-day window is not arbitrary; it is pinned to
  the interest-free credit-card grace period.
- Contrast with the venture/debt model he explicitly rejects (2–3 years to
  breakeven on outside/seed capital). Cross-links to scaling constraints: with
  acquisition solved, the binding constraints move to operations and hiring.
