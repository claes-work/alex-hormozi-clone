# Why I Love Business

## Summary
Interview clip (a host probes; Alex answers) in which Alex explains what actually draws him to business: not the money or a grand mission, but the **micro-rewards** of the daily work. He says he loves writing his book, arguing with his editor about better phrasing, tweeting his thoughts, and having discussions like this one because business is his favorite topic. He frames his whole psychology around reward-seeking and **punishment-aversion**: he has learned he strongly dislikes "punishment" (things he doesn't like) and avoids them "like the plague," so he arranges his life to do as many rewarding things as frequently as possible — which, he notes, looks like punishment (grueling work) from the outside. He stresses that his love of business landing at "the intersection of making a ton of money" was **luck**, not design; he didn't even know he'd like business when he started. On the harder question — how does someone who *doesn't* have a "thing" find theirs — Alex prescribes being **directionally correct rather than absolutely correct**: most people freeze trying to pick the perfect thing when they have no perspective from which to judge, because they haven't done anything yet. His model is the rat that only needs to know "there's cheese out there" and "there's a cat behind me" — moving *away from the cat* (things you hate) raises the odds of getting closer to the cheese even without knowing where it is. He illustrates with his own rapid iterations: choosing his first business among frozen yogurt, test prep, and a gym; hating his job and Baltimore and simply moving to the furthest place away (California); then subtracting the grunt-work he hated and doing fitness because he liked it. He argues you can't truly "like nothing," because the brain is wired to repeat what rewarded it before — so the practical move is to note what rewarded you (or where the "cat" is) and act. The host reframes this as a "reverse role model" / avoid-being-stupid lens and names perfectionism as "procrastination masquerading as quality control," which Alex agrees is a **decision-making fallacy** — the same one that stops people from investing because no investment is perfect. A short tangent: since holding cash guarantees a loss to inflation, not investing is the only guaranteed way to get no return; inflation likely pressures investors (not operators) to deploy capital faster. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says what he loves about business is the **micro-rewards he gets every day** — writing his book, refining phrasing with his editor, tweeting his thoughts, having discussions about his favorite topic — not a single distant outcome.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes himself as strongly **punishment-averse** ("things you don't like") and reward-seeking: he avoids disliked tasks and stacks rewarding ones as frequently as possible, which "from the outside to a lot of people looks like punishment."
- As of March 2026, Alex says his love of business coinciding with **making a lot of money was luck** ("that just happened to be the intersection") — he didn't know he'd like business when he started.
- As of March 2026, Alex's advice for finding "your thing": be **directionally correct rather than absolutely correct**; people fail by chasing the perfect choice when they have no perspective to judge from because they haven't done anything yet.
- As of March 2026, Alex's rat/cat/cheese heuristic: you don't need to know where the cheese is — you only need to know cheese exists and a cat is behind you; moving *away from what you hate* raises the odds of getting closer to what you want, even without certainty.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he has lived by **rapid iteration, not picking the right thing** — his first business was chosen among frozen yogurt, test prep, and a gym; he moved from Baltimore to California (the furthest place) just to escape a job and city he hated, then did fitness because he liked it.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims you cannot genuinely "like nothing," because the **brain is wired to repeat behaviors that rewarded it** in the past; so identify what rewarded you (or where the "cat" is) and move.
- As of March 2026, Alex agrees perfectionism / needing to know the exact right thing before moving is a **decision-making fallacy** — the same one that stops people investing, since every investment carries downside and there is no perfect one.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes that holding cash is the **only guaranteed way to get no return** (inflation erodes it); inflation likely pressures *investors* more than operators to deploy capital faster, because a higher perceived cost of capital creates urgency to do something with money rather than let it sit.
