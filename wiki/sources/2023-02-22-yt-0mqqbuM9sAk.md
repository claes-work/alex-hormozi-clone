# I Built 4 Businesses In A Row To Show It's Not Luck

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi walks through the four consecutive companies he took past $10M — Gym Launch, Prestige Labs, ALAN (useallen.com), and Acquisition.com — framing the repetition as proof that his results are a system, not luck. For each business he covers how he landed the first ~10 customers off his existing distribution base, the growth curve, and the biggest lessons and mistakes. Recurring playbook threads: client-financed acquisition, seeding launches from a captive audience, aligned incentives, exceptional product, and (at Acquisition.com) hiring top-down.

## Key claims (the repeatable playbook)

- **Seed every new business from an existing distribution base.** Prestige Labs, ALAN, and the early Acquisition.com deals all pulled their first customers from the audience/relationships built by the previous business. He "rolls out" in waves (first 10, then 20–30, then 20–30, then a big launch to everyone).
- **Client-financed acquisition (his core framework).** Get the customer to finance acquiring the next customer: if the first sale exceeds the cost to acquire + fulfill that customer *plus* the cost to acquire + fulfill the next one, capital stops being a constraint — "you can put a dollar in and get two customers back." He says he has used this in every business since Gym Launch.
- **Aggressive acquisition / outspending competitors.** Because rivals ran loss-leader models and he ran client-financed acquisition, "all my gyms were able to just crush everyone else because we'd get outspend[ing]."
- **Product must be exceptional; the inch between good and great takes years but compounds via word of mouth.** He says it took 6 years to assemble the Gym Launch deliverable before he could monetize it.
- **Find the model by surveying the community for the few common behaviors.** For a given problem (churn, margin, leads, close rates) he'd form a hypothesis, survey the community for top performers, put ~20 on a call, and extract only the *few things they were all doing* — not everything.
- **Aligned incentives make more money.** ALAN was paid on percentage of leads that showed up, so small business owner, agency, and ALAN all wanted the same outcome — top-down alignment drove scale.
- **Sell up the leverage chain.** ALAN broke through only after switching from selling small businesses to selling agencies (each agency = ~50 downstream businesses), then pitching to big agency audiences via webinars — one pitch could yield a thousand customers.
- **Hire top-down when well-capitalized.** At Acquisition.com he hired directors "better than us" first and delegated down, versus bottom-up in every prior business. Says he'll always hire top-down from now on.
- **Every business incurs debt — choose which kind.** Financial vs. management vs. cultural vs. technical vs. "ignorance debt." Being well-capitalized let him pay down financial debt to avoid the others.
- **Recruiting talent as the single core competency** — leveraged by inbound volume, PE-firm prestige, and central training at Holdco.
- **Software-specific lessons (from ALAN):** need an in-house CTO (dev shops are "about as close to a complete scam as you can come"); run pricing surveys early (repackaging pricing unlocked 4x pricing power); scheduling throughput (days/hours open, time-slot increments) can swing appointment show-ups 50–200%.
