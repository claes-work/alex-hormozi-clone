# What I Learned After Countless Failed Business Partnerships

## Summary

This clip is drawn from a group coaching call in which Hormozi fields live member
questions about whether and how to structure business partnerships. He answers two
members: one weighing adding a partner for a weak lead-generation function, and one
trying to cleanly exit a 50/50 LLC with a non-performing partner. Only Alex-attributed
statements are captured below; the members' questions are context, not persona material.
His throughline: partners are almost never necessary, equity is a liability you only give
up for control or an exit, and most "partner" needs are better solved with a
performance-based agreement.

## Key claims

- (2021) The only valid reasons to take on a partner are that they have **skills you don't
  have, money you don't have, or time you don't have** — nothing else.
- (2021) The most common (and worst) way people form partnerships is "I'm cool, you're
  cool, we love the same thing, let's go into business together" — because then one of you
  is not necessary and you are just diluting your equity.
- (2021) Before the honeymoon phase ("you guys are in lust"), you must define all the
  "ugly" upfront: roles, responsibilities, split, exit strategy, what happens on divorce.
- (2021) Equity is only worth wanting for two reasons: **control**, or the ability to
  **make money on an exit/sale**. Absent those, "equity sucks" because it is just liability.
- (2021) If you are not going to sell and the other person does not want control, do **not**
  make it an equity partnership — structure a **performance agreement** instead
  (percentage of revenue or profit tied to milestones the person actually drives).
- (2021) Recommended structure for a scaling contributor: **phantom equity** — equity that
  only pays out on a sale and is forfeited if the person leaves — optionally paired with
  **profit-share or rev-share**. (In the case discussed: capped at ~a third of the company,
  majority always retained by the founder.)
- (2021) Having a business need does not mean the person filling it must own equity: "just
  because you have a need doesn't mean they have to be an equity owner" — like paying an
  accountant, not making them a partner. E-commerce agencies routinely take ~10% of
  attributed top-line revenue with no equity.
- (2021) On dissolving a partnership: separate the **equity** piece (transfer shares / one
  party takes 100%, or dissolve and form a new entity) from the **debt** piece (split what
  is owed, formalized with a one-page promissory note).
- (2021) The biggest hack in hard conversations: "the way that you talk [about] someone
  behind their back is literally what you should just tell them" — then ask "what do you
  think's fair?"
- (2021) Ask a would-be equity partner directly "what do you want?" — surface whether they
  want upside, a piece of what they bring in, or control — and structure to that instead of
  defaulting to shares.
