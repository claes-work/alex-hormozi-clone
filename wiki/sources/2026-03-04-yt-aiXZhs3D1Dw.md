# "I'm broke. How do I make money?"

## Summary
Short clip answering the starting-from-zero question. An off-camera interviewer frames it: people who want more money often just don't know what the avenues are. Alex's answer is all his own words. He starts from first principles: money is made by getting it from someone else, and (short of theft) they only hand it over **voluntarily in exchange for something you give them first** — his praise of capitalism as voluntary exchange where both parties say thank you. So the task is to **find something viable to trade**. Two broad routes: **sell stuff** (buy/source where it's cheap or inconvenient and resell at a margin — the margin is partly the convenience you deliver) or **sell skills/outcomes** (develop expertise). For a beginner, he strongly recommends selling **someone else's stuff first** via referral / ambassador / affiliate programs — "almost every business I've ever owned has an affiliate program" — because it isolates a single skill (promote → get paid) you can repeat, and later you "eat up more of the responsibility" to earn more of the margin. The best way to learn to sell is to sell something; the first thing you sell is not the last thing you sell. He then tells his origin story: his first money from a stranger was a **$200 check** from a woman at his powerlifting gym for an hour-long nutrition consultation at a pizza place — he had state records and visible proof, so people asked him for help. That seeds his life thesis: **learn something → get good → have evidence you're good → people ask you for help on that thing**, laddering up (get in shape → started fitness businesses → six gyms by 26 off cash flow → licensing business at $4M/month → advising bigger businesses). Crucially, each step was to get good at the thing, not to reach the next step — the path "revealed itself as I took more steps." His closing lesson: the early fallacy is trying to figure out the entire path before taking the first step. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

- As of March 2026, the practical move is to find something **viable to trade**: either sell physical stuff at a margin (sourcing what's inconvenient for the buyer to get themselves, so part of the margin is the convenience delivered) or sell skills/outcomes via developed expertise.
- As of March 2026, a beginner should usually **sell someone else's stuff first** through referral / ambassador / affiliate programs; almost every business Alex has owned has an affiliate program, and he deliberately won't name his own so the point stays general.
- As of March 2026, the advantage of promoting others' products is that you only have to master **one skill** — promote and get paid — which you can repeat; over time you can "eat up more of the responsibility" to keep more of the margin.
- As of March 2026, "the best way to learn to sell is to sell something," and "the first thing you sell is not the last thing you're going to sell."
- As of March 2026 (origin story): the first money Alex ever made from a stranger was a **$200 check** for a ~1-hour nutrition consultation (at a pizza place) for a woman at his powerlifting gym, given because he was a visible state-record holder in good shape.
