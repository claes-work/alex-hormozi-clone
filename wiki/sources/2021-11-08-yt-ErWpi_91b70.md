# Why daily routines make you poor & fragile [trigger warning] (2021-11-08)

## Summary

In this early (2021) mindset video, Alex Hormozi delivers a deliberately
provocative argument that two character traits keep entrepreneurs stuck: (1) the
limiting "I am" statements they speak over themselves, and (2) dependence on
rigid routines and rituals, which he frames as "crutches" and "superstitions"
that make a person fragile and non-resilient. Prompted by a dinner with younger
entrepreneurs doing a couple million a year, he argues that identity should be
treated as adjustable ("I have a tendency toward…") rather than fixed ("I am…"),
and that anything you *must* do to function — coffee, pills to sleep, a fixed
morning routine — has become a dependency you would not want a competitor to
exploit. He cites acquisition.com doing "about 85 million a year in revenue" at
the time of recording.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- Growth follows the theory of constraints: a system grows to its constraint and
  no further; most people add "potential" to the strong links instead of
  attacking the weakest one, so they never actually grow. [2021]
- The three growth components for an entrepreneur are skills, character traits,
  and beliefs; less-experienced entrepreneurs' limiting traits ("out points") are
  easier for an outside observer to spot. [2021]
- "I am" statements are among the most powerful things you speak over yourself
  because they encode deeply held beliefs about your identity. [2021]
- Your identity should be whatever is required to grow; traits are claimed, not
  intrinsic, so limiting ones can be dropped. [2021]
- Replace definitive self-labels ("I am disorganized / bad with money / short
  fuse") with conditional language ("I have a tendency toward…", "I have a
  proclivity for…") that leaves room to change. [2021]
- The test for any self-described trait: "Does this belief serve me?" If not,
  stop saying it. [2021]
- He personally believed he was "bad at math" until age 26, an example of a
  false, limiting self-label he later shed. [2021]
- Crutches and superstitions are dangerous: becoming dependent on a routine makes
  you weaker, not stronger. [2021]
- Terms like "I have to / I must / I should / I need to" around routines are
  claims about how the world must be over things you don't control. [2021]
- Examples of crutches he names: needing alcohol, a blunt, edibles, or pills to
  fall asleep; needing coffee to feel normal; orange glasses and other rituals. [2021]
- Humans hundreds of years ago slept fine and functioned without coffee, pharma
  sleep aids, or these rituals — evidence the dependencies are self-created. [2021]
- Benefits from stimulants/rituals decay: coffee makes you feel better at first,
  then you need it just to feel normal, so the benefit is gone. To keep getting a
  benefit you must *cycle* things — which by definition means *not* having a fixed
  routine, because novelty is what confers the temporary boost. [2021]
- Depending on a "magical routine" makes you fragile; he would *hope* a competitor
  had such crutches so a disruption to their routine would take them out for the
  day. [2021]
- His stated ideal: to be able to sleep on a cot in a closet and be just as
  effective as someone whose entire life is "completely dialed in." [2021]
- He frames it with the Rocky IV contrast: Drago with all his fancy science,
  supplements and equipment vs. Rocky training outdoors and simply getting it
  done. [2021]
- Closing principle: succeed independent of circumstances you create — "You are
  source, not reaction." [2021]

## Topic notes

**Apparent tension with his pro-habit / consistency content — reconciled.** On
its face, "routines make you poor & fragile" collides with [[2021-07-02-yt-bkPwFqa89gE]]
("Unconventional Productivity Habits That Made Me Successful"), where he credits
a small set of consistent habits (plan 1–3 tasks the night before, wake early,
eat the same meals, fixed bedtime). The tension is largely superficial: **this
video is anti-*rigidity / anti-dependence*, not anti-*habit*.** The through-line
is identical — in the July video he already warns that "anything that gives you a
short-term boost oftentimes just becomes a crutch," says he is "very very
sensitive to superstitions and having crutches," and keeps his routine minimal
precisely to stay "resilient and adaptable." This November video sharpens the
same "crutch" thesis into its provocative extreme.

Two genuine points of friction remain for a synthesis pass to watch:

1. **Consistency vs. cycling.** In July he prizes a *consistent* bedtime and
   identical daily meals (removing decisions); here he argues you must *cycle*
   inputs so novelty keeps conferring benefit, and that this means "inherently
   you're not going to have a routine." His likely reconciliation: cycle
   *stimulants/rituals you'd otherwise become dependent on* (coffee, sleep aids),
   while *keeping* low-cost decision-eliminating habits that you are not
   physiologically hooked on. Worth confirming against later sources.

2. **"You are source, not reaction."** The identity/"I am" argument (traits are
   chosen, not fixed) is new relative to the July video and belongs in
   [[persona/beliefs]] on a later synthesis pass if promoted to L3.
