# How to Get High-Level Talent to Join Your Startup

## Summary

An interview clip (a second speaker interviews Alex Hormozi; all substantive
claims below are Alex-attributed — the interviewer only asks questions and
affirms) on how a resource-constrained startup persuades a genuinely exceptional,
well-paid, comfortable senior person to leave security and join. Alex splits the
problem into a **hard side** (compensation levers) and a **soft side**
(the emotional/psychological sell). On the hard side he unpacks the four elements
of equity — control, risk, profits, and sale/enterprise-value upside — and notes
you can grant actual shares *or* contract around any of these elements
synthetically; in practice a senior hire usually won't take the risk and won't
get control (if it's your company), so what you can offer them is profit
distributions and sale upside. His preferred negotiation opener is simply asking
the person "what would it take?" — a frame that also *assumes the yes*. On the
soft side he argues recruiting a co-founder or early leader is fundamentally a
**sale**: the pitch is essentially the same one you'd give an investor, except
they're investing time, life, and expertise instead of capital. He cites the
Steve Jobs / John Sculley "sugar water vs. change the world" recruiting line as
the emotional archetype. Closes with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

Overlaps the same-day companion video "How to Actually Hire Good Talent"
([[2026-02-24-yt-wN0Sq8Y3HcQ]]); what is **distinct here** is the narrow focus on
*attracting senior/co-founder-level talent to an early, cash-poor startup* — the
equity-structure mechanics and the "recruiting = a sale / investment pitch"
framing — rather than the general hiring-process mechanics of the companion clip.

## Key claims (2026-02-24, paraphrased)

- Recruiting a top, well-paid, comfortable person to a startup has two sides: a
  hard side (compensation) and a soft side (the emotional/psychological sell).
  (paraphrase)
- A cash-poor startup has a few compensation levers: cash pay, and upside via
  equity or shares. (paraphrase)
- For non-software companies (most of his audience), equity has four elements:
  **control** (who calls the shots), **risk** (who is liable if it all goes
  wrong), **profits** (who gets the cash-flow distributions), and **sale /
  enterprise value** (who participates in the upside if/when you sell).
  (paraphrase)
- You can grant actual shares/stock, OR you can *contract around* any of these
  four elements synthetically — there is lots of legal structure available for
  doing so. (paraphrase)
- In practice a senior hire usually doesn't want the risk and won't get control
  if it's your company, so what you're really offering them is profit
  distributions plus upside on a sale. (paraphrase)
- A powerful way to open the negotiation is simply asking the person what they
  want; his favorite question for this scenario is "what would it take?" — you
  don't have to guess, they'll tell you. (paraphrase)
- The "what would it take?" frame is powerful partly because it *assumes the
  yes* — it presupposes the person is going to come. (interviewer framed this;
  Alex agreed) (paraphrase)
- Persuading an exceptional person to join your mission is fundamentally a sale.
  (paraphrase)
- The pitch you give a potential co-founder or early leader is more or less the
  same pitch you'd give an investor — except instead of investing capital,
  they're investing their time, their life, and their expertise; you're still
  fundamentally asking for an investment. (paraphrase)
- The emotional archetype for this recruiting sell is the Steve Jobs line to
  John Sculley (then at Pepsi): do you want to keep selling sugar water, or come
  change the world. (paraphrase)
- Promo: acquisition.com/roadmap — a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+
  that fewer than 1% of companies finish, broken down by eight business
  functions with the constraint symptoms and the steps to graduate each stage;
  validated across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar.
  (paraphrase)
