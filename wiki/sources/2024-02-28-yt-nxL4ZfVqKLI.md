# Seriously, watch this before you go to college

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi walks through the college-vs-career decision as a
"game of one or zero," scoring points for each side using national data (tuition
inflation, $1.76T student loan debt, median lifetime earnings by education level, odds
of out-earning a bachelor's holder, industry-by-industry ROI). His net position is
that it is a nuanced, individualized decision driven by ROI (Warren Buffett's
"difference between what you pay and what you get"): college makes sense for those who
get large financial aid, come from lower-socioeconomic/less-educated families (where it
changes the "zip code" / network and family trajectory), or get into a top-20/Ivy
school; it makes less sense for well-off families who already have the network and
would incur debt plus four lost years of career development. Either path, he insists,
is really about choosing to *learn, not earn* in the first four years.

## Key claims

- The core lens is ROI, framed as Warren Buffett's investment question: "the difference
  between what you pay and what you get" — more education making more money isn't the
  point, whether it's worth what it costs is. (paraphrase)
- Tuition cost has roughly doubled in recent decades while median starting salary has
  stayed roughly flat — "you pay twice as much for the same thing." (paraphrase)
- National student loan debt is ~$1.76 trillion — more than the GDP of every country
  but roughly the top 10. (paraphrase; his figures)
- Student debt is uniquely bad because interest rates are high and you cannot bankrupt
  out of it — you pay it for the rest of your life. (paraphrase)
- Median lifetime earnings by level (his figures): no high-school diploma <$1M; HS
  diploma ~$1.3M; some college ~$1.5M; bachelor's ~$2.2M; master's ~$2.6M; doctoral
  ~$3.2M; professional ~$3.6M. (paraphrase)
- Odds of out-earning a median bachelor's holder: no HS diploma ~7.3% (1 in 12); HS
  diploma ~1 in 6; some college ~1 in 4; associate's ~1 in 4; master's ~6 in 10;
  doctoral ~7 in 10; professional ~83%. He stresses these are not the guarantees
  colleges advertise — e.g. 27% of doctoral holders still don't out-earn a bachelor's.
  (paraphrase)
- ROI depends heavily on industry: for personal services, blue collar, health support,
  sales and office, higher education makes little difference; for science/technology,
  health professional, managerial and professional, it makes a bigger difference. So
  the decision hinges on "what do I want to do." (paraphrase)
- Controlling for SAT, socioeconomic status and other factors, college adds ~no value
  for white or Asian students but a significant 30–40% median-earnings increase for
  Latino or Black students. (paraphrase; cites an unnamed research study)
- The single best predictor of earning income — above race, gender, even IQ — is the
  zip code you were born in. His theory: college lets you "figuratively change your zip
  code" by changing your environment and, crucially, who you compare yourself to.
  (paraphrase)
- Strongest pro-college case: being the *first* person in your family to go — it
  changes the trajectory of your progeny/family tree; for those not first, it matters
  much less. (paraphrase)
- The traits that let you win at college are the same that let you win at business and
  life — so if you already have them (e.g. a high enough SAT for an Ivy), you likely
  don't strictly need the school. (paraphrase)
- The alumni network / social-experience arguments are weak on an ROI basis: for
  ~$50k/year you could "buy" a network of higher earners directly, or party hard,
  without the degree — though he concedes it's harder to meet as many same-age people
  and that socially those four years would be more fun. (paraphrase)
- Top-20 / Ivy schools (Harvard, Princeton, Stanford, Yale, etc.) *are* worth full
  price even with loans — for the personal-brand halo and because their graduates
  become "the ruling class"; large endowments mean generous financial aid even for
  non-ruling-class admits, improving the trade. (paraphrase)
- Not all degrees are equal: a Harvard PhD out-earns a "State U" PhD (top firms, faster
  progression, stronger network); the 75th percentile at elite schools can be far above
  the all-school median. (paraphrase)
- His honest advice: if you come from a lower-educated / lower-socioeconomic family and
  get financial aid of ~50%+ of tuition, go — you pay less and get more. If you come
  from a highly educated, higher-socioeconomic family with no favorable financial terms,
  it makes less sense — counterintuitively, the better-off families are the ones who
  most demand college ("this box is already checked for them at birth"). Big caveat: if
  the career *requires* a degree (doctor, lawyer, accountant), it requires a degree.
  (paraphrase)
- A degree won't hand you a life plan — "if you don't have a plan for what you want to
  do with your life, don't expect that $200,000 in four years is somehow going to give
  you a plan." Loving a class (e.g. biology) isn't a career path. (paraphrase)
- How to maximize the no-college path: still prioritize education (informal), get paid
  to learn at a job, then spend that money learning more; and specifically — pay skilled
  people by the hour to learn from them, or buy their course/book/videos. Entrepreneurs
  love helping people who take initiative and execute. (paraphrase)
- How to maximize the college path: treat it as a job, take 18–21 credits, graduate in
  2–3 years, use summer school to save money, and maximize social surface area (clubs,
  fraternities) for network and "luck surface area." (paraphrase)
- Times have changed: hierarchy, tenure and pedigree used to matter a ton; now that
  information is free online — "you can learn more on YouTube than you can from most
  colleges" — you don't need what was once required. (paraphrase)
- Expect family pushback if you skip college; they mean well and act out of love/fear of
  your downside, but you must decide for yourself — and you can fail *with* a degree too.
  (paraphrase)
