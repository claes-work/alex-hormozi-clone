# Inside the 1,000 Hours It Took to Write Money Models

## Summary

A behind-the-scenes livestream conversation (Alex Hormozi, Leila Hormozi, and one or more hosts/teammates) about the effort behind writing and launching *$100M Money Models* and its $100M+ launch. Alex details the sheer volume of collateral produced — 12 playbooks, the Lost Chapters (a full second book ~215 pages), Money Models (~187 pages), a 70-tactic audiobook, and an affiliate "black book" — and says Money Models alone took roughly 1,000 hours to write, though *Leads* was the hardest of the three. He explains his design philosophy (books should feel like a textbook and a kids' book at once), his four "meta concepts" behind the launch, and the Rockefeller/Standard Oil story that shapes how he thinks about paying a premium now to unlock future returns. The hosts and Leila emphasize the whole-company operation and the theme of "normalizing the unreasonable"; Alex closes with advice that success is "just so much work" and that the alpha is in figuring it out yourself. Only Alex-attributed lines below are persona training data; Leila and host material is context.

## Key claims

- The Money Models launch bundle wasn't just a book — it included 12 playbooks, the Lost Chapters (a full second book, ~215 pages, same length as *$100M Offers*), the Money Models book itself, a 70-business-tactic audiobook (a referral prize for referring 10 friends), and an affiliate "black book" (texts, emails, posts, ads — a full plug-and-play affiliate playbook). (Alex)
- Money Models took roughly 1,000 hours to write; it was ~187 pages ("180ish"), with intentionally big, colorful, image-heavy pages. (Alex)
- *Leads* was the hardest of the three books to write. (Alex)
- Big books cost far more to produce than small books; the size is deliberate so the book is physically easy to read. (host / Alex)
- The book deliberately avoids jargon (e.g., "cash conversion cycle" is described in plain terms, never named) to meet not-yet-entrepreneurs where they are. (Alex)
- Alex re-filmed the accompanying course three times to make it better. (Alex)
- The launch was rescheduled twice because acquisition.com's fast growth consumed internal resources; in the last two quarters they brought on six new leaders, one specifically to own the launch. (Alex)
- The "$100M green man" content concept was entirely the media team's idea, made faceless on purpose to ride viral trends without self-promotion; Alex says he scripted none of it. (Alex)
- The launch ran the full business simultaneously: ~90 support reps, ~300 phone reps trained from scratch, plus ~40 redeployed teammates (executives, HR, payroll staff) taking calls — 440+ trainees total. (Leila / host)
- The slide presentation was 1,700 slides. (host, confirmed by Alex context)
- Alex made the slides himself specifically to prove the work was not ghostwritten. (host, about Alex)
- Four "meta concepts" drove the launch: (1) get a book into the hands of every entrepreneur in America; (2) each book demonstrates its own concept via its launch — Offers launched with no advertising (word of mouth, ~10k followers, 99¢), Leads added advertising, Money Models added monetization, capped by a $100M launch to end the "$100M trilogy"; (3) model reciprocity by giving huge value away first, then making "donate books" the CTA so the audience models giving-first behavior; (4) break the non-fiction sales record previously held by politicians backed by taxpayer/"state-sponsored" media. (Alex)
- The trilogy spanned four years: Offers released July 2021, Leads August 2023, Money Models August 2025 — two years apart each, each adding the next layer (offer → advertising → monetization). (Alex, biographical)
- The launch surpassed $100M. (Alex)
- The donate-books offer was ~$6,000, well above the "never above three [price points]" unwritten rule; Alex broke it because charging three felt unfair when people pay $35,000 for one of the underlying things. (Alex)
- Alex became co-owner of Skool (~18 months prior) with Sam; the goal was to make Skool as close to free as possible, producing a $9/mo "hobby plan" at a lifetime-low rate. Deal talks started ~6 months before the Leads launch. (Alex)
- Alex's core advice: there is no silver bullet; success is just far more work than anyone anticipates, and it requires choosing what you will sacrifice. The "alpha" in doing something historic is figuring it out yourself. (Alex)

## Biographical facts (dated)

*All self-reported by Alex in this conversation unless noted.*

- **2021-07** — Released *$100M Offers*; launched it with no advertising, one post, ~10,000 followers, priced at 99¢, bundled with a free course. It spread on word of mouth and "sells thousands of copies to this day." (self-reported)
- **2023-08** — Released *$100M Leads* (the second book; added the advertising layer). Found *Leads* the hardest of the three to write. (self-reported)
- **~2024** (≈18 months before this launch) — Became co-owner of Skool alongside Sam; structured the deal to push Skool toward free access, resulting in a $9/mo "hobby plan" at a lifetime-low rate. Deal talks reportedly began ~6 months before the Leads launch. (self-reported)
- **2025-08** — Released *$100M Money Models* (the third book; added the monetization layer), ending the four-year "$100M trilogy" with a launch that surpassed $100M. (self-reported)
- Wrote all three books "more or less at the same time" as one massive project; early readers said it was "too much," so he split it into three (Offers, Leads, Money Models). (self-reported)
- Money Models took ~1,000 hours to write; ~187 pages. He also recorded the full audiobook and re-filmed the course three times. (self-reported)
- Describes himself as "a product of the alternative education system" — has bought "every course, book, tutoring, coaching, everything under the sun" to gain an advantage. (self-reported)
- Says he has been studying "historic deca-billionaires and beyond" — the wealthiest people in history — to understand their decision-making frameworks (cites Rockefeller's letters to his son). (self-reported)
- States his "number one value is honesty." (self-reported)
- Says giving-first happened to him "earlier on in my career," and that though he's "for sure been taken advantage of," giving first has produced more opportunities. (self-reported)
