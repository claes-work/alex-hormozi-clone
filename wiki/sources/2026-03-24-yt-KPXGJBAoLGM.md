# Helping a High School Tutoring Business Scale

## Summary
In a live workshop exchange, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a high school English tutoring business (roughly $20k/month revenue, $15k/month profit, six tutors, aiming for $1M/year) whose new "ongoing tutoring" ad campaign lowered cost-per-lead by 75% but produced low-quality traffic that failed to convert. Hormozi diagnoses a demand problem and argues the owner should return to selling a defined, short-duration front-end offer (a three-week summer program) that acts as a sales motion into long-term continuity. He recommends a performance guarantee and bundling the front-end program against a one-year package.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that you must "give people what they want in order to give them what they need" — sell a defined, short-duration offer on the front end because customers rarely buy the long-term outcome directly (paraphrase of his "ham and the garlic" parable).
- As of March 2026, Alex argues nobody buys a gym membership asking for "a lifestyle"; buyers want a defined end thing — a six-week challenge, 21-day detox, 28-day fix — and continuity is rarely sold directly.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a short defined front-end offer as a sales motion: it buys a longer window to educate the customer on what actually matters, then upsell into continuity once they see momentum.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises this owner to (1) go back to the three-week program that was selling, (2) refresh ad creative with more variation of the offer that worked, and (3) upsell three-week buyers into a one-year package.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends a guarantee tied to the outcome — e.g. a set point gain or top-10% score, or free continued tutoring until it's achieved — and dismisses the fear that students would study for years without results.
- Caller claim (unverified): the owner's February switch to an ongoing-tutoring ad cut cost-per-lead ~75% but drew ~100 low-quality leads that did not convert; Facebook targeting changes capped the maximum age at 25, surfacing students instead of the decision-making parents.
- Caller claim (unverified): one of the largest tutoring companies in the owner's state does ~$1M/month and sells only a one- or two-week free trial before the school year, which Alex cites as confirmation that a defined front-end offer outperforms selling continuity directly.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "So the moral of the story is you have to give people what they want in order to give them what they need."

> "No person ever bought a gym membership saying, 'I really want a lifestyle.' Never. Not once. Right? They want a six-week challenge. They want a 21-day detox. They want a 28-day fix."

> "If you sell a defined end thing on the front end, it really just becomes a sales motion to sell the thing that they then realize, because you have a longer period of time to educate them on what matters."

> "How do you upsell those people from the three-week into continuity? Which is you just take the three-week thing and then just bundle it against a one-year package."

> "You might say four points, or 10% on your score, or I'll keep tutoring you for free until you get it. That's pretty compelling."

> "People don't just keep studying for years and not get scores."
