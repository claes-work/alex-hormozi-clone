# How I Would Build a Business in 2026 (If I Had to Start Over)

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video arguing that the single most important business concept is not a tactic, funnel, or method but a *model* — specifically the ratio of lifetime gross profit (LTGP) to cost of acquiring a customer (CAC). He frames the number-one rule of business as "don't go out of business," which comes down to managing cash flow, and shows via his gym-industry "tale of two businesses" how a customer worth ~$1,000 in the first 30 days lets you outspend and effectively monopolize the attention auction against a competitor whose customer is worth $21. He then teaches back-of-napkin ways to compute LTGP and CAC, minimum ratio thresholds tied to how automated the business is, and levers for raising LTGP or lowering CAC.

## Key claims

- **The model beats the method.** Methods (DM hacks, viral hashtags, ad tricks) expire and are one-trick ponies; the enduring competitive moat is the model — the economics of the business itself. Anyone can copy a method, but a better model still outspends them.
- **The number-one rule of business is "don't go out of business," and cash flow is what prevents it.** Bootstrapped businesses (he says 99% of owners have no outside investors) live or die by cash; Silicon Valley just artificially inflates cash by raising money.
- **Tale of two gym businesses.** Industry standard was the "LBO" (low barrier offer): $21 for 21 days, then convert to a $99/month membership. His model ran 6-week challenges: ~$600 upfront day one, ~$200 supplements within 48 hours, and a ~$2,000 prepay-for-the-year at week three — blended ~$1,000 per customer in the first 30 days.
- **The business that makes more money per customer wins the attention auction.** Because you can spend more to acquire a customer, you can buy up ~100% of ad inventory and hold a *legal* monopoly on attention — not via network effects or undercutting, but purely by having better unit economics.
- **The competitor loses money acquiring customers:** ~$100 CAC (really ~$300 because only 1 in 3 converted to the $99 membership, which stayed ~6 months), so they need a marketing *budget*; with a superior model your "budget" is "spend as many dollars as you can while the numbers hold."
- **His gym-opening "black box":** put $5,000 in a bank account, sign a lease, run $100/day of ads → ~10 leads → close 2 → ~$600 each = $1,200 same day. Reinvest daily. Spending $500/day instead, one gym generated upwards of $100,000 in pre-sale sales in ~30 days — roughly the cost to fully outfit and open a gym. This let him open six locations in his early 20s off cash flow, each fully paid for by customers before opening day; cash-flow positive 6 weeks after launch.
- **The two numbers you must know:** (1) what a customer is worth to you over 30 days (gross profit), (2) what a customer costs you to acquire. Ideally #1 > #2.
- **Why the 30-day window:** it's about as long as most small businesses can handle from a cash-flow perspective, and it's the interest-free credit-card float period.
- **The concept named:** LTGP (lifetime gross profit), a.k.a. LTV / CLV — money left after cost of delivery. CAC = cost of acquiring a customer. The ratio is LTGP:CAC.
- **Back-of-napkin math:** CAC = total acquisition spend (ads + associated labor + commissions) ÷ new customers. LTV ≈ revenue ÷ total customers, then × gross-profit %. Gross profit % via COGS/cost-of-delivery ÷ customers. Done over a full year it's *more* accurate because good and bad months average out. He stresses it's "third grade math."
- **Minimum ratio thresholds tied to automation** across three components (lead gen, conversion/sales, delivery/fulfillment): all 3 automated → 3:1 (the Silicon Valley rule); 2 of 3 automated → ~6:1; 1 of 3 → ~9:1; none automated (people at every step) → 12:1+.
- **Why the ratio must rise as you scale:** CAC always goes up over time (rising CPMs, more competitors, colder audiences up the interest curve); you add management/infrastructure layers; later customers are less sold and often worth less; and adding manual labor (a 6th salesperson, a new marketer) incurs cost day one but returns only months later, so you need padding.
- **Levers to raise LTGP:** raise price, decrease costs, upsells, downsells (carefully — can cannibalize), financing, change payment terms to frontload cash, cross-sells (sell something different — "a burger with fries, not a bigger burger"). He demos applying all of these to a book and to a table.
- **Levers to lower CAC:** improve the offer, better ad creative (volume + quality, better hooks, remake winners), CRO (split-test pages, follow-up, phone scripts, team training), cheaper CPMs. But lowering CAC is not the goal — optimize the *ratio*, and never optimize cost-per-lead (cheap leads aren't customers).
- **Most owners want cheaper leads/customers; that's usually the last thing they need.** What they actually need is a better model so they can *afford* more expensive (better) leads. Given LTV $5,000 / CAC $500 (10:1) vs LTV $1,000 / CAC $200 (5:1), the first business is better despite costing 2x+ more per customer.
