# We fired everyone who was late...this is why..

## Summary

In this 2021 video Hormozi (then describing himself as owner of a portfolio doing
$85M/year in revenue) uses two contrasting management stories to argue that a business
is won on "hearts," not just "smarts." In the first, a gym-owner partner locks the door
at the start of a mandatory meeting and fires on the spot everyone who showed up late,
resetting the performance standard. In the second, a Smoothie King manager ("Vince")
quietly tells a new employee not to do the closing/cleaning tasks the owner had trained
him to do — a hidden saboteur of the owner's standards. Hormozi's thesis: strategy is
mostly commoditized, so execution and culture — leaders who set the tone and hold the
bar — decide which of two similarly-strategic companies wins.

## Key claims

- (2021) Hormozi says he had a "massive shift" in how he views business: he had always
  leaned toward strategy, acquisition, monetization, and pricing, but now believes those
  "smarts" are overestimated and the "hearts" (soft skills, culture, execution) matter
  more.
- (2021) A business has two components — "smarts" and "hearts" — and you need both; most
  people (himself included) overestimate the importance of smarts.
- (2021) Business strategy is "not that complex" — find something good people want, sell
  it for much more than it costs, do as much of that as possible; the problem is almost
  always execution, not strategy.
- (2021) People fail to do what you want not because the strategy is wrong but because
  they don't understand why it matters — a "heart" / soft-skill problem, not a tactics
  problem.
- (2021) A leader's job is to "set the tone" and then "carry the bar" — to be
  "intolerant of anything but excellence" and reinforce that standard so it is executed
  consistently across the company.
- (2021) When two companies have the same strategy (or the smaller one even has a better
  strategy), the one that executes and gets people to execute on its behalf wins — via
  better ad returns, profits, growth, and lower employee turnover.
- (2021) He now hires for character traits — interpersonal dynamics, self-awareness — as
  much as or more than for raw skill (finance, product, marketing/sales).
- (2021) Warns owners against a hidden "Vince" — a manager who tells staff they don't
  need to do the standard work the owner mandated, secretly destroying the business.
- (2021) Invokes John Wooden and the line "advanced people never don't do the basics":
  elite performers do the fundamentals (fast lead follow-up, structured sales calls,
  good onboarding) consistently; nothing they say is revolutionary — they just actually
  do it.

## Biographical / company detail

- Self-description (2021): "I own a portfolio of companies that does $85 million a year in
  revenue" — stated twice.
- The first story is set in his **Gym Launch** era: a gym owner (doing $4.2M/year across
  two locations) proposed a partnership where Hormozi would fill the gyms and the owner
  would operate them. Hormozi calls it "a horrible partnership." The door-locking /
  mass-firing was done by the *partner*, not Hormozi — Hormozi was a witness who took the
  lesson from it.
- The second story is from his stated **first job** as a "blender tender" at Smoothie
  King, under a small owner named "Dave" (2–3 locations) who trained him personally; the
  saboteur manager is given the pseudonym "Vince."
