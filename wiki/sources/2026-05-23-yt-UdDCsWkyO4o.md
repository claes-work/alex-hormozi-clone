# Maybe you shouldn't create content

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says he starts with proof before trying to build authority. If a beginner
lacks external achievements, he recommends effort-based proof, working for free, earning
testimonials, and becoming king of a very small niche. He also explains the upstart
advantage: access, care, and willingness to do more or charge less until proof creates a
virtuous cycle.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex prefers to begin with proof so people do not question whether he is legitimate.
- If someone lacks external achievements, they can create effort-based achievements.
- Making a very large amount of content and documenting learning can be a valid early
  proof path.
- Alex says every business he starts still begins with free work or free value so he can
  get feedback, learn pain points, and smooth the process.
- Getting 10 free customers and using their testimonials can be faster than trying to get
  10 paying customers from zero proof.
- More people knowing about a person's work usually does not make them less money.
- Beginners should avoid competing in broad categories dominated by people with much
  stronger proof.
- A beginner can win by becoming king of a tiny niche or "puddle."
- New entrants can compete against established players through access, personal care,
  responsiveness, and willingness to do more for less.
- Once proof exists, charging more can become a virtuous cycle.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I never want someone to question whether I'm legit or not."

> "Just be king of the itty bitty puddle."

> "I started at zero, too."
