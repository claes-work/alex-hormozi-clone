# The 3 Metrics That Actually Define a Brand

## Summary

In this teaching clip, Alex Hormozi defines brand through three metrics: influence, direction, and reach. Influence is whether the brand changes behavior, direction is whether that behavior moves toward or away from the desired action, and reach is how many people are affected. He argues that strong brands do not have to be polarizing, that brand growth requires pairing a brand with things the ideal audience likes, and that each new pairing risks losing some old audience while gaining a larger new one.

## Key claims (Alex's teaching only)

- Brand has three main metrics: influence, direction, and reach.
- Influence means how likely the brand is to change someone's behavior.
- Direction means whether the behavior moves toward or away from what the brand wants.
- Reach means how many people the brand changes behavior for.
- A weak brand has low recognition and little behavioral effect; a strong positive brand has broad recognition, strong influence, and behavior that moves toward the brand.
- Strong brands do not have to be polarizing by percentage, even if any large brand will have some haters in absolute numbers.
- Donald Trump is used as a polar strong-brand example; Taylor Swift, Mother Teresa, and Apple are used as strong positive-brand examples.
- Brand metrics apply even to small audiences; parents can be high-influence, low-reach brands.
- Brand building pairs the brand with things the highest percentage of the ideal audience already likes.
- Every new pairing risks losing some existing audience and gaining new audience; the bet is that the net result improves reach, influence, and positive direction.
- Alex wants his own brand associated with business value: people consume his content/books, make money, then associate growth and profit with him.
