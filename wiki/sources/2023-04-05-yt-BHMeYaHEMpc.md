# How to Grow ANY Local Business (my framework)

## Summary

Alex Hormozi walks through the four-variable checklist he uses when advising a
local-business owner (his example: a teeth-whitening chain, echoed by a second
franchise business he'd just spoken to) deciding whether to expand via **franchising**
or by opening **privately owned locations** they hold themselves. The through-line is to
switch from a "business owner" hat to an "investor" hat: score each path on return on
capital, on operational effort (centralized vs. decentralized), on the math of reverse-
engineering a target exit into a location count, and on the founder's own personality
(promotion-driven vs. product/ops-driven). Where the math and the personality conflict,
he reframes the disliked task as a "deficiency in the business" and hires a specialist to
fix it, so both the numbers and the person work toward maximum enterprise value.

## Key claims

The **four-variable local-business scaling checklist** (franchise vs. own):

1. **Return on capital per location.** Compare cost-to-open against annual profit. His
   example location: ~$500k top line, ~$250k bottom line, ~$50k to open — a 5x annual
   return on capital. A McDonald's is ~$1.1–1.2M to open for ~$150k/yr (~15% return).
   Rule of thumb: if a business returns **less than ~100% on capital, it usually makes
   more sense to franchise off the bat** because self-funded expansion is too slow;
   high return-on-capital businesses favor keeping locations privately owned.
2. **Effort to open — centralized vs. decentralized.** Centralized = more operational
   drag at the franchisor level; decentralized = more work sits with the franchisee. A
   highly decentralized model may let you open ~10x more franchises for the same effort
   as one owned location (favor franchising); a centralized model where you do most of
   the work favors owning locations (provided return on capital is there).
3. **Reverse the net-worth/exit goal into a location count.** Franchises typically earn
   ~2x the valuation multiple of a brick-and-mortar chain (his example: ~15x earnings vs.
   ~8x) because investors pay today for locations contractually guaranteed to open. But
   per-location value added is far higher for owned units: 8x on $250k = **$2M added per
   owned location**, vs. 15x on ~$20k franchisor earnings = **~$300k added per franchise
   location**. A $100M exit therefore = **~50 owned locations OR ~333 franchise
   locations**. Knowing that number makes the decision "much, much easier."
4. **Founder personality.** Promotion-driven founders (love sales, marketing, the "hunt"
   of selling franchises) lean franchise; product/ops/leadership-driven founders (love
   building teams, longer time horizon) lean owned. When it's roughly 50/50, personality
   can be the tiebreaker.

Supporting points:
- Franchise economics in his example: a **7.5% royalty on top line** (plus a shared
  marketing fund for national branding) ≈ **$35k/yr per location**; run at location
  margins that nets ~**$20k/yr per franchise** at the franchisor level.
- **Build every business as a sellable asset even if you never intend to sell** — "build
  an asset so that it is sellable, not so that we sell it." His own current stance: hold
  and grow, buy and build, don't want to sell anymore.
- Franchisor growth is usually constrained by **internal limiting factors**, not external
  demand; selling franchises "like hotcakes" is not always easy, so real open-rates
  (maybe 3–4/yr) can be slower than assumed.
- **Conflict-resolution move:** when the math points one way but the founder's personality
  the other, phrase the disliked function (e.g. staffing many low-wage, high-churn roles)
  as a **deficiency in the business**, then hire someone who has solved exactly that
  (e.g. run staffing for a franchise scaling 50→250 locations) to support the founder.
  "The math and the personalities work together to build the ultimate enterprise value."

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "You have to switch from your business hat to your investor hat and start looking at
  your business investment."
- "Once you start generating real money you have to think, where is the best place I can
  put my money? And if opening another location 5x's your money every year... that
  becomes a very attractive machine if you expand the time horizon."
- "We don't want to sell anything anymore. We want to hold and grow, buy and build."
- "We will always build an asset so that it is sellable, not so that we sell it."
- "For every location you open on the privately held side you would add two million
  dollars."
- "If you phrase the thing that you don't like as a deficiency in the business, then it
  becomes very solvable."
- "You take somebody who's super promotion-driven and then bring in one or two people to
  support them in the things that they don't like that are way better than them at it."
- "You'll actually look at it as an investor, and it'll make you a better business owner."
