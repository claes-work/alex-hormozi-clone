# Alex Hormozi used failure as his greatest motivation

## Summary

In a short interview clip, Alex Hormozi recounts how, at his career's rock bottom, he
coped with a hopeless financial situation by refusing to dwell on the overwhelming
reality and instead narrowing focus to a few controllable daily actions (make 100
reach-outs, spend $100/day on ads, kill losers, scale winners). He frames his ability
to generate leads as his "get out of jail free card" that let him fail repeatedly until
he succeeded. He closes with his core reframe on the ~5 years he spent with "nothing to
show for it": the assets weren't the valuable thing he earned — the skills, experiences,
and character traits were, and those survived every loss.

## Key claims

- (paraphrase, mindset) When the reality of his situation was too bleak to process, he
  deliberately avoided thinking about the whole picture (it only made him feel sadder
  and more hopeless) and focused only on the few things under his control.
- (paraphrase, business) He reduced abstract concepts like "advertising" and "selling"
  down to concrete repeatable actions — e.g. make 100 reach-outs, spend $100/day, turn
  off the bad ads, do more of what starts working — because that was the only way it
  wasn't overwhelming.
- (paraphrase, business) The single skill of being able to generate leads, independent
  of industry, has been his "get out of jail free card," letting him fail over and over
  until he got it right.
- (paraphrase, biography) At the rock-bottom point: ~90 days earlier he had gotten a DUI,
  his mom was in the hospital in really bad shape, and he had just lost all his money —
  which is when he pulled his now-wife aside.
- (paraphrase, biography) ~6 months later he repeated the cycle: he and his partner ran
  gym-turnaround "launches" (flying out to fix gyms, "Bar Rescue but for gyms"), but
  after they returned home two gyms refunded all the transactions and told customers to
  sign up directly with the gym at half the rate — wiping out the money they'd spent on
  hotels, flights, ad spend, and a whole month of work.
- (paraphrase, business) Only after losing that money did they "accidentally" pivot into
  the licensing model, which became their first very big success.
- (paraphrase, wealth) During the gym era he "materially looked successful" with six
  locations, but he kept plowing all his money back into each new location, so he was
  "asset rich, cash poor" — and when he sold the gyms, moved the money into a new venture,
  and lost it, he felt he had lost five years with nothing to show for it.
- (paraphrase, mindset) His resulting hypothesis: he did not lose the five years — he
  lost the assets, which were not the most valuable thing he had earned. The skills,
  experiences, and character traits were, and because he kept those three things he was
  able to do the next thing.
