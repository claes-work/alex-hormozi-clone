# The Actual Secret Behind Mastering Sales & Marketing (2021-04-08)

## Summary

Hormozi argues that the "secret" to mastering sales and marketing is not
studying — it is doing. He recounts that he never took a sales training or read
a sales book before he started selling, and that reading only became useful to
him after he had real reps and a specific bottleneck to solve. He frames excess
reading/course-buying as "productive procrastination" and prescribes execution
first, study second.

## Key claims

- (2021) He made most of his money *before* he read many books; he estimates he
  read maybe 30 books before he and Leila were "deca millionaires." Reading
  came when he had time and money, so calling books the cause of his success
  would be backwards.
- (2021) After his post-college consulting job he read self-help book after book
  for 6–12 months and found his life "literally the same," and that half the
  books contradicted each other. His fix: refuse to read a new book until he had
  executed everything in the current one — which turned him into a re-reader of
  a few books rather than a consumer of many.
- (2021) He never did a single sales training and never read a sales book before
  he started selling; he didn't even know the word for what he was doing was
  "sales" — he just needed to get people to sign up for his gym and give him
  money. **Proto-conviction-selling / pre-CLOSER framing.**
- (2021) Selling is mostly conviction — how much you believe in the product and
  how much you genuinely care about the prospect — far more than "judo chops"
  (tactics). Genuine care keeps you from convincing people to do what they
  shouldn't; genuine belief makes the sale easy.
- (2021) Most sales-people churn because they don't believe in the product and
  don't care about the prospect, creating cognitive dissonance between being a
  good person and needing to make money. Reps start hot then crash when they
  learn the product isn't what they were sold to sell.
- (2021) His personal learning method: when he wants a skill, he buys ONE course
  on that specific thing and executes it while going through it (e.g. wanting a
  webinar → buy a webinar course → follow it to build the webinar). You don't
  truly learn something until you apply it.
- (2021) His biggest gains came from paying people above him one-on-one to break
  his beliefs about what was possible — not to hand-hold him through skills.
  Acquiring skills means doing them, which means sucking for a while; even
  experts fail.
- (2021) Endorses the "slush fund" / learning-budget idea he credits to Ezra
  Firestone: decide in advance how much you're willing to "blow" per month on
  failure to learn a skill faster (5%, 10%, 25% of income), so you have no
  emotional attachment to nailing it the first time — it's an education
  investment, not a verdict on you. **Proto money-model / spend-to-learn logic.**
- (2021) His advice sequence for marketing: run an ad → to a landing page →
  collect contact info → pick up the phone and call them. Encounter the problem
  first, then seek the specific solution — don't try to pre-cover every solution
  to problems you haven't hit yet.
