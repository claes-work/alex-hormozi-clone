# Have the Hard Conversation

## Summary
Interview clip (Alex is the guest; an unnamed host prompts and interjects). The through-line is Alex's claim that most stuck decisions are really **unhad conversations**: "a lot of the decisions we make are predicated on the conversations we need to have." He builds a small mindset stack around this. First, **commitment = eliminating alternatives** — you can *say* you're committed, but you aren't until you remove the other options ("get out of jail free" cards); people decide to end a relationship, quit a job, or start something new, but only become committed once the alternatives are gone and action is forced. Second, on why unmade decisions feel so heavy: Alex describes a "hamster wheel" of replaying scenarios and reframes, but says underneath it is almost always **fear — and usually the fear of one specific person's judgment, not of "society."** His fix is to *name the person*: turning the amorphous "people/society/judgment" into "Tom" makes the fear concrete and usually absurd ("Looks like Tom's more in control of my life than I am"). He grounds this in a personal story: at 19 he was angry and blamed his parents for everything, then realized that by blaming them he was *handing them control* over his life — and the disgust at the people he hated most having that much power is what flipped him into taking control himself. The host offers a companion concept he calls **"anxiety cost"** (like opportunity cost, but for the time and attention consumed by an unmade decision), and Alex extends it into a **decision-speed** argument: you can move through life ~7x faster by moving your default from "decide by end of week" to "decide by end of day"; four decisions that take an average person a month, a fast operator makes in four days, and "what takes you a month to make a decision, we make in an hour." Asked whether his reputation for ruthless/decisive hiring-and-firing means he's unusual, Alex says pre-Baltimore he was a representative average person — high-achieving, but risk-averse and insecure — and that his **insecurities were the engine**: he did well not because he cared about school but because he cared what other people thought of him. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
