# Why Top Bankers in 1929 Only Worked 6 Hours a Day

## Summary

The other speaker discusses Andrew Ross Sorkin's book *1929* and examples of high-level
business figures who appeared not to work extreme hours. Alex responds by warning
against modeling the top of the mountain instead of the climb. He says a business can
continue growing if its required functions are performed, whether or not the founder is
personally doing the work, but founders often immediately use delegation freedom to start
another job.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed and speaker-separated, dated 2026-05-09)

- The other speaker summarizes *1929*, leverage before the Great Depression, and bankers
  whose routines looked lighter than modern founder work culture.
- Alex says the hard question is whether one should model someone's current life at the
  top of the mountain or the climb that got them there.
- Alex says he tries to avoid comparing his winter to another person's spring.
- Alex says a hypothetical perfect hire could take over nearly all of a founder's
  current responsibilities while the business keeps growing.
- Alex says many entrepreneurs delegate one job only to start the next one.
- Alex says whether a founder works or does not work, the business grows if the business
  performs the necessary functions.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Do I model the top of the mountain or do I model the climb?"

> "We're in different seasons."

> "As long as the business performs these functions, it will grow."
