# Helping a $650K Coaching Business Get to $2M

## Summary

Alex tells virtual-assistant coaching owners that recurring employee failure points to
an inadequate training system, not universally bad hires. Because VAs are both their
delivery product and their growth engine, he identifies training as the highest-leverage
constraint. He argues that entrepreneurs must select one priority, permit other fires to
burn, and re-rank only after the first constraint is removed.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex distinguishes having SOPs from having a system that reliably
  produces the required behavior.
- If every hire fails, the controllable explanation is the training process rather than
  a claim that all hires are incapable.
- In this business, VA training is unusually leveraged because it determines both
  product quality and the owners' ability to delegate growth work.
- He says feeling many constraints indicates a prioritization failure; selecting the
  most important problem is the entrepreneur's job.
- Teams need explicit permission to underperform on lower priorities while all resources
  address the main constraint.
- Alex self-reports resolving a three-month multi-funnel pixel problem in three days by
  ranking it above CRO and attribution and meeting twice daily.
- After the first constraint is fixed, remaining priorities should be reorganized rather
  than following a long static list.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "It's one thing to have SOPs, it's another thing to have a system where people do the thing you want them to do."

> "If you feel like you have many constraints, it means that you don't know how to prioritize."

> "We will solve first and we will allow these fires to burn."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: SOP-versus-behavior system; dynamic single-constraint prioritization and permission to neglect lower priorities.
