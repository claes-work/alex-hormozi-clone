# Alex Hormozi Reveals the RISKS of Info Businesses

## Summary
A short clip of Alex Hormozi arguing that pure information businesses (courses, info
products) are high-margin but strategically fragile, and that operators who want durable
"Enterprise Value" (a sellable, valuable asset) should transition as fast as they can to
service-based models. His core distinction: information has rapidly diminishing value once
consumed, whereas community, accountability, and feedback are "consumables" that retain
value month over month, so people keep paying for them. He recommends giving away the
information itself as a lead magnet and monetizing the implementation/service layered on top
— using a sales-coaching business as a worked example — and celebrates the competitive
position of taking what others charge for and giving it away free while making it better.

## Key claims
- Information products are "really high margins" and there is "nothing wrong with" them, but
  long term an operator should "transition as quickly as you can to a service-based business"
  — the caveat being *if you want Enterprise Value* (a sellable asset valuable to a potential
  buyer). Selling a new course every year that you don't mind being copied is a valid model
  he passes "no judgment" on; it's simply optimizing for a different goal than a sellable
  asset.
- **Consumables framework:** community, accountability, and feedback are "consumables" —
  after you use them you want more. A course video, once learned, you generally don't need
  again. Therefore information has diminishing value quickly, while accountability/feedback/
  community "retain value month over month over month," which is what makes customers keep
  paying to keep them.
- **Information as lead magnet:** use the valuable information as the free lead magnet to
  attract people, then build the "next natural needs" around it — wanting to make money
  doing it, get a job doing it, or get better at doing it — all of which are "offshoots of
  services you could offer."
- **Worked example (sales coaching):** rather than sell sales *information* (which "everyone
  does"), he would give away all the information and sell the implementation — listening to
  clients' calls, giving real-time feedback ("I'll listen in while you're on the phone and
  give you... say this instead"). The demonstrated expertise generates demand and trust, and
  can later be flipped into a recruiting business or a done-for-you service without ever
  selling the information.
- **Competitive positioning:** he "loves the position of taking what other people charge for
  and giving it away for free and making it better." Any business model "predicated on"
  something a competitor can give away free and improve on is exposed.
