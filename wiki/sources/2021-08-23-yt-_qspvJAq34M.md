# How I got 700 people to pay me $40,000 each...no bs..

## Summary
In this solo whiteboard/iPad video Hormozi teaches his Value Equation, the framework he considers possibly his biggest contribution to marketing. He frames it with a story: after a five-day marathon of Gym Launch events, his father calls to question whether the ~700 clients paying ~$42,000/year are getting their money's worth, and Alex rebuts him by walking through the components of value one prospect question at a time. He then defines value as a fraction with four components — dream outcome and perceived likelihood of achievement on top (increase these), time delay and effort & sacrifice on the bottom (decrease these). He illustrates each with contrasting examples (a $5 ebook vs. $50,000 liposuction, a first-time vs. 10,000th-surgery surgeon, personal training vs. lipo) and argues that driving the bottom of the fraction toward zero yields an "infinitely valuable" product, citing Amazon, Netflix, and Uber as businesses that won by attacking the downside. He closes by promoting his newly released $0.99 book and stating his portfolio goal of a billion dollars a year in revenue, currently at about $85 million.

## Key claims
- Value has four components arranged as a fraction: (dream outcome × perceived likelihood of achievement) / (time delay × effort & sacrifice). Increase the top, decrease the bottom.
- "Dream outcome" defines the category and is about how appealing/visceral the promised experience is; the more viscerally you describe it, the more valuable it's deemed and the more people will pay.
- Within a category, the other three variables determine price — same dream outcome (lose weight) ranges from a $5 ebook to $50,000 liposuction.
- Perceived likelihood of achievement was the last of the four he figured out; higher certainty (e.g. a surgeon's 10,000th surgery vs. their first) makes people pay more, even when they get less of the provider's time.
- More testimonials raise perceived likelihood of achievement, which decreases perceived risk and increases value; he says stacking testimonials was his early-career move.
- Time delay is the gap between paying and getting the result; "People will pay for speed" and "Fast beats free" — you can raise value without changing the thing, only by delivering the outcome faster.
- Time to fulfill something should never enter your own value equation — you can get less of a provider's time yet pay more.
- Effort is what someone must do that they don't want to do; sacrifice is what they must give up or stop doing that they enjoy — two sides of the same coin, both to be minimized.
- If you drive the bottom of the fraction to zero (immediate, no effort/sacrifice), you have an "infinitely valuable" product no matter how small the promise on top.
- The biggest companies win by decreasing the downside of the equation, which he calls where the biggest competitive moat lies: Uber (immediacy, pay-to-skip-the-line), Netflix (removed effort/sacrifice of watching), Amazon (two-hour drone delivery).
- High value (a 4/4 offer like lipo) sells $40,000 packages over the phone in under 15 minutes; a low-value offer (1/4, like personal training) requires an hour of "arm wrestling" to sell a $1,000 package.
- Getting the dream outcome right means depicting what the prospect actually wants: cites Dr. John Berardi (co-founder, Precision Nutrition) — people buying certifications actually want community / a socially acceptable excuse, not continuing education.
- His pricing/structure logic in the framing story: pay-as-you-earn and a strong ROI (make ~$239,000, pay ~$42,000, ~15 hrs/week, ~11 months) is what makes a high-ticket offer a yes.
