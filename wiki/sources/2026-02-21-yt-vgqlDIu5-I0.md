# How to Reduce Churn

## Summary

Short solo MoreMozi Q&A. Alex coaches "Emiliano" (caption spells it "Ameiliano"), an
architecture-firm owner, on where the best **revenue-retention** opportunities are for a
project-based business "started from scratch." His single move: **chunk up a level** — stop
measuring retention at the inherently one-off unit (a house, a building, a project) and find
the higher aggregation node you do repeat business with, then treat *that* node as your unit
of retention, acquisition, and LTV.

He illustrates with his own software company's pivot (caption-garbled name; almost certainly
**ALAN**, his lead-nurture SaaS): selling lead-nurture services direct to brick-and-mortar
SMBs failed because SMBs have inconsistent lead flow ("great — but how do we get leads?"),
which is a different business. The fix was to sell to businesses that *already have leads* —
**agencies**. An agency churns individual client logos in and out every month, but the
agency itself keeps paying month over month, so revenue retention *at the agency level* is
excellent even though the logos inside it constantly change.

Ported to the architecture firm: identify the referral partners / repeat counterparties
(contractors, referrers) who send business regularly, make those the "nodes of revenue," and
gear the whole sales/acquisition motion around acquiring more referral partners — each worth
a known X, costing a known Y to acquire, and retainable far better than any individual
project or logo.

Solo Alex throughout; the questioner only sets up the prompt (no second-speaker content
trains the persona).

## Key claims (2026-02-21, paraphrased unless quoted)

- ★ **"Chunk up a level" to find a durable retention node.** For businesses whose base unit
  is inherently one-off/churny (architecture projects, individual client logos), revenue
  retention is bad at that level. Move the unit of analysis *up* to whatever counterparty you
  do repeat business with, and measure/retain/acquire at that level instead. *(New framing:
  retention as a choice of unit-of-analysis, not just a set of fixes.)*
- ★ **The higher node retains even while its sub-units churn.** In his ALAN example, a small
  chiropractor-focused agency had ~10 chiropractors paying ~$2,000/mo; it lost ~2 and gained
  ~2 chiropractors per month, so the *logos* constantly turned over — yet the agency kept
  using the software every month. Aggregate churn of the sub-units washes out at the node, so
  node-level revenue retention was "super good." *(New concrete mechanic / worked example.)*
- ★ **Direct-to-SMB failed on lead flow; sell to whoever already has the input you depend on.**
  Selling lead-nurture to SMBs broke because SMBs have inconsistent lead flow — solving that
  is "a whole other problem, a different business." Agencies were the right customer because
  they already bring leads/clients. *(New: the specific ALAN pivot rationale, distinct from
  the "consumable inputs" idea already in the wiki.)*
- ★ **Make the node the atom of your unit economics.** Once referral partners are the nodes,
  the sales motion becomes "how do I advertise and acquire more of these referral partners,"
  each worth a known X at a known acquisition cost Y — and each retainable far more than the
  individual products/logos they bring on. *(New: recasts CAC/LTV around the aggregation node.)*
- **Architecture-firm application (paraphrase):** don't think at the house/building/project
  level; chunk up to the referral partners or repeat counterparties (he floats contractors and
  referrers), demonstrate retention of *them* rather than of projects, and those become the
  revenue nodes.
