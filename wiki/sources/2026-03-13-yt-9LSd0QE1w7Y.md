# Advice for Hard Days

## Summary
Podcast clip in which Alex talks through the mindset he's actively using to get through a bad season of his own life. His opening frame: "You're not having a bad year. You've had a few bad days that you're thinking about for the rest of the year." He argues the gap between a happy and an unhappy life is not the raw count of good vs. bad days but **which days you replay** over and over. So his current focus is small: just win the day — "a good day in a bad season is a bite-sized victory," and stringing a few together makes a bad season feel less hard. He cites Bill Ackman (paraphrasing a podcast) on the hardest stretch of Ackman's career — a divorce whose stakes were inflated by a ~$4 billion Pershing Square loss — where the trap was that nothing could be *finished* in a single day; Ackman's tactical answer was simply to **make progress**, which feels negligible day-to-day but at 30 and 90 days is materially different. Alex is candid that mood is still partly dictated by circumstance ("yes, it does"), but insists "sometimes progress is the W" and "maintaining in some seasons is winning." He grounds it in his own year: business is thriving ("I'm murdering it") while his body is not — a torn quad and a nagging neck issue — and he names his two great fears as losing his mind (mostly the fear of being a burden) and chronic pain. His central tactic is a deliberate **recalibration of scale**: take one good moment (a great podcast) and let it *make the day*, then the week, then let those good moments "eat up the season" — asking how little a thing can happen that still counts as a win. He describes a conscious decision on May 1st ("I have decreed that the rest of the year will be good"), tied to an HRV screenshot he shared, and pushes back on his own old habit of judging people who called small things the highlight of their week — because "your life is made up of small days like this." The bigger warning: waiting for the billion dollars, the marriage, or the main stage means "you're just holding your happiness hostage" — so what if something small could be something great? He closes on *why* he's investing so much discretionary effort here: he doesn't strictly *need* to work this hard, and the thing that would stop him reaching his ultimate goals is running out of steam — so he'd rather make the ride more enjoyable. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
