# The Best SALES TRAINING On The Internet

## Summary

A recorded keynote (originally delivered at Coaching Con) in which Alex Hormozi
teaches closing as a form of "power" — the ability to influence or direct events and
people. The talk frames selling with logic as a way to help prospects (and yourself)
make high-stakes decisions, and organizes objection handling around a single thesis:
every objection is a "distortion of reality" in which the prospect casts their power to
one of three sources — circumstances, others, or self. Alex walks through 21 selling
beliefs, then a full objection-handling map (time, money/value, fit, authority,
avoidance) with specific line-of-reasoning "overcomes" for each, closing on his
personal thesis that investing in education/skills is the one asset nobody can take.

## Key claims (sales training content)

- **Closing vs. selling**: Selling happens the entire time; the talk is specifically
  about *closing* — everything after you ask someone to buy. "Selling happens before
  you ask for the sale; closing happens after."
- **Obstacles vs. objections**: An obstacle is disagreement *before* you solicit the
  sale (easy to confront and destroy); an objection is disagreement *after* you've
  asked. Handle beliefs as obstacles before the stake is presented.
- **Sell logic, not just emotion**: Emotional buyers get excited, buy, then refund.
  Emotion lowers the action threshold and gets the step; logic makes the decision
  stick and produces good customers. People want to buy — you must help their logical
  brain justify the decision they already want to make.
- **Three sources of "casting power" (from Albert Ellis / CBT's three core
  distortions)**: (1) Circumstances — "I must get what I want when I want it";
  (2) Others — "people must treat me fairly or they deserve condemnation"; (3) Self —
  "I must do well or I am no good." Every objection maps to one of these.
- **The onion of blame**: prospects blame from the outside in — circumstances (time,
  money) → other people (spouse, kids, employees) → finally self. The objection tells
  you which layer you're on and how close you are to the truth.
- **Five common manifestations**: time, value/price, fit ("special snowflake"),
  authority ("must ask my spouse"), avoidance ("I need to think about it").
- **Time overcomes** — three angles: (macro) "you'll always be busy, so learn to do it
  now while busy so it sticks forever"; (micro) a good program *cuts* the 90% of
  ineffective activity filling your time; (when-then) the "one-then fallacy" — "when I
  have X then I'll do Y" reverses the real sequence ("I'll save money once I'm rich").
- **Money/value overcomes** — four frameworks: (1) "A lot for you is good" — if the
  price stings, that stake makes you try harder; (2) relative vs. absolute — if it does
  what it says, the value is there, so the real objection is belief, not price; (3)
  "What's money good for anyway" — you'll pay for the lesson either way, in money or in
  time (12 weeks vs. 12 years); (4) resourceful, not resources — self-made billionaires
  started at zero; people find money for emergencies/others but not themselves.
- **Fit overcomes**: (1) new identity → new priorities (the Sephora story — you spend
  on what your identity values); (2) "change the change" — what you've been doing
  produced what you have, pain of staying the same vs. pain of change; (3)
  hypothetical/unicorn close — "if it were perfect, would you do it?" isolates whether
  the issue is the program or the person.
- **Authority overcomes** ("must ask spouse"): isolate the objection and sidestep the
  absent partner; "do they approve of your current struggle? then why would they oppose
  the fix?"; reframe as **support, not permission** — asking permission means you'll
  blame them later and build a resentful marriage.
- **Avoidance overcomes** (past/present/future): sunk-cost ("you're not making a fast
  decision, you've been deciding for six years"); "don't let it burn you twice";
  rocking-chair visualization (you won't get more information by waiting — *you* are the
  information source); the three-question close (Do you believe it'll help you? Do you
  trust me to deliver? Do you think it'll work for you?) then "do you have or have
  access to the money?"; risk-free options logic (with a guarantee, only walking out the
  door guarantees failure).
- **The reversal (bonus close)**: "The reason you're telling yourself not to do this is
  the reason you should." The chain you're holding is the power you've cast away.
- **21 selling beliefs** include: expect and plan for "no" (no is the job); the person
  who cares most about the prospect wins the sale; selling is a transference of belief
  over a bridge of trust (you must believe it *and* be trusted); belief and trust are
  continuums, not binaries; seek to understand with "childlike curiosity"; closing is a
  dance, not a fight; record all your sales to study hot streaks; if you don't get a
  gasp at the price, you didn't go high enough.
