# I Posted ONLY Business Content for 30 Days [STRATEGY REVEALED]

## Summary

A solo walkthrough of a content-strategy experiment: after 35,000 posts over 40
months building a 7.8M-person audience, Hormozi made six deliberate shifts in his
content and reveals the data ~3–4 weeks in (with on-screen screenshots). The core
move is narrowing hard toward deep business education for his ideal customer
(business owners), optimizing for ad revenue rather than raw views. He reports that
essentially every metric he expected to tank instead improved, and lays out what his
media team is doing next.

## Key claims

The six shifts he made in his content:
1. **Edutainment → education.** Go all-in on education (which he defines as
   changing the viewer's behavior), with downstream effects on packaging, titling,
   and number of special effects.
2. **For us → for you.** Stop making videos his *team* liked; make them for the
   ideal customer (the business owner). He'd drifted into making content for his
   team without realizing it.
3. **Wide → narrow.** Drop wide topics (fitness, relationships, food) and talk only
   about business — even though only ~9% of people are business owners.
4. **Views → revenue.** Switch the primary metric from views to ad revenue, which
   pairs views with RPM (revenue per thousand views) as a quality signal. Higher RPM
   means more valuable eyeballs / the right people watching. He frames "paired KPIs"
   as a general business tool (a speed metric plus a quality metric per department).
5. **Shorts → longs.** Emphasize long-form. He says he saw no evidence for the
   "shorts create long viewers" thesis; longs drove the most applications, book
   sales, and opt-ins. (Shorts strategy revisited below.)
6. **Assuming more → assuming nothing.** Stop assuming viewers know who he is;
   re-introduce himself, avoid inside jokes that exclude newcomers, since most
   viewers don't know him.

Reported results (~3–4 weeks in, vs. prior 6-week average):
- **RPM up 68%** (revenue per thousand viewers) — wider topics showed much lower RPM,
  deeper business topics much higher.
- **~25% more comments per view** (engagement; he treats comments/shares as higher-
  value than likes).
- **Long-form views up ~29.56% across the channel** despite *fewer* absolute views
  per video — because education content needs far less post-editing, so he can make
  more of it.
- **Subscriber conversion up 24.6%**, driving higher absolute subscriber growth
  despite fewer views (called this the most surprising outcome).
- **~26% more opt-ins per week.**
- **~2x book sales** ("twice the sales, not 20%") despite fewer views.
- Applications (for acquisition.com) also up.
- Slightly fewer likes and negligible change in shares — dismissed as vanity metrics.

Nuances he adds:
- Some keynotes are **both deep and wide** — his highest revenue-per-view video is
  also his most-viewed; conceptual topics like leverage and branding apply to all
  business owners and can score high views *and* high RPM.
- He's fine with a video getting 10,000 views instead of 400,000 if those 10,000 are
  $10M+ business owners; views measure *relevance/breadth*, not quality. A 46-minute
  video on how he decided to sell his company applies to almost no one but was gold to
  the few big owners it reached.
- Reframe for small creators: free platform distribution is "infinite leverage"; even
  100 or 1,000 viewers is an audience he'd happily address in person (lunch-and-learns).

What the media team is doing next:
- **Volume negates luck** — create more content within the narrowed direction, keep
  testing variations. (Notes he's "lucky it worked the first time.")
- **Don't get the same scar twice** — mistakes are fine/lessons, but don't repeat a
  known one (keep introducing himself; avoid hi-fi production that underperforms with
  business owners).
- **Slides vs. heavy prep / stage didn't matter** — presenting on his HQ stage (to his
  team, which some comments mistook for pretending to be on a real stage) performed the
  same as just talking to a red camera or doing a whiteboard.
- **Show/say what only you can** — "state the facts and tell the truth" (never get in
  trouble; forces you to collect data); demonstrate via accomplishments *and* labor
  (e.g., "35,000 posts"). If you can't out-outcome people yet, you can out-work them.
- **Still doing wide shorts** — shorts' ROI is top-of-funnel brand/facial recognition
  that later gets retargeted with ads, not direct book sales/opt-ins; he also just
  likes making wide business-philosophy/personal-development content.
