# The Biggest Mistake People Make When Trying to Get Rich

## Summary
In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi argues that the biggest mistake people make in pursuing wealth is a "conflate sequence" error: they believe wealth comes from investing, when investing is the last thing you do, not the first. He reframes the active-vs-passive-income debate as a false binary, insisting that high active income (cash flow) is what funds later risky bets, and that all money is earned "per unit of time." An interviewer prompts the discussion and shares anecdotes about friends losing money in crypto; the clip ends with a promo for acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex describes himself as an "objectivist" who focuses only on what actions are observably required to create a business or income, rather than on manifesting, energy, or mindset abstractions.
- As of March 2026, he states the three observable requirements for a business: let people know about what you have, have something to sell, and make sure what you charge costs less than it costs to deliver — repeated as many times as possible.
- As of March 2026, he identifies the "conflate sequence" as the number-one misconception: people think making money comes from investing, when investing is the last thing you do, not the first.
- As of March 2026, he argues people wrongly believe active/earned income is not scalable, when in reality the wealthiest people typically have very high incomes, and it is the excess cash flow from that income that lets them make big risky bets.
- As of March 2026, he frames get-rich-quick thinking as the "something for nothing fallacy," and says the more an opportunity feels like luck, the bigger the red flag.
- As of March 2026, he holds that controlling more variables gives greater influence over the outcome; if you don't even know the variables, you are "100% gambling."
- As of March 2026, he warns that by the time retail/"Genpop" investors hear about something, it is usually the peak and too late; he dislikes speculative investing generally, citing the "greater fool theory."
- As of March 2026, he reframes income as "money per unit of time," dissolving the active/passive binary, and argues nothing is truly passive because a good passive investment still required diligence (e.g. reviewing ~100 deals to make one).
- As of March 2026, he rejects "never sell your time" as bad advice — arguing everyone would sell an hour for a billion dollars, so the real question is how much your time is worth and trading it up over a career.
- As of March 2026, he says a voluntary time-for-money exchange is not slavery but a beauty of capitalism where both parties expect to be better off; his content aims to equip people with skills so they get more per unit of traded time.
- As of March 2026, he claims his neighbor owns Panda Express, which did roughly $3.7 billion in revenue at about a 27% net margin, giving the owner about $935 million in personal (not investment) income; he can only make big bets because of ~45 years of regular cash flow. (Unverified third-party financial claim.)
- As of March 2026, he asserts you can build something very big in about 5 to 7 years, but most people spend those years "reliving the same 30 days over and over," jumping from thing to thing and never paying down their "ignorance tax."
- The interviewer (a caller, not Alex) recounts friends who put life savings into crypto after hearing of a "friend of a friend" who profited, and lost $10,000–$40,000; this is his anecdote, not Alex's claim.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Making money comes from investing and investing is the last thing you do, not the first thing you do."

> "The people who have the most money typically have tremendously high incomes and it's because of the excess of cash flow from that income are they able now to make big swings with riskier bets that sometimes pay off and sometimes don't."

> "The more it feels like luck is usually where you should have your first red flag."

> "If you control all the variables then you can predict the outcome... the greater number of variables we control, the greater influence we have over the outcome."

> "This is your life savings. Would you put it on black at the casino? Probably not. This is really not that different than that. Except at the casino, you have odds at least."

> "I prefer to think about instead of thinking of investments and active income, I think of it just money per unit of time."

> "Never sell your time. It's like, okay, well if someone gave you a billion dollars for an hour would you not sell that? I would. So it's a question of how much is your time worth?"

> "It's a voluntary exchange and if you don't think the price is worth it then don't make the trade. And that's one of the beauties of capitalism is it's two parties both saying they'll be better off."

> "You can build something very big in about 5 to seven years. And the problem is that I think most people spend that same 5 to seven years reliving the same 30 days over and over again, jumping from thing to thing to thing and never actually getting the root set so that they can pay down their ignorance tax of not knowing enough."
