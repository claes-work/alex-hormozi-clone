# How to Become Better at Long-Term Thinking

/ L2 · @MoreMozi · 2026-02-15 · [watch](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OljNyzzVf7c)

Solo Alex answering an interviewer's opening question (podcast clip; second speaker asks
the setup question only — all substantive content is Alex, first person).

## Summary

Alex reframes long-term thinking as a practical problem of *what to do in the meantime*,
not a virtue of patience. He argues "be patient" is a terrible directive because nobody
knows what to *do* with it; experts don't have more impulse control, they invent more ways
to win between now and the payoff. His method: chunk a long project (treadmill / marathon)
into manageable segments, and engineer frequent "mini-reinforcers" (small wins along the
way) that keep you fed while a slow "long loop of reinforcement" pays off at the end. He
adds that more time invested in a project (not merely more elapsed time) improves quality
because it gives creative solutions room to arrive — citing his year-planned Prestige Labs
launch as his best-ever launch versus impatient 12-week launches. He closes on the cost of
mastery: doubling output can take 5x the work and tripling it 10x (diminishing returns on
input), but the last increments of improvement carry outsized value (Olympic-margin
example), which is why he's willing to grind years for a top-tier business book.

## Key claims (2026-02-15, paraphrased)

- **"Patience" is a bad instruction because it isn't active.** Nobody knows what to *do*
  when told to be patient. He's "patient" for his 10-year goals precisely by staying active
  on other things (like this podcast) while time elapses.
- **Experts don't have more impulse control — they find more ways to win in the meantime.**
  Reframing self-control as a design problem, not a willpower trait.
- ★ **The method: chunk + mini-reinforcers + long loop.** Break a long effort into
  manageable segments (treadmill: "only five more five-minute chunks"; marathon: not "run
  26 miles" but "how do I break this into a chunk I can manage?"), and deliberately create
  frequent small victories along the way (a rewritten chapter, a visual that nails a
  concept) so you're reinforced continuously — the "long loop of reinforcement" pays out at
  the end, but the mini-reinforcers sustain you through it.
- **More time *invested* (not just elapsed) raises quality**, partly because a longer
  working window gives creative solutions time to surface for the problems you're chewing on.
- **Planning horizon beats speed for big launches.** His best launch ever (Prestige Labs)
  was planned for a year — contingencies, staffing, beta + a 5x-bigger beta, automated
  inventory, payout timing, support-to-ticket ratios, supply chain for 10–20x month-over-
  month scaling — versus impatient "let's do this in 12 weeks" launches. "That doesn't
  happen in a week."
- **You can't shortcut the work; many variables must be studied.** Invokes behavioral
  psychologist B.F. Skinner ("many variables exist and therefore many variables must be
  studied") against the appeal of idioms/shortcuts for complex outcomes.
- **Diminishing returns on input, outsized returns on the last increments.** Doubling
  output may cost ~5x the work, tripling ~10x. Yet the marginal improvements at the top
  matter most: an Olympic sprinter trains four years to shave a tenth of a second, but
  first vs. second place is "everything." A beginner improves faster than an elite, but the
  elite's last four years of gains are what matter.
- **Applied to his own goal:** he wants to write books that land in the canon ("the five
  books you have to read"), which is why he'll grind years to make the *first* book, not the
  third, the one people name.

## Overlap / distinctiveness

Overlaps with the existing "longest time horizon wins / delayed gratification / endure"
thread ([[../topics/mindset/discipline-and-decision-making]]) — the time-horizon and
sacrifice framing is familiar. **Distinct here:** the reframe that *patience is not active*
and the concrete operating method — **win in the meantime via chunking + engineered
mini-reinforcers atop a long reinforcement loop** — plus the diminishing-returns-of-input /
outsized-value-of-last-increments pairing (Olympic margin) as the case for enduring the
grind. The "more time invested improves quality because creative solutions need time" point
is also a sharper articulation than prior pages.
