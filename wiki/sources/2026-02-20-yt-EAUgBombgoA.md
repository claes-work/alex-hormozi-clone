# How I Eliminate Distractions

## Summary

In this short interview clip, Alex Hormozi walks an interviewer through the concrete
environment-design system he uses to eliminate distractions and hit "peak productivity per
unit of time." His thesis: most productivity hacks reduce to "remove everything that isn't
work." He describes his office setup (no outside light, minimized sound, earplugs plus
headphones for true silence), a phone-blocking app that kills all notifications — texts,
calls, Slack — combined with physically relocating the phone across the building so
unblocking it takes effort, and a kitchen timer set to how long he expects a task to take.
He defines productivity as output per unit of time, treats short breaks (a 5–10 minute walk
outside for natural light) as productive because they raise net output over a long day, and
reframes the timer experience positively (surprise at how little time has passed = "I have
so much time"). He resists the "superhuman willpower" narrative, framing behavior change as
overcoming inertia via carrots and sticks, and closes with personal backstory — quitting his
job as the hardest decision of his life, calling himself risk-averse, graduating from
Vanderbilt in three years — plus a promo for the acquisition.com/roadmap lead magnet. A
second speaker (interviewer) asks the questions and pushes back; only Alex-attributed
material is captured below.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) The vast majority of productivity hacks boil down to one principle: remove everything that isn't work — "anything that is not the work distracts you from the work."
- ★ (2026-02-20, paraphrase) His office environment is deliberately stripped: no outside light, minimized sound, and he wears earplugs plus headphones for true silence (no music while working).
- ★ (2026-02-20, paraphrase) He uses a phone-blocking app that makes the phone a "dead element" — it blocks everything, not just social media: texts, phone calls, Slacks. To unblock it he must physically walk across the building, so the friction of retrieving it is intentional.
- ★ (2026-02-20, paraphrase) He sets a kitchen timer for how long he expects a task to last; with nothing else to do, this produces his peak productivity per unit of time.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) Productivity has two components — a temporal component and an output component — so he defines productivity as maximizing output per unit of time.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) Few people actually work faster; the gain comes from not working when you want to be working. Deleting non-work time is the real lever.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) He distinguishes distraction from rest at a micro/macro level: a 5–10 minute walk (outside, for natural light) after ~45–60 minutes of work is "productive rest" because it raises net output over a long day — you can work 12 productive hours instead of 8, or get more done in the same 12.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) He is not distracted by watching the clock; when he checks the timer and little time has passed, he reads it positively ("I have so much time" / "I have six more") rather than as pressure.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) On whether it's "science or just you": he grants there is decent data supporting distraction removal, but tells people to do whatever works for them — light distracts him personally, but if greenery/natural light helps you, do that.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) He rejects the "superhuman willpower" framing. People don't change because they're more rewarded by their current path than by the new one — "carrots and sticks": enough carrot of staying the same and enough stick of others' judgment keeps them stuck; change is just overcoming inertia.
- ★ (2026-02-20, paraphrase, self-reported bio) Quitting his job remains to this day the hardest decision of his life; he says he is actually very risk-averse and only did it because he was so miserable — had he been at even a slightly better job he'd probably never have become an entrepreneur.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase, self-reported bio) He claims no high predisposition toward entrepreneurship: he didn't start businesses young, did well in school, studied hard, got good grades, graduated from Vanderbilt in three years, then took a good job on paper; only after ~two years did he decide he'd rather take a shot than go to business school.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase) He can take bigger bets now only because he has more — losing isn't the end of the world; at the time, quitting felt like risking everything.
- ★ (2026-02-20, paraphrase, workout habit) While lifting he listens to the same ~20–30 songs he's had for ~15 years — Linkin Park, 50 Cent (the *Get Rich or Die Tryin'* album), Green Day, Third Eye Blind — a shuffle list of about a hundred; he wants a good song specifically when about to hit a work set.
- (2026-02-20, paraphrase, promo) Closing CTA promotes a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ (broken down by eight business functions — the constraint, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate), at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I eliminate it by eliminating it. And that sounds ridiculous, but like I think the vast majority of productivity hacks can get boiled down to remove everything that isn't work."

> "There's a great app that you can use that like blocks your phone so like nothing can get through. So including text, phone calls, Slacks, everything. Not just social media, like it's a dead element essentially. And you have to physically move across the building, which is how I do it for mine, to un-mess-up your phone."

> "I just set a kitchen timer for how long I expect a task to last, that is when I'm like peak productivity per unit of time. Because there's nothing else to do."

> "Maximizing output per unit of time is productivity as I define it."

> "For the most part it's just deleting the things that aren't work, cuz few people actually work faster. You just don't work when you want to be working."

> "I don't want to pretend like I have some superhuman willpower... I think of everything in carrots and sticks. It's like they have enough stick of other people judging and enough carrot of staying the same that they don't change. They just have to overcome inertia."

> "When I quit my job, which was still to this day the hardest decision I've ever had to make in my whole life, because believe it or not, I'm actually very risk-averse... it was because I was so miserable. It was the only reason I was able to do it."
