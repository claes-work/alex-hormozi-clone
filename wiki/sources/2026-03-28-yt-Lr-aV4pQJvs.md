# Helping a Coaching Business Scale

## Summary
In a live workshop Q&A, Alex Hormozi coaches a Norwegian business owner whose coaching/marketing-course business has been stuck at ~$1M revenue (50% profit) for ten years across several ventures. Hormozi reframes the owner's "focus" problem: the three offerings (a $3K course, a group-coaching "mastermind," and a $25K VIP) are one business's products, not separate businesses. He diagnoses the constraint as demand/lead flow, prescribes running the twice-a-year launch four times a year, and argues the real ad ceiling is creative quality, not Norway's small market. He also introduces the barbell investing strategy for entrepreneurs.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues entrepreneurs should invest via a "barbell strategy": reinvest every possible dollar into the active business they control and know best (highest returns), and only put genuine surplus into fully passive assets, accepting a lower return there.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that an investment is only passive if you can forget about it for a year or two; the moment you start hedging or monitoring it, it is no longer passive.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes multiple products sold to one avatar (course, mastermind, VIP upsell) as a single business, not multiple businesses — the owner's felt "focus" problem is over-expansion of avatar/focus plus likely underpricing.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the owner is demand-constrained (twice the leads would roughly double revenue), so the fix is more lead flow, not more products.
- As of March 2026, Alex's prescription to double the business is to run the $3K-course launch four times a year (once a quarter) instead of twice, marketing more to cold traffic while hitting the warm list only twice to avoid burnout.
- As of March 2026, Alex maintains an ad-spend ceiling is solved by a superior offer, conversion optimization, or better ads/creative — never by the market's size; rising ad costs are "a bad ads thing," not a Norway thing.
- As of March 2026, Alex cites the Old Spice "man on a horse" campaign as the hypothetical extreme of advertising, claiming Old Spice went from ~10-20% to over 70% of the men's soap market share off essentially one ad. (Alex's recollected figures — unverified.)
- As of March 2026, Alex argues getting good at organic content makes ads easy, because organic must earn reach while ads pay for it; he says some of his highest-converting acquisition.com ads are just top organic videos with a five-second call-to-action appended.
- Caller claims (unverified): he is from Norway, has held ~$1M revenue for 10 years, runs at 50% profit, sells to Norwegian customers, only uses ads plus two launches a year, and finds his content "mediocre."

## Notable verbatim quotes
