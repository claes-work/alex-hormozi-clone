# How to Stop Feeling Behind in Your Business

## Summary
In this event Q&A clip, Alex Hormozi responds to a female founder who feels she is moving "too slow." Her previous venture was a $32M grant-funded, venture-style startup she scaled from 2 to 133 staff in 18 months; she now bootstraps a new business helping career women productize themselves. Hormozi reframes her sense of being behind as an artifact of comparing a bootstrapped company to her prior externally-funded one, and lays out how every business incurs some form of "debt" (financial vs. technical/leadership/management), how bootstrapped businesses grow slower by nature, and how hiring timing is fundamentally a risk-tolerance and consumption-vs-investment decision.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims he has yet to meet an entrepreneur who says they are going too slow — nearly everyone feels they are going too fast — and treats the caller's "too slow" feeling as unusual but normal.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues entrepreneurs should focus on inputs, not outputs: revenue and profit are outputs that "occur" as a result of inputs, so the diagnostic question is which inputs are not happening fast enough.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims every business on Earth incurs debt from day one; only the type differs. Bootstrapped firms take on leadership, management, and technical debt; venture-backed firms take on financial debt but can buy the CRM, exec team, and tech stack on day one — trading dilution or repayment for less technical/leadership debt.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes the bootstrapped cycle: the founder works "double time," doing two or three jobs to generate cash from the savings of not paying someone, then affording the hire that levels them up to a higher-paying job to take over.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims bootstrapped businesses take longer to grow but are "the most fundamentally sound" long-term.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the leverage he now enjoys is funding his own new businesses himself — starting like a venture-backed company with leadership and tooling in place, without diluting, so the company owes him money but he owns it.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames hire timing as a risk question, citing his tweet "your lifestyle is your competitor's opportunity" — required distributions are money that can't be reinvested; a competitor without a family who reinvests everything will beat you, but this only matters in network-effect / race-to-the-top businesses (rare), not e.g. a dry cleaner.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the entrepreneur's dilemma as consumption versus investment — hiring drags growth forward at the cost of the founder's own paycheck, and the right timing depends on the individual's risk tolerance.
- Caller claim (unverified): she built a prior early-childhood-education shared-services startup, funded to $32M and scaled from 2 to 133 staff in 18 months; it was a grant-funded public-private partnership with social outcomes and no revenue/profit targets, which she left because she was not the owner.
