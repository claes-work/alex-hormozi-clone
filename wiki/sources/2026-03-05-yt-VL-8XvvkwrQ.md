# How to Not Lose Money in a New Opportunity

## Summary
Short solo clip on risk management for entering a new venture or opportunity. Alex's core argument: **if you can't articulate how you could lose money on something, you will lose it** — because your inability to name the downside is just an index of how little you know. When an opportunity looks flawless ("there's no way I can lose money on this"), that feeling is the warning sign. His method: (1) figure out your downside and *all* the different ways it can occur, (2) attach a reasonable likelihood to each, and (3) **multiply that likelihood by three**, to correct for the entrepreneur's built-in optimism ("rose-colored glasses"). He frames optimism as a double-edged quintessential entrepreneurial trait: you *need* it to start anything, but you can't carry that same optimism into *every other* opportunity that comes along, because that gets you "split between multiple things" and loses your money. Implicitly a focus / opportunity-cost argument dressed as risk analysis. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (100M scaling roadmap, 10 growth stages, book-a-call to Vegas).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, if you don't know how you can lose money on something, you will lose it — inability to name the downside is "an index of how little you know."
- As of March 2026, an opportunity that looks amazing and feels impossible to lose on is precisely the one you'll "lose your ass" on, because the flawless appearance reflects your ignorance, not the deal's safety.
- As of March 2026, his risk process: identify the downside and all the ways it can occur, attach a reasonable likelihood to each, and then multiply that likelihood by three to offset optimistic bias.
- As of March 2026, optimism is a quintessential, necessary entrepreneurial trait — you must have it to start — but it becomes a liability when applied to every subsequent opportunity, because it splits you across multiple things and loses money.
