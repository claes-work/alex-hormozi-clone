# How to Progress Faster Than 99% of People

## Summary

In this conversational clip, Alex Hormozi walks a co-host through his habit of "just defining terms" — what he says people mistake for him "liking philosophy." He builds a chain of operational definitions: **learning** = same condition, new behavior; **intelligence** = the *rate* of learning (how fast you change your behavior); and because rate is trainable, he argues you can effectively control "how intelligent" you are by changing your behavior faster — which is the video's progress thesis. He extends the method to **resilience** (the interval of time a bad thing affects you) and **discipline**, sparring with the co-host over whether discipline is "action or lack thereof" (the co-host's proposal) versus Alex's own definition: the number of times you can do a punishing behavior without receiving a reward. He closes on why he gets "hate clips" (people disagree on definitions), a defense of "live and let live" against internet-age judgment, and his refusal to follow the conventional life "pattern."

The co-host is an active participant here (he proposes the "discipline = action" definition and pushes back), so speaker attribution matters — only Alex-attributed lines below feed the persona.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed, dated 2026-02-15, paraphrased)

- **Learning is "same condition, new behavior."** You are in the same situation but you change how you act — if you do, you've learned. Example: phone rings, you answer badly; someone tells you to read the script; next call in the same condition you read the script — that's learning. ★ (clean operational framing)
- **Intelligence is the rate of learning.** If one person changes their behavior after two phone calls and another needs five, the two-call person is "more intelligent" by definition. ★
- **You can therefore control how "intelligent" you are** — because if you change your behavior faster, you are more intelligent by that definition. He dismisses IQ as the wrong question: what matters is "how quickly can you change your behavior." ★ (this is the core "progress faster" method — intelligence reframed as a trainable speed-of-behavior-change, not a fixed score)
- **His "philosophy" is really just defining terms.** People say "Alex likes philosophy," but he says he simply prefers to nail down what a word means before arguing about it; the books he'll eventually write would each just be defining a term.
- **Resilience is the interval of time an actual bad thing affects you** — shorter interval, more resilient.
- **Discipline is the number of times you can do a punishing behavior without receiving a reward** (in a row). It is not binary ("disciplined vs. not") but a quantity — "how disciplined are you." (Overlaps with his established discipline thread; the "count of punishing reps without reward" phrasing is the distinctive cut here.)
- **Liking the activity disqualifies it as discipline.** Gym trainers who woke at 5 a.m. to train were "not disciplined" in his view — because they liked it. Discipline is doing things you don't like, for an extended period, without an immediate reward (e.g. eating clean, where the payoff is delayed).
- **He tests his definitions by trying to break them.** If a counterexample fits the word but not the definition (e.g. "you don't need discipline to have sex," which is an action but not disciplined), the definition fails and must be revised. (Method note: definitions must survive attempted counterexamples.)
- **"Hate clips" come from disagreement over definitions**, not substance — someone defines a word differently, and under their definition his statement reads as "bad."
- **All criticism reduces to "he lives his life in a way I would not prefer."** His answer: live and let live — live your life the way you prefer. He notes the internet has made "live and let live" oddly unacceptable.
- **He deliberately rejects the conventional life "pattern"** (wife by 28, kids by 30, sell the business by 35, white picket fence, first divorce, second wife by 40) — he's "in the mix" and not following it.
- **"Exceptional" life = being an exception.** Using his own definition of exceptional, someone living a genuinely rare/odd life is, by definition, an exception.
