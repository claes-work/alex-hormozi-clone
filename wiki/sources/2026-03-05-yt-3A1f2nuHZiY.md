# You're Running Out of Time...

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex attacks the "30 isn't the new 20" mindset, arguing it is "an excuse to take 10 years longer to accomplish the same thing." He gives two objections to the "life is long, you can mess around in your 20s" advice. First, it assumes you won't die — which he calls arrogant, noting that by your 30s you know people from high school who have died (overdoses, shootings, car accidents, brain cancer — all of which happened to his own classmates). Second, it pushes people toward "dead end jobs" — jobs with no direct correlation to personal growth (which he distinguishes from pay: "you can work for free and grow more than anyone else"). He frames the core cost through his **"ignorance debt"** concept: everyone carries a constant debt of what they don't yet know, and the goal is to pay it down as fast as possible; wasting one of your most productive decades leaves you a decade behind your peers. He cites MrBeast — young in years but "old in business terms" — versus someone who starts a business in their mid-40s, to show that time-in-the-ring compounds. For those already 30/40/50 who feel they took the wrong advice, his answer is the "best time to plant a tree" logic: the best time was to start at 10, but "the second best time is to start now"; the alternative is choosing to lose. He argues quitting because you're older rests on a "faulty premise" — you'll always know more in the future than the past, so the only move is to take action with what you know today, and action itself is the first payment on ignorance debt. On the "30-year-old barista": no judgment if you love it, but if you wish you were doing something else, that's the problem — usually rooted in fear, anxiety, or a false label from someone else. His prescription is to define the fear into concrete, measurable actions (e.g. "read two hours a night on a specific subject until I can make a decision within 30 days"). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (the $100M scaling roadmap quiz).
