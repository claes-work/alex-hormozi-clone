# Should I Go to College or Scale My Business?

## Summary
In a live Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a fitness-coaching business owner (roughly $250-320k/year revenue, planning to start medical school) who is torn between pursuing an MD and scaling the business full-time. Hormozi reframes the dilemma: the caller's paralysis stems not from the MD-vs-business tradeoff but from not having decided what they actually want to optimize for. He walks the caller through a regret-minimization test that resolves the decision toward doing the MD.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that the hardest part of any life decision is figuring out what you want; once that is clear, the "how" is the easy part, and easy levers exist for any defined goal (more money → make the number go up; more impact → grow the number of people reached).
- As of March 2026, Alex says people get stuck for years in "I'm not sure what to do" because they are afraid of making a decision and of the path not taken.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames deciding via the etymology of "decide" (Latin *decidere*, "to cut off"): the real question is which future you are willing to cut off.
- As of March 2026, Alex uses a regret-minimization test — "when you're 85 and look back, which one do you regret more?" — as the tool to resolve the choice; the caller answers that not doing the MD would be the bigger regret, and Alex treats that as the decision made.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the MD path as a concrete tradeoff: roughly six years of under-earning in exchange for higher earning potential later and the establishment credibility of the MD title as a brand asset.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the fair comparison is not "me with an MD vs. me without an MD," but "me with an MD but six years behind vs. me with a six-year head start without an MD."
- As of March 2026, Alex states his "biggest pet peeve in the world" is people asking others to solve their life decision when they have not defined what they want to optimize against.
- Caller claims (unverified): the fitness company does ~$250k/year (later stated as declining from ~$320k to ~$270k over the past year); the caller aspires to write books, speak worldwide, and become a preventive-health expert; the caller cites Dr. Mark Hyman and Peter Attia (MD) as models, and notes Andrew Huberman is a PhD, not an MD.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "This decision is so hard for you because you have nothing to optimize against. Like solve my life decision. What do you want? Don't know. Hard to solve."

> "If you said I want to make more money, cool, easy levers to pull, make big number go up, happy face. If you're like, I want to have impact, then it's like, cool, make the number of people that you're interacting with go up, happy face. If it's I'm just not sure what I want to do with my life, that's a you thing. Once you figure that out, figuring out what to do is the hard part. Getting it's the easy part."

> "I think way more people get stuck in the just years of like, I'm not sure what to do, and it's because they're afraid of making a decision. They're afraid of the path not taken."

> "If you think about what deciding is, decidere, it's Latin, which means to cut off. And so the question is, which future do you want to cut off?"

> "When you're 85 and you look back, which one do you regret more?"

> "So, you just have to eat for six years and realize you're not going to make as much, and that's the trade."

> "What's going to matter more is that once you become a doctor, then that's like you're using the establishment as your brand credibility."
