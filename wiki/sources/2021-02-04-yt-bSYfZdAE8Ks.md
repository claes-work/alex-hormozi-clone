# Don't Start a Business Until You Watch This... (2021-02-04)

## Summary

A reflective solo video (recorded in downtown Austin) in which Hormozi shares what he calls his most profound perspective shift of 2020: moving from a CEO/operator mindset fixated on this week/month/quarter to an **investor/shareholder mindset** operating on a five-year time horizon. He argues that extending your time horizon turns time from a threat/liability into an asset that compounds in your favor, dramatically reduces anxiety, and changes which projects and metrics you prioritize. Despite the clickbait title, the "advice before you start a business" is really this mindset lesson plus a practical shift toward building compounding inbound/leading-indicator growth.

## Key claims

- (2021) Hormozi's biggest 2020 lesson: shift from **CEO to investor/shareholder/board-member mindset**, thinking in **years and decades** rather than weeks and months. [paraphrase]
- (2021) His early success "ruined" him: hitting millions in profit year one and **~$20M profit in year two** made that his benchmark, so anything less felt like failure — a "poisonous / toxic" viewpoint. [paraphrase]
- (2021) The framing question: **is time a threat/liability working against me, or an asset working for me?** Extending the time horizon makes compounding work *for* you. [paraphrase]
- (2021) He uses a **five-year** horizon because it's one he can actually believe in; a mentor (who sold a company for ~$3.4B) told him to think in a **100-year timeline**, which he accepts intellectually but doesn't feel "real" yet. [paraphrase]
- (2021) **Fast vs. inevitable**: prefer repeatable actions that, done consistently over a long enough horizon, *inevitably* reach the goal — over chasing fast wins. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Shift toward **inbound / "pool-based" marketing** (creating a water hole people come to) alongside outbound (manual outreach + paid ads). He previously never believed in SEO/inbound; changed his mind over the prior ~12 months because a compounding asset can produce e.g. **3,000 applications a month** after ~5 years of building. [paraphrase]
- (2021) He distinguishes **outbound** (cold call/email/DM, and paid ads — technically still "putting ads out and getting responses") from **inbound/pool-based** marketing. [paraphrase]
- (2021) **Leading indicator of growth**: rather than targeting week-over-week sales, pick the one non-revenue metric that, if tripled, would make it "unreasonable" for the company not to triple. For him that's **number of qualified/sales-accepted inbound applications**. He notes he already had leading indicators for churn (customer activation) but never one for growth. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Increasing sales 5%/month has too many short-term variables; increasing **inbound applications 5%/month** is far more consistent and controllable (add outreach people, add content channels, add paid platforms). [paraphrase]
- (2021) Longer horizons reduce ego-fragility and stress: monthly ups/downs stop affecting him; teams execute better when not judged on one bad week. [paraphrase]
- (2021, self-reported) Gym Launch **profit was cut roughly in half by the pandemic**: ~$13.4M profit the prior year vs. ~$6M this year (books not yet final) — his lowest profit year in a while — but he's unbothered, framing it as a momentary disruption he expects to recover from and surpass within three years. [paraphrase]
- (2021) He's greenlighting a smaller Gym Launch initiative expected to generate **~20 new clients/month** and explicitly told his GM **not to measure ROI until next year** — something he says he'd never have done a year or two earlier. [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Do I see time as a threat? Do I see time as a liability... or is time an asset that is working for me?... If I extend my time horizon, then time stops being a liability and a threat and becomes an asset."

> "I have tried to shift my perspective to wanting to do tasks and actions on a repeated basis that I know that if I repeat them over a long enough time horizon will lead me to inevitably accomplishing my goal."

> "You get so caught in the monthly goals and weekly goals that you forget to make the long-term investments that are important and not urgent."

> "What one thing, if I tripled it in our company... non-sales related... that if I were to triple it, it would be unreasonable to believe that my company would not triple as well."

> "The single greatest constraint that most people have... is they can't do anything consistently."

> "When I can hitch my cart to something that is inevitable, like the passing of time... then you're aligned with forces that will grow you without your permission."

> "Let's not measure the ROI until, you know, next year. And I don't think I would ever have said something like that a year or two ago."
