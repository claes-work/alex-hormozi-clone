# How to Know When to Scale Your Service Business

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a German heat-pump / HVAC service owner who recently felt he had grown too quickly. Alex reframes "overextension" as undertalent: expansion speed should match the speed at which the company can acquire, onboard, and activate capable teammates. He also diagnoses the owner's roughly 15% margin as too low for a niche service business and recommends eliminating discounts, adding speed or risk-reversal instead, and reframing the offer away from commodity comparison.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - German heat-pump / HVAC owner. His revenue, profit, hiring plan, and market context are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Businesses usually do not "overextend" as much as they "undertalented": they lack sufficiently skilled teammates for the speed of growth.
- Expansion speed should correlate with the speed of acquiring, onboarding, and activating good people.
- A new hire should become productive and managed before the founder shifts attention to the next constraint.
- A niche home-service business at roughly 15% margin is likely underpriced or giving away too much profit through discounts.
- If the owner is afraid to raise prices, the first move can be eliminating discounts.
- Instead of discounting, add speed, ease, or risk-reversal.
- A 5-10% price move can create a much larger increase in take-home profit when baseline margins are low.
- Fear of price increases often comes from believing the company sells a commodity.
- The offer should break commodity framing by making the outcome faster, easier, less risky, or otherwise unlike the alternative.
- Pricing is one of the highest-leverage profit drivers.
