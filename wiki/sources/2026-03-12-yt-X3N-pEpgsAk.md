# How to Find the Girl You'll Marry

## Summary
Interview clip (Alex as guest, with an unnamed host — likely Chris Williamson, who supplies the ">>" turns and shares his own recent-breakup dating story). Alex lays out his partner-attraction and partner-selection philosophy in two halves. **First, becoming attractive** reduces to three levers for young men: *get in shape, get rich, don't be a dick.* He argues young guys should get in shape now because "you'll never have more time and less responsibility," and that almost all humans at ideal body weight and normal musculature are physically attractive, so fitness should at minimum not be a self-imposed constraint. "Get rich" is really about *signal*: citing Scott Galloway, he says what attracts is the demonstrated *ability to gain resources* more than the resources themselves — the hungry, ambitious young guy can beat the trust-fund kid, same reason the broke-but-high-upside artist is attractive. "Don't be a dick" means running the bucket of behaviors that make a target partner want to keep fraternizing with you — specifically doing things that are *non-negative to you but beneficial to her.* **Second, selecting and keeping a long-term mate**, Alex reframes mate choice as finding the "fewest common denominators": borrowing Bezos ("focus on what won't change") and Ben Horowitz ("your passions will change"), he says surface-level traits evaporate over a 50-year horizon, so you should select on the handful of durable core behaviors ("behaviors under duress"), not a long list — "a handful of big rocks rather than hundreds of pebbles," ideally three non-negotiables and ignore the rest. He stresses stating your non-negotiables upfront (he did on the first or second date: "I work a lot… I work out… I will not give that up"), and minimizing the gap between performed self and baseline self because a partner "will find out eventually." On keeping the marriage, he describes what he and Leila did: a **"divorce course"** by inversion right after marrying (finding every common divorce cause — money, kids, tactical preferences, communication — and pre-solving them); **"rounding up"** (defaulting to the charitable interpretation: "we're married, I love you"); treating friction as *behavior preferences* rather than proof of not being loved; and Leila's "superpower" of near-instant, permanent responsiveness to feedback. He closes on his work-obsession stance and the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
