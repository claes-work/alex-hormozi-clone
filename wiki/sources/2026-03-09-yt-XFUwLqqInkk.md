# One Solution That Solves 99% of Problems

## Summary
Solo monologue (the `>>` turns are only audience `[laughter]`, all speech is Alex's). Alex's "one solution" is really one mechanism: **skill acquisition through volume plus feedback**, framed as the way to make money and get in shape and thereby shrink most (male) problems. He argues money comes from being *useful*, and the skills you build chasing usefulness (especially sales/conversation) generalize across domains — talking to customers, dating, negotiating a car or house. He claims every centimillionaire/billionaire friend he has started in a **high-volume sales environment** (door-knocking, cold-calling, in-person) for 3–5 years as a faceless rep, because "skill development happens through volume and feedback loops" — the number of repetitions times the improvement between them. He stresses the feedback half most people skip: even the 1% who post content daily for a year usually fail to review their top-10 and bottom-10 outputs, pick one thing that worked, and build a checklist to repeat it. He tells his own origin story — his first-ever sale, with zero training, where he let a woman leave to "get her credit card" and the gym owners laughed at him — to counter the myth he "walked out of the womb closing deals." He recounts developing his warm, high-energy phone-appointment-setting voice ("John? … This is Alex … from Facebook"), the half-giggle-between-words tone that builds rapport and makes objections easier to pivot, and his bouncing on a small $100 trampoline between appointments to manufacture energy (he says he isn't naturally high-energy, just willing to keep working). The **banana story** (a kid biting into an unpeeled banana) grounds his distinction between being *dumb* and being *inexperienced* — beginners just lack reps, so make every mistake as fast as possible without self-judgment. He closes on two mindset frames: work harder not to be "alpha" but to close the gap between your current and ideal self (the gap gets filled with skills, skills come from failed reps), and **"patient with outputs, impatient with inputs"** — patience means finding something to do in the meantime; since you control inputs (action) not outputs (money), use action itself as the coping mechanism for impatience. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims most male problems shrink if you get in shape and make money — problems don't disappear, they just get smaller and you gain resources to handle them.
- As of March 2026, making money requires providing value/utility to an employer or customers; pursuing usefulness builds ancillary, transferable skills (sales/conversation skills carry over to dating, negotiating cars/houses, etc.).
- As of March 2026, every centimillionaire and billionaire friend he has "to a person" started in a high-volume sales environment (door-knocking, cold-calling, in-person) — spending 3–5 years, not months, as a faceless rep having many daily conversations with strangers.
- As of March 2026, his core mechanism: skill development = number of repetitions × improvement between repetitions; brute-forcing volume works only if paired with feedback.
- As of March 2026, the feedback method: review your top-10 and bottom-10 outputs, ask how to do more of the best and less of the worst, pick one thing done well, repeat it in every future attempt, and build a checklist that becomes a skill.
- As of March 2026, most people who "post every day for a year" (already only ~1%) still fail because they skip the review step — posting without examining what to do better is not a feedback loop.
- As of March 2026, he had zero sales training and no concept of the word "sales" at his first sale; his early skill came from Facebook ads being cheap in 2013, which gave him massive rep volume (15–20 in-person consults a day plus phone leads).
- As of March 2026, appointment-setting technique: open with the name as a question ("John?"), keep a warm high-energy tone with half-giggles between words to build rapport, which raises show-up rates and makes objection-handling easier than a neutral tone that leaves "no room for messing up."
- As of March 2026, he says he is not naturally high-energy; he manufactured energy (e.g. bouncing on a ~$100 trampoline between appointments — "try and be in a bad mood on a trampoline") and is characterized by continuing to work rather than innate enthusiasm.
- As of March 2026, there is a big difference between being dumb and being inexperienced (the banana story); beginners just lack reps, so make mistakes as fast as possible, don't judge yourself, and treat each "that didn't work" as walking the minefield toward the goal.
- As of March 2026, he tells young men to work harder not as alpha chest-beating but to shrink the gap between current and ideal self — that gap is filled by skills, skills require failed reps to learn from, and doing more reps in less time shrinks the distance faster.
- As of March 2026, his "patient with outputs, impatient with inputs" frame: patience = figuring out something to do in the meantime; you control inputs (action) not outputs (money that comes afterward); when you feel things should happen faster, use action as your coping mechanism.
