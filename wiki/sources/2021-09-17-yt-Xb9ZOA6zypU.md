# My 7 year journey to making my first million.

## Summary
In this solo, autobiographical video, Alex Hormozi narrates the roughly six-to-twelve-month stretch (late 2016 into 2017) in which he went "all in" on Gym Launch and made his first million dollars in profit. He frames the arc around a DUI head-on collision that pushed him to confront delayed decisions and focus on one business, then fire-sold his other companies, lost nearly everything to a business partner and a payment processor, and clawed his way back through a done-for-you gym-launch model that ultimately pivoted into a licensing/consulting business. He stresses profit over revenue, the "theory of constraints," and the idea that the entrepreneurial journey compounds skills, character traits, and beliefs — the true assets — rather than the businesses themselves. He closes by tracing the explosive month-over-month revenue growth once the consulting product proved itself, attributing the takeoff mostly to word of mouth and product quality rather than marketing. The video doubles as a lesson that "the path is not straight" and that most people want to skip the first five years of skill-building.

## Key claims
- He is "so big on profit rather than revenue" because at the time he was already making a couple million dollars a year across his various businesses but "taking home basically nothing" by focusing on revenue for status.
- He believes in the "theory of constraints": a system grows up to its bottleneck; limiting beliefs are a form of constraint (self-reported framing).
- The Gym Launch done-for-you offer: "I would fill your gym in 30 days for free" — he spent his own money on ads, hotels, food, everything, and kept only the upfront cash he collected; the gym owner kept all afterward-contract value. He asked for a $500 refundable deposit to reserve the date.
- When he and Leila ran a launch, they saw on average ~200 people in 21 days, averaging ~10 sales a day.
- He was running roughly 9–10 "businesses" at once, including six gyms and two agencies, and was "spread wildly too thin"; he was drinking half a bottle of Johnny Walker a night to fall asleep (self-reported).
- After confronting his situation he sold five of the six gyms, shut down the two agencies, and put the sale proceeds into the last remaining gym.
- His partner at the last facility believed Alex had been taking distributions (which Alex says he hadn't), and took what he believed was rightful compensation, draining the rest of the money from the account.
- After winding everything down and writing refund checks to customers, he had $23,000 left — "after selling six businesses and all the stuff."
- A payment processor placed him in an "annual review" and then held ~$100,000 of funds for six months, citing "regular activity" (from the refund checks) and that his single-brick-and-mortar processing was now being used for what looked like a "virtual business."
- He owed a salesman a ~$22,000 commission check on sales for which he had never received the money; he wired the $22,000 (out of his $23,000), leaving himself with $1,000, on the principle of "do right by everyone."
- He had a credit card with a $100,000 limit and launched six gyms starting Dec 26, going into debt at ~$3,300/day (hotel, airfare, food, car, ad spend, commissions for six salesmen).
- With no way to process payments after the cancellation, he found Alex Roy, who specialized in high-risk processing and could get him a $50,000/month limit per processor; Alex stacked multiple processors ($50k, then $100k, then $150k) to "catch the falling plane."
- Refunds eventually reached 35% of all sales — "an astronomical number" — because he had no control over fulfillment; other people fulfilled contracts he had sold, and gym owners told clients to "refund and sign up through me for less."
- Two facilities' refund activity produced a ~$100,000 hit that wiped out his savings; he learned the lesson "you want to control everything end to end."
- The pivot: he wrote "one of the best sales pages of my entire life" in two-to-three days out of desperation, moved weight-loss sales online (all margin minus ad spend), doing ~$1,000/day.
- The consulting pivot began when a gym owner asked to be taught the model; Alex quoted $6,000 to deter him ("the highest number I could possibly imagine"), the owner said yes, then he quoted $8,000 to the next, and every one after said yes — doing $60,000 in sales in a day, then ~$300,000 the next month.
- He called ~32 gyms he had already launched and sold them the consulting program; the average gym that used it did $30,000 in collected cash in the next 30 days.
- He priced the system at ~$16,000, reasoning owners would "make double their money in the first 30 days," while comparable franchise systems charge ~$500,000.
- Monthly revenue after takeoff: ~$100k → $300k → $480k → $780k → $1M → $1.2M → $1.5M → $1.8M → $2.2M, growing "like a rocket."
- He did "three million in profit just in the last four or five months of that year," and "the next year we did 17 million in profit," attributing the jump to switching from a service to media/licensing (code and media) with near-zero cost of reproduction and higher gross margins.
- Central thesis: businesses are "manifestations" of your real assets — the skills, character traits, and beliefs you accumulate; "the amount of money you'll make will be predicted by the value that you provide to the marketplace."
- He cites his book *$100M Offers* (99 cents, launched with one Instagram post) selling ~1,000 copies a day with "no funnels, no ads," as evidence that a good product carries itself.
- He states he went "from zero to 30 million in the next year in revenue" once the next opportunity lined up, crediting accumulated skills.
