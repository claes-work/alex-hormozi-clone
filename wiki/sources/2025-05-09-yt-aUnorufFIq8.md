# How to Get Anyone to Do Anything You Want

## Summary

A solo talk in which Alex lays out his "STAR system" — a five-step diagnostic framework for figuring out why someone didn't do what you asked, and getting them to do it. When a task isn't completed, you walk sequentially through five possible failure points: (1) they didn't know you wanted it done, (2) they didn't know what you wanted (defined as behaviors/outcomes), (3) they didn't know how, (4) they didn't know when it was due, and (5) circumstances/obstacles blocked them — behind which sits the real question of motivation. He argues the framework de-personalizes hard conversations, doubles as a self-diagnostic for business owners, and ties into training ("document, demonstrate, duplicate"), deadlines ("end of day beats end of week"), and a closing riff on questioning the self-imposed "rules" that limit a business.

## Key claims

- Getting others to do what you want is "arguably the most important skill" because it unlocks every other skill every other person has.
- **STAR system** = five sequential diagnostic questions when a task fails:
  1. **Did they know you wanted them to do it?** Solution: write it down. Internal saying: "if it isn't written, it never happened." Following up repeatedly signals a task actually matters.
  2. **Did they know *what* you wanted** — defined in terms of behaviors or outcomes, not vague labels? Vague instructions ("be less creepy," "stop harassing Courtney," "be nice") fail because people don't know what the label means in behavior. Clarity is high-leverage work: 2-3 extra minutes of specificity saves hours.
  3. **Did they know *how* to do it?** Solution: training. "Everything is trainable" — some things just have more sub-skills laddering up ("be kind" = ~500 behaviors; aptitude might be ~20, so attitude is harder to train than aptitude). Diagnose which specific sub-step they can't do.
  4. **Did they know *when* it was due?** Solution: deadlines — but push further: ask how long the actual work takes, and what else is on their plate, to gauge real workload and priority.
  5. **Circumstances/obstacles** — something blocked them (the "I didn't have eggs" problem). This is where you judge the person's *agency* — was the obstacle real, or an ego-protecting excuse?
- Order matters: you run the first four before naming circumstances, because once you mention obstacles everyone blames that one (it protects the ego). The first four give many chances for the failure to *not* be an attack on the person.
- The higher a person's skill/expertise, the vaguer your instructions can be; the lower the skill, the more specific ("chunking it down").
- **Training method: document, demonstrate, duplicate** — you write it down, you do it in front of them, they do it in front of you. (Referenced as detailed in his *$100M Leads* book, ~pp. 207-208, "how to get employees to do anything.")
- Prompt-engineering analogy: if you can't clearly instruct AI (which has more context and tries harder to understand you), you can't instruct a human — "we communicate like monkeys," so be crystal clear.
- **"End of day" as default org speed**: an end-of-day-default organization moves ~7x faster than default; an end-of-month org works at ~1/30th the pace of an end-of-day org. This compounding is part of why "the rich get richer" — doers can genuinely out-work others by orders of magnitude.
- Motivation = "the opposite of deprivation"; to motivate someone is to *temporarily increase the relative value of an outcome* (or decrease the pain of it). Same underlying mechanism as persuasion and sales.
- Reframe a struggling person's current pain as a *skill deficiency on the way to what they said they want* — one of the most motivating moves.
- Replace "leader" with "driver" in your mind — you drive things to get done ("Where is that? How can we do it faster? What can I pull off your plate?").
- Culture/leadership metaphor: you must be "so potent that a drop of you into an ocean of clear water turns the entire water your color"; organizations regress to the mean, so holding the line takes constant energy.
- Closing reframe: be wary of statements you treat as facts ("no one can sell like me") — they exist as truth only in your mind. Convert "no one can" (a dead end) into a *how* question you don't yet know the answer to (solvable). Most self-imposed business rules ("can't charge more than that," "can't pay that much — not industry standard") are arbitrary; if everyone in your industry does it and makes no money, that's a reason *not* to do it.
