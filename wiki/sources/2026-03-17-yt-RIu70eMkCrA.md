# what is self-love?

## Summary
Podcast clip in which Alex is prompted (by a co-host / interviewer, whose turns appear as `>>`) to define self-love. His answer inverts the common therapeutic framing: **self-love is holding yourself to a higher standard than anyone else does — believing in yourself more than anybody else does.** He operationalizes it as believing in "the 85-year-old version of you who's exactly who you want to be," then "ruthlessly" measuring the discrepancy between that ideal and who you are now, and closing it one system and one step at a time. He explicitly **rejects the self-acceptance movement**: you accept *outcomes and circumstances* (the hand you were dealt — height, race, sex), but framing acceptance as "I'm comfortable with who I am and I cannot be better" is "the biggest embodiment of failure" he can imagine — even "egotistical." He distinguishes "I am good enough" (for my current state, given the work I've put in — but I could be better) from "I am good" (a full stop). He shares his own foothold from ~2012–2013, a period when he hated himself: the realization that **he didn't need to *deserve* success to get it** — if he still did the actions (workout, eat a certain way), he could still get the result even as "a shitty person." That felt like a "cheat code" and was his "first solace." He ties this to a broader thesis: hard times are a *requisite* for success, so struggle is the feeling of being on the right path, not the wrong one; and inexperienced operators can't read the signs the first time (he tells a portfolio-CEO story about diagnosing a "missing finance function" — the value of consuming others' content is learning "what the experience feels like" so you can recognize where you are). The back half pivots to **high agency and personal brand**: everyone has a unique brand but conforms to what they think others want, becoming boring and resenting it; the fix is to start at "square zero" and ask "What do I want?" His own wardrobe (functional, comfortable) came from asking what 85-year-old Alex would wear once he was no longer performing the Gym Launch "head of a community of gyms" role. He extends the "who would I date / what would I do if everyone was gone" test to dating and the "stick-to-it muscle" (make each attempt last longer before failing = progress + doing what others won't). He closes on his recurring "the bar has never been set lower" riff — half of Americans are in debt / negative net worth, so just not being overweight and being gainfully employed already separates you — and engages a co-host's "black pill" tangent (LMS: looks/money/status), pushing back that the black-pill worldview "casts an expectation on the universe that it shouldn't be this way," which he likens to the hater whose words only hurt because part of you fears they're right. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
