# Helping 4 Educational Business Owners Build a $1M Business in 25 Minutes

## Summary

Rapid live coaching in which Alex Hormozi works through four education/coaching business owners' scaling problems: a local home-flipping educator wanting to go national, a motocross-training camp operator, a real-estate-agent coach who wants a brand manager, and a sales-coaching/lead-gen agency selling to financial advisers. Alex's recurring themes: dominate a local base before expanding, use content and paid ads as complementary (not either/or) growth levers, never copy an undercutting competitor, and separate one-time "skill" value from ongoing "consumable" value in information/coaching businesses. Owners' situations are context; only Alex's advice below is persona data.

## Key claims (Alex's advice)

- **Conquer a city before a country.** "In order to conquer the world, you have to conquer a country first... before you conquer a country, you got to conquer a city." When armies over-expand they collapse — fortify your base before chasing a national stage.
- **Content is the leverage tool for impact at scale**, not trying to convert every American into a customer. Make content about the business you already run and distribute it free with leverage; this scratches the "impact itch" while the local business funds it.
- **Free the founder's time via the COO to do the work only they can do** (e.g. content), not to abandon the business they built.
- **Typical info/coaching offer structure:** a done-with-you offer at ~$15k–$25k and a total turnkey ("done-for-you") offer at ~$100k. Owners underprice because they only see the ~200 people who reach them by word of mouth; ads expand the addressable pool.
- **~9% of Americans have a $1M+ net worth (incl. home equity)** — more of your market can afford a premium offer than you assume; "they just look older."
- **Content vs. ads pyramid model:** organic content grows the base of the pyramid (more eyeballs, same conversion %, more revenue) — a long-term seed-planting play. Ads keep the base the same but "move the line down," monetizing deeper into the existing audience — a fast lever. Over a 10-year horizon do both in parallel.
- **Paid ads typically give a one-time ~3–5x lift** off an organic baseline (not a guarantee).
- **Your brand is arguably your most important asset — don't outsource it.** Poach a brand manager in-house from a brand you admire (LinkedIn, ChatGPT search to find who's behind it) and offer more money.
- **Core vs. ancillary functions:** every business has attraction, conversion, delivery (core to value creation). IT, recruiting, finance are ancillary — necessary but not core.
- **Switching from warm to cold traffic breaks the sales motion.** Selling straight-to-checkout or one-to-many to a warm audience won't convert the same when ads turn on cold traffic; ads and sales motion must both be good — one great and one weak fails.
- **Never copy an undercutting competitor.** Focus on the customer and you'll never go out of business; let the competitor discover for himself that undercutting isn't profitable.
- **Separate consumables from one-time value** in coaching/education: people wrongly try to build recurring revenue out of front-end skill delivery, but value drops once the customer has the skill. One-time transformations are worth more than ongoing consumables. Ask: if we only sold the ongoing part, what would it be worth? (Much less.) "Big head, long tail" — charge a large upfront and make continuity small enough to be a no-brainer.
- **Sell only the avatars that convert and stick.** Segment on demographics, quantifiables, and behaviors; selling to everyone who "identifies" with the niche loads you with customers who can't pay. Tightening the avatar raises CAC but fixes the retention/activation problem.
- **Reverse-engineer activation from behaviors:** find what activated-and-sticking customers did that churned look-alikes didn't; that becomes the activation process.
- **Team sales training (the "boiler room" model):** drill clients' teams every morning like your own — role-play the part of the script they're struggling with. Break sales training into five parts: intro, disco, offer, objections/looping, and a fifth "hot topic" day. Keep every part of the script crisp or "they'll [mess] up something."
