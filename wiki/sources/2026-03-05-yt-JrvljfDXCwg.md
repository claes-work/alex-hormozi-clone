# Think for Yourself or the World Will Think for You

## Summary
Solo monologue spanning two loosely connected lessons. The first and title lesson is on **high agency** — Alex defines agency as "being able to reason for yourself from the things that you can observe or have observed," and argues that most people pick their identities "off the shelf": they adopt pre-canned belief sets, apparel, and worldviews as a package (his archetypes: the hipster with gauges and beanie, the redneck with flannel and trucker hat) without ever reasoning that they actually agree with all 100% of that package. His prescription is to interrogate *every component* of your life — shoes, shorts, shirt, politics, sports teams, who you marry, what business you start — with two questions: "What do I want, and does this thing help me get it?" Living deliberately rather than by default is **higher cost short-term** (you must actually decide, and you suffer rejection from people whose off-the-shelf beliefs you didn't adopt) but **lower cost long-term** (you don't wake up 10 years later wondering how you got here, mediocre/overweight/in debt/divorced like everyone you copied). Interesting people, he says, combine multiple self-reasoned components into one "box" that makes onlookers double-take (his example: a meathead body dressed like a redneck who quotes ancient philosophers and is extraordinarily wealthy). He ties this directly to marketing: a unique brand must be built "from the bottom up" from your own life experiences and reasoned beliefs — and if you can't explain why you believe what you believe, you believe what someone else told you. He personalizes it with the "little sandals / close-toed shoes" he wore for two years because they served everything he wanted (gym, pool, beach, climbing, restaurants) and he's married so he doesn't care who thinks they look cool. The second lesson (labeled "Number six" — a clip lifted from a longer numbered list) is that **a hard conversation up front can save you millions of dollars and years of life**: opening with Rockefeller's "a friendship founded on business is better than a business founded on friendship," Alex recounts that this was a tough year with **eight lawsuits**, all against friends or friendly acquaintances, mostly business deals from 6–7 years ago that only "came due" once they started making money. His takeaways: don't extend trust that hasn't been earned; for your first 10 years in business put everything in contracts; front-load the discomfort by explicitly discussing exit, death, disagreement, and sale up front while stakes are low. He closes with Sharran's **five levels of friendship** (past → other people/gossip → ideas → doing things together → making/losing/spending money together), naming Acquisition.com president Sharran as a "friendship founded on business" of 5+ years with whom he now does handshake deals but still writes out the big stuff. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex defines agency as being able to reason for yourself from what you can observe, and says being unique requires high agency; at its lowest level it's simply being willing to like a person, show, or movie that most people don't.
- As of March 2026, he argues most people pick their identity "off the shelf" — pre-canned beliefs, apparel, and worldviews adopted as a package (hipster, redneck) without ever confirming they agree with all of it, which is a symptom of never having reasoned for themselves.
- As of March 2026, his method for agency is to approach every component of life (shoes, clothes, politics, sports teams, marriage, business) with two questions: "What do I want, and does this thing help me get it?"
- As of March 2026, he claims interesting people combine multiple self-reasoned components into one package that makes others double-take (e.g., a meathead body, redneck dress, quotes ancient philosophers, extraordinarily wealthy), and that this attracts attention.
- As of March 2026, high agency is higher cost short-term (you must decide, and you suffer rejection) but lower cost long-term (you avoid waking up 10 years later not knowing how you got there), and you should *hope* to find discrepancies between your conclusions and everyone else's because you don't want to end up "unhappy, mediocre, overweight, in debt, and divorced" like them.
- As of March 2026, he ties agency to marketing: a unique brand must be built "from the bottom up" from your own reasoned perspective and life experiences, because no one shares your exact experiences; if you can't explain why you believe what you believe, you believe what someone else told you.
- As of March 2026 (his own example), he wore "little sandals"/close-toed shoes for two years because they served everything he wanted (gym, pool, beach, climbing, restaurants) and dismisses whether they "look cool" because he's married and doesn't care what other men or unavailable women think.
- As of March 2026, he asserts a hard conversation up front can save millions of dollars and years of life, citing Rockefeller: "a friendship founded on business is better than a business founded on friendship."
- As of March 2026, he says you should not extend trust that hasn't been earned; employees get trust by necessity, but a business partnership requires a track record — a large data set of consistent behavior across circumstances (his definition of trust: believing a person will act as they consistently have before).
- As of March 2026 (self-reported), Alex had eight lawsuits that year, mostly early in the year, and *all* were with people who were friends or friendly — business deals from ~6 years ago that only "came due" once they finally started making real money.
- As of March 2026, his prescription: for your first 10 years in business put everything in contracts (handshake deals can come later after a 10-year track record); front-load the discomfort by explicitly discussing what happens if either partner exits, dies, disagrees on deploying money, or wants to sell — do it now at low stakes, not later at high stakes.
- As of March 2026, he frames the contract ask as protecting the relationship: "I want everything in writing so there are no misunderstandings and no unspoken expectations," because the last thing he'd want is to disappoint a partner over an expectation he never knew about or was unwilling to meet.
- As of March 2026, he relays Sharran's five levels of friendship as a litmus test for friendship quality: (1) can only talk about the past, (2) only talks about other people/gossip, (3) can talk about ideas, (4) can do things/execute together, (5) can make, lose, and spend money together — money cuts through the crap because it reveals how someone behaves around a reinforcer.
- As of March 2026, he names Acquisition.com president Sharran as a 5+ year "friendship founded on business" (per the Rockefeller quote) with whom he now does handshake deals on many things but still writes out the really big deals "just so we're on literally the same page."
