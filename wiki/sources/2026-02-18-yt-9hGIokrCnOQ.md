# You Need to Hire A Players

**Source:** [@MoreMozi YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9hGIokrCnOQ) · 2026-02-18 · L2

## Summary

A two-speaker interview clip (Alex + an unnamed interviewer who runs a founder
portfolio). The through-line is why early founders systematically under-weight hiring:
"people **are** the organization," so enterprise value is the collective skill of who
you assemble, not what one owner can personally do. Alex restacks several already-known
frameworks — **aggregate intellectual horsepower**, **barrels vs. ammunition** (Keith
Rabois), **document → demonstrate → duplicate** (the 3Ds), and **"assembling not
building"** — and then spends the back half on the *emotional* mechanics of removing a
bad senior hire, using three fresh analogies.

**Distinctness for dedup:** heavy conceptual overlap with the existing hiring corpus and
with the sibling batch videos (this batch's "Hire Better Talent" / "How to Hire
A-Players"). Almost every *framework* here is already synthesized. What this clip adds
that the others likely don't: (1) a **square-root law** of value creation, (2) an
**ownership-% litmus** (rich founders own small stakes), and (3) a set of **firing-pain
reframe analogies** (bar-tab, marital-affair, rotten-branch). Treat the frameworks as
duplicates; harvest only those three items. Note: the vivid personal hiring stories
(Prada-friend accountant, rap-battle marketing director, "Katy Leon" star-hires-stars)
are the **interviewer's**, NOT Alex's — do not train persona on them.

## Key claims

All Alex-attributed and dated 2026-02-18 unless marked. Paraphrase unless quoted.

- **People are the organization.** An org's potential ceiling is directly correlated with
  the *aggregate* intellectual horsepower of everyone in it. If you're the smartest person
  and could do every job best, the business is capped at one person's horsepower and one
  person's lifetimes — an unbeatable limit no matter how fast you learn. *(Already a
  core wiki theme: aggregate skill set / horsepower.)*
- ★ **Ownership-% litmus for "add horsepower, dilute yourself."** Almost none of the
  world's richest people own 100% of their business; most own small single-digit stakes —
  Jensen Huang ~4% of Nvidia, Bezos ~7–9% of Amazon, Elon ~20% of Tesla. "It takes a lot
  of horses to take a chariot to the moon." More intellectual horsepower raises the peak
  higher and faster, which is worth the dilution. *(Specific ownership figures self-cited
  by Alex, approximate.)*
- **Barrels vs. ammunition (credits Keith Rabois / PayPal mafia).** After product-market
  fit you hire to scale output, assuming throughput rises proportionally (10 → 50 people =
  5x). It doesn't. Most people are *ammunition*; a rare few are *barrels* — the
  rate-limiting cannons that actually determine how many shots get fired. Growth =
  finding/adding more barrels, not stacking ammunition. *(Already in wiki as "barrels vs.
  bullets.")*
- ★ **Square-root law of value creation.** In an organization, roughly the *square root*
  of the headcount produces ~50% of the work/value — e.g. ~10 people out of 100 create
  half the value. Reinforces why finding the few "barrels" dominates.
- **Standards rise; entrepreneurship as a video game.** Each new company clears the prior
  one's levels faster because you carry pattern recognition — but you always hit fresh
  "virgin land" at the next level where you have no template yet. *(Restates known
  standards-rise / pattern-recognition theme.)*
- **Hiring is pattern recognition, tiered by revenue.** You learn what "exceptional" looks
  like per function only by cycling through hires (e.g. three salespeople before you find
  a killer; ~18 months to nail a first great sales manager). Crucially the *archetype
  changes by stage*: a director of sales at $1–10M is a different person from one at
  $10–100M, and so on up. Experienced operators talk about **"assembling"** businesses,
  not building them. *(Assembling / tiered-archetype both already in wiki.)*
- **3Ds training when you don't yet know what "good" looks like: do the job yourself,
  then document → demonstrate → duplicate.** Document every step into a checklist,
  demonstrate it in front of the hire, have them duplicate it in front of you. Nuance:
  when you try to demonstrate you often discover *you don't actually follow your own
  checklist* — so you fix the checklist until it reliably produces the output, then hand
  it over. This is why bootstrap founders end up knowing more (they had to learn it to
  teach it). *(3Ds already in wiki; the "you won't follow your own checklist" nuance is
  the fresh detail.)*
- **Risk/reward rises with seniority of hire.** A senior mis-hire (skills but no culture
  fit, or culture fit but no skills, especially a *leader*) is "an entirely rotten branch"
  — you end up scooping out three-quarters of what sat underneath them because they were
  ineffective or working against the mission.
- ★ **Firing-pain reframe (three analogies).** Removing bad senior hires *feels* wrong but
  is right — the pain is the cost of righting a prior wrong, not evidence the removal is
  bad. (a) **Bar tab:** the guy who orders rounds of shots for everyone then dips — the
  team still has to pay the bill; scooping out the "deadwood" is paying that bill. (b)
  **Marital affair:** confessing an affair is agony for both, which tempts you to think
  telling the truth was wrong — but the *cheating* was the wrong; telling the truth is
  *making it right*. Likewise a hard termination conversation feels wrong but is the
  correction. (c) **Rotten branch / bad ground:** the pain comes because "we planted it on
  bad ground." Businesses stay stuck for years because they avoid these conversations.
- **Alex retells a mentor's decades framing** (context, second-hand): 20s = about the
  destination, 30s = about the journey, 40s = about the company / doing it with people you
  like. Used to motivate caring about *who* you build with.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The potential of an organization is directly correlated with the aggregate intellectual
> horsepower of everyone contained within it." (Alex)

> "It takes a lot of horses to take a chariot to the moon." (Alex)

> "The square root of the number of people in a company generate 50% of the work. So you

> because you're righting a wrong." (Alex, on firing bad senior hires)
