# How to Scale Two Companies at the Same Time

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a commercial construction owner who has also started a faster-growing elevator company. Alex says the elevator company appears to be the better opportunity because it is niche, recurring, and grew quickly despite split attention. He gives the "reasonable" advice of hiring leaders to run the construction company, but says the more aggressive "Alex advice" would be to fire-sale or exit the weaker opportunity quickly and focus fully on the stronger one, because the opportunity cost of attention can exceed the value of a perfect exit.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - commercial construction and elevator-company owner. His revenue, profit, recurring-revenue reasoning, sale timeline, and business mix are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The owner has already started the second business, so the question is now allocation, not whether to start it.
- The elevator company appears more sellable and more attractive because it is niche, recurring, and fast-growing.
- The "reasonable" path is to spend construction profit hiring two or three leaders so the old company can run without the owner.
- The more aggressive Hormozi-style path is to burn down or fire-sale the weaker opportunity if the stronger one is clearly orders of magnitude better.
- Alex says he fire-sold six gyms in 90 days and made in total what he should have made on one, then within six months was doing $1M/month on the next thing.
- He frames the old business as not wasted if it built the skills that make the next opportunity work.
- The opportunity cost of attention can be larger than the money gained from optimizing the exit.
- Other people may call this irrational, but Alex says he prioritizes opportunity cost over social approval.
