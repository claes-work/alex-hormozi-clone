# When should you quit?

## Summary

Live mentoring Q&A. A couple runs two businesses: a real-estate **wholesaling**
business (just over a year old, ~$250k revenue, net-negative, a couple hundred
thousand in debt) and a newer real-estate **investing-coaching** business run by
his partner "Tammy," who has a long track record in real-estate investing. They ask
Alex a single question: how do you decide when to give up on something (the
wholesaling business) versus push through?

Alex gives his **push-vs-pivot heuristic**: keep pushing until a *fundamental
original assumption* about the opportunity is proven wrong; if no original
assumption has been falsified, then everything you're paying is just "the cost of
figuring it out" — and while that cost still exists, push through. He diagnoses this
specific case as *not* a broken opportunity (lots of people make money wholesaling)
but a **lead-generation problem** plus a skills gap. His prescription: keep the
proven investing + coaching arm, and rebuild the wholesaling business **from scratch
with no outside money** — because wholesaling is designed to be started from nothing,
and they "skipped levels" by jetpacking capital onto a skill set that wasn't there.
He closes with the personal-growth argument (you'll always wonder if you could have
done it alone; there's real power in knowing you can do it with nothing) and an
ethics point: it's hard to coach others on a thing you're currently losing money at
(the coach here is clean because she coaches on rentals/syndications, not
wholesaling). Only Alex is treated as persona-relevant; the couple's statements are
context.

## Key claims (2026-02-22, paraphrased unless quoted)

- **Push vs. pivot is a personal decision, but his reasoned rule is:** keep pushing
  until a *fundamental assumption that was originally true is proven wrong.* Ask: what
  information did I originally assume was correct about this opportunity that is no
  longer true? ★ (paraphrase)
- **If no original assumption has been proven wrong, the losses so far are simply "the
  cost of figuring it out" — and as long as that cost still exists, push through it.**
  ★ (paraphrase)
- Distinguish a **broken opportunity** from a **broken execution.** In this case the
  opportunity is fine — "tons of people make money wholesaling" — so it's not a
  no-one-succeeds market; the real problem is a **lead-generation issue.** (paraphrase)
- **Skills-before-money principle:** money is a "jetpack on your existing skill set."
  If you have money but not the skill, the jetpack just makes you crash faster —
  they got the weapon but not the skill to wield it. (paraphrase)
- They **skipped levels**: wholesaling is specifically designed to be started with
  nothing, so starting it with capital (and without the skill) bypassed the very
  learning the model is built to force. (paraphrase)
- **Prescription:** keep the proven real-estate investing arm and the coaching around
  it (she's good at it and has the track record), and rebuild the wholesaling
  business **100% on your own with no money from your partner** — you don't need
  anyone else's help to do it. (paraphrase)
- Rebuilding it yourself matters for a **psychological / respect reason**: even if
  the money-backed version worked, you'd always wonder whether you could have done it
  alone; doing it from nothing changes you and earns self-respect. (paraphrase)
- **There is real power (and reduced fear) in knowing you can do it with nothing.**
  Alex grounds this in having "lost it all before and gotten it back," which is why
  he can operate with little fear — he clarifies this was gym-adjacent, not the exact
  same venture. (paraphrase)
- **Ethics of coaching:** it's hard to justify coaching people on a thing you're
  currently losing money at; the coach here is clean because she coaches on rentals
  and syndications (10 years) rather than on the wholesaling she's struggling with.
  (paraphrase)
- Tactically, for the wholesaling lead-gen fix: use the **many free / outbound
  methods** of getting wholesaling leads; learning outbound will feed the investment
  side, because the best deals you source you keep, and you flip the rest. (paraphrase)
- Their failure had specific causes they name: blindly following an authority
  (a coaching program) "exactly," plus **"ignorance debt"** and choosing too short a
  time horizon — which "crushed it really quick." (context, couple-attributed)
