# The Levels of Wealth

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks the questions; Alex answers — only Alex's words are voice data). Prompted on where wealth stops unlocking new things and hits diminishing returns, Alex lays out **three levels of wealth**: (1) the *"I don't have to worry about any basic needs"* level; (2) the *"I don't have to worry about discretionary spending"* level (within reason — going out for drinks, ordering appetizers and not just the cheapest thing on the menu); and (3) **luxury wealth**, roughly **$100k/month** of spend. The crucial caveat: the levels are individual because the real multiplier is your **savings-vs-spending ratio** — how much of your income you're willing to spend. He and Leila have always lived on **less than 10%** of what they make (now closer to **5%**), because both are genuinely risk-averse; a 5:1 saver (save 80%, spend 20%) needs 5x the income to move from one level to the next, so the same "level" sits at very different income points per person. He contrasts this with people he knows clearing eight figures who still split entrees at dinner ("we literally made more money than this dinner cost while sitting here"). On his own spending, he says he spends almost exclusively on **information and access** — recounting that Leila once offered a man $350k for a single hour so Alex could learn from him — because knowledge has always been his highest-return purchase ("every penny I've made has come from knowledge… if I can save a year of my life for 350 grand, why would I not?"). On what happens to the un-spent 90%: he's a heavy **cash stacker** who likes liquidity so he can "go on big offense" when he wants, and isn't chasing S&P returns because he already makes a lot. He notes that if he wanted a purely passive, chill life he'd put everything into a **dividend ETF** (3–4%/yr plus appreciation), and that qualified dividends are taxed at the lower capital-gains rate (~20–23%). But the next level of the game for him is **taking big bets**: major **media** investments over the next 12–18 months (he notes it costs him ~$1/subscriber/month across his whole media team but he makes more than $1/subscriber/year in AdSense alone, before deal flow), and moving from **minority to majority acquisitions** — buying 100% of companies that fit the portfolio (e.g. an accounting firm he could fold into HoldCo and feed with the conglomerate's buying power). His stated goal is to get to a **$10 billion** portfolio, but the **mission** is to make "real business knowledge available for everyone" — which is why he makes the books, courses, YouTube, shorts and podcasts. Emotionally it resonates because business education is "what set me free" — free from corporate wage slavery, giving him **optionality**, all he says he wants. He positions himself as unusual among business talking heads (vs. Andy Frisella, Ed Mylett, Gary Vaynerchuk) in that he actually came up through paid courses, seminars and coaching programs himself. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
