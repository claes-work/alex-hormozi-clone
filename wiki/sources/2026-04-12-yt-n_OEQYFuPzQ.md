# How to Find Work You'll Actually Stick With

## Summary

Alex argues that passion usually follows competence rather than producing it. Enjoyment
does not prove ability or market demand, and mastery requires repetitive work that may
not feel enjoyable. Because interests change with age, he recommends choosing an
existing, paid market for a current strength to create options later.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says competence can be compared more concretely than passion.
- People often enjoy activities because they became skilled at them, not the reverse.
- Enjoying an activity does not guarantee ability or commercial value.
- Mastery requires monotonous practice that differs from casually enjoying the game.
- Alex self-reports his father advising him to make money first so he could later choose
  freely; he describes that advice as practical and valuable.
- Since passions change across decades, he says demanding one perfect lifelong calling
  creates harmful pressure.
- He recommends looking for existing paid demand at the intersection of current ability
  and a skill one is willing to improve.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Passion usually then comes from competence, not creates competence."

> "Getting good at the game are two very different things."

> "Why don't we be practical now so that we can give ourselves options later?"

## Notes

Reinforces competence-before-passion and practical market selection.
