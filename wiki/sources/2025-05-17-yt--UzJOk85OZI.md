# How To Progress Way Faster Than Anyone Else

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex argues that faster progress comes not from finding the
perfect plan but from having conviction in a sensible plan and sticking to it. He
uses a "paths up a mountain" metaphor: switching strategies repeatedly forces you
back down and costs more time than gritting through the unknown on one path. He then
operationalizes *why* people fail to execute a good plan, breaking it into three
business levers — culture (rules of reinforcement), talent (raising the hiring bar),
and training (translating vague values into concrete behaviors).

## Key claims

- Most of the time you need perfect conviction about your plan more than a perfect
  plan; there are usually multiple sensible paths up the mountain, and none work if
  you keep jumping between them.
- Mission = getting to the top; strategy = how you prioritize resources to get there.
  Ability to execute/stick to the path predicts success far more than the path itself.
- Most people already have a decent idea of what to do (his fitness analogy: eat less
  crap, move more, lift, progressively add weight/reps) — they just don't do it.
- "Fallacy of the perfect pick": people wait endlessly for the perfect choice. This
  happens at every level ($5M/yr owner too); the mountain just gets bigger.
- Personal example: before making more money he was CEO of nine businesses (dental
  agency, chiropractic agency, a fly-out launch business, six gym locations),
  spread too thin to focus, making revenue but no money, stressed out of his mind.
- The hard part is the *not knowing* — figuring out the unknown — and that is where
  people quit, not from lack of hard work. The "bar for excellence has never been so
  low" because competition quits at the first sign of difficulty.
- Execution failure is a surface problem; underneath it is culture + talent (+ training).
- **Culture** = the rules of reinforcement — what gets rewarded and punished.
  Reinforced by (1) who you hire and fire, and (2) which behaviors you do/don't allow.
  Not rewarding good behavior, or ignoring a person 5 min late to a meeting, teaches
  the whole team what's acceptable. Feedback tool: **start, stop, keep**.
- **Talent** = the people doing the behaviors. Most owners' hiring bar is too low;
  raising the quality of people around him always increased his income. Arbitrage:
  the 50th-percentile hire for a role might cost $75k, the 90th-percentile ~$115k —
  ~30% more cost for someone maybe ~5x more effective. Hire "rainmakers."
- Interview tactic: ask what someone does in their off time / what they're obsessed
  with. If their discretionary time isn't spent on the skill the role needs, they'll
  always be moonlighting toward their real passion.
- **Training**: hire for *potential vs. actual* — lower roles skew to potential, higher
  roles to actual; he'll take a generally intelligent, hard-working person over a more
  experienced but less intelligent one. Training = translating big abstract words
  (e.g. "accountability") into concrete observable behaviors so feedback is specific.
- Solar-sales company example: six "pillars of sales leadership" from an off-site
  changed no behavior until "accountability" was broken into concrete daily actions
  (text your team each morning, end-of-week reports, daily kudos, drill strugglers).
- You can beat 99% of people not by being smarter or luckier but by being willing to
  endure pain and uncertainty longer.
