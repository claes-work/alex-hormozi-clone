# How I CRUSH copycats, thieves and competition

## Summary
In this solo video Hormozi lays out a proto-version of his competitive-moats and anti-copycat playbook, drawn from ~22 instances over five years of clients or employees repurposing his licensing material, badmouthing him, and soliciting his clients or staff. He argues the emotional response (taking it as a personal affront) and reactive counter-moves only hurt your own business, since most customers never even notice, and instead offers a set of reframes and actions: copycats are downstream of you and can never exceed their source, so the correct response is to get louder about your own message and never acknowledge them. His throughline is that the durable moat is out-serving everyone — the person who gives the most to customers wins.

## Key claims (2021)
- Predicting others' behavior: you become "astonishingly good" at predicting what people do if you assume they will always act in their own best interest; to get people to do what you want, align their self-interest with yours and harness their selfish nature toward a common goal.
- Over-focusing on copycats backfires: earlier in his career he even changed his business model in response to someone soliciting his clients, and the only result was hurting his own business — the whole conflict was magnified in his head while most customers had no idea it was happening.
- Reframe 1 — "they need me, I do not need them": he is the source of the thing being copied, so copycats are always a subset of him and cannot become bigger than their source.
- Reframe 2: anyone who operates by copying admits they are a follower, not a leader, and therefore cannot lead you.
- Reframe 3: copycats operate from scarcity — they hoard and stay focused on you rather than their customers, which signals to the marketplace that you are the focus, not them.
- Action — never acknowledge them: "Coke never acknowledges Pepsi; the classic never acknowledges the inferior." Speaking a competitor's name elevates them to your level, so you gain nothing and give them credit.
- Action — if they get loud, you get louder about yourself, your solutions, your purpose and mission; channel the angry/nervous energy into marketing harder and selling harder, not at the competitor. Company saying: "beat them in the field."
- The ultimate moat: the person who always gives the most to their customers wins, period. Quotes the HubSpot founder that you don't truly have a customer until someone has offered them a cheaper version and they chose not to take it.
- If you're confident in the value you provide, you shouldn't worry about competitors; if you are worried, double down on customer value.
- Having something worth having means people will try to take it; the only way to own something no one will take is to own something no one wants — so attempted theft is an indicator of desirability and a sign of success.
- Values test for reactions: act only in ways your 80–90-year-old self would be proud of; avoid actions taken out of malice.
- Winning is anticlimactic: he claims to be "22 for 22" against people who tried to take what was his; defeated rivals fade into obscurity with a whimper rather than a dramatic surrender, so there's no victory lap — the reward is just the scoreboard, the business, the clients, and the impact.
