# Give Me 47 minutes, And I'll Fix Your Small Business

## Summary

Solo Hormozi video walking through the risks that keep businesses under $10M/year from
becoming durable, sellable assets. He frames each risk as a "single point of failure"
that turns a company into a high-paying job rather than an asset, and gives concrete
fixes for each: keyman risk, single-channel risk, key-customer risk, and single-vendor
risk. He anchors the lessons in his own history (Gym Launch, Prestige Labs, ALAN, lost
payment processors) and ties the whole thing to the $46.2M sale of his companies to
American Pacific Group in 2021. The consistent solution across all four is redundancy
plus transferring skills into repeatable process and people so the business runs without
any one irreplaceable part.

## Key claims (the fix-your-business frameworks)

- **Keyman risk** = a single person vital to operations whose departure would materially
  shrink or destroy the business's earnings. Its opposite is **redundancy**. If you have
  keyman risk, "you don't have an asset, you have a high-paying job."
- Keyman risk shows up in three primary functional areas — **marketing** (getting
  leads), **sales/conversion** (getting prospects to pay), and **product/goods &
  services** (delivery) — plus a fourth, **operations**, which is "the glue" connecting
  people to functions (he cites Leila's role at Acquisition.com as an operations example).
- Two ways to solve keyman risk: (1) **process + people** — document the actual steps,
  then hand them to people who run them; (2) **incentives** — vesting/equity over 3–5
  years to keep a keyman who isn't you. If it's you, only option 1 is available.
- **The R&D department fix** (how he replaced himself as keyman in Gym Launch delivery):
  identify problems by listening to customers → rank problems → deploy pooled resources
  (spend ~$50K/month, fractionalized across customers paying $3–4K/month) → test ads
  across ~20 representative markets (demographics, market sizes, operator quality) → hand
  off winners to the distribution base. Turning his innovation process into a documented
  system replaced "the person with a system."
- **Skill transfer as the highest-leverage move**: once you learn a valuable skill, the
  most valuable next thing is learning to transfer it to another person. "Assemble
  companies, assemble people, then transfer the skills" to wherever the bottleneck is.
- **The three D's of teaching / delegation**: **Document** (create the checklist of what
  must occur) → **Demonstrate** (do the checklist in front of them, sticking to it) →
  **Duplicate** (they do it in front of you). Break processes into more/fewer steps
  based on the trainee's skill level ("regress" the checklist for lower levels).
- **Litmus test for good delegation**: after you hand off the responsibility and someone
  else does the actions, the department's performance stays neutral or goes up. Handing
  something off without a feedback loop is abdication, not delegation.
- **Supply-constrained small business, 3 options in order**: (1) **raise prices** (least
  work, immediate money — "getting paid more for the same thing"); (2) **increase the
  service ratio** (1-on-1 → 1-on-5/10, or same team serving more clients via
  process/tech/training); (3) **get someone else to do the work** (the only one that
  actually removes keyman risk and shifts you from owning a job to owning an asset).
- **Single-channel risk** = if more than half of your customers or leads come from one
  place. If it stops (ad account banned, platform rules change, domain flagged, organic
  ban), the business is materially hit. Example: a portfolio teeth-whitening chain reliant
  on outbound lost 50–70% of sales overnight when platform messaging rules changed;
  survived because recurring revenue had been built from ~5% to >60% of revenue.
- Volatility scales with size: a 20% channel dip is one salesperson's calendar going 100%
  → 80% for a small business, but 20 of 100 salespeople with empty calendars at scale.
- **Small-business channel advice**: run one primary cold-acquisition channel (outbound,
  affiliates, ads, or organic) **plus organic** — but the organic is not there to acquire
  customers, it's to **amplify** the other channels (a "pulse" so prospects who Google you
  find something). One post a week is enough. This nuances his older "one channel, one
  avatar, one product up to $1M/year" rule.
- **Adding a second channel (bigger business), in order**: (1) shore up **long-term
  nurture** (content + email follow-up; email ROI cited as 36:1 to 42:1); (2) build a
  **referral engine** (ask multiple times, creatively, across the customer journey); then
  (3) **invest in the second channel** using the padding from steps 1–2. A second channel
  can double sales or more, and doubling revenue more than doubles profit, while making
  the business less risky and more valuable.
- **"Don't do two news"**: when launching a new channel, don't also put a new person on
  it — you won't know which "new" is the problem. Put a known-good person on the new
  channel. Better still: transfer the first channel's skill to someone else first
  (without dropping performance), then you become the "old guy" on the new channel.
- Founders should intimately understand the **core economic engine** — how you get
  customers (marketing + sales) and how you deliver value. Non-core functions (accounting,
  HR, payroll) can be outsourced.
- **Key-customer risk** = if any customer (or associated group) leaving tomorrow would
  drop revenue 20%+. 20% is the threshold because at typical 10–40% margins, losing that
  much revenue can wipe out half or all profit. Example: an agency owner with 10 customers
  where Google alone was 70% of revenue.
- **Four fixes for key-customer risk**: (1) **get more minnows** (add many small
  customers so the whale shrinks by percentage); (2) **lock the whale into a long-term
  contract** (3/5/7 years, with a breakup fee ~10–20% of contract value, or required 6–12
  month notice); (3) **get more whales** (his preferred — "you're in the whale business");
  (4) **let the whale swim by** — decline the deal if landing that customer type isn't
  repeatable and would force you to restructure pricing/delivery around a one-off. Story:
  a recruiting firm (~$6–700K/month) was advised to decline a ~$200K/month whale contract
  because it demanded custom terms and there was no reliable channel to acquire more like it.
- **Single-vendor risk** = one outside vendor is responsible for a key business function
  (manufacturing, ads, SEO, dev, payment processing). Three of his own disasters:
  - **Prestige Labs**: a single manufacturer stole ~$400K by asking for earlier and
    earlier prepayment (for discounts) and using the cash to run his own business; ~6
    months of stockouts and lost revenue/reputation during the vendor transition.
  - **ALAN** (software for brick-and-mortar lead automation): 100% of development was in an
    outside dev shop; as sales grew, the shop's monthly dev bill ballooned from ~$100K to
    $200–500K/month. Lesson: keep software/dev in-house so it's at least controllable keyman
    risk.
  - **Payment processors** (twice): lost his only processor at 25 (told in *$100M Offers* —
    "the only problem I couldn't save with sales"); later a shady processor charged $150K to
    let him transfer away. Now he always keeps multiple approved processors that can each
    handle full monthly volume — for redundancy *and* negotiating leverage.
- **Four solutions to key-vendor risk**: (1) **redundancy** ("sometimes redundancy looks
  like waste but is actually insurance the day you need it" — the fire-insurance analogy);
  (2) **mutually assured destruction** (the bigger you are as a % of the vendor's business,
  the more they need you — "owe a bank a million, they own you; owe a billion, you own
  them"); (3) **covenants & terms** (lead time / notice, breakup fee, SLA-linked
  fees/fines that hedge your downside); (4) **acqui-hire / acquisition** to permanently
  align incentives with a critical, well-performing vendor.
- Because he's been burned so many times, he now believes **marketing, sales, and
  delivery should be in-house** — too important to outsource.
- Framing device: fixing these risks is like fixing up a house before selling it — you
  patch the cracks and realize "this house ain't so bad." Making a business sellable makes
  it better whether or not you ever sell. He notes he "compiled the six most common
  mistakes" from talking to 1,000+ business owners in person and made a fuller video.
