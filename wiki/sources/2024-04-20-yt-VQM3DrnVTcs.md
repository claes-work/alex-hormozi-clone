# $100M CEO Explains How to Build A Brand in 2024

## Summary
In this solo presentation, Alex Hormozi lays out his working definition of branding and argues that most marketers' definitions are too vague to act on. He defines branding as "a deliberate pairing of things through an outcome" — pairing your product with an outcome (or with people/experiences the ideal customer already likes) so that the association drives future purchases. He walks through three promised sections: what (good) branding is, why it makes money, and how to start and grow one. He illustrates with Yeti, Harley, Apple, Coca-Cola, the Bud Light / Dylan Mulvaney vs. Shane Gillis/UFC pairings, Nike with LeBron/Tiger, Dolce & Gabbana with Kim Kardashian, and cattle branding as the historical origin. He introduces three brand metrics (influence, direction, reach), contrasts polar brands (Trump) with large-positive brands (Taylor Swift, Mother Teresa, Apple), and closes with a pitch for his free book courses at acquisition.com and his co-ownership of Skool.

## Key claims
- Branding is defined as "a deliberate pairing of things through an outcome"; good branding is the deliberate pairing of your business with good outcomes for your ideal customers.
- Advertising and branding are distinct: advertising is "letting people know about your stuff"; branding is "the pairing that occurs as a result."
- The Bud Light / Dylan Mulvaney campaign was "good advertising but bad branding" for its specific conservative-male base — lots of reach, but a pairing many core customers disliked, which cost sales. Pairing with Shane Gillis and the UFC helped sales recover.
- Whether a pairing is good or bad is objectively measurable for a business by whether it nets more money.
- A strong brand converts a commoditized product (e.g. a $5 white T-shirt) into a premium product; customers pay more to associate themselves with the brand's outcome. He cites a 12x price example ($5 to ~$60).
- Good branding stacks three financial benefits: premium pricing, better advertising response (claims a Nike swoosh could get ~6x the click/buy rate vs. a generic tee at ~0.5%), and customer loyalty / repeat purchase, plus protection from competitors.
- Claims his own results: 7.8M-person audience across platforms in ~40 months, over 1M copies of his last two books sold, and acquisition.com holding-company deals worth hundreds of millions.
- Historical origin of branding is literal cattle branding — a seared symbol that changed human behavior toward the animal.
- Brand has three metrics: influence (likelihood of changing behavior), direction (toward or away), and reach (how many people it changes behavior for).
- Strong brands need not be polar: Trump is polar (strong toward + strong away), while Taylor Swift, Mother Teresa, and Apple are large-positive brands. You do not have to seek controversy to build a strong brand.
- The "bouquet" model: individual products, values, experiences, and people are like scattered flowers; the brand is the deliberate association assembled between them. Narrow the brand by talking about one topic; broaden it by moving to tangential topics.
- Distant/random pairings hurt a brand because the associations are hard to make; most people's brands form by accident, good branding happens on purpose.
- One bad pairing (a "rotten flower," e.g. a DUI) can hurt a brand; the recovery method is not to erase it but to "overwhelm" the audience with more of the stuff they like until the bad pairing shrinks into irrelevance (cites Kanye).
- The product itself does most of the branding after purchase; a premium price demands the product be "dialed," or you get the first purchase but not the repeat ones.
- His own positioning: he deliberately associates himself with business value / making people money, via content and books for small business owners.
- Two supporting Warren Buffett quotes he endorses: pricing power is the single most important decision in evaluating a business; and "it takes 20 years to build a reputation and 5 minutes to ruin it."
