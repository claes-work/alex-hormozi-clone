# He Buys Businesses but Can't Make Them Grow

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a buy-and-hold software acquirer with four portfolio companies, about $12M revenue, and a goal of $90M within five years. The founder asks how to balance growing current companies with acquiring more. Alex says his own answer is delegation: portfolio teams run the businesses while his highest-leverage role is demand generation. For the caller, Alex suggests the acquired companies may simply be too small and founder-limited; better businesses and stronger operators may solve many of the growth problems.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A holding-company owner should spend time on the one activity that makes all other goals easier, then hire for the rest.
- For Alex, the highest-leverage role is demand generation because brand creates talent, deal flow, and other resources.
- Acquisition deal flow is relatively straightforward compared with fixing many small founder-limited businesses.
- Very small acquisitions often have many problems at once, which is why many private-equity firms avoid them.
- A founder who caps at a small size and sells may not have the ambition or ability to grow from small to much larger.
- Buying bigger businesses can reduce some problems because the founders/operators who built them are often stronger.
- Replacing founders is difficult; private equity can destroy businesses when it removes the person who actually knows how to run them.
- Alex says he gave back or sold his shares in most of 24 acquisitions because the operators were not winners.
