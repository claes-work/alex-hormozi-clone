# Why I Don't Go Home for the Holidays

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi explains why he stopped going home for the
holidays: returning to old family and hometown environments pulls him back into
the person he deliberately left to escape. He argues that people from your past
try to "meet you in your past" and reinforce an old identity, and that curating a
"contained and pristine" environment — cutting out people who detract from who you
want to become — is what growth and exceptional achievement require. Explicitly
framed as a message to his younger self ("young mosy") and to viewers who dread
the holidays; he notes it is a refresh of a similar video he made 4–5 years earlier.

## Key claims (his view on family / time / relationships)

- He does not go home for the holidays because "I don't like what going back home
  does for me" — not just family, but friends, places, and environments too.
- He left home because he wanted to become a different person, change his
  environment, and "start clean."
- People from your past "try and meet me in my past and treat me in a way that I
  used to accept" — often indirectly (e.g. an uncle saying "oh you know how you
  are"), which pressures you to accept a label of who you used to be.
- You teach and reinforce others how to treat you; if someone speaks to you in a
  way you don't accept (e.g. "get over here" in a bad tone), you control your
  response, not their words. He models refusing to respond and asking to be spoken
  to differently.
- Saying "I'm working on it" still accepts the other person's frame that you are
  that way; to be a different person you must behave as that person already would.
- People struggle to see others change because change reminds them of the changes
  they've been incapable of making — their own deficiencies and comfort zones.
- Litmus test for navigating holiday conversations: simply tell the truth. State
  facts about why you don't want to attend or be around certain people, rather than
  inventing a BS excuse (which they can overturn).
- Reward vs. punishment asymmetry: reward memories don't decay, but the memory of
  punishment fades over time — so you remember the good of a family/relationship
  and forget the bad, then get pulled back in. He actively cues himself to remember
  why he left.
- Rest/leisure is productive if it nets more output afterward; if you must recover
  *from* your holiday, you lost the benefit. Being home doesn't give others free
  access to your time.
- The wealthiest people he meets have the most "contained and pristine"
  environments; he cites Elon Musk, Jeff Bezos, and Steve Jobs as intolerant of
  low-standard people around them.
- To be exceptional is, by definition, to be an exception; most people will
  pressure you to "get back in line."
- Practical outs: send money for presents instead of attending; don't respond to
  texts from people you don't want to see; set conditions with a host (e.g. don't
  seat me next to / I won't talk to a specific person).
- Cites Epictetus (paraphrased): you can't get angry at a rock — shout at it, it
  stays a rock — so when others get upset, "be the rock": acknowledge them but
  don't change your behavior.
