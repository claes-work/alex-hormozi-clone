# How to Get Hired Anywhere in the World

## Summary

A short `@MoreMozi` clip (opens mid-conversation) on how to get hired at a company you
want to work for. Two speakers: an interviewer/questioner (NOT Alex) and Alex, who
gives the advice. Alex's core tactic: don't approach a company empty-handed — look at
the roles they actually have posted (e.g. a data-entry position), offer to do that
specific job, then use the extra time inside the business to have lunch with people,
build an internal network, and leverage those relationships to acquire more skills and
move toward the work you actually want.

The questioner raises the idea of approaching places that aren't hiring / don't need
help and saying "I'll take on any role." Alex qualifies this with a supply/demand
lens: a company with a big social-media presence gets so many people offering to work
for it that the supply/demand is against you, whereas a low-visibility business (his
example: an HVAC or electrical company) will more likely let you do some work. He
frames the whole thing as arbitrage — "the big arbitrage in life is finding
supply/demand discrepancies in your favor": you supply work, they demand it, but if
too many people supply it, they dictate the terms. The clip ends with his standard CTA
for the free 10-stage zero-to-$100M+ roadmap (acquisition.com/roadmap).

## Key claims (2026-02-21, paraphrased unless quoted; Alex-attributed only)

- To get hired somewhere you want to work, don't go in empty-handed. Look at the jobs
  the company actually has available and offer to do that specific role (his example: a
  data-entry position) rather than pitching a vague "I'll do anything."
- Once you're in the door doing the posted role, use the extra/slack time to have lunch
  with people and build your network *inside* the business. Leverage those
  relationships to gain more skills, move your way up, and learn what you actually want
  to learn.
- Whether to approach a company that isn't hiring / doesn't need help depends on the
  company's visibility. A business with a big social-media presence has so many people
  already asking to work for it (huge supply) that the supply/demand is not in your
  favor — they can dictate their own terms, and if they don't need you, they don't.
- Low-visibility, non-media businesses are a better target: go to an HVAC or electrical
  company and you can probably find some work they'll let you do, because far fewer
  people are lining up to offer it.
- General principle: "the big arbitrage in life is finding supply/demand discrepancies
  that are in your favor." You have a supply of work; they have a demand. When supply is
  scarce relative to demand, you have leverage; when supply is abundant, the buyer
  (employer) dictates terms.
