# Helping a Building Management Agency Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client sells management services to board members of commercially-zoned property owners associations ("HOAs except for your office"): a $1.8M/year business paying him $625k/year for ~90 minutes of work a week, built to 147 associations acquired haphazardly by word of mouth from brokers, developers, and existing association owners. He's been at it since 2003, hit repeated "brick walls" trying to systematize prospecting, and effectively coasted into treating it as an "annuity." He now has a full week of hours free and wants to scale revenue while keeping margins. Alex reframes the whole business as a **center-of-influence (COI) referral play**: the HOA boards are *not* the customer — the brokers and COIs who refer them are. He splits the growth motion into a **farming side** and a **hunting side**. Farming: map the exact process that turned a past referrer into an active one, then install a deliberate communication cadence with existing brokers so you capture 100% of their referrals instead of 10% and stay top-of-mind. Hunting: treat broker acquisition as a funnel with a cost — estimate buildings-per-activated-broker per year, count average attempts needed to activate one, and reverse-engineer what produced the first referral. The core mental shift: what happened *by accident* with the original brokers must be made to happen *on purpose*, by treating the referrer's customer experience as the real product. Alex anchors this with his **Allan/software** story (a Gym-Launch-era lesson): he wrongly thought the SMBs whose leads the software worked were the customer, when the real, low-churn customers were the *agencies* that white-labeled it — agency logos stayed while their SMB clients churned. Chunking the business up to the durable customer transformed retention. He tells the client to build all marketing toward brokers/COIs — their customer journey, activation point, re-engagement cadence, events, reciprocity — and to get aggressive now that a full 40-hour week is available. Ends with the roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes a referral-driven service business: the end-user accounts (here, the HOA/association boards) are not the real customer — the **brokers and centers of influence who refer them are the customer**, and all marketing should target the referrers.
- As of March 2026, Alex splits referral growth into two motions: a **farming side** (deepen and systematize existing referrer relationships) and a **hunting side** (acquire new referrers as a measurable, cost-based funnel).
- As of March 2026, on farming: install a deliberate communication cadence with existing brokers so you're reminded to solicit new business and stay top-of-mind — the goal is to capture 100% of a broker's referrals rather than the ~10% you get by default.
- As of March 2026, on hunting: treat broker acquisition like a funnel — estimate how many buildings an activated broker sends per year (e.g. two or three), how many average attempts it takes to activate one broker, and what it takes to earn that first referral.
- As of March 2026, the core operating principle: what happened "on accident" with your original referrers must be made to happen "on purpose" — be deliberate about the referrer's customer experience and see them as the real customer.
- As of March 2026, Alex's canonical reframing example (Allan/Gym-Launch-era software): he wrongly treated the SMBs whose leads the software worked as customers, when the real customers were the **agencies that white-labeled the product** — SMB clients churned but agencies stayed, so retention was much higher once measured at the agency level.
- As of March 2026, "chunking up" to the durable customer stabilizes retention: individual end-clients (SMBs, or here HOAs) are volatile and churn, but the referrer/aggregator ("that 10 guy") stays roughly constant — "sell one, lose one" — with occasional expansion revenue when one grows.
- As of March 2026, Alex's suggested serve-the-referrer tactics: define the referrer's customer journey and activation point, re-engage them consistently, and run events for them so they "always feel reciprocity toward me."
- As of March 2026, Alex notes commercial constraints matter: because licensed brokers legally cannot take kickbacks, you "have to get really creative" about how you earn their loyalty.
