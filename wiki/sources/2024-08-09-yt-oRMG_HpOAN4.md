# 13 Years of No BS Business Advice in 79 Mins

## Summary
A solo, ~79-minute compilation in which Alex Hormozi walks through 20 numbered business
lessons distilled from 13 years of operating. It opens with his standard credentials
(13 years in business, nine companies sold, last company sold for $46.2M, current

## Key claims
- **Price anchor:** Add something 10-100x more expensive to your menu that you never plan to
  sell; it anchors your core offer up and lets you nudge its price. ~10% of customers ("whales")
  just want the most expensive thing. "The only thing worse than making a $1,000 offer to
  somebody with a $100 budget is making a $100 offer to someone with a $1,000 budget."
- **Advertise far more than feels comfortable.** "We need to be reminded more than we need to
  be taught." Founders get sick of their own advertising long before customers even register it
  (Henry Ford / CMO anecdote: "we haven't started running it yet").
- **The Core Four + Rule of 100:** the only four ways one person can get attention — warm outreach,
  cold outreach, content (one-to-many), paid ads (one-to-many). Baseline daily effort: 100 minutes
  of content, OR 100 outreaches, OR $100/day on ads. Do the "4x4" — four hours a day on the Core Four.
- **Until $100k/month, advertising is your whole focus.** After that, fix the "holes in the bucket"
  (retention, referrals, product) before scaling the front end again — this lets you stair-step.
- **Under $1M/year: one channel, one avatar, one product.** Don't open a second acquisition channel
  until ~$1M/month; Alex didn't open his until $4M/month, and outbound took 12 months to become half
  of revenue.
- **Always start for free** to lower stakes and accumulate proof; free customers pay you back three
  ways — testimonials, referrals, and converting to paid later. He claims he has never seen starting
  free fail to make him more money, and does it "at every level of business."
- **Proof over promise.** 1,000 testimonials beat any grand-slam offer. Four proof levers: recent > old;
  visual (video > photo > text); high volume (screenshot every review across all platforms); and
  capture pain (testimonials that open with pain convert higher). Collect proof at the moment of
  greatest satisfaction; sell at the moment of greatest deprivation.
- **Raising prices almost always makes more money** but you hear more no's. He tests 4-5x price
  differences, not 10-20%. Doubling a price with a 35% drop in conversion still doubled profit and cut
  delivery cost. Not raising 3-6%/year fails to keep up with inflation (a static $100 price from 2017
  = ~$79 in real terms). Cites Warren Buffett / See's Candies raising prices 50 years running.
- **Talk to customers to solve every business problem** (attributed to Paul Graham). Talk to super
  users (roadmap/retention), moderate and low users, and especially people who didn't buy or who
  churned. On a cancel/refund call: get *more* angry about the problem than they are, then ask "what
  would it take for me to make this right?"
- **The CLOSER sales framework:** Clarify why they're there; Label their problem; Overview past pain
  (the "pain cycle" — temporarily increase deprivation); Sell the vacation (not the plane flight —
  sell the destination, a three-point pitch); Explain away concerns (the five objection types:
  circumstance/time, money, decision-maker, or avoidance/fear of mistake); Reinforce the decision
  (the first 24 hours post-purchase determine how the customer judges the business).
- **Before doing anything new, do 10x more of what already works.** He asks portfolio founders "why
  can't we 10x this?" — two of three times the answer is "we could." Whatever blocks the 10x *is* the
  constraint. Warns against the "woman in the red dress" — the deceptive belief that a new thing is
  easier (it only looks easier because you don't yet know its problems). The five stages: uninformed
  optimism → informed pessimism → valley of despair → informed optimism → achievement; most people
  loop the first three forever.
- **Business is stress, full stop:** "Growth is stressful, stagnation is stressful, decline is
  stressful." Categorizing an issue as a "problem" is itself the larger source of stress.
- **Look-back window:** customers judge a purchase by the *most recent* transaction, so the less
  frequently you bill, the longer people stay. Bill for longer durations (annual/prepay). He'll cut
  price ~30% to get 3-6 months upfront for higher LTV. Offering (not defaulting) annual gets ~10-15%
  takers and can double cash flow — enabling a faster cash conversion cycle ("infinite money glitch";
  cites going from $1,000 in Dec 2016 to $4.4M/month in 20 months).
- **Sell sawdust / excess capacity:** monetize assets you already have without adding operational drag
  (Uber and Airbnb are built on other people's sawdust; renting gym space midday). Double/triple-dip
  the same activity (e.g., turn a portfolio intervention into content).
- **Arm your salespeople** with a library of content that overcomes each specific customer concern;
  let content do the selling.
- **Unify sales and advertising** under one leader (CRO — often the founder). Advertising and sales sit
  on one continuum from low- to high-information buyers; sales just fills the holes advertising didn't.
- **Three legs of the stool:** every business needs a head of acquisition, a head of delivery, and a
  head of operations — mapping to the only three ways to raise enterprise value (more customers, worth
  more, less risk). Operations is a vendor to the other two and should not make the big decisions.
- **The person with the highest standard (lowest tolerance) should be in charge** — not tenure,
  likability, or looks.
- **Every hire must raise the average bar of the team they join.** Two filters: (1) am I learning more
  from them in the interview than they are from me? (2) do they raise the team's bar? Interview 10-20,
  not 3. "Your best talent is in your future, not your past."
- **Five reasons someone isn't doing what you want:** (1) didn't know you wanted it, (2) didn't know how,
  (3) didn't know when (no deadline), (4) not motivated, (5) something is blocking them. Attack the
  reason, not the person. ("What if I invest in them and they leave? What if you don't and they stay?")
- **The hardest work is the work you don't know how to do.** Entrepreneurs retreat to "level two"
  problems they can already solve instead of the real priority. The bigger the rock, the bigger the bag
  of money behind it. Learning = "same condition, new behavior"; if your behavior doesn't change, you
  learned nothing.
