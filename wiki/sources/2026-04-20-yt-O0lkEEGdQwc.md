# How to Build an Offer People Actually Say Yes To

## Summary

Alex says strong offers often start by giving away far more than seems reasonable until
the prospect says yes, then trimming back what is not valued. For content and expertise,
he says proof and context carry most of the message; people should use "how I" content
while they are still on the come-up rather than pretending to teach universal "how to"
truths.

## Key claims

- Alex says investors such as Y Combinator increasingly like founders who have lived
  with the target problem for five to ten years.
- He recommends service businesses start with the "kitchen sink" offer: add value until
  prospects say yes, then trim to profitability.
- He says physical products must iterate the product to get a yes, while services can
  add delivery elements first and then remove what buyers do not care about.
- He says his early gym offer improved when he stopped selling commoditized memberships
  and started solving the whole weight-loss problem.
- He says proof matters more than wording because the message is consumed inside the
  speaker's context.
- He says his podcast existed for five years before broad attention arrived, and the
  same messages were consumed differently after objective proof.
- He recommends "how I" over "how to" for people documenting a journey without full
  proof yet.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Start with the kitchen sink."

> "Solve for context."

> "Stick to how I rather than how to."
