# Don't Slow Down When It Gets Easy

## Summary
Solo motivational monologue on his core rule: **"When it's easy, do more. When it's hard, do different."** Alex opens with an origin story — ~3 months into Gym Launch working, newly married, pacing ~$300–400k/month (his most materially transformative income leap, from looking at bankruptcy attorneys six months earlier). At a meetup of ten eight-figure entrepreneurs he was tempted to chase a new shiny thing (a supplement company); instead one attendee warned him that *if the economics really worked, someone bigger/better would copy it with more distribution — so when it gets easy is exactly when you double down and put the blinders on.* He and Leila left with "the devil inside" — the fear of losing everything — and worked around the clock against a **hypothetical enemy** trying to destroy them, so by the time real competitors moved, they were already far ahead.

From there he issues a tactical challenge (**work 30 days straight, 10–12 hours/day**) to prove you won't die, that you're "not made of glass," and that you have the gear. He invokes the Chinese **996** (9am–9pm, 6 days) as the true average bar, and "hard times, hard men." He shares his own current cadence (works more now than ever, ~10–11 days off in the year, a "day off" = fewer than 4 hours worked), dismisses the "mental health people," and says he gets far more fulfillment from "earning his shower" after a 16-hour day than from any pleasure-seeking — including a ritzy $5–10k/night resort vacation he abandoned after one day ("this is beautiful and I don't care… I wish I felt something"). He caveats it isn't anti-rest: the real goal is **maximum total net output over the longest period**, so sleep and mental rest matter, and unplugging however works for you (Netflix, gardening, working out) is fine. He found 7 days/week at 10–12 hours beat 18-hour days; a triple-shift gear he only needs ~once a year for 6–8 weeks. Closes with his **obsession with speed / "micro speed, macro patience"**: break tasks into real hours/minutes, use real (not arbitrary) deadlines, then relentlessly ask how to make it take less time (automate, delegate, delete). The Pac-Man metaphor: reject the timeline but accept the skills — pull requisite skills toward you as fast as possible (do a year of algebra in a week). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
