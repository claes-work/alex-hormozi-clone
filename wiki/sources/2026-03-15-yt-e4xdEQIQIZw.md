# Helping a MedSpa Business Owner Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A physician-owner of a wellness / weight-loss / men's health med-spa (single location, ~$3M revenue) wants to reach $20M across multiple locations in 2–5 years. She opens by blaming "bandwidth," then "sales," then a "bookkeeping issue" with EBITDA. Alex runs his diagnostic drill-down to locate the *real* constraint. He cuts through the accounting fog with the simplest possible profit test — "at the beginning of the month versus the end of the month, how much did the bank account go up?" — and establishes the true numbers: ~$3M top line, ~$700k business profit, plus a ~$200k medical-director salary/draws → roughly **$1M/year in seller discretionary earnings (~30% margin)**. That healthy margin lets him dismiss money and sales as the binding constraint: "if you made an extra $300,000 in profit, would that change your life?" — she says not really, proving sales isn't the constraint. The real constraint is **her time**: she spends ~80% of her time being a physician (in the chair) and only ~20% being an owner. His prescription is **manpower** — hire another physician for ~$200k out of the ~$1M she earns, buy back all that clinical time, and redeploy it to run the business better. He surfaces her real objection (waiting for a "unicorn" doctor who fits the culture and shows empathy) and reframes it: weight-loss medicine is easy to hire for, she stays the owner and culture-keeper, so "it's not hard to find — I just have to do it." He also names the pattern of a scattered operator with "a lot of half-built bridges": the fix is *removing* time-sinks before building anything new. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses the binding constraint before prescribing: he separates the owner's stated problems ("bandwidth," "sales," "bookkeeping") from the actual one, which turns out to be her time.
- As of March 2026, to cut through accounting/EBITDA confusion he uses the simplest real-profit test: how much the bank account grew from the start to the end of the month.
- As of March 2026, he treats a business making ~$3M top line and ~$700k profit — plus ~$200k in owner salary/draws, so ~$1M/year in seller discretionary earnings, ~30% margins — as financially healthy, meaning money is not the constraint.
- As of March 2026, he tests whether sales is the real constraint with a "would it change your life?" question: since an extra ~$300k in profit wouldn't change her life, fixing sales is not the constraint (even though nearly every medical company could improve its sales process).
- As of March 2026, the constraint for this owner is time/manpower: ~80% of her time goes to being a physician, ~20% to being a business owner, so the highest-use fix is to stop doing the low-value work.
- As of March 2026, the prescription is to hire a replacement physician (~$200k/year) out of the owner's ~$1M earnings to buy back her clinical time, leaving ~$700k of profit and freeing her to run and grow the business.
- As of March 2026, he reframes the "I need a unicorn" objection: weight-loss medicine is easy to hire for; the owner remains the owner and culture-keeper, so the hire is genuinely available — "it's not hard to find, I just have to do it."
- As of March 2026, a scattered operator has "a lot of half-built bridges"; the move is to *remove* time-consuming activities rather than start building more.
- As of March 2026, he notes that if the same business had only ~$400k profit (thin margins), you couldn't yet afford to hire the replacement and would have to fix other problems first — the healthy margin is what makes the manpower fix available.
