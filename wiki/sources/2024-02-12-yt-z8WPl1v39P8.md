# Alex Hormozi Explains Why You NEED To Build A Brand in 2024

## Summary

In this podcast appearance, Alex Hormozi recounts how his biggest mistake with Prestige Labs (his supplement company) was treating the business purely transactionally — leaning on his hardcore sales skill set instead of building a brand. He uses the story of a friend who stayed a First Form athlete despite Alex offering better products and higher commissions to illustrate that a weak or negative brand costs you deals no amount of direct-response tactics can win. His belief evolved from "brand and direct response are equally important" to "brand over everything," which is why he built Acquisition.com deliberately on content — the one customer-acquisition channel he had never used. He lays out his "give give give until they ask" content philosophy and the jab-jab-jab-right-hook ratio, arguing that growing platforms give nothing but value, and that brand-built customers are more bought-in and share your values.

## Key claims

- His biggest Prestige Labs mistake was treating the supplement business purely transactionally — building the whole brand off transactional sales and hardcore closing — which he says is the norm for the supplement industry (and "if everyone else is doing it it's probably not the best idea").
- Being elite at sales gave him a distortion of reality: "everything looks like a sales hammer when you're good at sales." He couldn't understand why a friend kept selling First Form over Prestige Labs despite Alex's better products and higher commissions.
- The real reason the friend didn't switch: Alex had no brand (or a negative brand), and the friend didn't want to associate his own brand with Alex's weaker one. The thing Alex missed was "the soft stuff" — the brand stuff.
- His belief progressed brick by brick: first "brand and direct response are equally important," then "brand over [everything] because if the brand's right everything else follows."
- There are eight ways to get customers: four one person can do (reach out to friends/family individually, reach out to strangers individually, post content, run paid ads) and four leveraging other people (referrals, affiliates, agencies, employees). He had built businesses off every one except content — so he built Acquisition.com on content deliberately.
- Only through building content did he understand that "it was all about brand."
- Content philosophy: give give give until they ask; if no one's asking, keep giving.
- Platform ad-to-content ratios reveal the strategy: TV is ~47 min content to 13 min ads, Facebook is roughly three feed posts to one ad — the maintenance ratio for a mature platform. Growing platforms (e.g. early TikTok) ran no ads, all content. So growth = all give.
- Direct-response "secrets the 1% don't want you to know" are nonsense: "there is no secret, but there are things that the 99% refuse to believe."
- Brand-building is slower — you "eat it" for one to three years while finding your voice and values — but customers who come after consuming your value first are more bought-in and share your values, forming a base that compounds ("too little for you to notice until it becomes too big for you to ignore").
