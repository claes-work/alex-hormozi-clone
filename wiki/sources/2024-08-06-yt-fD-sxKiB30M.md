# How the top 1% make their money

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video contrasting real estate (where "the majority of millionaires are made") with private equity (where "the majority of billionaires are made") to explain how the ultra-wealthy compound value. He teaches that a business's worth is `multiple × EBITDA`, that the multiple is a direct function of how reliable and growing the profit is, and that flipping risks into "pillars of value" can turn a near-worthless business into a $40M asset in 12–24 months. He closes with six multiple levers (debt capacity, organic growth, categorization, size premium, age) and a strong argument for long-term focus over chasing new opportunity vehicles.

## Key claims

- The majority of millionaires are made in real estate; the majority of billionaires are made in private equity. (paraphrase)
- Real estate makes money two ways: appreciation (natural or forced) and monthly cash flow from tenants. It's a simpler, more reliable business model, but has an upside ceiling — you won't get a 100x deal in real estate; you can in private equity "all the time." (paraphrase)
- Real estate's reliability depends on continued population growth; he cites Japan's declining real-estate market (falling population) as the counterexample and calls the population assumption "a big ass risk." (paraphrase)
- A business's value ≈ multiple × EBITDA (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization). The two variables an owner can influence are the multiple and the EBITDA. (paraphrase)
- The multiple is a direct correlation to risk — specifically how likely the business keeps making money if the owner leaves. Guaranteed continuation → high multiple; "might burn down in 6 months" → ~0.5x or effectively zero. (paraphrase)
- At acquisition.com they systematically list a business's risks/negatives and "flip the risks into pillars of value," which counts twice: removing a discount and adding to the multiple. (paraphrase)
- A $3M-revenue / $1M-profit business is nearly unsellable to institutions (too small, keyman risk) but with a few moves (hire CEO/COO/marketing director, fix pricing, spend more on marketing) can reach $12M revenue / $5M profit and an 8x multiple = a $40M business in 12–24 months. (paraphrase)
- Two identical $10M-revenue / $3M-profit businesses can be worth wildly different amounts: a declining, transactional, founder-dependent one is unsellable (worth ~0 to outsiders); a growing, all-recurring-revenue one with expanding margins might get a 12x multiple = ~$36M. (paraphrase)
- There are only three ways to make a business more valuable: (1) increase number of customers, (2) increase their value/LTV, (3) decrease risk / increase reliability. Every dollar or hour spent must ladder up to one of these. (paraphrase)
- Six multiple levers (his A–E framework): (A) debt capacity — a function of cash flow, unlike fixed-percentage home down payments; (B) organic growth (marketing/sales/pricing) vs inorganic growth (acquiring companies); (C) categorization — traditional service < tech-enabled service < SaaS multiples, and moving one category over can add ~3 turns; (D) size premium — bigger profit earns a higher multiple ("double multiplier effect"); (E) age — older businesses are more valuable. (paraphrase)
- Institutional investors "begin to really take notice" at about $5M profit/year (~20–40x territory at that mark he cites loosely); crossing $10M profit adds a size premium. (paraphrase)
- Big funds (BlackRock, Blackstone, State Street) have minimum check sizes — he cites a bank friend's $150M minimum — so they pay operators a premium to aggregate assets into a single writable check. (paraphrase)
- Patience compounds: waiting one more year to grow $5M → $8M profit can turn a $25M sale into a $75M sale — 3x for one year of work. (paraphrase)
- A mediocre opportunity executed to infinity beats an inferior opportunity you keep switching to; chasing a new business every ~6 months is why people stay poor. Most of these value drivers require time, so focus is the mechanism. (paraphrase)
