# Make More Profit than 99% of People

## Summary

Solo whiteboard-style video (with an audience Q&A tail) in which Alex Hormozi
argues that pricing is the single largest profit lever — roughly a 3x stronger
lever on profit than acquiring more customers or reducing churn. He walks through
five interlocking pricing concepts: the price-to-value discrepancy over time,
separating one-time vs. consumable value (the "big head, long tail" model),
billing cadence (monthly vs. quarterly vs. annual) as a churn driver, the
"look-back window" mechanism behind churn, and picking businesses whose customers
"already don't cancel." He closes with Q&A on raising prices, inflation/CPI
clauses, contracts, and cumulative value reporting.

## Key claims

- **Pricing is the largest profit lever.** Optimizing pricing has ~6x stronger
  impact on profitability than getting more customers, and ~2x stronger than
  decreasing churn or increasing purchase frequency. (Stated at open as ~6x/2x;
  later restated as pricing being "3 times stronger" than the other two levers.)
- **Price-to-value discrepancy:** if price stays fixed while delivered value
  declines, customers cancel when value falls to (or below) price. Three lines
  matter — cost, price, and value; the gap between price and value is "customer
  surplus" (goodwill), the gap between price and cost is profit.
- **One-time vs. consumable value:** most owners (especially in education/info)
  fail to separate value the customer captures once (a course — value drops to
  ~zero once the skill is learned) from value consumed monthly (community,
  accountability/support, hosting, software, ads help). Mismatching these causes
  churn.
- **Big head, long tail pricing model:** charge a higher one-time upfront fee for
  the one-time value, then a lower recurring fee matched to the consumable value
  (e.g., $5,000 upfront + $200–$300/month). Keeps a positive price-to-value delta
  over time so people don't cancel. Discussed in more depth in his offers book.
- **Billing cadence drives churn (ProfitWell study cited):** monthly billing ≈
  10.7% monthly churn; quarterly ≈ 5%; annual ≈ 2%.
- **LTV = price ÷ churn.** At $100/month: 10.7% churn ≈ $950 LTV; 5% churn ≈
  $2,000; 2% churn ≈ $5,000. Changing cadence alone can ~5x LTV per customer.
- **Look-back window mechanism:** churn is directly correlated with frequency of
  billing. Longer intervals let the customer average good and bad months together
  ("what have you done for me lately"), raising perceived value and renewal odds.
- **Prefer less money on annual over more on monthly:** e.g., $5K paid upfront
  annually beats $12K billed $1K/month, because annual renewal rate is far higher
  (often keep 80%+ of customers at 2% churn), and it compounds year over year.
- **Get in the right boat:** rather than obsessing over getting people to buy
  again, sell things people structurally don't cancel (insurance, alarm systems,
  payment processing, banking, cable, cell phone) vs. things they do (gym
  memberships).
- **Raise price until conversion-rate × price stops increasing gross profit.**
  It's a math problem; test the price. Easier to go low-to-high than high-to-low.
- **Annual frontloads cash,** boosting ability to offset customer-acquisition cost
  and get aggressive on acquisition.
- **Inflation:** include a CPI-matching clause in continuity contracts; adjust
  infrequently (every 2–3 years) because people dislike price changes.
- **Contracts** are only worth the creditworthiness of the signer. They matter for
  Enterprise; for consumers they mostly just add front-end friction and reduce
  sales without reducing back-end cancellation (Visa/MasterCard side with
  consumers on chargebacks). Contracts adhered to more in Europe than the US.
- **Annual incentive stacking:** offer annual at ~16% less ("buy 10 get 2 free")
  plus status perks (VIP tier); the ~5x LTV lift minus the 16% discount still
  nets ~4.5x, so it's worth it.
