# The Biggest Influence on My Life

## Summary
Live talk (with a co-panelist/moderator who chimes in twice). Despite the framing title, Alex does not name a person (parent, mentor, book) as his biggest influence — the "influence" he describes is a **self-coaching practice he invented**: consulting an imagined 85-year-old future version of himself he calls **"Solomon."** He sets it up by saying any good source of advice needs three things — **competence, context, and alignment** — and that modern therapy failed him on all three (he'd spend 90 minutes providing context to someone less competent whose incentive was to book him again next week). He then cites the **Solomon Paradox** (King Solomon gave great advice to others but ran his own life into shambles) and research showing people give better advice about their own problems when the problem is disguised as someone else's — "we give better advice than we follow." His fix: every Monday, the first hour of his week is blocked as "Solomon" on his calendar, and he holds a written chat-style dialogue with his 85-year-old self, who already knows how the game ends, embodies the traits Alex wants, and has "zero ego." He shares a story: a multi-year project where a vendor's broken promise cost him ~$15 million; in a Solomon session the future self told him **"I am the product that you were building, not the thing… I know what you put into that. And I am satisfied. That is enough,"** which resolved his disappointment. The frame does double duty: it holds him ruthlessly accountable (future-Alex knows the delta between how hard he tried and how hard it looked — "how do I close that delta" is what keeps him motivated), and it strips out external judgment (85-year-old me doesn't care what "Susan"/"John"/"Tom" thinks). The co-panelist ties it to Don Miguel Ruiz's "ruthless self-love." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues a trustworthy source of advice needs three things: **competence, context, and alignment**; a source that is competent and has context but is misaligned "is a great way to get destroyed."
- As of March 2026, he says modern therapy failed him on all three: he spent ~90 minutes providing context to someone possibly less competent than him, whose incentive (alignment) was for him to return the following week — "a broken model."
- As of March 2026, he invokes the **Solomon Paradox**: King Solomon gave famously wise advice to others while his own personal life was in shambles; research shows people give better advice when their own situation is disguised as someone else's — "we give better advice than we follow."
- As of March 2026, his practice: he imagines **"end-of-game Alex" — an 85-year-old version of himself** who has accomplished what Alex wants and embodies the traits he wants, and asks what that self would say; he blocks the first hour of every Monday as "Solomon" and conducts it as a written chat because he thinks through writing.
- As of March 2026 (self-reported story): a multi-year project he had built up to went wrong because a vendor broke a promise, costing him about **$15 million** in real money; he had trusted the vendor because others had used them successfully, which he considered a reasonable decision at the time.
- As of March 2026, the Solomon session resolved his disappointment when his future self told him he (Alex, the person he is becoming) is "the product," not the project, and that given what Alex put in, future-Alex is satisfied — "that is enough."
- As of March 2026, he says the practice keeps him honest because he cannot enroll his future self in a flattering story ("he knows if I'm bullshitting"); future-Alex knows the **delta between how hard he tried and how hard it looks when he has nothing left in the tank**, and closing that delta is what keeps him motivated.
- As of March 2026, he defines his personal "win" as **dying with nothing left in the tank** — "I'll know at the end how hard I tried, and that is what I've been trying to make my W."
- As of March 2026, the frame removes external judgment: naming the specific people you fear judgment from (it's never "people," it's "two people") makes the fear real and dismissible; his 85-year-old self "doesn't give a [expletive] what Susan thinks." He applies this to his hesitation over selling the company.
- As of March 2026, he admits he **struggles with gratitude** — he can readily name 20 problems in his life but stalls at naming five good things, landing on "I am alive"; imagining Solomon reborn into his present life (young body, wife still young) is his workaround for feeling gratitude.
