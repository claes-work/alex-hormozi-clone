# The Best Bet in Business

## Summary
In this clip Alex Hormozi talks with an unnamed interviewer/host about talent acquisition as the highest-return activity in business. He frames hiring A-players — paying a premium for top talent that produces outsized output — as "the best bet in Vegas," discusses how acquisition.com aggressively recruits and places leadership talent into its portfolio companies, and shares heuristics for spotting elite candidates (FOMO in the interview, learning from the candidate, "two-sided fit" at 90 days). The interviewer contributes concepts like Keith Rabois's "barrels versus bullets," which Alex builds on.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the single highest return on capital acquisition.com has as buying talent — hiring an executive for ~$200,000/year who generates ~$20 million/year — calling it a "no brainer" trade. (Illustrative figures.)
- As of March 2026, Alex says acquisition.com fills roughly three to four leadership positions per week across its portfolio companies, running near-continuous "open interviews" and hiring exceptional people even without a defined role for them yet.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues you can pay A-talent 30% above market and get roughly 10x the output, and that there is never a good reason to pay below market — the premium for the right person is the best trade available.
- As of March 2026, Alex names as one of the biggest early-career mistakes (his own included) failing to understand the difference between A and B talent.
- As of March 2026, Alex shares a hiring litmus test he attributes to Leila: if you are not learning from the candidate during the interview about the thing they will own, don't hire them — because it means you know more than them, they need you, and you gain no leverage.
- As of March 2026, Alex says an organization only grows if the company's cumulative knowledge base grows; if a hire adds no knowledge you don't already have, the org doesn't grow, and a business is capped by the founder's brain if the founder is the smartest person in it.
- As of March 2026, Alex says you should feel FOMO (and feel "like an idiot" about the candidate's subject area) when talking to a high-caliber prospect, and that one bad hire wrongly convinces entrepreneurs they can't hire — pattern recognition improves with reps.
- As of March 2026, Alex cites acquisition.com HR metrics: cost-to-fill below industry average, time-to-fill around 22 days, and a "two-sided fit" metric where both the hire and their manager confirm a perfect match 90 days later. (Self-reported; "two-sided fit" credited to their director of HR, who came from PE firm Audax.)
- Alex recounts advising a portfolio company to hire an ex-CEO sales director they were hesitant about, telling them acquisition.com would hire him regardless and place him elsewhere; the company then took him. (Anecdote.)
- The interviewer (unverified caller claim) attributes "barrels versus bullets" to Keith Rabois — barrels solve open-ended problems, bullets execute instructions — and advises always hiring a barrel; Alex agrees it maps to his point.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "That's the highest return on capital that we have is buy someone... buy talent for 200,000 a year, make 20 million a year. No brainer."

> "You can pay A talent 30% above market and get 10X the output. And there's no sense in ever paying below market in my opinion... it's like the best best bet in Vegas."

> "If you are not learning on the interview from the candidate about the thing that they're supposed to take over, you shouldn't hire them. Because it means that you know more than them, which means that you're required for them to be successful, which means that they need you, and you no longer get leverage."

> "If you were the smartest person in your business, then it means that the business will be capped by your brain."

> "An organization grows if the cumulative knowledge base of the company grows. And so, if you bring somebody in who you have their entire complete knowledge set about whatever the subject matter is, then the organization doesn't grow."

> "You make one bad hire and then you think, I can't hire people. Like you're going to make mistakes. It's part of the game. You get better and better at pattern recognition."

> "You should feel FOMO when you're talking to the person."

> "You should feel like an idiot when you're talking to high-caliber prospects."
