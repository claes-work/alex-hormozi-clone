# Why The First $100k is the Toughest | Charlie Munger Secrets

## Summary

In this short 2021 solo podcast episode, Hormozi unpacks why the first $100,000 is
the hardest money to make. He credits the core idea to Charlie Munger (Warren
Buffett's business partner), who said "the first hundred grand is a bitch," then
builds his own explanation on top of it: at the $100k goal level people can only see
time-for-money vehicles and have no leverage, so they must do everything themselves.
He argues that once past that threshold you can buy back low-value time, stack skills,
and raise your permanent income floor toward million- and ten-million-dollar
opportunities. He relates it to his own life, saying the first $100k in his bank
account was the most meaningful money he ever made.

## Key claims

### Relayed from Charlie Munger (2021 framing)
- The first $100,000 is the hardest money to accumulate — Hormozi attributes this
  explicitly to Charlie Munger, described as Warren Buffett's business partner, and
  says the "first hundred grand is a bitch" is a real Munger quote listeners can look
  up. Hormozi endorses it rather than claiming it as his own.

### Hormozi's own additions (2021)
- The reason the first $100k is hard: when you set the goal at $100k, you only think
  of "$100k opportunities" — i.e., time-for-money vehicles. You can't reach $10M by
  doing a $100k play 100 times; that requires a fundamentally different way of
  operating. (paraphrase)
- Second reason: with no money you have no leverage, so you must do everything
  yourself, wear every hat, and learn every part of the business — lots of time for
  small output. (paraphrase)
- Once you have ~$100k you gain leverage: you can pay for staff, advertising, outbound,
  and higher-ROI production, and stop worrying about rent. (paraphrase)
- All businesses are arbitrage — e.g., a service business buys labor and sells it to
  the market at a higher price, adding value so the whole is greater than the sum of
  the parts. (paraphrase)
- Buy back your time in reverse order of value: outsource your lowest-value tasks
  (e.g., admin/scheduling you can hire at ~$10/hr) first. (paraphrase)
- Common mistake: people outsource work but then don't refill that reclaimed time with
  higher-value work — so they just pay others and coast at the same income instead of
  growing. (paraphrase)
- "You can't be busy and broke" — if you're busy you should be making money; if you're
  broke you have time, so you can always trade time for money. Feeling busy-and-broke
  means you're wasting time. (paraphrase)
- Skill-stacking raises your permanent income floor ("basement"): each skill acquired
  lifts the level you can fall back to. He claims that after learning to sell he'd
  never make less than ~$250k/year (could always sell cars), and after adding
  marketing his floor rose toward ~$3M/year. (paraphrase, self-reported figures)
- He paid to acquire every valuable skill he has, to acquire it faster; deliberate
  practice plus mentors/experts speeds skill acquisition, but volume of work is still
  required. (paraphrase)
- Illustrative "nugget": he claims no one in the US needs to earn under $25/hr because
  a one-day phlebotomist certification qualifies you for ~$25/hr; people don't do it
  out of ignorance, not inability. (paraphrase, unverified claim)
- Reframe failures as skill acquisition: "your work works on you more than you work on
  it," so even a year that fell short of $100k built foundational skills. (paraphrase)
