# How to Build a Business Someone Wants to Buy

## Summary
A short clip (under two minutes) in which Alex Hormozi answers an audience question about whether a "school group" (online community/course platform) can be a sellable asset. He argues that sellability has nothing to do with the platform or business category and everything to do with the underlying economics: retention, gross margins, diversified acquisition, and owner-independence. He uses analogies (Amazon store, dry cleaner, Shopify store) to make the point that within any category good businesses are sellable and bad ones aren't. The clip closes with a promotion for his free "10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+" at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-13. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-13) A "school group" (community/course platform) can be a sellable asset, just like an Amazon store can be.
- (2026-02-13) Whether a business is sellable has nothing to do with the platform or category it sits on ("school group") and everything to do with the fundamental economics of the business that underpin the revenue.
- (2026-02-13) The features that make a business a sellable asset are: high customer retention (he cites ~80% annual retention as an example), high gross margins, multiple acquisition channels for bringing customers in the door, and the business not relying on the owner (him or her).
- (2026-02-13) It is those underlying economic features — not the category — that separate a sellable asset from one that isn't.
- (2026-02-13) Asking "is a school community sellable?" is like asking "is a dry cleaner sellable?" — good ones are and bad ones aren't. (Analogy: sellability is a property of the specific business's quality, not its category.)
- (2026-02-13) [Attribution: other speaker, Alex affirms] The other speaker notes that with e-commerce essentially everything runs on Shopify, so it's not about the platform — it's about the product, the customers, the reviews, the brand, and the awareness; that is what makes a business. Alex agrees ("Yeah," "for sure").
