# Helping a Door-to-Door Service Business Get More Customers

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a residential exterior-cleaning business (power washing, solar-panel cleaning, window cleaning — "Supreme Power Washing"; ~$500k top-line revenue, ~$250k / 50% margins) wants to reach $1M and thinks the path is to build a new acquisition channel targeting recurring **commercial/B2B** clients, after noticing one commercial account was bigger, more stable, and recurred monthly. Alex pushes back on the instinct to change the business model. He maps the owner's channels onto his standard **four ways to get customers besides referrals** — paid ads, organic content, cold outreach (OB), and affiliates — and finds the business already has two things working: door-to-door knocking (~20 deals/month at ~$500 avg ≈ $10k/month) and paid ads (~$2,000/month spend via a third party, likely already profitable but untracked). His core diagnosis: the owner doesn't have an attribution problem disguised as a strategy problem. **Step one is put attribution tracking in place** (so spend-vs-return is measurable); **step two, once attribution proves it's good, spend as much as possible** on the working channel — bumping $2k/month toward $10k rather than jumping all at once (two to four, two to six, at a comfortable pace). Only after tapping that out and generating cash flow should the owner consider opening a second paid channel or spinning up more door-to-door reps. Alex notes his usual rule is one acquisition channel per business, but **local businesses are his exception** — in a small market you have to reach a limited population every way you can. His verdict: don't chase a new avatar/model when you have working channels you're not even measuring; do more of what works. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex advises against building a whole new acquisition channel for a new avatar (residential → commercial) when the business already has one or two channels that appear to be working — "do more of what's already working."
- As of March 2026, there are four ways to get customers besides referrals: paid ads, organic content, cold outreach (OB / outbound), and affiliates — and a business should focus on getting one of them working.
- As of March 2026, a business relying on inbound referrals and a ringing phone does not have a real acquisition channel — it lacks a repeatable, controllable way of getting customers in the door.
- As of March 2026, the first step before scaling ad spend is attribution tracking (measuring what you spend versus what you make); whoever runs your ads should provide it, and if they can't, find someone who can.
- As of March 2026, a rough profitability read: at $2,000/month (~$500/week) spend with a ~$500 average deal, getting more than one deal a week from ads means it's probably working — possibly at 5-to-1 or 10-to-1.
- As of March 2026, once attribution proves the channel is good, spend as much as you can there; scale gradually (two to four, two to six) rather than jumping to 5x immediately, at a pace the owner is comfortable with.
- As of March 2026, only after maxing out the working channel and building cash flow should the owner decide whether to open a second paid channel or add more door-to-door reps.
- As of March 2026, Alex's default is one acquisition channel per business, but **local businesses are the exception** — in a small market (his example: a town of ~50,000 people) you must reach the limited population through as many channels as possible.
- As of March 2026, Alex judged the business healthy at ~50% margins; the real gap was that the owner "just don't know if what you're doing is working."
