# How I make friends with BILLIONAIRES [5 Steps]

## Summary
A short solo video in which Hormozi lays out five personal "rules" for networking with high-status people (framed as meeting millionaires and billionaires) without seeming like a "sleaze." He frames deliberate networking as a giving-first process aimed at improving the other person's life while improving his own, and credits much of the good in his life to chance conversations and connections. No specific billionaires are named. (Self-reported.)

## Key claims (2021)
- **Rule 1 — Remember and repeat the name.** On shaking hands he says the person's name and keeps opening sentences with it (aiming to say it ~10 times) so it sticks; a person's own name is "their favorite word to hear," and using it signals care and respect (2021).
- **Rule 2 — Get contact info and text immediately.** He texts a funny anecdote about himself or the just-had conversation, including both his own and their name plus a memorable identifier (e.g. "Alex Hormozi, mustache gym guy"), so the contact is saved, rapport is built early, and a later look-back at the thread triggers a positive memory (2021).
- **Rule 3 — Provide value first, unsolicited.** The goal of a conversation should be figuring out how to give value to the person, not what they can do for you; the more unsolicited work you do for them, the more they respect you and want to keep you in their lives (2021).
- **Rule 4 (his "biggest secret") — Always say yes to the first ask.** People often only ask once; if you miss the first ask (a Zoom, a lunch, a meetup) they may never ask again, so he rearranges his schedule to accommodate it. The mirror rule: when *you* are the one asking, ask many times, because busy people flaking usually has nothing to do with you (2021).
- **Rule 5 — Never ask for anything.** Asking makes people assume you are using them. Instead, when catching up, describe a problem you're facing; if rapport exists and they want the relationship, they will offer help on their own. Because people are reciprocal by nature, giving well and unsolicited while never asking leads them to give back — and if they don't, they weren't worth having in your network anyway (2021).
