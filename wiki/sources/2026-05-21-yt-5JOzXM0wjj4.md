# The 3 Things Everyone Gets Wrong About Feedback

## Summary

Alex Hormozi frames criticism as a fixed cost: people criticize action and inaction alike.
He says feedback should be weighted toward people closest to the goal, not people closest
to the person. He also argues that people take years to get the first 20 hours of skill
practice and recommends immersive shutdown days to compress learning.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If someone does nothing, people criticize; if someone does something, people criticize.
- Criticism is a fixed cost.
- It may take about 20 hours to become proficient in many skills, but people often take
  years to get those first 20 hours.
- Not all feedback is equal.
- People should listen to those closest to their goals, not necessarily those closest to
  them personally.
- Criticism from people who have not achieved the goal should be discounted.
- The veteran frame asks how a person would feel if the same bad experience happened a
  thousand times in a row, then invites them to feel that way now.
- Alex learned to build webpages by locking down a weekend, turning off distractions, and
  copying a simple page.
- He had put off the task for weeks but finished by noon after dedicating two full days.
- Fear and ignorance can feel wide but shallow once the work starts.
- Longer intense sessions beat many short sessions because short sessions waste time
  reorienting.
- Alex is a strong advocate of full shutdown days for learning a skill.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Criticism is a fixed cost."

> "You want to listen to the people who are closest to your goals."

> "Fear is a mile wide and an inch deep. So is ignorance."
