# There's No Easy Business

## Summary

Live Q&A between Alex Hormozi and an audience member who runs a local CPA / CFO-advisory
and tax-planning firm (~$2.9M/year projected, ~190 monthly recurring clients, all-organic,
~30–35% annual growth, 20–25% margins). The owner wants to reach $20M and thinks the answer
is to finally market the books and courses he has made ("Tax-Free Millionaire," "Fire Your
CPA"). He has even paused new sales while he figures things out.

Hormozi reframes the diagnosis: the business is **supply-constrained**, not
demand-constrained, so more marketing is the wrong lever — the firm already can't service
the demand it has. He pushes back on starting a second business (a course business) when the
existing service business is valuable and sticky, and warns that the course path only looks
easier because you haven't lived its pain yet. His prescribed sequence: increase operating
leverage (offshore talent to give the existing team 2–3x capacity) → build a data layer →
add an AI layer on top → then resume selling, and only then use the books/courses as
marketing assets to drive demand. Threaded through is his recurring "hard things are hard"
theme: every business, including the course one, sucks in its own way; the friction of
early AI adoption is exactly where the money is made.

## Key claims
_(dated 2026-02-14; paraphrased unless quoted below)_

- ★ Whether a business is supply-constrained or demand-constrained determines the lever: this
  owner keeps asking "how do I market more?" but he can't even service the customers he has,
  so marketing is the wrong move — fix the supply constraint first.
- ★ Diagnostic heuristic: nobody who has bad churn voluntarily stops selling. The fact that
  the owner is comfortable pausing new sales is itself evidence his product is sticky and
  better than he thinks.
- Don't start a second business (courses) when you already own a valuable, sticky one — the
  course path only looks easier because you haven't experienced its specific pain yet.
- Course/low-ticket customers are the opposite of sticky: they expect you to do everything,
  and a $17 buyer who isn't a "tax-free millionaire" yet will blame you.
- ★ To be an "AI-first" company you must first be a **data-first** company: AI works on data,
  so you need complete data capture and an architecture that models all elements of the
  business before you can layer AI (and reinforcement training) on top. Using ChatGPT for
  emails is not being AI-first.
- Prescribed growth sequence for this firm: (1) increase operating leverage via offshore
  talent giving each existing employee 2–3x capacity (external headcount, not internal),
  (2) add a data layer, (3) add an AI layer for further leverage, (4) resume selling, then
  (5) if still needed, deploy the books/courses as marketing assets to generate demand.
- Increasing operating leverage will likely also increase margin (currently 20–25%).
- The AI transition is a once-in-a-generation move, analogous to cloud computing 25 years ago
  (CDs → cloud) that produced the last wave of software billionaires; the friction of the
  pieces not yet fitting together is precisely the problem you get paid to solve.
- "Selling zero new units per month will not grow the business" — making that number more
  than zero reliably grows the firm and makes more money.
- Save the existing books and courses "in your back pocket" as demand-generation assets for
  when supply capacity is fixed — there is nothing wrong with them, they're just the wrong
  first move.
