# Helping a Boat Rental Company Scale

## Summary
Live workshop Q&A. The client owns a boat-club business (five locations, ~50 boats, ~$2M top line, ~$600k profit → ~30% margins, ~$2M in debt) built on a **membership model**: a $5,000 initiation fee plus $500/month on 5-year contracts. He's been at it 13 years, feels stuck, and arrived weighing three paths — buy an existing club (would more than double the business), open a third-market location (going from 5 to 10 locations), or **sell** (another mentor, "Ed," told him to sell because he doesn't love boats). Alex first reframes the exit impulse: don't expect a business to *bring you joy* or "fill a hole in your heart" — almost all businesses look the same three levels up (directors of sales/marketing/IT, HR, legal, "sucks every other day"), and the constant across a person's failed executive assistants is themselves. He tells the three-stonecutters parable and the fitness-founder-turned-cookie-company story ("he wasn't in love with cookies, he was in love with being excellent") to argue business should be an *expression of your values*, not a source of identity. Then he demolishes the case for selling on the numbers: with **very high margins on each incremental membership** (fixed costs already covered, so each new member drops disproportionately to the bottom line), a customer acquisition cost of only ~$266 (later ~$300–400 with commissions) against a $5,000 upfront + $500/month 5-year contract, this is a great business — "Why are we getting out of this business?" The real problem is the debt (eating most of the cash flow; ~6–7 years to pay off after tax) creating a "crushing weight" that makes selling feel attractive. Alex's prescription is to **scale volume**, not exit: the owner is unconsciously spending just enough on marketing to clear fixed costs and then "exhaling," working hard to pay everyone else (landlord, licenser, employees) but "forgetting to pay himself" — "when it gets easy is when you go hard." Concrete levers: spend far more than the current ~$6k/month on paid ads (Google AdWords primary), improve creative and keyword targeting, tighten attribution, juice the already-strong referral system (~1/3 of new members come from referrals), and run easy CRO/funnel fixes to lift revenue per lead. He validates the unit economics as healthy (4% lead-to-close, 33% close on people who come in) and dismisses industry benchmarks entirely ("Zero [__] about industry standard… Why would I care about the average industry person? They all suck"). The licensing structure (marks + reservation software; members pay $30/month to corporate out of the $500, ~6%) is deemed not "strangling" and doesn't block a sale. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, a membership/rental business with high fixed costs has **very high margins on each incremental membership** — once fixed costs are covered, each additional member drops disproportionately to the bottom line, so the growth lever is selling far more memberships.
- As of March 2026, the boat-club unit economics are strong: ~$266 cost of acquisition (~$300–400 with sales commissions) against a $5,000 initiation fee plus $500/month on 5-year contracts — Alex's verdict: "Why are we getting out of this business?"
- As of March 2026, the real constraint is **debt, not the business model** — ~$2M in debt eating most of the cash flow (~6–7 years to pay off after tax) creates a "crushing weight" that makes exiting feel more attractive than it should.
- As of March 2026, owners unconsciously spend just enough on marketing to clear fixed costs, then "exhale" at being profitable — but that means working hard to pay the landlord, licenser, and employees while "forgetting to pay yourself"; the fix is to "go ham" on marketing. "When it gets easy is when you go hard."
- As of March 2026, don't expect a business to bring you joy or "fill a hole in your heart" — almost all businesses look the same three levels up (director-level hires, HR, legal, "sucks every other day"), and the common thread across your failed hires/assistants is you. See business as an *expression of your values* and a place to build skill (the cookie-founder "wasn't in love with cookies, he was in love with being excellent").
- As of March 2026, industry benchmarks are worthless: "the average industry person… they all suck, they don't make money" — measure against what's actually possible, not the average operator.
- As of March 2026, the scaling levers for this business (in order): spend more on paid ads (currently ~$6k/month, Google AdWords primary) with better creative and keyword targeting, fix attribution, systematize/juice referrals (~1/3 of members already come from referrals), and run easy CRO on the funnel to raise revenue per lead.
- As of March 2026, a licensing structure (marks + reservation software, ~6% of membership paid by members to corporate) does not "strangle" the business and does not prevent selling it — you own and can sell the business under an existing agreement without giving away top line.
- As of March 2026, diagnostic heuristic: if close rate is low (e.g. 10%) with acceptable lead costs, it's a sales-motion or offer problem; if lead costs are too high, look at the offer — but here lead cost and close rates are fine, so the answer is simply *more volume*, not fixing the offer.
