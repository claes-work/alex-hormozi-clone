# This Video Should be REQUIRED Viewing For Business Owners

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video explaining how to turn a linear business (where growth requires
continuously pouring in more founder effort and money) into a self-reinforcing growth
loop: customers get results → results drive reviews and word of mouth → those get more
customers. He then gives four tactics for systematically generating reviews: (1) a
surprise free gift paired with a third-party review ask, (2) merch given in exchange for
reviews, (3) an instant point-of-sale discount for leaving a review, and (4) unlockable
bonuses (media/training or a VIP trial of higher-tier service), especially for B2B and
digital businesses. He closes by promoting the free digital version of his *$100M Leads*
book for the referral growth equation.

## Key claims

- Every business starts with founder/investor effort and money, which gets customers,
  who get results, which generate reviews/word of mouth, which get more customers. Most
  businesses lack a good process at the "results → reviews" and "reviews → customers"
  steps, so they stay linear: put more in, get more out.
- The goal is a self-reinforcing system where each component builds momentum and drives
  the next, creating sustainable growth without continuous new founder/investor input.
- **Tactic 1 — surprise gift + third-party ask.** After delivering the core service,
  give a surprise free gift scaled to spend (mints for ~$20, a ~$5 dessert for ~$100, a
  ~$50 gift for ~$1,000). This banks reciprocity. Then a *different* person (manager,
  shift manager) makes the review ask, framed as "we'll pay this person $50 if you leave
  a review" — incentivizing the staffer, not the customer, so the customer feels they're
  paying back the person who helped them. The further the asker is from the business
  owner, the more compelling. (Subject to local advertising laws.)
- Incremental reviews are highly valuable: the business with the most reviews can capture
  ~90% of search volume even at a slightly lower star rating (Leila's Yelp example: 4.6
  stars / 1,000 reviews beat 5 stars / 20 reviews).
- A new business with few reviews should value each additional review far more than an
  established one with 10,000 reviews, but should keep the review system running to
  "grease the groove" of the growth loop.
- Word of mouth is the highest percentage of sales across any market; the higher the
  stakes of the purchase, the more referrals matter. Even on low-ticket first sales,
  conversion depends on the proof demonstrated.
- **Display proof everywhere.** Once reviews come in, show them (e.g. a lobby with
  five-star reviews floor to ceiling). In-person businesses get a double effect because
  a customer walking into a lobby of five-star reviews anchors the business's quality.
- Review rate is a high-quality customer-satisfaction metric: track what percentage of a
  server's tables (or a field tech's jobs) leave a five-star review. If one employee
  outperforms, find out what they do differently and teach everyone.
- **Tactic 1's Step 3 — make it easy.** Bring a QR code on a small A-frame next to the
  check. Over time you can shift which platform the QR points to (Yelp → Google →
  Facebook/Meta → Instagram follow → text-list opt-in) to boost whichever you want next.
- Restaurant math example: a manager spending ~5 minutes hitting 10 tables/hour, at ~1 in
  10 leaving a review, produces ~10 five-star reviews/day ≈ ~4,000/year.
- **Tactic 2 — merch.** At his second gym (starting from zero reviews), Alex gave away
  ~$5 t-shirts in exchange for members leaving reviews on Yelp, Google, and Facebook plus
  a check-in (four actions). It produced ~15 five-star reviews overnight plus organic
  word of mouth from people wearing the shirts. One $5 shirt covers multiple advertising
  surfaces (Yelp, Google, Meta, plus organic). Works best for B2C / recurring-membership
  businesses with brand affinity; if the brand isn't strong yet, put an inspirational
  quote/saying on the front (e.g. "beast mode engaged") and the logo on the back/sleeve.
- **Tactic 3 — instant discount.** Offer a discount proportional to gross profit and
  average ticket, delivered at point of sale. Budget-diner example: $1 off (~5% of ticket)
  = paying ~$5,000 for 5,000 reviews. Sequence: present price → get acceptance → offer
  discount → get yes → verify the review was done → recalculate → hand over final check.
- Latency is one of the strongest multipliers of a persuasive tool; risk and delay work
  similarly in the mind. An instant "available right now" discount lets you give far less
  than a delayed "20% off your next visit." Most people would rather have $2 off today
  than $100 in a year.
- **Meta-frame of the three tactics:** #1 adds something with no direct exchange (pure
  reciprocity, goodwill to the server); #2 is a direct exchange with the customer; #3
  removes something bad (price).
- **Tactic 4 (bonus) — unlockables.** For B2B / national / digital businesses, unlock
  either media (training/event) or a VIP trial of a higher service tier in exchange for a
  review. Add-on delivery cost is usually low; the trial can convert customers into more
  valuable ones over time, so you "get paid to get reviews." IRL version: an annual event
  whose entry ticket is having left a review.
- Closing metaphor: you want the engine to run without you — "you are lighter fluid, not
  the log," because if you're the log you eventually burn out.
- Promotes the free digital/audio version of the *$100M Leads* book, whose customer
  referral chapter contains the referral growth equation and seven referral tactics.
