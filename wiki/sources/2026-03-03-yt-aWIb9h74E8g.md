# How to Learn Anything

## Summary
Interview clip (an interviewer asks questions; only Alex's words are used as voice/belief data). Alex lays out his theory of skill acquisition and training, mostly through a sales-team lens. Core belief: **anything can be trained** — even extreme cases are "just" a question of how many resources the institution is willing to invest. His mechanism: **every skill decomposes into subskills**, and "the more skilled the individual, the vaguer the instruction" — a master needs only a one-line directive ("start a company around this") because they already own all the subskills, whereas a beginner must be walked from "turn on the phone, hit this button." Learning speed varies (a month vs. two years), and "who cares" how long it takes if you believe you're worth the long-term investment. He then moves into how he actually *runs* training on his ~40-person sales team: hire an exceptional **sales director** (really a "sales operator" who sells the team's conviction, runs culture, and role-plays "until their eyes bleed"); demand **script adherence** as the first controllable ("you follow the script. Period." — if you follow it and don't close, "you have won"; a team-wide failure means *he* must fix the script); train primarily via **game tape** — reps watch 60–100 hours of recordings of top performers so the script lives in their subconscious; he prefers showing **positives** (what to do) over negatives; new reps take reduced call loads (half/third schedules) to protect leads, since "the most expensive thing… is waste leads on an inexperienced rep"; and reviews use **self-discovery** ("pause — what do you think you could have done better?" — he won't tell them, to avoid killing their headspace). Peers comment only on what a rep did *well*; managers handle corrections one-on-one, drilling the "most minute level" of wording until reps "breathe it" and say the same words every time. Once that culture sets, the team self-polices anyone who deviates from the script. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's foundational belief: **anything can be trained** — with enough resources even the hardest cases are teachable; the only real question is whether it's worth the institution's investment in that particular person.
- As of March 2026, "the more skilled the individual, the vaguer the instruction" — mastery means you can act on a one-line directive because you already hold all the subskills; a beginner needs step-by-step instruction from the bottom up.
- As of March 2026, **every skill has subskills underneath it**; break any skill down far enough and anyone can learn it, so proficiency is a function of how much training you're willing to invest.
- As of March 2026, learning speed differs by person and by the "building blocks" they arrive with (e.g. an ex-athlete handles rejection less emotionally) — but the time it takes (a month vs. two years) "doesn't matter" if you believe you're worth the long-term investment.
- As of March 2026, the first hire when scaling a ~40-person sales team was an **exceptional sales director** — really a "sales operator" who may not sell much himself but sells the team's conviction, runs culture and training, genuinely cares about people, and will repeat himself and role-play endlessly.
- As of March 2026, his first objective with a sales team is **script adherence**, because it gives him a controllable: if a rep follows the script/cadence and still doesn't close, "you have won" — and if everyone fails, the script is wrong and that's on him. Most teams instead have "10 guys selling 10 different ways," which is why they rely on recruiting naturally good salespeople.
- As of March 2026, training comes down to **shadowing game footage**: new reps should watch 60–100 hours of recordings of top performers doing great work so the script embeds in their subconscious; he prefers training on **positives** (what to do) over negatives.
- As of March 2026, "the most expensive thing… as a business owner is waste leads on an inexperienced rep" — so new reps take reduced call loads (half/third schedules) and reviews happen with a manager.
- As of March 2026, correction uses **self-discovery**: watch the recording, pause, and ask "what do you think you could have done better?" rather than telling them — because being told/yelled at makes people conclude they suck and hurts their headspace.
- As of March 2026, team game-tape sessions run weekly; peers comment only on what a rep did **well**, and managers deliver corrections privately one-on-one, drilling wording at the "most minute level" until reps "breathe it" and say the same words every time.
- As of March 2026, once the script-adherence culture is set, the team itself polices deviators ("this guy's not following the script… get this guy out of here"), which lets the business control variables and actually have a teachable sales *system*.
