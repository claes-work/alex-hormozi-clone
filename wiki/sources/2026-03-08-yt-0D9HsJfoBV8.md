# It Just Takes Work...

## Summary
Solo, confessional monologue. Alex opens by recounting that a day earlier he found an old file with a 13-year-old goal — to make $10,000/month in income — and it triggered a visceral memory of how much pain he was in then. He frames the whole clip as: the reason he makes his content is that he's "been there" and doesn't want it for anyone. He recaps his origin: started his first brick-and-mortar gym at 23, knew nothing (didn't even know he could hire employees), so he cleaned, did billing, sales, taught sessions, and fixed equipment himself — partly from ignorance, partly because he couldn't afford anything else. His core argument is that there is no secret "one play" for people who have little: when you have low leverage, the only thing you have is **time**, and the way out is "sheer brute force" — willing yourself out by doing an enormous amount of soul-driving work on top of a normal life (job, eat, sleep, clean, *then* the work day starts). He rejects the self-help/"power of positive thinking"/affirmations genre because it didn't work for him; his content is explicitly a counter to it, and he stresses he's not prescribing his way to everyone — only "if what you've been doing hasn't been working, then consider this." He's candid that his fuel wasn't passion or love of clients' transformations but **anger** — the fear of being wrong and having "the people back home be right." He tells signature stories: humbling himself from a white-collar consulting job down to minimum wage; clients patronizingly asking if he went to college (implying he was illiterate) and choosing to "be rich" over "be right" by moving past the insult to keep the sale; letting a kid draw on the wall in permanent marker rather than scold him and lose the sale to the mom. He introduces his "eat the glass" / timeline metaphor: pull the timeline of life toward you — it's coarse glass at the start, gradually turning neutral then sweeter — but you must endure the glass period; take tomorrow's pain today to get ahead. He shares that at the gym, once Saturday sessions ended at noon his phone never rang (no one wanted to see Alex; new to Huntington Beach from Baltimore), and he used that isolation to study marketing and selling — and stopped watching the Ravens to cut his last tie to home. He explains he had **no plan B** worth living for: at his consulting job, making good money with peer approval, he'd think each night that he'd be okay not waking up — so failure meant returning to that, which was "worse than dying." He closes with his deliberately extreme mental frame: throughout history people have worked seven days a week, every waking hour, under threat of their children's death — so that capacity exists in humans, and invoking it kept him out of the "woe is me" cycle and refocused on "what can I do now?" Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
