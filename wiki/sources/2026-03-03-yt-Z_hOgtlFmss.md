# How to Make More Money as an Employee

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-screen host asks the questions; only Alex's answers are used as voice/knowledge data). The host frames the problem: most people think in terms of a "3, 5% raise," and asks for a 30,000-foot roadmap to scale W-2 income from **$50k to $500k inside a job** (Alex deliberately picks the "hard version" — inside a career, not by starting a company). Alex's answer is an **internal-entrepreneur** playbook.

Prerequisite: **pick the right vessel.** You can't make $500k in a business that only makes $500k; to earn $500k you'd realistically need to work at a company doing **at least $10M/year, probably $20M+** (unless you're the CEO). He notes there are far more large private employers than people assume — family-owned manufacturers doing $100M–$500M/year that never appear on the S&P 500.

Core move: **tie yourself as close to revenue as humanly possible and take on as much risk as humanly possible** — become an internal entrepreneur. Because entrepreneurship is fundamentally about taking more risk, an employee who behaves like a vendor can capture a slice of the value they create. His rule of thumb: your pay is always a **discount on the money you make someone else** — exactly how it works for an outside entrepreneur too. If you can bring an extra $5M into the business, most decent business owners would agree to give you ~10% of it.

Practical framing: **think of yourself as a vendor/service provider to the business, not an employee**, and be able to **clearly explain how what you do makes the company money.** He calls this a "wonderful exercise" (useful for owners too): walk around and ask customer service, finance, legal, "How do you make us money?" — people who understand business can articulate it, even when a role's contribution is second- or third-order away from primary revenue. From there, make **performance-based, upside-sharing asks**, e.g. customer-success rep: "our churn is 10%, let me lead a project to cut it to 5% — if I do, will you bump my pay?" The formula: "I believe I can generate this much value; I'd like a small percentage of it — are you open to it?"

If the owner refuses any upside, then capped upside is real — and Alex's advice is to **leave** (he thinks refusing is a dumb move by the owner). Finally, **the more of the outcome you own — the more money and time YOU put at risk — the more upside you unlock.** Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (for business owners).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, to earn a given salary you must work at a company large enough to pay it: you can't make $500k at a business that makes $500k. Making ~$500k/year realistically requires a company doing **at least $10M/year, likely $20M+**, unless you're the CEO.
- As of March 2026, there are many more large private employers than people assume — family-owned manufacturers doing **$100M–$500M/year** that never appear on the S&P 500.
- As of March 2026, the core lever to scale employee income is to **tie your role as close to revenue as possible and take on as much risk as possible** — become an "internal entrepreneur."
- As of March 2026, your compensation is inherently a **discount on the money you generate for someone else** — the same mechanism that governs an outside entrepreneur's pay (B2B: make a business more money, keep a smaller percentage of it; do it for many businesses and you make a lot).
- As of March 2026, employees should **think of themselves as a vendor/service provider to the business**, not as an employee.
- As of March 2026, a key exercise (for employees and owners): **be able to clearly explain how your role makes the company money.** Many roles contribute at second- or third-order distance from primary revenue; people who understand business can still articulate it.
- As of March 2026, the way to unlock raises is a **performance- and upside-based ask** — name the value you'll create and request a small percentage of it (e.g. "cut churn from 10% to 5% for a pay bump," or "bring an extra $5M for ~10% of it").
- As of March 2026, if an owner refuses any upside-sharing, capped upside is real and the right move is to **leave** — Alex considers such refusal a poor decision by the owner.
- As of March 2026, **the more of the outcome you own — the more money and time you personally put at risk — the more of the upside you can capture.**
