# The Ultimate Sales Training for 2026 [Full Course]

## Summary

A ~90-minute solo sales masterclass in which Alex Hormozi lays out his complete
sales system across six sections: sales multipliers (operational levers around
the sale), sales training (how to coach a team), pre-sale work (click-to-call),
the sales conversation (opening, scripting, the CLOSER framework, three-pillar
pitch), closing and objection handling, and sales mindset. He frames sales as
"passing conviction from one person to another over a bridge of trust" and an
education process, and repeatedly stresses that the biggest revenue gains often
come from things *around* the sale (speed-to-lead, scheduling, show-up rates)
rather than from what is said on the call. Data is attributed to a Harvard
Business Review study and to Alon (his ~4,000-appointments/day scheduling
software company). Being solo, the entire video is Alex-attributed and is dense
persona/voice-training material.

## Key claims

### Sales multipliers (operational levers)
- **Sell 7 days a week.** Two extra days = 104 days/year = a ~29% revenue
  increase minimum; weekend bookers are available *then*, and pulling weekend
  bookings into same-/next-day slots raises show-up rates too.
- **Respond to leads in <1 minute.** Contacting a lead within 60 seconds gives a
  cited ~391% increase in likelihood to close (could "4X your business").
  ~50% of prospects buy from whoever responds first, not the "best" option.
  Beyond 5 minutes, close likelihood drops ~80%.
- **Offer "call now"** as an option in the funnel (highest possible intent);
  hire another rep if coverage is the bottleneck and the revenue lift exceeds
  the onboarding cost.
- **15-minute time slots** (not 1-hour) raise schedule and show rates; faking
  limited availability to look busy is dumb and just reduces convenience.
- **Off-the-call SOP:** between calls reps should call fresh leads, qualify
  them, and pull future appointments forward to same-day (higher show rates).
- **Feed the killers:** best leads to best closers, worst leads to worst closers
  — lowest net waste. Illustrated with the timeshare rep whose income went
  $250K→$3M and 5X'd the location when leads stopped being round-robined.
- **Coach show-up rates, not just close rates** — usually a bigger, easier win.
  Kudos/bonuses for highest show-up rate.
- **Reminder sequence:** automated reminders (that *look* automated) plus manual
  personalized touches at 24 hours before, morning-of, and 1 hour before; prefer
  iMessage/"blue" messages, voice memos and video.
- **Same-day / next-day availability only** — higher show rates; hire more reps
  to absorb demand.

### Sales training
- **Daily huddles = role play** (team-wide weakness); **1-on-1s** = one
  person-specific fix. Role play with fast feedback, **one piece of feedback at a
  time**; trainability is inversely correlated with feedback speed. Drill the
  correct line 5-6 times in quick succession once they get it.
- Sales manager must have **no ego**; the best closer is usually **not** the best
  manager (Phil Jackson vs. Michael Jordan analogy).
- **Competition = us vs. them**, not us vs. ourselves — pick a common enemy.
- **Game-tape review:** record every call; put sales + customer success (CS) on
  the same weekly review call so each sees the full customer lifecycle — reduces
  over-promising and back-outs. Learned from software/Alon.
- **Record yourself when "hot"** and analyze it — "good closers try to get hot;
  great closers never get cold" (systematize the hotness).
- In a slump, **read testimonials** to rebuild conviction; conviction produces
  correct tonality. Origin story: highest close rates fell on gym "weigh-out"
  days because reps had just watched people get results.

### Pre-sale (the sale starts at the click)
- The sale begins the moment the customer engages, not on the phone; continuity
  from ad → setter script → closer matters.
- **Edify the closer** (Cialdini) and edify the business (being "on the list").
- **BANT** qualification (IBM): Budget, Authority, Need, Timing — collect before
  asking for money.
- Favorite outbound script: *"Hey [business], I'm calling about [competitor],
  give me a call back."*
- Local-lead call opener: "[Name]? … It's Alex … [pause] … I'm calling because
  you opted in on Facebook…"; give two time options (2 or 4), not open-ended.
- **Prep 5 minutes** before every call; never make a customer repeat themselves
  (repeating *to* them is strongly positive).

### The sales conversation
- Open with **proof, promise, plan** (same as content) — sets the agenda and
  controls the frame; takes ~15-20 seconds.
- **Follow the f***ing script**: script adherence is drilled first; the first
  two-thirds should be near word-for-word. Tone matters beyond words — encode
  **emphasis (pauses), tonality (up/down), pacing (slow/fast)** into the script
  via bold/caps/underline/italics. ("I didn't say he hit his wife" emphasis demo.)
- **CLOSER framework** (transactional 1-2 call closes):
  - **C** – Clarify why they're here (tie to a problem, not "information").
  - **L** – Label them with the problem/gap (present vs. desired).
  - **O** – Overview past pain (the "pain cycle"; deprivation creates motivation;
    "the pain is the pitch"; quantify time/effort/status/cost-of-inaction).
  - **S** – Sell the vacation (Maui, not the TSA line) via a **three-pillar
    pitch** (humans think in threes; chunk up to 3; ≤2 min; statement + metaphor).
  - **E** – Explain away concerns (objection handling / closing).
  - **R** – Reinforce the decision (post-purchase handshake, not handoff; BAMFAM
    = book-a-meeting-from-a-meeting).
- Three-pillar pitch examples: fitness = nutrition + fitness + accountability;
  leads = timely + qualified + exclusive. Metaphors: three-legged stool,
  toothbrush (external → internal accountability), Amazing Race backseat driver,
  SEO vs. paid = investing vs. income.

### Closing / objection handling
- **Train back-to-front** (payment processing first, then the ask, then the
  script). Closing begins when someone says no; expect no; no ≠ failure.
- **When they say yes, shut up** and close ("Lego Star Wars" analogy).
- **Prep = smooth** — nothing a prospect says should surprise you.
- Rapid-fire stall questions: "What's your main concern? / What are you afraid of
  having happen? / What variables are you using to decide? / **What makes this a
  yes?** / What makes this a no?"
- **All-purpose closes** (80/20): "Do you think the reason you're on the phone
  today is that you've struggled to make this decision in the past?"; the
  **1-to-10 close** ("what would make it a 10?" / "why not a 1?").
- **Details are death traps** — never give a "blind answer"; ask a question about
  their question; **mechanic/secretary close** ("can't ethically answer before we
  look under the hood"); a detail objection is really a **preference objection**
  ("change the variables, change the outcome").
- **AAA / triple-A method**: Acknowledge → Associate (positive person/authority
  "foils") → Ask the next question.
- Laws/isms: **"He who asks the most times gets the most sales"**; the person
  asking is the person closing; **be like smoke** (no one can disagree with a
  question); childlike curiosity ("huh, that's interesting"); "you become rich by
  making them right."
- **Kill zombies early** — load BANT-type objections before asking for money
  (before stakes are on the table); same side of the table.
- **Ask permission before getting real** ("Can I be a coach instead of a
  friend?") or use foils if you lack personal authority.
- **Stack closes** — hit a hesitating prospect from multiple angles on the same
  obstacle.
- **Understand, don't memorize**: time = prioritization; decision-maker = support
  not permission; details = preference; money = value + resourcefulness (Ferrari
  for $5K); stalls = decision avoidance (fear or not knowing how to decide).
- **Zoom out when lost**; **best-case / worst-case** close (esp. with a
  guarantee); **when in doubt, say it back.**
- FAQ overcomes: cheap-competitor close ("if ours was the same as theirs, which
  would you pick? … that's why we're more"); ID close (in-person and Zoom);
  decision-maker 4-step (do-it-anyway smoke screen → foil their concern → past
  agreements → support not permission); "give me some time" ("what will you do
  instead — will it make you more than this?"); anti-guarantee; "let me use your
  free stuff first" (agree — either you succeed and hit a new problem, or you
  don't and we help); Enterprise/government sales are the exception (multi-threaded,
  committee decisions — much of this doesn't apply).

### Sales mindset (reframed as behaviors)
- **The person who cares most about the prospect wins**; keep the human first;
  learn to turn prospects away.
- **Volume negates luck** — skill and volume compound; the best salespeople love
  selling (they got skilled); gains come at the end of mastery.
- **Sales resumés don't matter** — prefer group interviews to resume screening;
  hire for the **lowest skill deficiency** (nuance on "attitude vs. aptitude").
- **Compensation = recruiting, not performance** — only comp change that reliably
  works is paying on **cash collected**; behavior is changed by fast feedback
  loops, not delayed rewards.
- **Sales and marketing are one continuum** (personalized marketing); put them
  under one Chief Revenue Officer. Bigger purchase = longer "runway" of education.
- **Say less** — aim for prospect ~two-thirds of talk time; let silence sit.
- **"Don't be cute"** (Long Island PE CEO) — do the fundamentals; two fat guys up
  the middle. Great salespeople never *don't* do the basics.
- **No for now ≠ no forever** — follow up every 3-6 months (memes, voice notes).
- **Be a trashman / yellow = gold** — take the worst leads to sharpen skill
  (lead scoring context).
- **Objections don't mean no** — "that's expensive" is often just a reaction.
- **We don't negotiate with terrorists** — never change price to close; favorite
  overcome to "can you do it for less?" is **"we could do it for more"**
  (counter-anchor); change *terms* (payment plan / feature downsell), not price.
- **People want to buy** — give them an excuse/justification.
- **Be kind, not nice** — closers ask hard questions because they care; avoid
  "commission breath."
