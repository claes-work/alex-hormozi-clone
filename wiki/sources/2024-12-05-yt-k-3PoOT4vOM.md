# How to Make Money So Fast It Feels ILLEGAL

## Summary

Solo Hormozi lecture laying out three "beliefs he had to break" to make disproportionate money fast: (1) sell on value, not cost, using the [[../topics/business/value-equation|value equation]]; (2) compress the cash conversion cycle via "client-financed acquisition" so customers pay for their own acquisition and the next customer's; and (3) understand "wealth alchemy" — the arbitrage between the permanent cost of acquiring a customer, the recurring revenue they generate, and the enterprise-value multiple applied to that revenue, which grows tax-free until sale. He illustrates each with his own history (United Fitness gyms, Gym Launch, licensing, Prestige Labs, ALAN).

## Key claims

- He claims that as a kid in his 20s he made more in a year than the CEOs of McDonald's, Ford, Motorola, Yahoo, and Ikea combined. (self-reported)
- Value equation has four components: **dream outcome**, **perceived likelihood of achievement** (inverse = risk), **time delay**, and **effort & sacrifice**. The most valuable offer is immediate, effortless, guaranteed, and exactly what the buyer wants.
- "You can't sell out of your wallet — you have to sell off the value you're creating for someone else." His gym-owner licensing event charged $42,000/year per attendee (~700+ people); competitors charged roughly a tenth.
- Price is the single largest lever on profit. His table example: repricing a $100 table (20% net) to a $1,000 value-positioned table yields ~$620 net — a 31x profit increase from a 10x price increase.
- A consulting client in his same space had similar CAC, sales team, and close rate, but Hormozi made ~8x more revenue per customer and ~50x more profit per customer.
- Cites a McKinsey survey: 85% of companies competed on price, only 1% won on price. Quotes Dan Kennedy: no reward for being the second-cheapest competitor.
- **Client-financed acquisition**: get first transaction to pay back CAC + cost of goods + the NEXT customer's CAC (e.g. make $250 to cover $100 CAC + $100 next CAC + $50 COGS), then repeat.
- Target formula: **2x CAC + COGS = 30-day cash collected** (≈ gross profit). Obsesses over 30 days because that is interest-free credit-card float available to anyone.
- His numbers (self-reported): Dec 2016 had $1,000 in the bank after losing everything twice; 100:1 return on ad spend; United Fitness opened a new location every ~6 months on cash flow with ~$5,000 down; Gym Launch did 32+ launches in ~18 months; licensing went 0 → $10M sales in 10 months, then ~$26M topline / ~$16M EBITDA in year two; Prestige Labs and ALAN each hit $1.7M/month (Prestige at launch, ALAN in six months).
- People love buying but hate being sold — make offers when convenient. Buyers enter a "hyper buying cycle" (marathon-runner buying shoes, then socks, shorts, coach, app). Weight-loss customers who declined gym services and were invited to a nutrition seminar spent more on average than those who bought gym services.
- **Wealth alchemy**: with 1/3 trial-to-customer and 1/3 customer-to-permanent, a $100 CAC becomes a ~$900 permanent CAC; at $100/mo ($1,200/yr) and a 10x topline SaaS multiple that's $12,000 enterprise value per customer. 10,000 customers = ~$9M spend → $12M annual revenue + $120M tax-free enterprise value.
- Enterprise-value growth on an unsold, appreciating business is tax-free and compounds; a $3M cash-flow business taxed 50% ($1.5M) but adding $120M in net worth effectively pays ~1.2% tax on the total gain.
- **Permanent customers** are the real leverage: two firms both reaching 300 customers, but the one that retains (100 + 100 + 100) is far more valuable than the churn factory that must keep doubling acquisition. Defines a permanent customer as one retained ~2+ years.
- Distinguishes **recurring** (subscription, e.g. Netflix) vs **reoccurring** (repeat purchase, e.g. Coca-Cola / Costco). Claims Starbucks LTV per customer is ~$14,000 at CAC similar to Dunkin — engineering repeat visits is the difference. (Note: the transcript reads "$114,000" once and "$14,000" twice; $14,000 is the intended figure.)
- Says he has gone through four "wealth warps" / periods of rapid net-worth change, most of his money made in short bursts followed by plateaus.
- Realized top Forbes-list founders (Musk, Zuckerberg) stayed in high-tax California because unrealized business-value growth is untaxed — a bigger lever than any tax loophole.
