# It's Okay to Not Know What You're Doing

## Summary

Alex argues that not knowing what one is doing is normal during growth. He broadens the
point into a critique of pathologizing ordinary human states such as tiredness,
sadness, hunger, frustration, or feeling misunderstood. He also makes a strong personal
argument against overuse of psychiatric medication, while acknowledging that some people
may have neurological or medical conditions that require care.

## Key claims (Alex's self-report and opinion, dated 2026-05-13)

- Alex says not knowing what one is doing is normal when attempting something that
  requires growth.
- He argues that many ordinary human states are mislabeled as problems rather than
  treated as part of life.
- He criticizes "should" statements and if-then happiness rules that make people believe
  something is wrong with them.
- He says his family background includes two physician parents and a physician
  grandfather.
- He says people close to him have been harmed by SSRI use or medication combinations.
- He expresses a strong anti-overmedication position but leaves room for some people to
  need medical or neurological treatment.
- He prefers addressing root causes over adopting a label as identity.
