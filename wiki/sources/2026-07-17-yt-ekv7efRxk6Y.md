# 'How Do You Keep Going When It Sucks?'

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A on entrepreneur psychology rather than tactics. The questioner
(introduces himself as **Cody Romness**) owns three gyms in Los Angeles, is working on a
fourth, does ~$2M revenue and wants $4M within three years. His actual question is not
about a bottleneck but about endurance: recalling Alex's story of being in a dark place
with Gym Launch before the accelerator model emerged, he asks how Alex kept the faith in
the darker moments — and whether you just keep moving the goalposts or the feeling ever
goes away.

Alex's answer is unusually personal and philosophical. He prefaces it as a "relatively
bleak perspective" (ignore it if your worldview works for you): his premise is that **life
is suffering, so suffering is a constant** — he was miserable during the year off he took
during the sale, miserable growing a business, miserable plateauing; "whether we're
shrinking, I'm doing nothing, we're growing, or plateauing, misery is just a constant." At
19 he adopted a crude mantra — "f*** happiness" — because he could never feel happy, and
shifted instead to **"I'll just be useful."** Usefulness works as a goal because it's
inherently service-oriented (you must be useful *to someone*) and it gives you something
to do (to be useful you have to learn). He then gets concrete about his own present:
**this is the hardest year he's had in the last 8 years** — he ranked his last 13 years
and this is the **fifth-worst overall, worst of the last eight**. For calibration: the
year he "lost everything twice" was only his second-worst; the worst was his first year
in business. Everything is relative — better problems now, but problems nonetheless.

He illustrates his behavior-first frame ("boiling everything down to: what will I do?")
with a fresh loss: **a bad business decision that just cost him $17 million in cash**.
His processing: nothing about his life changed — same food, same wife, same job, same
team, same building, same city — "there was a number that no longer is there," so **the
suffering is optional, voluntary**. This leads to the resilience principle Cody asks him
to repeat: **the less you change your lifestyle as your business succeeds, the more
resilient you are**, because fewer things can ever change anything about your life. His
image: set your anchor 100 feet below the surface — the choppiness of the waves doesn't
matter; chase the blips at the surface and you get higher highs but also the crashing of
the waves.

Two more philosophical layers: (1) a Buddhist point — suffering exists only because there
is capacity for joy; **the opposite of joy is apathy, not suffering** (love can flip to
hate fast, but you don't go from love to apathy overnight). (2) **Hedonic adaptation runs
in reverse too**: we adapt to suffering, so effective punishment — like effective pleasure
— requires changing *intensity and variety*, and "what's nasty about business is that it's
very good at changing the intensity and variety of punishment" ("I didn't know I could
f*** up *that* way"). The second lawsuit or employee betrayal hurts less; staying in the
same vein gets less painful but you also forfeit the hedonic pleasure of the novelty of
growth — hence both joy and suffering live in growth. Asked whether his mentors share this
operating perspective, he demurs: everyone has their own modus operandi; "my content is
never a sermon, it's always just a documentary." He admits the thing he struggles with
most is **nihilism — "why bother"** — since he doesn't need to do any of this; he does it
because being useful gives him "some inkling of meaning," and he is "definitely in a
season right now where it's tougher." The video ends with the standard
acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift CTA (10 stages, functions across product, marketing,
sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Suffering is a constant across every business state.** Whether shrinking, doing
  nothing, growing, or plateauing, misery is constant — it's just different. He took a
  year off during the sale and was miserable then too. He frames this explicitly as a
  bleak personal worldview, offered only if yours isn't working.
- **"Be useful" replaced happiness as his life mantra at age 19.** He gave up on feeling
  happy ("f*** happiness") and chose usefulness instead: it's inherently
  service-oriented (useful *to someone*) and self-propelling (to be useful you must learn
  things). It has served him well and gives him "some inkling of meaning."
- **2026 is his hardest year in the last 8 — fifth-worst of the last 13.** He ranked his
  last 13 years: this is the fifth-worst overall and the worst of the last eight. The
  year he lost everything twice was his second-worst; the worst was his first year in
  business. Everything is relative: better problems now, but problems nonetheless.
- **He recently lost $17 million in cash on a bad business decision.** His processing
  frame: nothing about his daily life changed (same food, same wife, same job, same team,
  same building, same city) — only "a number that no longer is there." Therefore the
  suffering is optional and voluntary — a choice.
- **Lifestyle anchoring = resilience.** The less you change your lifestyle as your
  business succeeds, the more resilient you are, because business volatility can no
  longer change anything about your life. Analogy: an anchor set 100 ft below the
  surface doesn't feel the chop; live at the surface and you get higher highs but also
  crashing waves.
- **Behavior is his overarching frame for reality.** Everything boils down to "what will
  I do, and how does this affect what I do?" His two triage questions in a setback:
  (1) what does this change about my life? (2) what am I going to do instead? The answer
  has almost never been to change what he's doing — it's "I will learn and I will get
  better."
- **The opposite of joy is apathy, not suffering.** (Attributed by Alex to Buddhist
  philosophy.) Suffering exists because there is capacity for joy; one can't exist
  without the other. Love flips to hate quickly, but never to apathy overnight. He
  suffers in the game because it also contains great moments.
- **Hedonic adaptation works in reverse — we adapt to suffering too.** To keep punishing
  (or pleasing) someone effectively you must vary intensity and variety; business is
  "very good at changing the intensity and variety of punishment." The second employee
  betrayal or lawsuit hurts less. Staying in the same vein becomes less painful (fewer
  novel wounds) but forfeits the hedonic pleasure of the novelty of growth — you miss
  both sides, which is why growth contains both joy and suffering.
- **"Never a sermon, always a documentary."** His content is a record of what worked for
  him, not a prescription; everyone has their own modus operandi. He has never claimed
  to be the happiest guy.
- **His biggest personal struggle is nihilism.** "Why bother" — he doesn't need to do any
  of this; he does it to be useful. He has bad days and is currently in a tougher season.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz
  that locates a business and prescribes actions per function (product, marketing, sales,
  customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance); thank-you page books a call, with an
  in-person Vegas session if they can help.
