# Why You Should Want People to Copy You

## Summary

Alex argues that imitation is less dangerous than irrelevance. Copycats can see the
visible "what," but not the underlying "why," so they lag when conditions change. He
uses Gym Launch's R&D process as an example of staying ahead through tests, failed
experiments, and understanding the system's physics.

## Key claims (Alex's advice, dated 2026-05-07)

- Alex says people should worry more when no one copies them than when many people do.
- He says copycats rely on the source and do not understand the underlying reasons.
- He says copying the visible formula does not teach someone how to iterate when
  conditions change.
- He self-reports keeping a list of people who tried to take Gym Launch material and
  sell it as their own until the list became too long.
- He says none of those imitators reached even a tenth of Gym Launch's size.
- He says Gym Launch stayed ahead because it invested in R&D and tested plays every 14
  days.
- He self-reports spending $50K to $100K on some tests.
- He says many tests were worse than the control, and that negative result still saved
  licensees money.
- He says the real advantage is understanding the system's dynamics and staying in the
  weeds.
- He says second through tenth place may copy number one, but the winner receives the
  spoils.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "They only know the what, but not the why."

> "The day that no one copies you is far far more frightening."

> "You have to presume that you win in the weeds."
