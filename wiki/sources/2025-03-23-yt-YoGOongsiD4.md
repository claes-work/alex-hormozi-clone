# Discover Why Alex Hormozi Chose to Invest in Skool

## Summary

Solo explainer monologue in which Alex Hormozi walks through his investment
thesis for becoming co-owner of Skool — which he frames as his single largest
investment to date, made only after ~6 months of talking with the founder. He
uses a "Lollapalooza effect" lens (crediting Charlie Munger): the decision is
driven not by one factor but by several stacked ones — a huge education market
undergoing a crisscross shift from formal to alternative/online education, his
own background serving creators, mission alignment, and unusually strong
underlying business metrics (his "golden ratio" of referrals to churn). He
presents the case as a reusable decision-making framework, not just a Skool ad.

## Key claims

- **Why he invested (stacked reasons / "Lollapalooza effect").** Alex says
  explosive opportunities come from multiple factors that each add an
  incremental boost but multiply into an insane outcome; he sees that with Skool.
  He attributes the "Lollapalooza effect" framing to Charlie Munger.
- **Big-picture #1 — the education market and its crisscross.** Education is
  ~$10T globally / ~$1.3T US (self-reported, "directionally correct" figures).
  It splits into formal education (2-year + 4-year degrees, ~$850B US) and
  alternative / e-learning / online education (~$200–250B). Alex claims formal
  is *shrinking ~6%/yr* (losing ~$50B/yr) while alternative is *growing ~20%/yr*
  (gaining ~$40B/yr) — a near-symmetric "crisscross" transfer of buying behavior
  he wants to be on the right side of.
- **Thesis on the shift.** People increasingly see that formal education doesn't
  deliver the promised ROI, and can instead learn income-producing skills
  directly from creators. Money is being pushed down from the "mediocre middle"
  of universities (he exempts top schools "for a while") to creators teaching a
  niche skill. Example: a hairstylist learns Instagram from a creator for ~$1,000
  vs. two years and ~$15K at school — "the ROI is not there."
- **Big-picture #2 — his personal fit.** His first two companies he sold at scale
  (Gym Launch, Prestige Labs) served creators/owners; earlier companies were only
  "low seven figure" exits. He came from a direct-response marketing background
  selling media and licensing to brick-and-mortar gyms, so he knows this model.
- **Big-picture #3 — audience/mission fit.** His audience is split between
  business owners and aspiring business owners; his mission is to "make real
  business accessible for everyone," which he says aligns with Skool's mission to
  make business fun and easy for everyone.
- **Education is the un-disrupted industry.** Hotels got Airbnb, taxis got Uber,
  but "no one's disrupted education" — it's still completely fragmented with no
  single place everyone goes to learn. His bet: Skool will be that company.
- **Why Skool specifically (#4) — passive growth litmus test.** He won't promote
  a company that only grows while advertising and declines when ads stop; he
  wants passive/organic growth.
- **The "golden ratio."** Passive growth rate = % of customers who refer a new
  customer per period ÷ % who churn/exit per period. If 10% refer and 10% leave,
  the business stays flat; if 20% refer and 10% leave, it doubles every interval
  regardless of size. Most businesses have ~2% referring vs. ~10% leaving, so
  they must fill the bucket with ads — a sign they don't know how to build a good
  product. Once referrals exceed churn, *then* you "pour gas on the flame" with
  marketing to scale the doubling on a bigger base.
- **Skool's metrics.** He says he was "blown away" — he'd never seen a company
  with metrics as good as Skool's, despite reviewing "several thousand businesses
  a month" that reach out to Acquisition.com for minority/majority investment.
  Skool's passive growth rate is what most intrigued him.
- **Brand risk.** Because he's putting his brand behind it and endorsing it, the
  product had to be genuinely good — if referred people have a bad experience,
  they blame the referrer (him). He says he'd been "looking for years" for
  something that met his standard to promote; nothing had until Skool.
