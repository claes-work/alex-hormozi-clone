# How to Give Yourself Good Advice

## Summary
Interview clip in which Alex explains his self-coaching method: consulting an imagined **85-year-old future self he calls "Solomon."** The name comes from the **Solomon Paradox** — the Biblical King Solomon gave brilliant counsel to other kingdoms yet ran his own life into shambles (infidelity, a broken relationship with his son). The paradox: humans are better at *giving* advice than *following* it. Alex frames good counsel as requiring three things simultaneously — **aligned incentives, competence, and complete context** — and argues that the higher you climb in business, the harder it gets to find any one person with all three. Executive coaches/therapists failed him on all three axes: their financial incentive was to keep him paying (misaligned), he burned ~60 minutes just giving context, and on business problems he was more competent than they were (he's running a ~$200M/year portfolio; a solopreneur therapist can't advise on resource allocation at that scale). His solution: since he already gives good advice to others, "maybe I have the answers and I just need to package it differently." So he holds written, chat-style back-and-forth "sessions" with Solomon — the first hour of every Monday, an ongoing document — because that future self is the only counselor with aligned incentives, complete context, and equal competence. Sessions run 7-45 minutes (no fixed hour, no commute, no context dump); he keeps hitting enter until he reaches the root of the issue and gets a line that "closes the loop." Worked example: a project he spent two years on collapsed because of an incompetent (not malicious) vendor he'd paid several hundred thousand dollars specifically to insure against exactly that outcome — costing him **~$15M**. Solomon's resolving line: *"I am the asset you are building. And I am satisfied with the effort you put in. And that is enough."* The reframe: the person he is becoming is the ultimate asset ("billion is just a reflection of the asset I'm trying to build"), and the only fair benchmark is not other people (a high achiever beats them anyway) but his future self, who alone knows how much harder he could have tried. He ties this to the *Leads* book presentation — 200 hours over 28 days, so he felt no pre-stage nerves because there was "nothing more I could have done." On the $15M loss he adds: reasonable decisions can still lose; bigger stakes just add zeros ("if you want to make a billion dollars, you have to be willing to lose 100 million"); over-vetting everything tips into value-destroying micromanagement (cites Ray Dalio on not regretting missed opportunities). He says he falls short of Solomon's standard *every single day* (including being kinder to Leila). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
