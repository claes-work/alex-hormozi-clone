# How to Choose the Right Business to Start

## Summary
In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi answers how much self-awareness is needed to decide what business to pursue. Rather than prescribing a specific business, he reframes the question as one of first principles: business has foundational truths that stay constant while environmental conditions change, so the entrepreneur must apply those truths to their own current context of skills, resources, and assets. He argues that time is the primary currency everyone trades, and the goal is the highest return on that time. He lays out three functions every business must perform — attract attention, convert attention, deliver — and says each should be built for maximum leverage. He ties this to the two theses behind acquisition.com's logo (a fulcrum for leverage; supply and demand), advocates skill acquisition when you start with nothing, and closes with a plug for a free 10-stage scaling roadmap at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-12. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-12) There are foundational truths of business that stay constant, while the conditions of the environment change; you have to apply those truths to whatever the current condition is.
- (2026-02-12) "First principles" is a term far more people say than actually understand.
- (2026-02-12) [attribution: Andrew Wilkinson, quoted by Alex] Alex cites a tweet he loves by Andrew Wilkinson: "every entrepreneur ever: here's the winning number for my lottery ticket" — the point being the drawing already happened, so you can't cash that ticket in (i.e., past-tense success stories can't be replicated by copying).
- (2026-02-12) Assuming a purely economic goal of making money, time is the primary currency you trade, and you make dollars over a period of time.
- (2026-02-12) Alex rejects the "never trade time for dollars" advice — everyone trades time for dollars; some people are just more efficient at it. Even exchanges not denominated in time still occur over time.
- (2026-02-12) What you are seeking is the highest return on your time.
- (2026-02-12) Within a business context there are three things that must occur: attract attention, convert attention, and deliver something for that attention. You want as much leverage as possible in each — as little time as possible required for the most output.
- (2026-02-12) Acquisition.com is built on two primary theses represented in its logo (a triangle): a fulcrum for leverage, and, inside it, supply and demand. Supply and demand are the two foundational principles of business; leverage is how you get as much out of it as possible.
- (2026-02-12) Assets can also be skills and resources — whatever you have available. If you have nothing, all you have is your brain, your hands, and the time you can put toward learning something.
- (2026-02-12) Skill acquisition is one of the primary things you can do — informally or alternatively educating yourself (not formally) on super-tactical things.
- (2026-02-12) You have to have something to sell (the offer), and ideally the offer has as much leverage as possible — e.g., software or media, which you can "cut once, sell a thousand times."
- (2026-02-12) On conversion, an automated mechanism (a checkout page or video sales letter where people buy without a phone salesperson) has higher leverage; adding a phone salesperson lowers leverage. You want the highest-leverage opportunity.
- (2026-02-12) On advertising/deliverability, reaching people one-on-one is lower leverage than making one piece of content that a million people see. Over time you move from low leverage to high leverage — same inputs, significantly more output.
- (2026-02-12) The real question someone is asking when choosing a business is "how do I get the most for what I put in," and you have to reason within your current context of skills, resources, and assets to derive the solution for you.
- (2026-02-12) [promotional] Alex offers a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ (which he says fewer than 1% of companies finish), broken down by eight business functions — the constraint at each stage, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate — available at acquisition.com/roadmap.
