# Why I Have High Standards

## Summary
Short clip (Alex as a guest in conversation; the host contributes a beverage-can anecdote and brief affirmations). Alex defends high standards as the engine of great work. His core argument: **"control freak" is a label low-standards people use for high-standards people** — the accusations (control freak, micromanager, too picky) are "stones" thrown by outsiders to change your behavior and get you to stop; the correct response is to say "yes," because the real standard of "right" is simply *right — done without error*, and it only *seems* insane because people's margin for what counts as "right" differs. He ties great products not to a "silver bullet" but to **"a hundred golden BBs"** — hundreds of tiny improvements a person who doesn't care won't do (packaging, weight, color scheme, shelf placement, RGB, how a name looks on hats/shirts/sites) because they're "checking a box" instead of making something people love. He argues the best work is **made for yourself, not an audience**: making it for yourself feels selfish but actually serves the thousands of people just like you, whereas committee "commercial work" becomes a "Frankenstein" that resonates with no one because it needs one vision. He reframes high standards as **a gift** — for a while he felt ashamed of it ("you feel like a bother"), but realized it's "probably one of the only reasons" he's had any success; using that frame "given me permission to be me in an unreasonable world." Closes on **volume**: for his book launch he practiced the full presentation three times a day for 30 days (100 run-throughs, recording and editing himself each time), so the live delivery to ~half a million people looked effortless — "well, I did it a hundred times." The first five reps make you "great"; the next 95 make it a "masterpiece," and that's where most people fall off. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues "control freak" is a word people with low standards use to describe people with high standards; the person isn't a control freak or anxious — "you just want it done right the first time" and "you care."
- As of March 2026, he frames wanting something done a specific way as pulling an idea "from your mind into reality"; if it isn't done that way, it isn't what you imagined, so outsiders "throw stones" (control freak / micromanager / high standards) to try to make you stop.
- As of March 2026, his stance on those accusations is to answer "yes," because with enough will the thing gets done his way regardless — so it's less painful to do it right the first time (you may have to redo it three or four times, but you'll still end up doing it his way).
- As of March 2026, he claims great human achievements came from "one man or woman who had an idea and just wouldn't let people shake it from them."
- As of March 2026, he says the standard of "right" isn't actually insane — "it's just right… done without error"; what varies between people is the *margin* they'll accept as "right."
- As of March 2026, he repeats his product thesis: "there's no silver bullets, only hundreds of golden BB's" — great products come from a hundred tiny improvements (shipping, weight, color scheme, shelf placement, appearance on hats/shirts/sites, RGB) that people who are "trying to check a box" won't do.
- As of March 2026, he argues the best work is made *for yourself*, not an audience: commercial committee work "rinse and recycle stuff that actually solves no one's problems"; making it for yourself feels selfish "but when you make it for yourself, you actually make it for everyone" because thousands of people share your depth of understanding.
- As of March 2026, he says a big project needs many people but still "one vision," or it becomes a "Frankenstein" where everyone copy-pastes past work and it resonates with no one.
- As of March 2026, he reframes high standards as a "gift": it makes more work for everyone else (so the majority are reasonably opposed to it), "but the product of that work is so much better" and is what makes work last and make an impact.
- As of March 2026, he says he felt ashamed of his high standards for a while ("you feel like a bother"), and realized "probably last year" that not stopping is "probably one of the only reasons why I've had any success."
- As of March 2026, on volume: for his book launch he practiced the full presentation three times a day, every day for 30 days — 100 full-length run-throughs, recording himself, watching and editing in real time, then redoing it — so it "came out… flawlessly" live to ~half a million people; people called him "a natural" but "I did it a hundred times."
- As of March 2026, he claims the first five repetitions make you feel "so much better," but "the difference between five and the next 95 times is what goes from being great to being a masterpiece" — that next 95 is where most people fall off and why most people aren't world-class.
