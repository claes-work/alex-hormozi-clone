# How to master the Game of Business

## Summary

In a short interview clip on The Skool School channel, Alex Hormozi explains why business
stays motivating (it rewards skill, so building skill compounds the reward) and why a
tight feedback loop on repeated reps beats mindless volume. He then walks through how he
would rebuild a business from zero on Skool with no reputation: get five people results
for free, then monetize with high-ticket one-on-one/small-group offers before layering in
a low-cost continuity membership funded by ads. A host from The Skool School asks the
questions and reflects back.

## Key claims

- Business keeps you coming back because it rewards you when you do it well, the same way
  food rewards eating — motivation follows reward, not the other way around.
- Loving business is largely a **skill deficiency** problem: the more skilled you are at
  something, the more ways you have found for it to reward you (his writing example).
- The goal in business is to "pay down your ignorance as fast as humanly possible," which
  means failing a lot early while keeping your eye on the bigger prize.
- Volume alone is not enough — you must also get better. Posting 100 pieces of content
  without ever reviewing which performed is wasted; by math, some outperform others.
- The method: after 100 reps, study the best- and worst-performing pieces, do more of what
  worked and less of what didn't, repeat for the next 100 — that is a feedback cycle.
- If you start a feedback cycle and don't stop, over a long enough timeline you win.
- Beginner playbook for communities: do outreach until you get your first five people,
  serve them for free, document it, then repeat with another five.
- You control when you start monetizing — keep serving free batches "until you get bored of
  getting people results"; boredom with results is the signal you're good enough to charge.
- Rebuilding from zero on Skool with no reputation: reach out to people you know and offer a
  **professional skill** — usually just whatever job you've had (accountant → taxes, etc.),
  because the marketplace already paid you for it, which proves there's value.
- He would sell **one-time offers over memberships early** because they generate cash
  faster: one-on-one or small-group bundles of calls priced roughly **$500 to $5,000**.
- Then add continuity (a membership), and use the cash generated to advertise — driving
  traffic mostly to the continuity and upselling over the phone.
- You must be willing to get on the phone. "Not scalable at $10 a month" is a bad objection
  when you have no members — your time is free; you can be time-rich or money-rich, not both.
- Simplest funnel: free community → one-time upsell inside it → drive people to the free
  thing → provide value → upsell them into the one-time offer.
