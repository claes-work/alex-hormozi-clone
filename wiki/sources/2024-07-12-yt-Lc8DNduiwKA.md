# 13 Ways to DESTROY Your Competition (Legally)

## Summary
In this solo presentation, Alex Hormozi lays out 13 "competitive moats" — structural advantages that make a business harder for competitors to disrupt and that (unlike most businesses) get stronger rather than weaker as the company scales. He argues you need only one of these woven into the DNA of your business to build a massive, defensible company, and for each moat he pairs a large-corporate example (Apple, Google, De Beers, Amazon, Coca-Cola, Waste Management) with a small-business adaptation. He closes by tying all 13 back to the Greek root of "monopoly" (mono = one, polein = to sell): every moat is ultimately a way to make it so only you can sell.

## Key claims (the 13 competitive tactics)
1. **Network effects** — a business whose value increases as more customers join (customer base like Facebook/telecom, product ecosystems like Apple, or buyer groups like a dentist association gaining buying power). Opposite of a traditional service business, which degrades as more customers are added.
2. **Exclusive control over a resource or technology** — owning scarce physical supply (De Beers controlling diamond supply to keep prices high) or proprietary tech/data/patents (Google search's 20-year data lead as an insurmountable barrier).
3. **Government regulations and licensing** — government grants/monopolies (Amtrak, utilities like Con Edison), licenses (casino, lottery, liquor licenses) that legally exclude competitors. Simply knowing how to navigate the regulation is itself an advantage.
4. **Economies of scale** — a lower per-unit cost basis from volume lets you undercut and profit where smaller competitors can't (low-cost-leader strategy). Cites his software company (formerly ~4,000–5,000 appointments/day) getting cheaper per-message costs than any new entrant.
5. **Vertical integration** — owning the chain from supply to distribution (Tesla, De Beers) to capture margin at every step, control quality, and reduce reliance/risk on outside vendors. His self-publishing (owning printing/distribution of his own books) as a 1.0 example.
6. **Strong brand identity and customer loyalty** (his stated favorite) — brand becomes synonymous with the product (Google/search, Kleenex, Band-Aid, Nike); lets you charge premium prices at higher conversion. Buildable with skill and time rather than capital.
7. **Strategic pricing** — predatory pricing (temporarily undercutting/copying features into a bundle at the same price, e.g. Microsoft, Instagram Stories vs. Snapchat) and price discrimination (charging different people different prices by spending power, e.g. airlines). He ethically prefers tweaking the offer slightly (e.g. adding a cocktail) so the two products aren't identical.
8. **Exclusive contracts** — locking up supply (Hershey's cocoa), customers (Lockheed Martin / government defense contracts), or distribution (Coca-Cola/Pepsi in every retailer). Small businesses win these by adding perks bigger players won't.
9. **Huge capital requirements** — businesses that cost a fortune to enter (SpaceX, ExxonMobil oil drilling, pharmaceutical drug development) automatically exclude ~99.9% of competitors. Raising or saving capital lets you pick less-crowded vehicles.
10. **Intellectual property** — patents/copyrights/trademarks (his book can't be legally copied; Mickey Mouse) and trade secrets (Coca-Cola recipe; claims Monster paid ~$500M to buy its own recipe). Enables royalty/licensing businesses (Oreo licensing its brand/flavor for ~8% of revenue).
11. **Acquisitions and mergers** — you don't destroy competitors, you assimilate them; roll-ups create economies of scale, vertical integration, or distribution control (Meta buying Instagram/WhatsApp; Waste Management built by buying fragmented routes). Advises a $60M roofing client to buy his lead-gen agencies rather than keep buying leads.
12. **Innovative business model** — "find what everyone wants, make it better, give it away for free, and monetize another way" (freemium): Google Docs vs. Microsoft, Spotify. Cost to acquire = cost to deliver ÷ conversion rate. His gym examples (free gym membership tied to a meals subscription, or free gym as a lead magnet for personal training).
13. **Control over distribution channels** — like exclusive contracts but stronger because you own (not rent) the channel, so competitors can't break away and you profit from others' business too (Amazon Prime + its own delivery network, taking half to two-thirds of third-party sellers' revenue). Small-scale: dominate local distribution or own a specific market segment's relationships.
