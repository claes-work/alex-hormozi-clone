# The One Thing Killing Your Business: Lack of Focus

## Summary

In this extended coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a publishing / book-adventure / business-school founder who has many promising projects and asks for clarity. Alex says she must pick the "10-year-old" business: the one customers already come for and that has the best economics. He frames focus as trading novelty for loyalty, says life will not give a person what they want if they want everything, and gives a tactical rule: channel creative energy into marketing, keep a list of ideas, and only change the business when the expected risk-adjusted gain exceeds the disruption cost.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Publishing / book-adventure founder** - the coachee. Her businesses, children, TV show, event sales, and personal life are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Talented founders have more opportunities they could crush, which means more things they must say no to.
- Continuing to chase novelty preserves excitement but costs ultimate impact and business size.
- Building something large requires giving up the novelty of new projects.
- If customers consistently come for one thing, that is probably the business to focus on.
- A founder cannot pursue every opportunity; to make one opportunity worth pursuing, they must say no to nearly all others.
- Alex says he stayed stuck in multi-year plateaus because he wanted to pursue everything.
- Focus requires trading novelty for loyalty to the business, similar to choosing depth in relationships over novelty.
- Alex says he may only have two or three big entrepreneurial seasons left and currently plans for Acquisition.com to be the long-term swing.
- Creative energy should be channeled into parts of the business that need novelty, especially marketing/content, instead of into new business models.
- Alex says he puts his ADD into content so it distracts him from damaging the business.
- Any operational change may create an immediate performance drop; Alex uses a rough 20% disruption cost as a decision razor.
- He only considers changes with a risk-adjusted expected improvement large enough to justify the guaranteed disruption.
- Founders should keep a private list of ideas, let them season for weeks, and only bring them to the team if they still look materially worth the disruption.
