# What It Takes to Build a Good Product

## Summary
In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi explains why Skool (the community platform he co-owns) is succeeding, framing product quality as "100 golden BBs and no silver bullets" — hundreds of small details rather than one big feature. He lays out his philosophy for building strong online communities: approximate access at a fractionalized price, optimize for value-per-second rather than seconds-of-value, and deliver fewer-but-better things because overwhelm is the top cancellation reason. An interviewer (a Skool community owner) prompts the discussion; only Alex's statements are treated as persona training data.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that great products come from "100 golden BBs and no silver bullets" — hundreds of small details compounded, not a single decisive feature.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he invested in and became co-owner of Skool after seeing (as "a business guy") strong referral rates and consistent monthly growth "with zero marketing" for years, which signaled a real product; he "wrote a massive check" as a result.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames communities as "fractionalized access" — members get a small slice of the owner at a much lower price than one-on-one would cost.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues good communities optimize "value per second rather than seconds of value"; stuffing in 100 features makes members feel they get less if they only use one, versus using one of one.
- As of March 2026, Alex names overwhelm as the number-one reason people cancel communities; the fix is fewer, better, high-consumption offerings — each worth the price on its own.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes Skool Games' structure: one weekly call plus one 8-hour monthly deep-dive training with the top-winning community owners (unlocked after getting three members into your own community).
- As of March 2026, Alex credits Skool's engagement to gamification built by a dev team (led by "Daniel") from a gaming background (Riot Games); points for engagement incentivize high-quality posts and lift overall community quality.
- As of March 2026, Alex says Skool's origin traces to co-founder Sam Ovens (of Consulting.com), who saw buyers watch a few course videos then live in a poorly-performing Facebook group — motivating a platform that unifies courses, scheduling, community, leaderboards, and gamification.
- As of March 2026, Alex favors immortalizing the best community/member posts as "artifacts" tagged into the course curriculum, and notes many people prefer reading a concise post to watching a rambling video.
- Caller claim (unverified): one Skool super-user with tens of thousands of members says she pays ~$400,000/year in support on Facebook because posts get ~5% reach, and that Skool's 100% post reach would let her cut that cost.
- Caller claim (unverified): Skool has grown its community with observed positive behavior and "none" of the hate seen in some communities, with members helping each other within an hour.
