# You Always Have Debt

## Summary
Live talk to a "woo woo"-leaning entrepreneur community (audience polled by revenue band: pre-revenue up to $1M+). Alex opens with his **"you always incur debt"** framing: starting a business is never debt-free, so the real question is *which kind* of debt you take on. He names three types beyond the obvious **financial debt** — **management debt** (hiring people underskilled for the opportunity) and **technical debt** (lacking the infrastructure to build the business on) — and advises taking on the debt that is "easiest to repay as fast as possible," which gives more levers to structure the business toward the goal. He then maps advice to revenue altitude: **sub-$1M is all marketing and sales** (product "matters significantly less" at that level because you're too small to move word-of-mouth — he says bluntly you could "scam people for a million dollars a year for the rest of your life" on marketing/sales alone, which is why some low-level advice is bad), while **on the longest time horizon the only thing that matters is product**. He describes his own 180-degree evolution: began obsessed with sales → discovered marketing (sales one-to-many) → realized marketing/sales is "a perpetual hamster wheel because nothing stacks," and that real wealth requires **accumulation** — getting paid today for work done years ago, only possible via a product people still use or praise. His current build model: don't add gas (marketing/sales) until the product is so good it generates referrals every month and grows on its own, *then* layer marketing and sales on top. Practical middle section: **do the math / reverse-engineer the target** ($1M/yr = $20k/week = ~$3k/day), the world's-simplest growth formula **units sold × lifetime value**, and a calendar test — if an activity isn't getting you more customers or making them worth more, stop. Closes with a concrete beginner outreach playbook (build a contact list from phone, old emails/sent messages, and social followers; ~100 outreaches/day ≈ 5 hours; founder's job in the beginning is promotion / pulling the business out of obscurity) referencing his Leads book's "eight ways to let people know about your stuff." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, starting any business always incurs debt; the strategic question is *which type* — Alex names **financial debt**, **management debt** (hiring people underskilled for the opportunity), and **technical debt** (lacking the infrastructure to build on).
- As of March 2026, the rule is to take on "the debt that is easiest to repay as fast as possible"; thinking this way from the onset gives more levers to structure the business toward the goal.
- As of March 2026, goals of ~$100k–$1M/year are achievable owning 100% of the equity and just developing skills — "I don't think you need much more than that."
- As of March 2026, sub-$1M "everything comes down to marketing and sales"; product "matters significantly less at that level" because you aren't big enough to move word-of-mouth given how big the world is.
- As of March 2026, purely on marketing/sales skill "you could pretty much scam people for a million dollars a year for the rest of your life" — which is why some people who make around that level give bad advice ("that's all you need"), true for them but not for someone building something lasting.
- As of March 2026, on the longest time horizon "the only thing that matters is product" — how good the thing is, and whether people feel they're "always on the better end of the deal."
- As of March 2026, Alex has gone "180 degrees" from his start: sales → marketing (sales one-to-many, "sales on steroids") → realizing it's "a perpetual hamster wheel because nothing stacks."
- As of March 2026, building something big requires **accumulation**: "I want to get paid today for work I did three years ago," only possible via a product a past customer still uses or still praises; otherwise you're "always hunting every month to find your next nut." "Everyone's going to always work, but you won't always accumulate."
- As of March 2026, his current build model: don't put gas (marketing/sales) on it until the product is right, everyone loves it, referrals come every month and it grows without marketing/sales — *then* add marketing and sales on top.
- As of March 2026, many entrepreneurs (especially "woo woo" ones) skip the math; you can't "woo woo your way to money" — start by reverse-engineering what you want the numbers to look like ($1M/yr ≈ $20k/week ≈ $3k/day).
- As of March 2026, the "world's simplest formula" for growth is **number of units sold × lifetime value**; to grow you either sell more units or make customers worth more — everything else overcomplicates it.
- As of March 2026, time is the beginner's main asset; audit the calendar — if an activity isn't getting more customers or making them worth more, stop; "if you work every hour of the day and you're not making more money, you're working on the wrong stuff."
- As of March 2026, if you're not making money you can "probably eliminate 90%" of what you're doing because it clearly isn't working.
- As of March 2026 (per his Leads book), there are **eight ways to let people know about your stuff** — the same eight at $1M as at $1B; four you do yourself, four you use other people for.
- As of March 2026, the cheapest first move is warm outreach asking contacts if they *know anyone* interested (lower pressure; "nine times out of ten" the person is themselves interested); build a list by exporting phone contacts, old email sent-messages, and social followers/DM contacts.
- As of March 2026, Alex advocates "violence in terms of action" — ~100 outreaches/day (~5 hours, ~20/hour); "welcome to business… and being broke at the same time." He did this himself from ~4–5am until ~10am before starting his day when he had less means.
- As of March 2026, "your job as the founder in the beginning is always promotion" — the business only exists in your mind and you have to make it exist outside; letting people know you exist pulls it out of obscurity.
