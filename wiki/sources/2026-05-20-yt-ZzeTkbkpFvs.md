# The Problem With the Agency Model

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says agency models are easy to reach roughly $1 million to $10 million, but
become harder beyond that because of human capital and low-quality avatars. The way up is
scaling the avatar: moving from very small businesses to customers with more headcount,
lower churn, higher ability to pay, and the internal capacity to use the agency's narrow
service.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Agencies can be comparatively easy to get to $1 million, $3 million, or even $10
  million per year.
- Beyond that, agencies become difficult because they rely on human capital and skilled
  delivery.
- Many early agencies serve very small and small businesses.
- To study an industry, Alex looks at what the biggest businesses in that industry do.
- The biggest agencies tend to serve Fortune 100-style clients with large contracts,
  long terms, and low churn.
- Scaling an agency is often scaling the avatar.
- A narrow agency service should be sold to customers who can already handle the adjacent
  functions, such as sales.
- If the agency wants to take over more of the client's business, the avatar may be too
  weak.
- Every business incurs debt: talent debt, tech debt, financial debt, or reputational
  debt.
- Sometimes a young business sells weak customers to pay bills, but it should draw the
  line as soon as possible.
- A more correct avatar can support higher prices, less operational drag, better morale,
  stickier relationships, and more profit even with slower revenue growth.
- Counterintuitive moves are often required because the intuitive ones have already been
  tried.
- Growing may require selling fewer people with higher qualifications.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The process of agency scaling is very much scaling the avatar."

> "You want to incur the debt that's the easiest to pay back."

> "For me to grow I need to sell fewer people and have higher qualifications."
