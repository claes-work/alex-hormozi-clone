# The Obvious List Nobody Does

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says people usually do not fail because they lack a hidden magical list;
they fail because they do not execute the obvious list. He connects this to fitness,
investing, income, and the Solomon paradox: people can often give good advice to others
but fail to follow the same advice themselves. His preferred method is to ask what an
80-year-old version of oneself would tell the current self to do.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The magic is not in unknown work; it is in the obvious work people already know they
  should do.
- In many situations, the real question is why someone is not doing the obvious thing.
- If a person is not willing to do the research or hold for the right time horizon, Alex
  says they should not invest and should instead increase active income.
- People are often better at giving advice than following it.
- Projecting into an older future self can separate the advice from current excuses.
- The clues of success are in plain sight; the price tag is the part people resist.
- People may not give up on dreams so much as decide the cost is too expensive.
- It is acceptable to decide a dream is not worth the price and stop chasing it.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "It's an obvious list nobody does."

> "The clues of success are in plain sight."

> "They see what it takes to get their dreams and then decide it's too expensive."
