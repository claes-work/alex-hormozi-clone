# Act with Urgency

## Summary
Solo monologue aimed at entrepreneurs. Alex argues that a defining habit of richer and more successful people is that they **act with urgency**, and that you can gauge how successful someone will be by how quickly they turn thought into action — "the gap between thoughts and action." He frames personal potency as the ability to shrink that gap: the hypothetical extreme is a god figure for whom thought and reality are the same (he thinks a universe and it appears), and we approximate power by minimizing the time between thinking, deciding, and doing. He notes that once no more information can be collected, you should decide — and decision *is* elimination (the word "decide" from Latin *decidere*, "to cut off"): ask what you're eliminating by choosing. His tactical hack: whenever you say "I'll get this done by end of week," ask "can I do it by end of day?" — a ~7x increase in pace; end-of-month → end-of-week is 4x; end-of-day → end-of-hour compounds further, and some people work 100x faster than you. He cites Leila's rule (from a mentor): "if it's a 5-minute task and you have 5 minutes, do it now" — she clears her plate instantly rather than letting things accumulate. He then attacks a "falseness propagated by social media" — the "you're young, you've got time" message — because it gives permission to be lazy. He counters with a mortality/time argument: tomorrow isn't guaranteed (a healthy peer his age just died of stomach cancer); only 3 in 10,000 live to 100; the US male median lifespan is 74, so the literal midpoint is ~37 — but because subjective time accelerates with age (one year is 20% of a 5-year-old's life vs. 2.5% at 40, roughly 9x faster), the *felt* midpoint is closer to 21, which is why the first 20 years feel packed with life events. His closing charges: (1) tomorrow isn't guaranteed; (2) nothing will be easy — skip past the fantasy that something's a shortcut and assume you don't know enough (FOMO = slow down, you're missing something); (3) it takes time and reps to get good — nobody was instantly good, and "naturals" simply forgot the prior work that generalized (his own "natural" language ability came from a bilingual childhood). Therefore: the sooner you start, the sooner you arrive — and don't stop until you do. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
