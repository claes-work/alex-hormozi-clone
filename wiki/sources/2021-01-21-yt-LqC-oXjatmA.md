# The Key To Finding a Profitable Market (2021-01-21)

## Summary

In this short solo video, Hormozi answers a recurring audience question — "how do I know I have a good market?" — by laying out his personal checklist for evaluating a market before committing to serve it. He gives one overriding rule (serve the market you can create the most value for, especially where you have personal experience) and, for those without any market experience, a four-part market-quality checklist. This is an early (2021) proto-version of the market-selection framework he would later codify in *$100M Offers* as the "starving crowd" and the four market indicators.

> ⚠️ PROTO-FRAMEWORK: This is an early articulation of Hormozi's market-selection criteria (later formalized in *$100M Offers*, 2021, as the "starving crowd" concept and four-market-indicators list: pain, purchasing power, easy to target, growing). Treat the wording here as the 2021 pre-book version, not the final published framework.

## Key claims

- (2021) First filter for market choice: as long as a market is **not dying** (normal or growing), pick the one you can **provide the most value to** given your skill set. [paraphrase]
- (2021) **Personal experience in a market trumps everything** — knowing the customers' pains firsthand lets you empathize, build a better product, and win. Recommend serving that market first. [paraphrase]
- (2021) If you have no market experience, evaluate markets against **four criteria** — and you need **all four**, not three. [paraphrase]
  1. **Pain** — is the market in desperate need / "starving" for the solution? A "nice to have" doesn't get people to pull out their wallet, especially in a bad economy. The timeless pains are **health, wealth, and relationships**. [paraphrase]
  2. **Growing** — is the market expanding rather than shrinking? [paraphrase]
  3. **Easy to find** — can you reliably target and reach the prospects (associations, groups, shared channels, congregated in one place)? "If you can't find them, they'll never find you." [paraphrase]
  4. **Purchasing power** — do they have the money to buy? [paraphrase]
- (2021) Illustrative failure cases, each missing exactly one criterion: [paraphrase]
  - **Newspapers** — in pain, easy to find, had money, but **shrinking** (not growing). Same entrepreneur switched to selling **masks/PPE in manufacturing** and went from a few million dollars a *year* to a few million dollars a *week*.
  - **Unemployed resume coaching** — growing and in pain, but **no purchasing power** (they have no jobs / money).
  - **Plastic surgeons via ads seen only by nurses** — market fine, but **not easy to find/target** so the offer never reaches them.
- (2021) Worked example of applying the checklist: a relationship coach should serve **seniors seeking a second-half-of-life partner** (high pain from loneliness, growing as people age, easy to find, more money) rather than **college kids** (little money) — same core value proposition, better market, more money. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Hormozi says he uses this exact framework when deciding **which companies to invest in**: he checks the founder's experience, then whether the problem being solved matches these criteria. [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "As long as the market is not dying and is either normal or growing, then which of the markets can you provide the most value to?"

> "If you have an advantage inherently or you have experience... especially if it's personal experience, then I would usually recommend going after that market first, because you know their pains, you know what it feels like."

> "The first thing that I'm going to look for is, is the market in pain?... Are they starving for the thing that I'm looking for?"

> "Health, wealth, and relationships are the timeless needs of humanity."

> "As soon as he switched from selling newspapers to selling masks... in manufacturing, he went from a few million dollars a year to a few million dollars a week. Same entrepreneur simply applied it to a growing marketplace."

> "If you can't find them, they'll never find you."

> "One, they have to be in pain. Two, they have to be growing. Three, they have to be easy to find... Now, the fourth one is purchasing power... do they have the monies?"

> "You can't just have three of them... You want to have all four in any market you pick."

> "Even though I have the same core value proposition, I'm choosing to serve a better market that I will ultimately probably make more money with."
