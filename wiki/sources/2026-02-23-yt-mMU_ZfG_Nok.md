# Should you make lifestyle content?

## Summary

A short clip in which Alex argues against "flexing" lifestyle content — Lamborghinis, Rolexes, and other luxury-brand signaling — as a way to prove business or marketing credibility. His core position: borrowing the *brand* of expensive things to imply money-making ability only works on the poorest, least sophisticated audience. Serious buyers ("players who write million-dollar checks") don't believe it, and once you actually are rich, the objects stop mattering anyway. The stronger play is to demonstrate results directly — "just help you make money" and let the proof carry the credibility — after which your appearance (even a tank top) becomes irrelevant. He notes he was advised early on to wear a suit to be taken seriously as a businessman and deliberately rejected that, betting that delivering results would override any presentation concern.

A brief second-speaker line appears (an interviewer confirming "I didn't see any" Lamborghinis in his content); everything of substance is Alex-attributed.

## Key claims

_All claims Alex-attributed, dated 2026-02-23, paraphrased unless quoted below._

- Flexing luxury goods only impresses poor / unsophisticated audiences; it "fools poor people" and signals competence only "to people who have no money." (2026-02-23)
- The market that competes on this kind of status signaling is a low-quality ("scummy") market that competes only for the poorest customers. (2026-02-23)
- Serious buyers who write million- and five-million-dollar checks don't believe status-flex signaling. (2026-02-23)
- Once you're genuinely rich, luxury objects stop mattering — a Lamborghini is "a couple grand a month," trivial, so the wealthy move on to other things (e.g. jets) and eventually stop caring altogether; at that point it becomes about the quality of the content instead. (2026-02-23)
- The flex mechanism works by association: using the brand of a Lamborghini or Rolex to associate yourself with expensive things, so audiences generalize "expensive" into "must be good at making money." (2026-02-23)
- A stronger positioning strategy is to prove it directly — help the customer make money — because demonstrated results generate belief that appearance-based signaling cannot. (2026-02-23)
- If you actually make people money, your appearance is irrelevant — you can "wear a tank top and look like white trash" and still be credible. (2026-02-23)
- He was advised at the start of his brand to wear a suit to be taken seriously as a businessman, and deliberately rejected that advice, betting results would override presentation. (2026-02-23)
