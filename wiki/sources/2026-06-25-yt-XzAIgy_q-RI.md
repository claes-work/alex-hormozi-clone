# How to Win in a Commoditized Service Business

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises an exhaust-hood cleaning business in San Diego. The founder sees the service as commoditized and competes by beating price. Alex tells him to erase that premise: decommoditization starts by identifying everything that makes the current market hard, risky, or annoying, then removing those frictions and explaining the difference clearly.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - exhaust-hood cleaning business owner. His revenue, subscription model, customer type, pricing, and outbound channels are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The founder must believe the service is not a commodity before customers can believe it.
- Quality can be operationalized by listing everything that sucks about competing services and fixing those things.
- Alex defines "easy" as the absence of hard; value increases when the service removes friction.
- The business can decommoditize around complete jobs, reliability, willingness to come back, risk reduction, cadence, and customer effort.
- Route density can improve gross margins over time even if prices stay fixed.
- Alex would rather start with a higher service cadence and downsell than lead with the minimum cadence.
- Payment terms can pull cash forward if customers pay in full or more upfront.
- The offer should be built around a simple articulation: five things that suck in the market and how this company removes them.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "There's good, fast, or cheap. Pick two."

> "Erase that from your memory."

> "You have to believe before anybody else that it's not a commoditized service."

> "Good is basically the absence of hard."

> "You can't make something easy, you can only make it not hard."

> "Start at four, down sell to two."

> "I would like if I'm you, I'm putting all my effort into trying to figure out how to decommoditize my service."
