## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased unless quoted)

- A truly successful company needs more expertise than one person can accumulate in a
  lifetime, so almost all of business quickly becomes a recruiting game. (paraphrase)
- People are the highest-leverage input into a company, second only to "crazy
  technology." (paraphrase)
- ★ **Future A-player projection hack:** what you consider an A-player today is not what
  you'll consider one in five years; project yourself into the size of business you want,
  ask who would be an A-player at that scale, then come back to the present and solve for
  how to get that person working for you now. (paraphrase)
- ★ **Five-clone opportunity-cost exercise:** every business has at least one A-player
  (besides the owner); imagine having five of them. Estimating the payoff often lands at
  10x. If so: (1) the company 10x's; (2) enterprise value rises MORE than 10x because the
  business becomes far less reliant on the owner and more reliant on the team; (3) it
  happens faster because more "barrels" fire concurrently. Therefore ask what other
  activity could 10x the company, do it faster, and raise the valuation multiple — if you
  can't find one, you're working on the wrong stuff. (paraphrase)
- A-players want to work with other A-players and don't want to work with B/C players, so
  a cluster of A-players becomes self-policing and only hires more A-players — "it
  propagates." (paraphrase — note: delivered largely via the interviewer's Steve Jobs
  material, echoed by Alex)
- Recruiting top people can take a long, persistent courtship — Alex cites Jobs setting
  aside a year to 18 months to bring in specific hires, rejecting alternatives with
  "they're not John." (paraphrase, second-hand anecdote about Jobs)
