# The Million-Dollar Contract

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex explains a thought experiment he runs with every new hire at acquisition.com — what he calls the **10-year million-dollar contract**. The thing new employees say they want most is growth, so he asks them a hypothetical: if you could sign a contract with yourself that pays **nothing for 10 years** but on the 10th year adds a zero (or two, or three) to your net worth, would you sign it? Almost everyone says yes — and his reply is "Great. Now act like it." The point is that people massively **overestimate what they can do in a year and underestimate what they can do in a decade**; he notes even the biggest multi-billion-dollar companies typically took more than 5–7 years (often under 10) to get there. Once you *believe* the decade-scale outcome is available, you stop the frantic obsession over short-term money and instead ask, "What would I have to do to make it *unreasonable* that I don't 10x in 10 years?" — which leads to reasonable, compounding bets and makes winning "inevitable rather than a game of chance." He frames this as advice to his own past self "who lost it twice." The second half is his **self-as-the-asset** thesis: there's a gap between *potential earning* and *actualized earning*, and cashing in potential too early (taking the $100k salary now instead of the low-paid internship that 10x's your future earning) means "ripping up your million-dollar contract" for fast money today. The currency you invest in yourself is **time, experience, and skills**; skill is the output, time/effort the input. His memorable line: in the early days, when you have little money but lots of time and energy, don't put money in the **S&P 500 — put it in the SME 500** ("the asset that is you will always beat the stock market"). Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap scaling-roadmap promo.
