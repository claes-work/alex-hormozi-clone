# Firefighter by Day, $1.2M Profit by Night

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a San Diego ADU design-build founder who is also a firefighter captain. The coachee frames the question as whether to quit the fire department, but Alex reframes it as a mix of life-purpose, opportunity-cost, and fear. Alex says the business math favors quitting if more focused time can produce enough incremental profit, but he also warns that the fire job may function as paid study hall if the constrained schedule is making the founder unusually productive.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - ADU design-build founder and firefighter captain. His company, revenue, profit split, and job details are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A man needs a quest, and work is part of how Alex thinks people live, not just a financial activity.
- The correct decision is not just "quit when business income exceeds salary"; it depends on whether the day job removes or creates productive capacity.
- If the founder's business share is roughly $600K and the firefighter salary is about $168K, the decision can be reduced to the incremental profit needed to replace that salary after the ownership split.
- If the firehouse schedule gives the founder paid, focused work time, quitting could remove a constraint that is currently helping output.
- Alex ultimately diagnoses the founder as wanting to quit, being comfortable, believing the business can grow, and being afraid to act.
