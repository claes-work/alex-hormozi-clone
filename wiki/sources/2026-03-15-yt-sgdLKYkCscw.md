# Helping an AI Marketing Agency Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a three-person AI-production agency (himself plus two friends) that makes AI commercials and celebrity/AI models for brands; the founder is a 20-year veteran of advertising creative direction who pivoted into AI production. They are at ~$250k this year (much of it "torched" on R&D), burning ~$32k/month, with two full-time clients acquired via cold inbound from high-intent Google search. They charge $25k per ad and want to reach a $50k price point, anchoring against a ~$500k traditional broadcast commercial while their own cost per 30-second spot is ~$18k (trending down as they reuse established models). Alex's central reframe: **there is nothing special about AI** — customers only want customers, and the delivery mechanism ("a flaming stick," "wacky wavy inflatable arm guy," smoke signals) is irrelevant. He'd therefore make AI a *secret*: sell at the premium market rate ($400k–$500k) and pocket the ~$18k-cost margin rather than leading with the AI angle. His stronger recommendation, echoing a real company he studied that abandoned a DIY ad-maker "because no one cared" and pivoted to a more efficient agency model: stop selling few expensive hero commercials and instead solve the **exploding demand for high-volume creative**. He argues creative volume beats polish for paid-social — you need 50–100+ pieces because neither you nor the buyer knows which will win — and that acquisition.com itself would pay ~$100k/month for 200 good ads a month. He'd drive cost-per-ad down toward ~$50, position as "we take over your entire creative team" (aren't you tired of managing creatives, filming, hiring models, scripts), qualify buyers hard (spending $100k+/month or $1M+/year in ads, needing 50–100 pieces of creative, a year of profitable advertising with proven funnels/offers), and later bolt on media buying (the easiest part — one media buyer can run it). He frames this as the next iteration of the advertising agency: clients pay you to do all their ads once they have a proven funnel. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues there is nothing special about AI as such: customers only care about getting customers, not the mechanism that delivers them — so a marketing agency's tech stack is not the value proposition.
- As of March 2026, he'd almost make the use of AI a secret: sell an ad at the prevailing premium price (~$400k–$500k) while it costs ~$18k to produce, and "pocket the rest," rather than leading with the AI angle.
- As of March 2026, he cites a company he nearly acquired that had a DIY ad-making product "no one cared" about; it pivoted to offering agency services and won by making better ads for less and producing more creative than competitors could.
- As of March 2026, he'd bet on high *volume* of creative over fewer nicer ads, because demand for creative is "going through the roof" and paid-social (Meta) performance is about volume and varied look-and-feel, not individual hero spots.
- As of March 2026, he says acquisition.com would pay ~$100k/month for ~200 good ads a month purely because it saves time — evidence of gigantic demand for creative-at-volume.
- As of March 2026, he'd push cost-per-ad down toward ~$50 each (kept "pretty decent") and do far more of them, rather than $18k premium spots — because with 100 pieces nobody knows in advance which will work.
- As of March 2026, his recommended positioning: "we take over your entire creative team" — relieving buyers of managing creatives, filming, hiring models, and scripting — which is the actual value proposition.
- As of March 2026, media buying is the easiest part of running ads; one media buyer can handle it, so it's a natural add-on to a creative-fulfillment offer rather than the hard problem.
- As of March 2026, his qualification bar for this offer: buyers should be spending at least $100k/month (or $1M+/year) on ads, need at least 50–100 pieces of creative, and have ~a year of profitable advertising with proven funnels/offers — so target CPAs and working offers already exist and you're just taking the creative headache and cost basis off their plate.
- As of March 2026, he'd stay mid-market and up rather than chase true enterprise (which "takes forever") or drop to SMB, framing the offer as "the next iteration of what advertising agencies will become."
