# The Copy Rule I Learned From The Best Copywriter I Know (2020-12-31)

## Summary

In this short 2020 video, Hormozi explains the "Rule of One," a copywriting
principle he credits to Michael Masterson and Masterson's book *Great Leads*. The
rule holds that the most compelling sales messages center on a single big idea
rather than stacking many disconnected selling points ("toss salad copy"). Hormozi
says he wrote the rule down and keeps it in front of his computer, and that applying
it has made his own copy noticeably tighter and more effective.

## Key claims (2020)

- (2020, paraphrase) The Rule of One comes from Michael Masterson, whom Hormozi
  describes as the chief growth strategist at Agora Publishing — in his words the
  biggest information seller on the market, doing over a billion dollars a year — and
  the person who built Agora's acquisition machine of hundreds of copywriters.
- (2020, paraphrase) Masterson introduces the Rule of One as the first — and,
  per Hormozi, arguably most important — concept in his book *Great Leads*.
- (2020, paraphrase) The common mistake of copywriters and business owners is
  cramming as many good things as possible into a message hoping one lands; Masterson
  calls this "toss salad copy" (throw everything at the wall, see what sticks).
- (2020, paraphrase) Hormozi says Masterson reviewed the top 100 offers of all time
  from Agora's history and found that 91 of them centered on a single concept.
- (2020, paraphrase) A "one big idea" has three elements: it must be easy to
  understand, easy to believe, and interesting or unique.
- (2020, paraphrase) In copy for an ad or landing page, sub-bullets should only
  reinforce the single message — stories, arguments, and facts that all support the
  one central concept, not a scatter of unrelated points.
- (2020, paraphrase) The best-converting ads tend to be the simplest, most direct,
  and clearest to understand.
- (2020, paraphrase) Forcing yourself to pick only one idea makes you select more
  carefully and identify the strongest, most centralized concept — the "tip of the
  spear" of the message.
- (2020, paraphrase) A unique selling proposition should follow the Rule of One: one
  core, immediately understandable, counterintuitive, believable proposition rather
  than 19.
- (2020, paraphrase) Once the single idea is chosen, the rest of the sales message
  becomes transparent and simple to write; the hard part is figuring out what that
  one idea should be.
