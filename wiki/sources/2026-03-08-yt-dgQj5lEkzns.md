# I Make $1M/Year Teaching Arts and Crafts. How Do I Scale?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a seven-figure ($1M+/yr) low-ticket education business teaching crafters (mostly women 45+) to make stickers, monetized through a $27/month or $270/year membership (products range $7–$270). She wants to reach $3M/yr but believes her constraint is **30-day cash**: she collects ~$60 per new member in the first 30 days but pays ~$90 to acquire them on Meta ads, so acquisition takes many months (she estimates ~6) to recoup, blocking profitable scale. Churn is ~93% (annual), true LTV ~$300–385. Her ad funnel drives cold traffic to paid 3–5 day events with a $10 entry, where she pitches the $27/month (only ~10% take the annual). Alex's diagnosis: she is selling the wrong thing at the event. His fix is a **two-step / two-cart-close** structure. Step one: at a five-day selling event you must **sell the expensive thing** — make the **annual the only offer available**, wrapped with one or two big **annual-exclusive bonuses**, and cart-close it. He frames a **$300–600 impulse window for consumers** (300 low, 600 high, possibly higher) and notes a 16%-off annual vs. monthly isn't a real differentiator without exclusive bonuses. Step two: after the cart closes, run a **scoop-up / mop-up retargeting campaign** to everyone who saw the ads, pointing to the $27/month page with the key bonuses **removed** — this fixes the cash problem. He then layers a growth idea: add a **physical-product premium** (e.g., sell the printer with paper, a kit) because a physical component makes the pitch tangible and dramatically lifts a consumer's willingness to buy — his "people need a reason but need an excuse" logic, where a physical good legitimizes the purchase to a spouse and could support a ~$1,000 price point. On handling monthly requests: don't lie, but make the undesired option less convenient. Closes with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, at a multi-day (3–5 day) selling event you should **sell the expensive thing**, not the cheap recurring thing; five days of attention lets you close a bigger up-front offer.
- As of March 2026, the **impulse-purchase window for a consumer audience is roughly $300–600** ($300 low end, $600 high end), and you'll often make more money pricing at $500–600 than lower.
- As of March 2026, the fix for the client's 30-day-cash constraint is a **two-cart-close structure**: (1) during the event, make the **annual the only available offer** with one or two annual-exclusive bonuses and cart-close it; (2) after the event, run a **scoop-up/mop-up retargeting campaign** to everyone who saw the ads, sending them to the $27/month page with the key bonuses removed.
- As of March 2026, a 16%-off annual vs. monthly (e.g., $270/yr vs. $27/mo) is **not a compelling differentiator** on its own; the differentiation must come from bonuses that are exclusive to the annual (and later withheld from the monthly).
- As of March 2026, adding a **physical-product premium** (e.g., selling the printer with paper, or a kit) makes the pitch **tangible** and dramatically increases a consumer's willingness to purchase — potentially supporting a ~$1,000 price point.
- As of March 2026, Alex's consumer-psychology frame: **"people need a reason but have an excuse"** — a buyer already wants it (the reason), but a physical/tangible deliverable gives the **excuse** that legitimizes the purchase to a spouse (it produces or generates something, not "just a login").
- As of March 2026, when customers ask for the option you don't want them to take, **don't lie** — but you can make the undesired option **less convenient** to purchase (offer monthly, but explicitly without the bonuses).
- As of March 2026 (mechanics referenced): with ~93% annual churn, true LTV on the $27/month works out to roughly $385 ($27 ÷ 7%); Skool tracks membership by monthly **cohort**, letting you follow each launch cohort's churn over time.
