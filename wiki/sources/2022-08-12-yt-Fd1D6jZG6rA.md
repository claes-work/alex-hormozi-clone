# Can't Get Past $50,000 A Month In Your Gym? Here's WHY!

## Summary
A Gym Launch coaching video (part of a "seven money models" series tied to a free 100-page PDF of 30 frameworks) that profiles the "Level 2" gym — a facility doing $20,000–$50,000/month, typically under 250 members, with the owner still working in the business 40–60 hours a week. The speaker argues these gyms plateau at a ~$50k ceiling for two reasons: a customer-acquisition problem (can't profitably bring in new clients at scale) and a churn problem (a "leaky bucket" most owners deny having). He shows how to diagnose churn from flat revenue despite steady signups, and advises attacking acquisition or churn first depending on which is the bigger bottleneck.

## Key claims
- A "Level 2" gym typically has fewer than 250 members and revenue of $20,000–$50,000/month, and hits a ceiling around $50k it struggles to break past.
- The two things that cap gyms at this level are an acquisition problem and a churn problem; addressing both together is what moves the gym to the next level.
- The average gym charges roughly $120–$150/month, with actual average client value around $130 after discounts.
- If a gym makes $50k/month off only ~100 members, its pricing is well-aligned; if it takes ~250 members to hit $50k, the pricing is wrong and needs to be fixed first.
- Many gyms are unprofitable even at $50k+/month; some come in at $80k+/month and are still in the red, losing on average up to ~$7,000/month, with facility profit capping around $7k even when the owner takes a salary.
- Most gym owners don't believe they have a churn problem, but the numbers expose it: flat revenue over six months while still signing 10–15 new members per month means the gym is churning out 10–15 members per month.
- Diagnostic rule of thumb: if churn is ~10–12% but there's no acquisition system, fix acquisition first; if churn is ~10–12%+ and acquisition is already working, attack churn.
- Staffing at this level: owner still working in the business (40–60 hrs/week), two to five trainers on the floor, usually a manager near the top of the range, and sometimes a salesperson and admin/front desk.
- Claimed data basis: six years working with 4,500+ gyms across 19 countries, and revenue data gathered from 33,000+ gyms.
