# Poor People Buy Stuff. Ambitious People Buy Skills.

## Summary
Short solo monologue built around advice Alex says his father gave him when he was young: **"Rich people buy time. Poor people buy stuff. Ambitious people buy skills. Lazy people buy distraction."** He uses this four-quadrant spending taxonomy to make a year-end/new-year point about identity and self-honesty: to predict how your next year will go, look at what you spend money on and what fills your calendar, because "wherever you invest is what you will get more of." People are surprised when their bank balance and accomplishments fall short of what they wanted, but their credit-card statements and calendars make it "very obvious why those things didn't happen" — you **vote with your dollars** about what you care about and about the type of person you want to become. He pushes viewers to act for "current you," not just "future you" (current you is "the only you that's ever going to enjoy life"), and rarely-for-him asks viewers to comment the thing they'll actually do *today*. He illustrates identity-through-spending with a story: at Sephora with Leila he watched a saleswoman tell two young girls that "now that you guys are women, you need to start budgeting for this stuff" — Alex reads this as a beautiful lesson that **priorities are dictated by what we spend, not by what we say**; stepping into a new identity means matching it with new priorities and reallocated money. The mechanism he prescribes: decide the type of person you want to become, identify the actions that person takes, then align your calendar and budget so your investments yield the results you want. Closing analogy: people say "wouldn't it be great if I owned Apple?" but never buy the stock — if you want to be a future version of you, "start buying stock in that today," paying in both time and money. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
