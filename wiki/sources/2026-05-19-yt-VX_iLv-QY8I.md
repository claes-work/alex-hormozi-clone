# How to Figure Out What You're Good At (And Get Better)

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says skill acquisition is volume times feedback loops. Rather than asking
only what one is naturally good at, he suggests asking what one wants to get good at, then
using tutors, small groups, and communities to compress feedback and observe the small
behaviors that produce results.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Skill acquisition is volume multiplied by feedback loops.
- Asking what one wants to get good at may be more useful than asking what one is already
  good at.
- Natural predispositions may exist, but genetics are not controllable and may be hard to
  identify.
- Alex tends to skew toward nurture in nature-versus-nurture thinking.
- He gives his own language ability as an example: what felt natural was likely influenced
  by bilingual parents and speaking French as his first language.
- Learning a skill requires doing repetitions, then comparing the output with better
  examples.
- An expert tutor is the fastest way to learn.
- Small groups are the second-fastest path.
- Joining a community of people who already have the skill lets someone learn through
  observation and many feedback sources.
- Communities help separate core truths from mysticism in a skill domain.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "It's volume times feedback loops."

> "What do I want to get good at?"

> "The fastest thing to do is go into a community of people who already know how to do that thing."
