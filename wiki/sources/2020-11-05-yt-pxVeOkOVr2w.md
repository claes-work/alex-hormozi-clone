# The Offer Is King (2020-11-05)

## Summary

An early (Nov 2020) solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that the offer — not the copy,
creative, or sales skill — is the primary driver of business growth. He dissects three
"greatest offers of all time" (Domino's 30-minutes-or-free, Sony's 10 CDs for a penny,
FedEx overnight) into three components (believability, high ROI/value, and the business
model around it), then enumerates seven ways to "ethically use free" to acquire high volumes
of cheap, qualified leads and still monetize them. This predates the $100M Offers book (Aug
2021) and reads as raw seed material for his later offer and money-model frameworks. He also
mentions a book-in-progress titled *Acquire* ("building a better mousetrap").

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2020-11-05)

- The offer is the highest-leverage part of a business: with a good enough offer you don't
  have to sell hard, write great copy, or make great creative — people just sign up. Most
  businesses should be founded on one exceptional, irresistible core offer.
  **[Proto-Grand-Slam-Offer]** — this "irresistible core offer" language is an early version
  of the "Grand Slam Offer" concept later codified in *$100M Offers* (2021).
- The three greatest offers of all time (his examples): Domino's "30 minutes or it's free,"
  Sony's "10 CDs for a penny" (first trial offer, came with continuity), and FedEx
  "absolutely, positively overnight." First two have a pricing lever; FedEx is pure high-value.
- Every strong offer has three components: (1) **believability**, (2) **high ROI / value**
  communicable in a few words, and (3) the **business/monetization model** built around it.
  **[Proto-Value-Equation]** — the "high ROI offer that people deem valuable" framing is an
  early, informal precursor to the formal Value Equation (dream outcome / perceived likelihood
  / time / effort) of *$100M Offers*.
- Believability is undermined by offers that are "too good" — cites a marketer who offered
  "$1,000 back for every $1 you give me" and got zero responses. A **"reason why"** (e.g.
  overstocked, inventory going bad) restores believability of a discount. **[Early "reason why"
  principle]**
- Free is the single most valuable marketing skill: it yields the highest volume of leads, the
  lowest customer acquisition cost, and is (in his opinion) the key to massive, word-of-mouth
  growth — especially an irresistible free offer others can comprehend and repeat.
- Seven ways to use free while still monetizing:
  1. **Free bribe offer** — high-value item free *with purchase* of a smaller continuity (e.g.
     Frank Kern giving all his courses (~$12,000 value) free with a $397/mo newsletter); also a
     price anchor. Gym version: $1,000 challenge free when you join at $200/mo membership.
  2. **Limited free offer (A/B close)** — a stripped free trial (1 workout/week, no nutrition,
     no coach), then upsell a paid 28-day challenge with a guarantee; converts ~3 of 4 to paid.
  3. **Free trial + penalty (downsell)** — card on file, charged $50 for missed
     workouts/sessions; increases consumption, qualification, and back-end conversion.
  4. **Free with deposit** — his old "free 6-week challenge" (calls it likely the most popular
     fitness offer of the last decade): put money down, get it back or credited on hitting an
     outcome. Always stay compliant with advertising rules.
  5. **Free forever** — give the core service away and monetize an attached revenue stream;
     cites a friend's weight-loss franchise earning 5% on service, 95% on supplements. Give away
     high-margin/low-value; make them buy higher-perceived-value physical products (people value
     physical products over service). **[Proto-money-model / "monetize the second need" thinking.]**
  6. **Free giveaway** — run a giveaway (e.g. a month of meals, a $25,000 transformation
     challenge) to collect tons of cheap, hand-raising qualified leads; non-winners get a
     discount to join. Watch gambling compliance.
  7. **Free with commitment** — first month free in exchange for a longer contract (e.g.
     9-month); easy front-end sale, higher average contract value because price isn't confirmed
     up front.
- You must still *sell the continuity*, not just the trial — a common mistake is selling only
  the trial. ~30% will call to reverse an auto-bill they didn't mean to allow, causing
  chargebacks; only bill people who were genuinely sold and verbally agreed.
- Knowing your metrics (conversion %, retention %, average ticket → LTV) lets you "market with
  reckless abandon" because you know you win on the numbers. **[Early LTV / unit-economics
  emphasis]**
- Clarity test for an offer: someone should be able to explain it to another person in one or
  two sentences; if not, it isn't clear enough to a prospect.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The offer is king. So if you have a good enough offer, you don't have to sell hard. You
> don't have to have amazing copy or amazing creative."

> "Most businesses should be founded on a core offer that is exceptional... an irresistible
> offer. That is what it's going to grow the business."

> "There's a famous marketer who said, 'For every dollar you give me, I'll give you $1,000
> back.' He ran an ad in the paper and he got zero responses... it's so good that it's not
> believable."

> "Understanding how to use free as a marketer is probably, in my opinion, the single most
> valuable thing that you can learn how to do. It's learning how to build a better mousetrap."

> "Right now I'm working on a book called Acquire, and the subtext is building a better
> mousetrap — which is just kind of like how to structure offers in a way that you can get tons
> of volume, but also make money on the offers."

> "If you know those metrics, then you can market with reckless abandon because you know what
> your LTV is."

> "If you get the offer right, someone should be able to explain to someone else in like one
> sentence or two sentences what it is that the offer is. If you can't explain it like that,
> then it is not clear enough to a prospect."

> "If you get the offer right, it will make you more money than you know what to do with."
