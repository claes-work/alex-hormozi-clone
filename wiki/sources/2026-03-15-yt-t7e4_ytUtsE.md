# Helping a TikTok Shop Agency Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a membership business for TikTok Shop creators — education, brand matchmaking, and a creator community — that did $3.6M in revenue in its first year and wants to reach $30–40M over 2–3 years and eventually $200M ("the biggest in the industry"). They sell two membership tiers ($50 and $200), acquire almost entirely through **affiliates** (their own creators, who both coach and affiliate the community the way they affiliate physical products on TikTok Shop), and earn roughly 50/50 from membership fees and transaction fees on brand-creator deals. They're weighing whether to **raise capital** to build a full-scale matchmaking platform (currently a no-code build) and where to focus. Alex's answers are pointed. On capital: **don't raise** — they have sufficient cash flow to fund growth; eat the profit into dev if they want (tax-efficient), but "money makes you lazy" and "impairs your thinking ability" because it tempts you to solve with cash problems that cash can't solve. Getting a huge share of the market "doesn't require money" — "if it did, a lot of networks would win… that are well funded." On focus, he runs his signature **one-thing** exercise: if a year from now only one thing were true and it 10x'd the company, what would it be? The client's own answer surfaces the lever — big-name creators/influencers who bring their own audiences (they already have one coach at ~150k TikTok followers). Alex reframes it: they have one; what would it take to have 100? He tells them to find the **highest-leverage influencers** and "remove everything in my path that's not that." He names the macro tailwind — mainstream/direct-response is "dead and dying," attention and therefore trust now sits with creators, and the client's dependency on creators (their perceived "downfall") is in fact their biggest USP: "you get it for your opportunity, you're just not using it for you." He declines to prescribe on the blended pricing/business-model question without looking under the hood at delivery constraints, noting a cheap entry price makes model-mixing less of a concern (Shopify became a payments company on top of subscription revenue — "it's okay to have a blended model"). Ends with the roadmap promo.
