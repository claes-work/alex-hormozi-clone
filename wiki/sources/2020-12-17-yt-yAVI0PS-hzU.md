# Unlock High Income Skills Without a Degree

## Summary

In this solo talk, Hormozi argues that the belief "I'm inherently bad at X" is a
self-limiting trap: people conclude they lack a skill when they have simply never
invested the hours to acquire it. He reframes competence as a function of reasonable
effort over time — you should expect to be bad until you have practiced enough that
being bad would be "unreasonable" — and points to high-income skills like sales as
learnable paths to writing your own paycheck without a degree.

## Key claims

- (2020) Building on success has an order: first develop skills, then develop the
  character traits to execute them, then correct your beliefs so opportunity opens up —
  but belief is imbued throughout, because if you don't believe you can acquire a skill
  you never will.
- (2020) Concluding "I'm not good at sales / marketing / math" without having put in
  training hours is unreasonable; of course you're bad at something you have never done.
- (2020) Sales is a high-income skill: if you know how to sell and know where to look,
  you can effectively write your own paycheck.
- (2020) You can massively succeed in business using only basic arithmetic
  (multiplication, division, addition, subtraction) — or a calculator. People who say
  "I'm bad at math" usually just don't understand the underlying concept (e.g. "cost per
  acquisition" is a math statement expressed in words).
- (2020) Actions are "agnostic to your level of deservingness and skill": practice one
  hour a day for a year and you'll likely reach the top 5-10% simply because most people
  do nothing.
- (2020) If someone else has done a thing, it is therefore possible, and therefore you
  can do it too if you do the same actions.
- (2020) Personal example: he believed he was horrible at math until age 23, then forced
  himself to stop using a calculator and do all math mentally; for the GMAT he did "16
  phone books of problems" over four months (four hours a day) and hit the 99th
  percentile — proof that enough effort makes being bad unreasonable.
