# How to Grow Your Business So FAST it Makes Your Accountant Nervous

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi lecture framing all business growth problems as a single triage
question: is a business **supply constrained** (customers but not enough capacity) or
**demand constrained** (capacity but not enough customers)? He argues a business is
never both at once, walks through eight worked examples (consultant, SaaS, restaurant,
COVID e-commerce store, plumber/home services, enterprise software, course creator,
mobile app), and shows how the constraint continually "ping-pongs" — founders get stuck
for years by working the old constraint after it has already moved. Closes with a
diagnostic checklist and a plug for the free acquisition.com/roadmap scaling map.

## Key claims (fast-growth mechanics)

- **Triage first.** Before anything else, ask whether the business is supply or demand
  constrained. "You can either have too few customers or too little capacity, but never
  both at the same time."
- **The doubling test:** if you doubled ad budget / outreach / content tomorrow, would
  you double sales or just create a mess? A mess = supply problem; more sales = demand
  problem.
- **Growth = working hard on the *right* problem**, not just working hard. Fixing the
  wrong constraint wastes money/time and often makes things worse by further straining
  the system.
- **Supply-constrained fixes (service):** (1) raise prices a lot (50–100%, "singles and
  doubles"); (2) change the service-delivery ratio (1-to-1 → 1-to-many/small group);
  (3) hire and train more people. Beyond pricing, the three levers are **technology,
  training, and adding headcount**.
- **Raising prices is the fastest fix for a supply problem** and has a double/triple
  benefit: if a consultant doubles prices and loses ~30% of clients, he makes more money,
  serves fewer people, and frees time to build more capacity.
- **Demand-constrained fixes:** fix the acquisition channel (ads / outbound /
  affiliates / content) AND fix conversion. Reallocate resources away from an already-good
  product toward getting more people to know it exists.
- **Product vs. marketing asterisk:** a great product only grows itself via word of mouth
  if its customers have "high surface area exposure" to other potential customers (e.g.
  Facebook is for people). Niche/high-end/B2B products retain well but won't grow virally —
  you have to go get the business.
- **Products often get worse through addition.** You can improve a product by deleting
  things; constant change erodes product/service quality more than incremental tweaks add.
  Some of his best business years came when he was *less* involved and just let the team
  compound.
- **Restaurant example = mispricing, not marketing.** Weekend waits + empty weekdays →
  charge more on Fri/Sat (a separate weekend menu) to spread demand into the week; on
  ~10% margins a 20% price bump roughly triples profit on those days. Framing trick: say
  "weekdays are 20% less" instead of "weekends are 20% more."
- **Physical-product supply constraint (COVID store):** only two moves — sell something
  different (intangibles that won't run out: warranty, insurance, white-label an
  affiliate's product, add-on services) to generate cash, OR increase supply. Use the cash
  to pay vendors more to jump the line and to build redundant supply chains. "Lines" are
  not sacred — you can pay 20% more to get to the front.
- **Home-services / plumber example:** fully booked but thin margins isn't a customer or
  technician problem — it's a **sales/upsell problem**. Same jobs, teach 2–3-line upsell
  scripts, role-play each morning; upselling 50% of jobs raises revenue ~50% and most of
  it drops to the bottom line because costs barely move. "Step two is where all the profit
  is." He classifies this as technically demand-constrained (more revenue per customer
  funds the headcount to fix supply).
- **Enterprise-software example:** stuck 8 years despite 98% revenue retention; 500 demos
  → 2 closes/year. Not a lead-volume or headcount problem — a **closing / avatar problem**.
  They were pitching building *owners* (who wanted savings) while building *managers* held
  decision power and wanted no extra work (principal-agent problem). Rescripting to elevate
  the manager's status unlocked growth. "What customer do you really serve?"
- **Course-creator example:** breaking even on a zero-cost product = priced too low and/or
  ads-quality/quantity problem. Raise $1,000 → $3,000 (sales drop ~15–20%, revenue nearly
  triples) and add a $10,000 tier ~10% take. Sometimes you raise prices under a *demand*
  constraint too — so you can afford to spend more on ads.
- **Ads tactic:** take your best-performing organic content, add a 5-second CTA, run it as
  a paid ad. Send paid traffic to a page structured to convert (capture info first), not a
  pretty homepage — "people have very large motivation for very short periods of time."
- **Potential vs. throughput:** improving a website/product that isn't the constraint just
  raises *potential*, not *throughput* — like widening a 1-lane bottleneck to 5 lanes
  between two 4-lane stretches. His own acquisition.com site is deliberately "ugly" because
  it converts fine and isn't the constraint.
- **Mobile-app example:** great product, few thousand users, ~nothing on marketing = pure
  demand problem. Levers: optimize app-store listing (higher conversion → higher ranking +
  more platform-pushed traffic), pay for user acquisition, banner ads on other apps serving
  the same market, referral program.
- **The accordion / ping-pong:** solving a demand constraint creates a supply constraint
  and vice versa — it's the natural flow of business. Founders stay stuck for years because
  they keep working the *old* constraint after it moved without reallocating attention.
- **Diagnostic checklist for supply:** Could you serve more customers at your standard? Are
  you turning people away / running waitlists? Are your best slots/products always sold out?
  Do you run out of inventory / have shipping delays? Any "yes" = supply problem.
- Demand side splits into an **advertising** problem (generating demand) and a **conversion/
  sales** problem (capturing it); conversion failures trace to people, process, offer, or
  price. "Price doubles on both constraints" — undercharging starves your ad budget.
- ~Two-thirds of businesses he asks have been at the same revenue for 2+ years; he calls it
  solvable, not a sign of stupidity.
