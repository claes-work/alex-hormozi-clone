# Why you shouldn't fear criticism (2025-01-04)

## Summary

Largely a monologue in which Alex Hormozi reframes criticism: critics are
usually directionally right (most attempts fail) but wrong when it matters
most (the one that works), and starting is a "zero loss" game because a failed
attempt costs nothing and still builds skill. He ties this to an infinite-game
philosophy — the only objective is to keep playing, so not quitting is the
only skill that matters — and argues fear of being seen to fail is irrational
because no one notices your failures anyway.

## Key claims

- Critics are "more often correct but when it matters most incorrect" — like a
  judgmental parent right about every girlfriend except the one you marry. (paraphrase)
- Beginning is a zero-loss bet: a failed podcast/business attempt costs nothing
  and leaves you with more skill and experience than when you started. (paraphrase)
- Best investors optimize for outsized returns; the ideal early bet has no
  downside and limited upside, and you want to make as many as possible. (paraphrase)
- Infinite-game logic: any finite goal (richest man, #1) is temporary, so the
  only durable objective is to keep the game going — the player who just keeps
  playing can't be beaten. (paraphrase)
- Everyone looks like an "overnight success" because the years of failure went
  unseen, so fear of public failure is misplaced — people barely notice even
  when you win. (paraphrase)
- Endurance is robust when an end is visible (mouse-drowning and breath-holding
  analogies); entrepreneurship is hard precisely because the end is unknown. (paraphrase)
