# You Get Rich By Not Focusing On Getting Rich

## Summary
In this solo talk, Alex Hormozi argues that getting rich is an outcome (an output), while getting better is the input, and that most entrepreneurs stay stuck because they obsess over driving revenue instead of improving the product that actually creates wealth. He reframes scaling around product quality: a great product compounds via referrals and repeat purchases, lowering customer acquisition cost (CAC) and raising lifetime value (LTV), whereas a mediocre product traps you in the "selling business" — a race to the bottom on CAC where you must reload sales to zero every month. He stresses delayed gratification ("the point of the first phase is to learn, not to earn"), doing the "hundred tiny details" competitors are too lazy to do, and using data/activation-point analysis rather than made-up narratives about why a business succeeds or fails. He weaves in a soft pitch for Skool.com (community as an LTV lever), of which he became co-owner.

## Key claims
- Getting rich is an outcome/output; getting better is the input. Most entrepreneurs focus on the output (driving the revenue number up) instead of the thing that creates the wealth.
- Quoting John Paul DeJoria: "you don't want to be in the selling business." Businesses that can only grow by selling more units/customers are in a terrible position; ~95% of businesses are there, constantly struggling for leads.
- If the only way to grow is to advertise more, you must master every channel (affiliates, SEO, content, paid ads, email, outbound) and still reload all your sales to zero every month.
- The fix is to build "back to front": find something people don't stop buying, and fix the "holes in your bucket" (retention/product) rather than pouring more water (leads) in.
- He sells ~$1M/month in books without advertising them, because readers post about them and refer friends — a result of time spent on the product, not on ads.
- Offers only get customers in the door; how well you deliver on the offer is what keeps them paying and referring. (He notes he wrote a book on offers and it matters when starting out, but delivery is the long game.)
- Cookie-business story: a friend toured top cookie bakers to learn, then baked a numbered new batch every day for a year (365 batches) using tasters — building a premium local cookie business by refining the product and generating leads simultaneously.
- Not all progress shows up immediately in revenue; product improvement is a progress marker that pays off at a delay. Sales/lead improvements give instant dopamine, but "the monster money comes from the back end."
- There is no shortage of leads — traffic is effectively unlimited across all media. The real problem is you can't afford leads because your product isn't good enough to support the CAC.
- If you make the same money per customer as competitors, you're in a race to the bottom on CAC, chasing short-lived hacks. The biggest companies don't do Instagram/hashtag hacks because they've won the long game — the LTV game.
- Rephrasing Dan Kennedy ("he who can spend the most to acquire a customer wins") as "he who can make his customers the most valuable wins."
- Two advertising truths that never change: cost per eyeball (CPMs) always goes up, and at scale you advertise to colder, less-aware audiences so conversion efficiency falls. You combat this with referrals (built from product quality) and community.
- A referral engine cuts CAC (e.g. every customer bringing ~0.3 more customers cuts acquisition cost ~30%) while raising LTV; you need a compounding vehicle working in your favor to hold your LTV:CAC ratio at scale.
- Product is the end of the brand loop: a business is a promise; long-term success is your ability to deliver on it. Marketers who launch a new offer every 6 months do so because they can't keep promises / lack the skill of delivery — their reputation degrades into "shill / washed up."
- Quantitative test: if your CAC rises faster than average CPMs in your market, you have negative word of mouth working against you. Detractors (people who ask around before buying) are more numerous than most realize, lowering conversion over time — "the Invisible Hand of your product sucking."
- Brand-driven advertising is more profitable than "scammy direct response," just slower — returns like 30–100x, not 2–3x. Apple "prints money" because customers trust it will deliver on its promise and pre-buy the next product.
- Gym Launch anecdote: ~a year in, he launched "ALN" (automated lead nurture) and people who missed the webinar sent their credit card numbers sight-unseen — his first strong experience of reputation/promise-keeping doing the selling.
- Keep three lines as far apart as possible: value (high), price (premium), and cost to deliver (low) — the gap comes from doing the hundred details competitors won't (e.g. restaurant: greet by name, water in 30 sec, bread in 2 min, ask the customer's desired pace).
- "Your competition is lazy" — many customers are "a hair away from being a detractor" (pool-guy story: still paid but told neighbors not to use him). Businesses often don't even know the real reason they're winning (gym that was just the only one in a 5-mile radius).
- Citing "Professor Bergland" (Stanford): "it's better to know why you have failed than to succeed and not know why." Owners attribute failure to outside forces but credit themselves for success; both may be wrong without data.
- Reject "because" statements without data: when someone gives a causal claim, ask "how do you know that?" Gut feel with no tracking means nothing. People (including podcast guests and himself re: his childhood/father) invent narratives to explain outcomes they never actually measured.
- People invent excuses that only they need to believe, which excuse them from the hard product work they know they should do — and that work is the opportunity, because competitors avoid it too.
- Deliberately cap growth if needed: "we're going to stay at this revenue level until we get referrals over 30%," and only advertise enough to test/fix the product without broadcasting how mediocre it is.
- If he started a new cleaning business, year one would be nailing the model so no one leaves — because if you don't lose customers, your business will not stop growing (10 + 10 + 10... compounds to 20, 30, 40).
- Entrepreneurship is more a battle of wills than of intellect: willingness to delay gratification and "eat glass" (endure many failures) to get the product right is what separates small from big businesses.
- "Move fast and break things" (Facebook) reinterpreted as building back-to-front — product iteration for extended LTV and user activation; a compounding vehicle must be built into the business (for Facebook, activated users; for most businesses, product quality).
- Product is the highest-leverage work: "cut once, sell 100 times" — one improvement reaches every customer, whereas marketing is a linear relationship (market more to get more).
- Product output metrics: price commanded, number of recurring purchases / average orders per lifetime, and churn. Inputs are activation metrics — run a regression on the cohort that doesn't cancel to find activation points (e.g. an agency client getting a first SEO sale; a weight-loss client losing 7 lbs in the first two weeks), then drive onboarding toward that point.
- His second book sold to first-book buyers largely just because they learned it existed; the first book still sells thousands of copies monthly years later. To build a compounding model, "do the unscalable" (references a separate video on it).
