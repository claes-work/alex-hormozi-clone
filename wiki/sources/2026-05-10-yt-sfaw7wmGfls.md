# We Have No Idea What These Drugs Do to Kids

## Summary

Alex says that future generations may view today's psychiatric medication use, especially
for children, the way people now view older medical practices such as lobotomies or
electroshock therapy. He broadens the point into a systems argument: wherever there is a
real need, overuse or abuse of a solution can become much larger. He then shifts to his
preference for controllable games, long-term optimization, and stacking the deck with
ethical and legal unfair advantages.

## Key claims (Alex's self-report and opinion, dated 2026-05-10)

- Alex says psychiatric or "brain" medications are a modern candidate for something
  future people may judge harshly.
- He says people do not understand the brain well enough to be confident about what many
  psychiatric drugs do, especially to children.
- He compares this worry to earlier practices now widely judged negatively, such as
  lobotomies and electroshock therapy.
- He says wherever there is a real need, there may be far larger abuse of the solution.
- He frames himself as a global optimizer and local minimizer, willing in principle to
  sacrifice a local piece for the larger game.
- He says people and groups often optimize for the fastest reward and short-term benefit.
- He says he dislikes politics and focuses on games where he controls variables.
- He attributes to his father the line that he only plays games if he knows he will win,
  meaning he prepares and stacks advantages before entering.
- He says unfair advantages can be ethical and legal, such as waiting longer than
  competitors or optimizing for a different metric.
- He says he currently optimizes for brand rather than money.
