# Helping a Tattoo Artist Get to $35M/Year

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A tattoo-shop owner doing ~$4M/year in revenue wants to reach $35M/year, and names his blocker as **focus** — he runs several businesses at once: the tattoo studios (delivery), continuing education for other tattoo artists, and a nascent coaching offer for studio owners (an idea framed as "Gym Launch for the tattoo industry"). His stated goal is to build a company he can **exit**. Alex's first move is to surface the conflict: the tattoo/coaching business is intensely **face- and operator-driven** — value lives in the skill of individual star artists — which makes it "very, very tough to exit." He points to Gym Launch as the exception, not the rule: it only became sellable because he spent nearly two years stepping out of delivery (and 18 months out of the ad side) so the brand could stand without him. Alex then lays out **two decision paths** and says there is essentially no third: (1) the **information/education path** ("Gym Launch for tattoos") — makes more money in the short term but is very difficult to sell; or (2) the **corporate path** — keep growing the studios into a large brand, which takes longer but is far easier to sell. He reframes it as a personal, non-polar choice using a made-up 10-year comparison: ~$3M/year in personal income for 10 years (info path) versus ~$100k/year for 10 years and then a big exit at the end (corporate path) — "it's 10 years of living," so neither is right or wrong. He is blunt that pursuing either focused path likely means **shutting the studio down** ("shoot a kid in the head" / killing the thing you built in your own image), and that shutting a business is harder than starting one. He also kills the rationalization that owning the studios proves legitimacy: he owned **zero gyms** while running Gym Launch (he'd had six prior) precisely so he could focus. Finally, if the owner takes the info/coaching path, Alex flags the core problem of all education businesses — **once the buyer has your bag of tricks, "then what?"** — so the fix is engineering **consumables / stickiness** into the model (plus a sales motion) to drive long-term LTV, which is what let Gym Launch win. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (excluded from claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's blocker diagnosis: a ~$4M/year multi-business owner (studios + artist education + owner coaching) chasing $35M is losing to lack of **focus** across too many business models.
- As of March 2026, a tattoo business is even more **operator/skill-dependent** than gyms — value sits in individual star artists — so unless it's fully "mechanized" so operator quality doesn't matter, it is very hard to sell.
- As of March 2026, face/operator-driven businesses are hard to exit; Gym Launch only became sellable because Alex spent ~2 years out of delivery (and the last 18 months out of the ad side) so it became its own brand independent of him.
- As of March 2026, there are essentially **two paths, not three**: (1) the information/education path — makes more money short-term but is very difficult to sell; (2) the corporate path — build a large studio brand, which takes longer but is much easier to sell.
- As of March 2026, the exit-vs-income tradeoff is personal and non-polar: over 10 years the info path earns more each year but the corporate path pays off in a big final-year exit — "it's 10 years of living," so neither is right or wrong.
- As of March 2026, taking a focused path likely means **shutting the studio down**, and shutting a business down is more painful and harder than starting one.
- As of March 2026, owning the studios does **not** meaningfully prove legitimacy for a coaching/info business — Alex owned zero gyms while running Gym Launch (having had six before) so he could focus.
- As of March 2026, the core weakness of education businesses is that once a buyer has your "secret / bag of tricks," they have no reason to keep paying — so you must engineer **consumables and stickiness** (plus a sales motion) to drive long-term LTV; that is why Gym Launch was able to win.
- As of March 2026, scaling the studio side is personnel-heavy (~20 people per studio), which the owner views as a barrier; Alex counters that multi-unit scale is achievable (cites Panda Express's 2,600 locations).
