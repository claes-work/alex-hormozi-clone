# You Can Get to $10M/Year With Any Business

## Summary

In this short solo talk, Hormozi argues that almost any business vehicle can reach $10M/year, so most people's goals are achievable within their current opportunity — they just quit too early chasing shiny objects. He illustrates the "cost of switching" (starting over resets you behind your own head start, and growth is easier once you're bigger), then reframes the frustrations that make people quit as "features, not bugs" inherent to each business model. He sketches the differing scaling shapes of four business types (info/education, service, e-commerce, SaaS) and introduces a supply-constrained vs. demand-constrained lens for diagnosing whether a business is normal for its industry or genuinely off-track. He closes with a marriage analogy for the value of doubling down rather than always looking for the next thing.

## Key claims

All claims paraphrased from Alex Hormozi, dated 2026-02-26:

- Almost any business can reach $10M/year, with the exception of a brick-and-mortar business capped by a tiny local market — and even then you scale by opening in new markets. Most people's goals are achievable within their current vehicle; they are just too impatient and believe a shiny object will get them there faster.
- A useful test: "If I only did one thing for 40 years, would I be successful?" If the answer is probably yes, that is a strong reason to stick with what you're doing.
- The "cost of switching" framework: if you're in year 3 of your current business and start a new thing, year 1 of the new thing may look higher than year 2 of the old — but you're still behind, because it's really year 4 for you. Sticking with something is how you build and keep a head start.
- Growth is easier when you're bigger than when you're smaller, so switching also forfeits the compounding ease that comes with scale.
- "Features, not bugs" (he credits this as not his own invention): every business has an inherent element that makes it hard, and that difficulty is precisely why you're compensated for it. Cleaning businesses struggle to attract/retain quality talent; fitness businesses struggle with customer acquisition and retention because people are shaky about fitness goals (but stick with cleaners for a long time).
- Four business models have four different scaling shapes: **Info/education (coaching)** starts fast but is very hard to scale (almost no revenue retention — once people learn something, they leave, "like graduating school"); **SaaS/software** is the opposite (starts very slow, hard to build good product and hire engineers, usually unprofitable at low ticket for a long time, but eventually scales "to the moon"); **E-commerce** scales fast but hits cash-flow, inventory, supply-chain and 3PL/supplier issues as it grows; **Service** is the slowest but steadiest.
- People often just hit the crappy part of their chosen model and conclude something is wrong with their business, when it's just the nature of that model.
- Supply-constrained vs. demand-constrained lens: if lots of people want to do the work, there's high competition, low barriers to entry, and customers have many options (demand-constrained, e.g. weight-loss coaching — easy/cheap talent, high customer churn). If few can do the work, you have a supply-constrained industry (e.g. accounting firms — hard to find people who can do accounting, but high annual customer retention).
- You can apply the supply/demand-constraint lens at the business level and the industry level. If you are the inverse of your industry's normal constraint, you're usually doing something either really wrong or really right. Most of the time you match your industry's constraint — and inexperienced operators mistake that normal constraint for something being wrong.
- The cost of not committing: people spend multiple years not being all-in, then get upset it isn't growing, when they never really gave it a shot.
- Marriage analogy: quitting to chase the next opportunity is like a spouse with a wandering eye. From his "n equals one" marriage, going from "upset about something" to "really good" takes far less time than building the loyalty, trust, and track record from scratch with someone new.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you have the goal of getting to $10 million a year, you can pretty much do it in any business ... even if you were in a tiny market, could get to 10 million a year. You just open up in new markets. That's all you do."

> "Most people's goals are achievable within their current vehicle. They're just too impatient and believe that there's another shiny object that somehow will get there faster."

> "There's an element of every business that makes it hard, but that's what makes it a business and that's why you're compensated for it."

> "Many of you guys just hit the crappy part of whatever the opportunity that you're on is, and then say there's something wrong with my business, therefore I should stop, rather than seeing it as what it is, which is this is just the nature of how this business works.
