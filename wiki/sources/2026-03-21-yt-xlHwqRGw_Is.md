# Helping a Manufacturing Company Scale

## Summary
In this live Q&A clip, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a multi-generational family manufacturing business that sells lightweight equipment to home-service and industrial contractors. The owner (caller, unnamed) describes two competing constraints: a present-day sales-and-marketing constraint limiting growth, and an existential 24–36 month constraint as a dilapidated production facility's lease expires and needs relocation (consolidating the remaining 50% of a second P&L into a Texas "center of excellence"). Hormozi frames the decision using the Eisenhower matrix and recommends sequencing sales and marketing first to free up cash, then funding the move as a reinvestment with good return on capital. The clip closes with a promotion for Acquisition.com's free $100M scaling roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex classifies the caller's facility-move decision as "non-urgent and important" on the Eisenhower matrix — worth doing, but not the immediate priority.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises fixing the present constraint (sales and marketing) first because it is the current bottleneck limiting growth, then using the freed-up cash to fund the facility move.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes that crushing sales and marketing without addressing fulfillment would only accelerate the arrival of the next (supply/manufacturing) constraint.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends deploying freed cash flow into consolidating the manufacturing facility as a "reinvestment strategy" with a good return on capital, recapturing EBITDA via a consolidated cost structure.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises the owner NOT to pull existing marketing/sales staff onto the move; instead bring in a dedicated project manager to own the relocation — and if they're good, retain them long-term.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the move is roughly a "12-week project" and, unusually for him, endorses running it concurrently with the sales/marketing push since the two efforts need different leaders.
- Caller claim (unverified): the business is a multi-generational family business selling lightweight home-service/industrial contractor equipment, run as two separate P&Ls with divided teams, having relocated part of production from New York to Texas during COVID.
- Caller claim (unverified): a move would conservatively cost about 45% of one year's free cash flow; the business is profitable today.
