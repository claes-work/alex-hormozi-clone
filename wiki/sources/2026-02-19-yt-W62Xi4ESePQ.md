# Why "Follow Your Passion" Is Bad Advice

## Summary

Short clip (mostly a solo Alex answer, with a one-line interjection from an off-camera
host, plus an acquisition.com roadmap promo appended at the end). Alex argues against
"follow your passion" as starting advice: it sets too high a bar and makes beginners
wait for something they'll instantly fall in love with. His claim is that passion is an
*output*, not an input — you don't love things until you're good at them, and you only
get good after being bad first. So the prescription is high-volume action (even
disjointed) until you see progress, do more of it, get good, and *then* the passion
appears. In his words, you don't find your passion, you create it — through excellence
and consistently winning over time. He extends the same "paradox of choice" critique to
dating/romantic partners: chasing a "perfect key/perfect lock" (career or partner) is a
trap because no career and no partner is perfect. Everything has "overhead" (a Bezos
reference) — elements you don't want — and the expectation that everything will work out
is exactly what makes nothing work out for most people.

Overlap: this reinforces his existing "passion follows mastery / passion = suffering"
thread. What is distinct here is (a) the explicit *sequence* — suck → action → progress →
skill → liking → created passion — and (b) the framing that "follow your passion" fails
because it **sets the bar too high** for beginners, plus the dating/paradox-of-choice
analogy and the "everything has overhead" expectations argument. This sharpens the belief
enough to flag as an L3-candidate for `beliefs.md`. ★ L3-candidate

## Key claims (2026-02-19, paraphrased unless quoted)

- He resents "follow your passion" advice **in the short term, for people just
  starting** — not long-term. He believes the intention behind it is pure and right, but
  the execution fails people. (paraphrase)
- The advice sets too high a bar: people **wait** for a thing they'll fall in love with,
  and that waiting stalls them. (paraphrase)
- You don't fall in love with things unless you're good at them, and you only get good at
  things if you **suck first**. (paraphrase)
- The prescription: take lots of action, even if disjointed, until you notice progress —
  then do more of it, get good, start to like it, and the passion emerges. (paraphrase)
- You don't *find* your passion, you **create** it — through excellence and through
  finding ways to consistently win over time. (paraphrase)
- Lowering the bar would help beginners; the same dynamic applies to romantic partners.
  Younger people face the paradox of choice (too many swipe-rights / "fish in the sea")
  and hunt for a "perfect key / perfect lock" that unlocks everything — which doesn't
  exist. (paraphrase)
- Careers can be viewed the same way as marriage: just as there's no perfect partner,
  there's **no perfect career**. (paraphrase)
- Everything has "overhead" (attributed to a Bezos quote) — elements you don't want. The
  expectation that everything will work out is what makes nothing work out for most
  people. (paraphrase)
