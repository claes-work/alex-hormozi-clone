# If You Have Less Than $10,000 Saved. Please Watch This Video

## Summary

Solo talking-head video in which Alex Hormozi lays out a six-step plan for going
from broke to wealthy, framed around the core idea that "wealth is a decision" and a
ratio between what you earn and what you need. He argues most people leak their
lifetime earnings (a US minimum-wage worker earns ~$1M over a life; the median earner
~$3M) into debt and status spending, and that the fix is a Dave Ramsey–style ladder:
emergency fund, kill consumer debt, expand savings, cut risky spending, invest 15%
pre-tax plus 15% into yourself, and pay off the house. Throughout he ties frugality to
optionality — keeping personal risk low so he could take repeated business swings —
and reinforces the message with compound-interest math and personal anecdotes.

## Key claims

- The average American has a negative net worth; "babies are richer" because they
  haven't gone into debt.
- A US minimum-wage employee earns over $1M in a lifetime; the median US earner earns
  ~$3M. Because the money is coming regardless, staying poor is a decision about where
  it goes.
- ~35% of every American paycheck goes to banks/lenders for debt (mortgage, car,
  credit cards) — people don't count these as "debt."
- Paying off guaranteed-negative-return debt (e.g. 24% credit cards) beats chasing
  9–10% market returns; you can't win facing off against compound interest working
  against you.
- Six-step ladder (explicitly modeled on Dave Ramsey's baby steps):
  1. Save a starter emergency/"work" fund ($1,000–$5,000) — proves you can save.
  2. Pay off debt psychologically (smallest balance first for quick wins), focused on
     consumer debt/credit cards, not the house.
  3. Expand emergency savings to 3–6 months of living expenses in a high-interest
     account (~5–6%); lowers anxiety, enables offense.
  4. Eliminate risky spending — turn in car leases, buy a ~$5,000 car in cash,
     downgrade to one car, cut your "vice."
  5. Invest 15% of pre-tax income into assets that appreciate; ideally another 15%
     into your own education/skills — automate investing, add friction to spending.
  6. Pay off the mortgage (refinance if rates are lower, else 15-year fixed) so fixed
     costs are done and earning capacity snowballs.
- Compound-interest gameification: ~$250/month at 9% ≈ $1M in 40 years; $500/month ≈
  $2.1M; a 20-year-old adding $2,500/month for 55 years ≈ $52M. A $200 purchase today
  is ~$6,000 forgone at retirement (40 yrs, 9%).
- ~130 purchases of $6–7k add up to a million; personal spending has no upside,
  business risk does.
- Invest in yourself over the S&P if forced to choose — active income growth
  (e.g. buying a snowblower to 7x driveway output) beats passive returns.
- Additional habits/challenges: check accounts daily until volatility stops making
  sense (he stopped ~$20M); the "buy nothing" challenge; the "30x12" challenge
  (30 days straight, 12 hours/day, no days off) to prove you can work harder than you
  think.
- Charlie Munger's "the first $100k is a b***h" — probably ~$150–200k in today's
  dollars.
