# Most of Your 'Friends' Are Actually Enemies

## Summary
Short clip in which Alex reframes friendship through the single lens of goal-attainment. His test: for each friend, ask "does this person increase or decrease the possibility of failure with my goal?" — a binary, yes/no question with no in-between; if the answer is decrease, get rid of them. He dramatizes the cost with a probability model: if you'd have a 100% chance of hitting your goal, three "terrible friends" each dragging you down ~30% leave you at roughly 27% — likened to a video-game slow spell that halves your hits and saps your strength. His deeper claim is that many people mislabel proximity as friendship: these "friends" are "just there because they've always been there," not because they help you get where you're going; they have no goals and are at a different point in their journey (nothing wrong with that per se), but the moment you start "drinking glass" and living tomorrow's life today, they get upset. He offers a recent maxim — "there are no social obligations, only social consequences" — and argues those consequences are usually delivered by people talking to you less and avoiding you to punish you for skipping the party, the wedding, the dog's christening. Alex flips this into a win-win via second- and third-order thinking: if you felt *obligated* to go, you didn't want to go; so the "punishment" (no longer being invited) is exactly the outcome you wanted — you save time today and tomorrow. He says he's always been willing to tell a friend directly, "I think you are not good for me," and that when such people respond by downgrading your goals and casting their beliefs onto you, his answer is that he doesn't want their beliefs because he'd then have their life — which he doesn't want. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
