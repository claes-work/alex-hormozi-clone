# How Billionaires Make Their Billions

## Summary

Hormozi explains the core mental shift he took years to grasp: you don't *make* a
billion dollars, you *own* something worth a billion. He walks through business
valuation (a company earning profit trades at a multiple of earnings), shows how
growing profit multiplies net worth via the multiple (untaxed appreciation), and
argues you should reverse-engineer wealth goals in large increments and pursue
opportunities uncapped by your time. He closes with a leveraged-buyout example
showing why "smart money" pays 10x for a business.

## Key claims

- Three phases of his own understanding: (1) make $1B/year; (2) take home ~$1–2B over
  a lifetime; (3) the correct one — you *own* an asset worth $1B.
- Valuation mechanics: a business earning, say, $5M profit at a 10x multiple is worth
  $50M; growing profit from $5M to $6M adds ~$500K after-tax income but ~$10M of
  untaxed enterprise value ("you made an extra $10 million... because the thing that
  you own appreciated").
- Billionaire math: a company doing ~$80–100M EBITDA at ~15x = $1B+; you'd need to
  clear roughly $1.3–1.5M/week in take-home. Level-ups make once-crazy numbers feel
  small over time.
- Goals matter because of the *increments* you measure them in: a "six-figure" goal
  makes people think in tiny units and cap themselves; thinking in $10M forces
  bigger-unit reasoning (price point → units/day → calls/day → ad spend/call).
- Ideal opportunities have four traits: not limited by your time, serve a very large
  market, very high gross margin, long time horizon. He wouldn't pursue anything
  capped at $100M/year; aim for $10B or $50B.
- LBO example: buy the $50M business with $15M down + $35M loan; over 5 years it pays
  ~$40M back and grows to ~$125M value; after repaying the loan you net ~$120M — "that's
  why people do it."
- "You get paid for what you do; you get returns on what you own."
