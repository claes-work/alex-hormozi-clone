# I Spent 7 Years Building a Brand With a Shitty Product

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Jessica, a women's clothing founder who moved from printed T-shirts to cut-and-sew manufacturing and wants to scale from roughly $2-3M toward $10M. Alex separates the short-term direct-response path from the long-term brand path. For e-commerce, he argues that scale requires repeat purchase, word of mouth, creative volume, and backend email economics, not just more paid traffic. He recommends a horizontal creator/affiliate army around women who embody the brand, plus backend email and repeat-purchase work before scaling incentives.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Jessica** - the coachee. Her brand, revenue, manufacturing shift, ad mix, and email percentage are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Direct-response arbitrage can scale e-commerce quickly in the short term, but it does not usually create durable large outcomes by itself.
- At scale, CAC tends to rise unless the business has forces that push it down, especially word of mouth and repeat business.
- E-commerce operators must be good at brand because low-ticket products require revenue retention, lower click costs at scale, and repeat purchase.
- The strategic shift is: **do not get customers to make sales; make sales to get customers**.
- Meta's newer ad environment requires a very large amount of creative; the founder can either build creative internally, partner vertically with major influencers, or build a horizontal creator army.
- For this clothing brand, Alex recommends two or three figureheads who embody the brand values, then a broader micro/nano creator affiliate army making content, going live, and pitching the product.
- Before scaling incentives, the founder should fix email marketing and repeat-purchase tracking; 20% of revenue from email is "a little on the low end," and roughly 35% would be a better target in this case.
- Once backend retention and lifetime gross profit are understood, the brand can design creator incentives more strategically.
