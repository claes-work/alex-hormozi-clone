# Helping a Restaurant Business Get to $100M

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises the front-facing co-owner of a restaurant group doing ~$25M+ across six locations and five concepts, who wants to reach $100M and is stuck on measuring LTV:CAC. Alex argues restaurants and high-tech are polar opposites that converge on the same truth — in the long run, product is the only thing that matters — so the real growth lever is the food and the experience, not conventional marketing. He reframes their "we spend $0 on marketing" problem as the premium they pay across systems, service, and ingredients to drive word of mouth, and prescribes a repeatable grand-opening playbook (overstaffing, ad spend across channels, promotional giveaways) that only launches once the model is proven.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Restaurants and super-high-tech sit at **polar ends of business but converge on the same truth: in the long run, product is the only thing that matters.** You can do all the marketing you want, but if the food sucks, the food sucks.
- Product includes the whole package — food **plus** the experience, service, and environment. If the overall experience isn't **noteworthy**, you get lukewarm "yeah, it was all right" reactions, customers don't return, and word of mouth turns negative.
- **~90% of restaurant discovery is word of mouth and reviews** — very few people respond to a buy-one-get-one on Instagram.
- The one exception where hardcore marketing applies is the **grand opening** — every location should have a grand-opening strategy.
- **Never run a grand opening until the model is proven**: you should know that off the initial grand-opening push you can get a location to **full capacity within 6 months on word of mouth alone.** This is how all brick-and-mortar should work; restaurants are more exacerbated because gross margins are smaller.
- To aggressively grow, the lever is **mostly the food and the experience** — which is both the sad and the amazing part.
- **Reframe CAC for a business that "spends $0 on marketing":** the real marketing spend is the **premium you pay across systems, service, and ingredients to increase virality / word of mouth.** That spread ("alpha") is your marketing — just delivered in the form of product.
- To get the finance-minded partner to spend, build a **grand-opening playbook** specifying how much you overstaff, how much you spend on ads across all channels, and what promotional giveaways you run at launch.
- **Giveaways always happen** at launch. For super-premium concepts they don't disappear — they just become **exclusive and invite-only**, but work the same way.
