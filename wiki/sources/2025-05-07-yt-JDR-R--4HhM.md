# 14 Years of Marketing Advice in 35 Minutes

## Summary

A solo marketing masterclass in which Alex Hormozi shares what he frames as 12 internal
"hacks" (plus a bonus 13th) that his portfolio companies apply to every piece of copy or
advertising they produce. He opens by anchoring authority — 14 years in business, tens of
millions per year in paid ads across companies he actually owns — and then walks through a
copywriting checklist covering headlines, differentiation, audience call-outs, reason-why,
damaging admissions, show-don't-tell, status, urgency/scarcity, implied authority, PS
statements, CTAs, and third-grade reading level, closing with humor as the bonus. The
piece is dense, framework-heavy, and cross-references his own books (Offers, Leads) with
specific page numbers. It doubles as a funnel for his free $100M scaling roadmap
(acquisition.com/roadmap).

## Key claims (marketing frameworks)

The 12 + 1 copywriting checklist:

1. **Headlines come first** — quoting Ogilvy, "once you've written your headline you've
   spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar." A great headline can beat the control by
   2–5x (the "law of small numbers": going 1% → 3% CTR triples everyone downstream).
   Curiosity is king, different is ideal, sexy works; never run an ad without a headline
   (all ads have one — bad or good). Model the first 3 seconds (visual + verbal) of
   working organic content and ads; "steal like an artist" from other industries.
2. **Say what only you can say** — proof beats promise. Competitors can copy your offer
   but not your proof; it has to be earned. Be one of a kind by controlling the pond you
   are one of a kind in. AI avatars can lie but can never have *done* something — story
   and verification of facts grow more valuable as trust erodes.
3. **Always call out who you're looking for** — and, more powerfully, who you're *not*
   looking for (disqualify by revenue/weight/etc.). Polarize; don't be vanilla.
   Advertise to *one* person, not the masses, so the reader thinks "how does he know my
   secrets?" Ladder through the five levels of awareness (unaware → problem-aware →
   solution-aware → product-aware → most-aware). Specificity creates compelling copy;
   name the *moment* they experience the pain, not vague "are you overweight?" language.
4. **Always have a reason why** — the word "because" is one of the most influential words
   in English (Langer/Cialdini copier-line study: any reason beats no reason). The
   "fraternity party planner" — the bar for a reason is low; the reason need not be good,
   only present. Sell one step at a time; every line of copy sells the next line. "A
   confused mind does not buy, and a confused mind certainly does not click." Bonus if
   the reason ties to urgency/scarcity.
5. **Damaging admissions** (his possible favorite) — owning your flaws makes you more
   believable because it's rooted in truth. "What you sacrifice in promise, you gain in
   trust, and trust trumps promise." The linguistic mechanic: "[true, damaging
   statement] BUT [thing you want them to believe]" — the clause after "but" is what
   lands; flipping the order flips the emphasis (markers smell bad *but* write longer vs.
   write longer *but* smell bad). Eminem's "8 Mile" self-roast: claim your flaws so the
   critic has nothing left. Add humor for bonus points.
6. **Show, don't tell** — really means break down the reasoning and describe the *moment*
   and the sensory experience, not the abstract benefit. "Running out of order forms
   because customers read their cards too fast" instead of "get more sales." Describe the
   future good problems they'd prefer to have. Unique moment-description = naturally fresh
   copy no one else writes.
7. **Tie benefits to status** — go past benefits-not-features to *who awards the status*
   (family, friends, colleagues, rivals). Humans are competitive/status-seeking (mating +
   resources). Status is subgroup-specific (a mom's excelling kids vs. a hustle-bro's
   Lamborghini). Use past/present/future and the "what/who/when" framework (Leads book,
   p.140) for endless benefit angles.
8. **Urgency and scarcity** — distinct: **scarcity = a function of quantity**, **urgency
   = a function of time**. The only secret is making it *legitimate*. Say no to buyers
   after the deadline/cap to keep reputation — "dig your well before you're thirsty";
   under-sell demand so you always have more later. (Offers book: scarcity p.104,
   urgency p.112, four types of urgency.)
9. **Implied authority** — credentials, longevity, client rosters, "as seen on," tiny
   local "best of" awards (they let you open a sales convo). Aggregate numbers ("in
   aggregate we've done over 20 years of advertising"). Proof/social proof beats promise
   — the Yelp example: 4.6 stars with 4,000 reviews beats 5.0 with 3 reviews.
10. **Always have a PS statement** ("power sentence") — the headline and the PS are the
    two most-read parts of any ad; PPS is third. Top three PS plays: (a) in emails, a fun
    sign-off that trains the reader to always read/click the PS; (b) a disclaimer of who
    it's *not* for; (c) a one-sentence recap of the whole pitch.
11. **Clear CTA** — tell them exactly what to do next, stupid-simple, step by step, and
    tell them what happens next. "Say-do correspondence": when what you predicted comes
    true after they act, you gain influence (Martha Stewart recipe effect). Make
    promises, keep promises, repeatedly, contingent on their action. He named the company
    acquisition.com so the company name *is* the call to action.
12. **Third-grade reading level** — assume the prospect is rushed, distracted, doesn't
    care, and reads below third grade. Fancy = friction, simple = sales. Run every ad
    through a free reading-level tool. Short sentences, small words, big promises, big
    proof; few adverbs, better verbs ("sprinted" > "quickly ran"). But
    **simplicity trumps concision** — never pick fewer words over easier words.
    Nth-grade copy auto-disqualifies ~two-thirds of readers.

Bonus (13) — **Use humor.** Entertainment goes broad, education goes deep (education
changes behavior → the click). The Old Spice ad as highest-converting example. Humor is
the one he'd cut first (the other 12 convert without it) but it's the extra sizzle. Test
humor like comedians build specials — front-load the bits that land, iterate over many rooms.
