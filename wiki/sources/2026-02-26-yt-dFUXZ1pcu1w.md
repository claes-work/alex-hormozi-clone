# Success Has No Shortcuts

## Summary

In this solo clip, Alex Hormozi argues that the hard way is the only way, because any
genuine shortcut is instantly adopted by everyone and therefore stops being a shortcut.
He claims the things most people don't have are precisely the things that have no
shortcuts, and that people waste more time chasing a nonexistent easy way than the hard
way would have cost. He reframes "hard" as many small difficulties and proposes there
should be multiple distinct words for different flavors of hardship (sacrifice, effort,
risk, uncertainty), noting each new type feels novel even though it isn't. He anchors the
piece on a Paul Graham quote linking wealth to enduring an equivalent amount of pain, and
closes with a plug for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) The hard way is the easy way, because the easy way never
  actually gets you where you're trying to go — the shortcut never reaches the destination.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Real shortcuts do exist, but the moment one is discovered
  everyone immediately adopts it, so it stops being a shortcut and becomes just a normal
  thing everyone does (analogy: learning to tie knots/shoes was once a breakthrough).
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) The things you want that most people don't have have no
  shortcuts by definition — yet people waste more time searching for a nonexistent easy way
  than the hard/only way would have taken.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Hardship is usually not one big thing but roughly a hundred
  small things that make days, weeks, and months hard — a never-ending onslaught you keep
  going through because continuing is the only choice.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) What we imagine "hard" to be differs from how we actually
  experience it, partly because the nature of hard keeps changing; this is more a
  limitation in our vocabulary for hardship than anything else.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) There are many distinct flavors of hard, each presenting at
  different times: "sacrifice hard" (giving up things you enjoy), "effort hard" (doing
  things you hate and aren't good at), "risk hard" (the chance of losing what you currently
  have), and "uncertainty hard" (that all the sacrifice might be for nothing). Analogy:
  like Eskimos having many words for snow, he feels he should have ~25 words for hard.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Each new kind of hard feels different/novel, but it isn't — it's
  just that once you grow comfortable with and conquer one level, you get exposed to a new
  one.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Cites Paul Graham: to make a million dollars you have to endure
  a million dollars' worth of pain. In his opinion, people who build (not inherit)
  tremendous wealth simply have massive concentrations of pain.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Promotes his free acquisition.com/roadmap: a 10-stage roadmap
  from $0 to $100M+ (a milestone fewer than 1% of companies finish, which he says he's done
  multiple times), organized by stages of increasing headcount and broken down across eight
  business functions — what each constraint feels like, its symptoms, and the steps taken
  to graduate — applied across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar.
