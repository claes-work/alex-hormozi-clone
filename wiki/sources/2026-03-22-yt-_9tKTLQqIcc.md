# My Favorite Offer for Home Service Businesses

## Summary
Alex Hormozi walks a home-service business owner through his favorite offer structure for commoditized industries: an "on-time, on-budget guarantee" paired with a deliberate 20% price increase. He explains how the guarantee gives sales leverage to win bids, how a "no-change contingency" protects the provider, and frames the guarantee as one of three value vectors — risk, speed, and ease — that a commoditized business can win on.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says home services are highly commoditized (high demand, high supply, low skill barrier), which triggers a race to the bottom as bad players underprice.
- As of March 2026, his favorite offer for such businesses is an on-time, on-budget guarantee: raise price by 20%, then guarantee delivery by a set date and under a set number or refund the 20% margin.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues the guarantee wins bids because competitors quoting lower will never guarantee time and budget — making their lower price "make-believe."
- As of March 2026, he attaches a "no-change contingency": the guarantee holds only if the customer doesn't change the scope; since customers almost always change their mind, this voids the guarantee while still letting you win the bid.
- As of March 2026, he notes the downside is capped — in the rare case the customer doesn't change scope and you still go over, you simply refund the 20% price raise and keep your original margin, covering both sides.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames three vectors of value a commoditized business can win on: risk (being less risky, which long-term is reputation/brand premium and short-term is covenants and guarantees), speed, and ease (lower friction for the customer).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Any kind of home service, the big issue is commoditization. Like there's huge demand for it, there's a lot of supply, and so people are going to make money without really a lot of skill. And so then you have bad players who underprice, and then basically it's a race to the bottom, which sucks."

> "With me, when you pay this, you know that it will be done by this time and it will be done under this number, period. And if it's not, I'll give you my profit — which I run 20% margins — I'll give you 20% back."

> "Did they guarantee time and budget? No one will. And so I'm like, 'Oh yeah, well then I can do it for a dollar — as long as I don't have to guarantee time and budget.' ... Right, it's complete make-believe."

> "Now here's what makes it a little even sexier is that this guarantee is contingent on one very important thing, which is that they don't change their mind. Which of course they're going to change their mind. That's okay."

> "In the rare circumstance where they don't change their mind and you somehow go over, you just give back the price raise that we just did. And so you keep what you were going to make anyways. And so this covers you on both sides."

> "You either have to win on risk — as in you are less risky, which is what a brand premium does ... You can put covenants and guarantees if you don't have a brand, but long-term it's reputation. The next one is speed ... and then ease."
