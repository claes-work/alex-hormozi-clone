# Watch This If a Copycat Is Stealing Your Business

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises AJ, whose motocross training business discovered that five-day camps produce far better economics than single-day tour stops, while a copycat competitor is chasing the low-profit single-day model. Alex says the founder should hope the copycat copies the broken model. He also walks through price guilt, free content as the way to serve people who cannot pay, unit-economics comparison between event formats, and a costly lesson from Gym Launch: lowering prices and adding value in response to a competitor cost him millions without reducing churn.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **AJ** - the coachee. His motocross training business, revenue, event formats, pricing, copycat, and personal background are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If a copycat is copying the model that does not make money, the founder should hope the copycat keeps doing it.
- Free media is how the founder serves people who cannot afford the in-person product; paid products should be priced correctly for those who can afford them.
- A founder cannot personally serve even a tiny fraction of the people helped through free content, so charging enough for in-person work is not a betrayal of impact.
- The right comparison between one-day and five-day events is unit economics: return on financial capital and human capital.
- Pricing should keep rising until willingness to pay stops.
- Alex says focusing on the customer prevents going out of business; reacting to competitors can destroy economics.
- In Gym Launch, Alex says he reacted to a competitor by adding more value and lowering recurring price, cutting about $500K/month in top-line revenue and $6-7M/year in profit, with no improvement in churn.
- He estimates the mistake may have cost roughly $50M after exit multiple effects.
- The lesson: do not copy an unprofitable competitor; let them discover the broken model themselves.
