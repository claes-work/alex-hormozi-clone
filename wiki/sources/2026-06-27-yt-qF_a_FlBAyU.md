# Pumping ETFs for $20K/Month (Legally)

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a founder who provides social-media marketing and financial-influencer management for publicly traded ETFs and stocks. Alex says the founder's age is probably a self-imposed concern, then reframes the company from a services business to a media channel that sells access to trusted retail-investor audiences. He also distinguishes mid-level recruiting through ads from senior recruiting through headhunters, niche postings, or industry-specific outreach.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - founder of an ETF/stock social-media and influencer-management business. His revenue, client pricing, compliance context, and target customers are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The founder likely thinks about his age more than prospects do; demonstrated competence matters more than being 27.
- A $20K monthly price can be too low for $60B financial companies because it may not even register as a serious strategic spend.
- The business should position itself less like a marketing agency and more like a media company that owns or controls access to trusted financial audiences.
- The pricing comparison should be against paid media spend on platforms like Meta, not against ordinary services retainers.
- Trusted people talking about a product are more valuable than the company saying it is great itself.
- For S-tier clients, hiring talent from the target firms can be a strong acquisition strategy because the person brings relationships and credibility.
- Mid-level and lower-level talent can be recruited through job ads; senior talent usually comes through outreach, headhunters, or highly specific industry placements.
- Recruiting funnels obey the same rules as marketing funnels: if candidates do not respond, the offer, funnel, headline, and copy need work.
