# How to get what you want every time

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out five negotiation tactics he learned mostly from mentors "in the streets" rather than from books, drawn from his M&A and deal experience at acquisition.com. He applies each tactic across three contexts — employees/employers, vendors/customers, and partners (M&A, investment) — and threads a live example throughout: his ongoing negotiation to buy a ~$20M home for Leila. The five tactics are BATNA (best alternative), anchoring, MESO (multiple equivalent simultaneous offers), reciprocity/horse-trading, and framing.

## Key claims

- **You get what you negotiate, not what you deserve.** Negotiation is unavoidable "fruit of life" — everyone is at minimum an employee and a customer of vendors.
- **Tactic 1 — BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement):** the single greatest source of psychological power in a negotiation, and it is built *before* you sit down. Research cited (London Business School) that negotiators who know their alternatives set higher aspirations, make more aggressive first offers, and get better outcomes. Your BATNA is a counter-anchor and a decision standard: only accept deals better than your best alternative.
- Practical BATNA sourcing: as an employee, get another job offer before negotiating (but don't overuse it — you lose goodwill); as a buyer, get multiple bids; as a vendor, your BATNA is your other customers (a supply/demand leverage point). Never take the first offer.
- Having no other offers and needing the deal is "trying to win at poker only on bluffing" — a bad position. He'd rather hold pocket aces.
- With a strong BATNA you can overtly present the competing offer ("beat it or no worries") or hold it privately and swing higher, because your plan B is not bad.
- **Tactic 2 — Anchoring:** the first number set in a negotiation is the anchor, whether or not you disclaim it. Cites Kahneman ("Daniel Nobel's prize winner") on anchoring bias — people overweight initial information and adjust insufficiently. Anchor as high as possible; the big number shifts the whole range and even the *increments* people think in.
- You can also anchor increments, not just price. Live house example: listed $25M → dropped to $20M; he offered $15M; they countered ~$17M (moved $3M); he countered $15.25M (moved only $250K) to signal he won't move much, then stacked terms (all cash, plus wanting the ~$4M of furniture).
- "A lot of people are always way too fixated on the price and not enough on the terms."
- Anchoring pro-tip (put the other side at ease): after accepting a fast "yes," tell them you wouldn't have done it for less/more — makes them feel good regardless of whether it's true. He experienced this when buying a penthouse.
- Anchoring a mess-up: when a vendor damages something, don't ask "what can you knock off?" — ask what it would cost/inconvenience *them* to fix it, then use their own number as the discount. (Learned from a gym partner's custom front-desk story.)
- **Tactic 3 — MESO (multiple equivalent simultaneous offers):** present offers A/B/C with different price/term mixes, all acceptable to you. Embeds reciprocity, signals reasonableness, and teases out the other party's priorities without you revealing yours. Cites Journal of Personality and Social Psychology on MESO increasing mutually beneficial outcomes.
- Negotiation is **not** a zero-sum game — different parties value things differently, so trading on differently-weighted terms makes it positive-sum. This was a key early misunderstanding of his.
- Counter to a MESO (learned from friend "Shiron"/Chiron, "done more deals than anyone I know"): take the best part of each and propose "option D," or ask the other side to give you two or three versions and then recombine.
- **Tactic 4 — Reciprocity:** key to persuasion, but only works within cultures where reciprocity is the norm; in mixed-culture situations people from non-reciprocity cultures may exploit it. People sense *that* a concession was traded more than they weigh its *relative value* — so break your deal into many small pieces to trade more times (cash vs. financed, 30- vs. 90-day close, furniture, etc.). Use the value-equation variables — speed, price, risk, ease. He keeps a "deal sheet" with ~80 variables he can change so he can make concessions without moving price.
- **Tactic 5 — Framing:** most important for employees and vendors. Frame yourself/your offer as an *investment* with a return, not a cost/overhead; if you're on the other side, reframe it as a cost. People often accept whatever frame is presented. Example: "$5,000 investment → $15,000 in maintenance cost savings" beats "this costs five grand." Bring data to support the frame (e.g., resale-value data for pools/awnings: if a $100K pool adds $200K in home value, "the pool's free" and you get to enjoy it).

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "You have to negotiate and you get what you negotiate, not what you deserve. That may sound not fair, but it's also the truth."
- "You only want to sell when you don't want to sell. You want to buy when you don't want to buy because you have something else."
- "It's them versus me and it's who wants it less."
- "It's just trying to win at poker only on bluffing. It's a bad position to be in. I would rather have pocket aces."
- "A lot of people are always way too fixated on the price and not enough on the terms."
- "If there's ever someone who messes something up, instead of saying, 'Hey, what can you knock off the price?' Ask them what the big inconvenience would be for them, ascribe a price to it. And then they have a hard time backing down from that."
- "One of the big things that I misunderstood in the beginning is that I assumed negotiation was a zero-sum game. And it's never a zero-sum game because you're a different person. You have different needs."
- "For me, I have this big deal sheet that has 80 different things that I can change about a deal."
- "If someone knows they spent $100,000 on a pool and adds $100,000 to their house, I'm like, then the pool's free. Except you get to enjoy the pool the whole time."
