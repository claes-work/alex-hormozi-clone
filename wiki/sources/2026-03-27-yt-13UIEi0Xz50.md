# Why Figuring Out Why Is a Waste of Time

## Summary
In a podcast exchange, Alex Hormozi argues against spending time discovering the "why" behind one's own or others' behavior, holding that any "why" is just a self-crafted narrative that may not reflect reality and can never be verified. He advocates focusing on observable behaviors — what changed, to what extent, and whether it helps achieve a goal — because prediction enables control. A podcast host pushes back, saying the pursuit of understanding why (not the answer itself) is one of his greatest sources of pleasure; Hormozi concedes learning is enjoyable but frames narrative-seeking as "mental masturbation" that neither he nor the host disputes is fun, just not useful for changing behavior.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that for most people "figuring out why" means accepting a self-crafted narrative that may have no bearing on reality, so he focuses purely on observable behavior because it better predicts one's own and others' actions.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues you will never actually know why you did something — only adopt a narrative — because you cannot relive the same circumstances (an "n equals one" problem).
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the useful question as behavioral: identify which specific behaviors changed (e.g. after a relationship), measure the extent, and ask only whether keeping them increases or decreases the likelihood of achieving a goal — if it decreases it, change it; if not, keep it.
- As of March 2026, Alex links prediction to control: understanding the dependent variables lets you predict, predicting lets you influence the variables, and influencing them lets you control the outcome/system.
- As of March 2026, Alex states this is a reason he is "not the biggest fan of therapy as is traditionally practiced," using a tennis-grip analogy — a coach changing your grip need not first discover why you held it that way.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes keeping three questions posted on his wall to interrogate vague claims: "What does that mean? How do you know that? And why does it matter?"
- As of March 2026, Alex calls narrative-seeking "mental masturbation" but explicitly does not decry it — if people enjoy it, that is fine; it just does not help change behavior.
- (Caller claim, not Alex) The podcast host contends the pursuit of trying to work out why — even when wrong or incomplete — is intrinsically fascinating and a top source of pleasure, citing his love of evolutionary psychology and 900-plus episodes of his podcast.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "When they figured out why, it means that I have crafted a narrative that I accept, which may or may not have any bearing on reality. And so I focus purely on the observable because they are far more predictable in terms of predicting your own behavior and predicting other people's behavior."

> "There are so many variables. Why do I need to know why? And I never will anyways."

> "If you can predict, it means you can control, which people don't like that. But if you can predict what's going to happen, it means that you know what the variables are and you can influence those variables, which you can influence the outcome, which means you can control the system."

> "You'll never know why. You will have a narrative that you've accepted."

> "If I were playing tennis and I went to a coach and they said, 'Hey, I need you to change your grip from this to this,' would I spend the vast majority of that session with the coach being like, 'Let's try to discover why you hold the grip that way?'"

> "The reason I have three statements on my wall... What does that mean? How do you know that? And why does it matter?"

> "I push back on narrative so heavily because, in a way — and take this not as a slight — I see a lot of that as mental masturbation."

> "Does me counting my calories and being a diva when I order decrease the likelihood that I find the next mate? If the answer is yes, then I'll change the behavior. If no, [forget] it."
