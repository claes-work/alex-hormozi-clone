# Stop Making Excuses

## Summary

In this short solo clip, Alex Hormozi argues that excuses are corrosive precisely
*because* they may be valid. He reframes accountability around a Leila Hormozi quote —
"it's not your fault, but it is still your problem" — and characterizes an excuse as a
"permission slip for mediocrity": a request for others to grant you respect without the
outcome. His core claim is that the only person truly persuaded by an excuse is yourself,
and that you forfeit self-respect by knowing you could have done more. He shares a
personal mantra he uses when he doesn't want to do something. The clip closes with a plug
for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Excuses are dangerous because they may genuinely be valid —
  the person may actually be right about their circumstances — which is exactly what makes
  them hard to reject.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) He cites Leila Hormozi's line that something can be not your
  fault yet still your problem; either way you have to do something about it, or you wait
  and continue not living the life you want.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) An excuse is fundamentally "a permission slip for mediocrity" —
  a way to seek permission from everyone else to keep receiving respect without producing
  the outcome, on the basis of extenuating circumstances.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) The only person who actually believes an excuse is the person
  making it. People who love you (e.g. your mother) may affirm you're a "special
  snowflake," but you won't earn anyone's real respect that way.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) The respect that matters most is your own, and you can never
  fully earn it via excuses because you'll always know you could have done more or better —
  that knowledge is what keeps him up at night.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) His personal mantra for unwanted tasks is "I will do what is
  required." Doing what's required is a higher standard than doing your best, because what's
  required may currently exceed your best — but your best can improve over time.
- (2026-02-26, paraphrase) Promotes his free acquisition.com/roadmap: a 10-stage roadmap
  from $0 to $100M+ (a milestone he says fewer than 1% of companies reach and he has done
  multiple times), broken down across eight business functions, describing each
  constraint's symptoms and the steps taken to graduate — applied across software, physical
  products, service, and brick-and-mortar businesses.
