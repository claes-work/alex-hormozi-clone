# Helping an Online Coach Doing $1M/Year Scale

> Live coaching Q&A. The questioners are two founders (audience), **not** Alex. Their
> business facts and numbers are **self-reported**. Only Alex's answers are attributed to
> him below.

## Summary

Two young founders run an online coaching business that sells a $397 digital training
program to youth soccer players who want to go pro. They self-report ~$1M/year in
revenue (near-all profit, all organic traffic, no paid ads) and say they want to reach
$12M and eventually **sell** the company. Advisors have told them it's a "leads issue —
do more volume."

Alex reframes the problem around their actual goal. He argues a purely digital,
one-time-purchase business built on the founder's face **won't be sellable**, and that
"building to sell" rarely works unless you enter a boring, roll-up-style business.
He lays out two divergent paths: (1) the **make-more-money path** — add a high-ticket
backend upsell to existing buyers, then reinvest the excess cash into paid ads (his
"two triples" to ~$1M/month, ~$3–4M/year profit on ~$10M revenue), which is the lowest
operational change but produces an unsellable company; or (2) the **build-to-sell
path** — create a differentiated branded (likely physical) product, or convert the
education into a recurring subscription with enterprise value, which is slower and
harder but sellable. He also floats turning the customer base into a discount
**"association"** whose aggregate buying power creates a genuine network effect.

## Key claims

_All dated 2026-02-18. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Attributed to Alex
Hormozi._

- A purely **digital, one-time product** built around the founder's own face will not
  produce a sellable company; if an exit is the goal, that model is the wrong vehicle.
- To build something sellable you need either (a) a differentiated, brand-heavy,
  quality-obsessed **physical product** — expect Chinese dupes once you show any success,
  which is exactly why brand matters — or (b) a **recurring/subscription** model (e.g.
  selling the education to teams) that carries higher enterprise value.
- **"Build it to sell it" rarely works.** It's genuinely rare unless you go into a boring
  business; experienced M&A operators rolling up (e.g. HVAC) is a different game from a
  founder who "wants to start a business and sell it." Instead, step into the customer's
  shoes and provide consistent value long enough that the thing becomes valuable enough
  for someone to buy.
- Being the only player in a space does **not** mean you're playing the wrong game.
- ★ Turn the buyer base into an **"association"**: aggregate the members' buying power to
  negotiate discounts on soccer-related products. Target ~10x value (a $400/year member
  saves ~$4,000/year), which makes membership extremely sticky and creates a **true
  network effect** — unlike a service business, where adding customers tends to *degrade*
  service quality, a network business gets *stronger* (more buying/negotiating power) as
  it grows.
- The core LTV lever: with a one-time product, to grow you must **either make each
  customer worth more or sell to more customers — usually both.**
- ★ The **"two triples"** growth path for their situation: (1) first triple — call every
  existing $400 buyer and sell them a ~$4,000 offer; (2) second triple — reinvest the
  excess cash from the first triple into **paid ads** (which they must learn). Result:
  roughly $1M/month, moving from ~$1M/year profit on $1M revenue to ~$3–4M/year profit on
  ~$10M revenue. Higher earnings, but **not a sellable company.**
- Two concrete offer options: **Option 1** — add a **backend** of higher-touch services
  (e.g. ~1 in 20, maybe 1 in 4, of $400 buyers take a $4,000 offer). **Option 2** — keep
  it digital and add a **recurring element** (make the $397 renew every 6 months or
  annually). Adding a more expensive thing is significantly easier than going physical.
- On sequencing: don't overthink front-end vs backend — **just call the people who bought
  the $400 thing and ask them to buy the $4,000 thing.**
- Sellable businesses are more valuable precisely **because they're slower and harder to
  build.** Choice of path is a values/lifestyle question: young founders can just take the
  money now, or build the harder recurring/branded asset for a big exit.
