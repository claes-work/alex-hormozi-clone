# Your Offer Isn't Bad, Your Timing Is

## Summary

A short sales-teaching clip in which Alex Hormozi argues that _when_ you sell
is as important as _what_ or _how_ you sell — "the right offer at the wrong time
is still the wrong offer." His central rule: sell at the **point of greatest
deprivation, not the point of greatest value**. He illustrates with a restaurant
analogy — you would never upsell a second steak to a full customer, but you
would to a starving one before the first bite — and generalizes it: motivation
to act on anything equals the amount of lack (deprivation) a person feels. In a
monetary context the deprivation is not physiological but _perceived_, so the
seller's job is to build up the perception of lack (e.g. exposing someone to
much richer people makes them feel poor and motivates them to get richer). A
second person is present but only interjects ("Mhm", "Yeah"); all substantive
content is Alex. See [[../topics/business/sales]] and
[[../topics/business/offers]].

## Key claims

_(All from 2026-02-15; single substantive speaker, Alex Hormozi — paraphrased.)_

- ★ **Sell at the point of greatest deprivation, not the point of greatest
  value.** These two points only sometimes intersect. Once you have delivered
  value, you should create deprivation around the _next_ thing rather than
  pitching while the buyer feels satisfied. This is the fresh framing of this
  clip — a distinct timing rule for _when_ to make the offer.
- (paraphrase) A common entrepreneur mistake is not _what_ or _how_ they sell
  but _when_ — "the right offer at the wrong time is still the wrong offer." So
  sales timing is a first-class variable, not an afterthought.
- (paraphrase) People wrongly assume the moment a buyer has received a lot of
  value is the moment to sell more. Satisfaction kills motivation; a full
  customer will not buy a second steak no matter how good the first was.
- (paraphrase) Motivation to take action on anything equals the amount of lack /
  deprivation a person feels — you are motivated to sleep when exhausted, eat
  when starving. Sell to the buyer while the "gap" is open, before they are
  satisfied.
- (paraphrase) This is why a pitch is "all about the pain": agitate the pain and
  stretch the gap between where the buyer is and where they want to be, making
  it bigger.
- (paraphrase) In a monetary (business) context the deprivation gap is not
  physiological but **perceived**, so the seller must build up the _perception_
  of deprivation.
- (paraphrase) Example of manufacturing perceived deprivation: being around
  people much richer than you makes you feel poor, which motivates you to get
  richer; being the richest person you know removes that motivation.

## Overlap note

This connects to Hormozi's existing offers material — buyer readiness and the
idea that offers themselves don't "decay" (bad products/timing do). What is
**distinct** here is the explicit _timing rule_: sell at the point of greatest
**deprivation**, not the point of greatest **value**, and the corollary that a
seller can manufacture _perceived_ deprivation in a monetary context. This is
also a natural upsell/timing principle — pitch the next thing right after value
is delivered but before satisfaction sets in — so it cross-links to
[[../topics/business/upsell-offers]]. Promote the deprivation-timing framing on
a synthesis pass.
