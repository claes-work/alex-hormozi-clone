# A Mediocre Plan Beats a Perfect One Every Time

## Summary

Alex argues that the money is made in the wait: the hard part is sticking to a good-enough
plan long enough for reinforcement to arrive. A mediocre plan followed consistently beats
a perfect plan abandoned early. He connects this to fitness, launch preparation, and the
"stick-to-it muscle." He also pushes back on "must be nice" responses by asking what the
mindset of helplessness actually gets the person.

## Key claims

- A good-enough plan followed consistently beats an ideal plan that is not followed.
- The "stick-to-it muscle" grows after the first reward reinforces the behavior.
- Self-reported: after a well-prepared presentation removed his anxiety, he realized that
  level of preparation had become the new bar every time.
- Sympathy may be valid, but a helplessness mindset does not create outcomes.
- For any constraint, find the person with more constraints who still did the thing, then
  study what they do differently.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "A mediocre plan that's stuck to always outperforms an amazing and perfect plan."
