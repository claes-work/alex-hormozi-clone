# Hard Money Lending EXPLAINED [I lent $3,000,000 using this] (2021-09-22)

## Summary

Hormozi walks through a real hard-money (private) lending deal he made — a $2.5M note against a distressed 334-unit Memphis building bought out of foreclosure — as a "part two" reporting what actually happened. He details the deal structure, the risk mitigations that made it safe, the ~$100K/month returns, and an unexpected episode in which the borrower voluntarily corrected an invoicing error in Hormozi's favor. His framing lesson: getting rich is about mitigating downside and, above all, that trust is the most valuable currency in business.

> ⚠️ SELF-REPORTED: The entire deal — amounts, terms, and the borrower's conduct — is Hormozi's own account and is not independently verified here.

## Key claims

- (2021, self-reported) He wired $2.5M cash on a $2.5M note to buyers purchasing a 334-unit building out of foreclosure in Memphis — condemned, in a "war zone," with squatters and drug dealers; the low price relative to unit count was why the deal appealed to him.
- (2021) He likes hard money / private lending because you effectively become the bank: you set the deal structure, defining upside and downside so you can mitigate downside risk.
- (2021) Wealth philosophy stated here: the rich focus on downside, not upside (the poor buy lottery tickets thinking only of upside); getting rich is about making fewer mistakes and using time as an asset rather than a liability.
- (2021, self-reported) Downside protections layered into the deal: (1) two borrowers **personally guaranteed** the note, each with net worth over $2.5M; (2) the building had **three standing offers of $5M-$5.6M**, and the $5.6M buyer had put down **$300K earnest money**; (3) his cash paid off the last lienholder so he took the **first/preferred title position** — no other liens ahead of him if it defaulted.
- (2021, self-reported) Payment terms: **4% per month** interest on $2.5M = **$100,000/month**; after four months (deal was supposed to close in 60 days) the rate **doubled to ~$200K/month** to reflect added risk; plus a **two-month minimum carry** guaranteeing at least $200K regardless of how fast they repaid.
- (2021, self-reported) The lawyer error / trust episode: Hormozi's lawyer under-invoiced the payoff at $275K. The borrower's own lawyer spotted it and advised staying silent to save ~$100K; the borrower refused, disclosed the error, fired his lawyer, and paid the correct amount (~$300K + $3,333/day, ~$350K total). Hormozi got back a check for ~$2.85M.
- (2021) He frames the two failures via his "three circles" (skill, ethics, hard work): his lawyer's was an error of *skill*, the borrower's lawyer's was an error of *ethics*.
- (2021) Trust is the most valuable currency — "you only get one name" — and the borrower's honesty was, in his view, more valuable than the deal itself, opening the door to $10M-$50M future deals.
- (2021, self-reported) Tax note: gains are ordinary income, not capital gains, taxed ~50%, so net was ~$175K after tax — which is why he requires very high returns on private lending; he'd still make the loan again for a 10-20% annual return.
- (2021) He says he doesn't sell anything on the channel or run a mastermind because he can make ~$400K just by moving money; acquisition.com trainings are free and the book is 99 cents.
