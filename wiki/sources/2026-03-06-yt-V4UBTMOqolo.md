# How to Buy Skills

## Summary
Solo monologue on treating skill acquisition as investing rather than spending. Alex's core thesis: "the only thing more expensive than education is not being educated." He argues that the value of any education equals the **delta between where you are and where you want to go** — if your income goal is $1M/year and you make $100k, the education that closes that gap is worth roughly that difference, so a $50k spend to learn a million-dollar-per-year skill means every year you *don't* do it you forgo $950k. He reframes the vocabulary: people use "poor people language" (calling education "expensive") when they should use "investor language" — an investment is never "expensive," it just has a cost and a return, and the asset happens to be you (uniquely, one you can't sell but can grow). He illustrates his willingness to pay with a story: he wrote a **$350,000 check for a single dinner with a man worth $7 billion**, escalating offers (100k → 200k → 250k → 350k) that the man kept declining until, at 350k, he agreed to dinner and never actually took the money — Alex reads it as the billionaire testing whether he'd truly pay. He describes reinvesting nearly all surplus into his own education, network, and surroundings — "my biggest expense was me" — on workshops, seminars, and coaching programs, an industry he says is full of "shitty people" from which he still extracted enormous value (including learning what *not* to do). He then lays out his method for extracting value from any paid program: (1) do everything they say to the letter the first time, (2) take what works and toss the rest ("only take in that which makes you stronger" — a frame he borrows from the sword of Gryffindor in Harry Potter), (3) if it fails, use your other skills to patch the gap, and (4) find the program's success story and reverse-engineer what they did that the teaching omitted. He closes with a **bridge/bricks metaphor**: education is bricks you lay toward money; results differ across people not because a program is a "rip-off" but because successful people already owned the missing bricks from prior learning — you wouldn't call a house's foundation a rip-off for not being a whole house, nor call an arithmetic teacher a moron because someone else later taught you calculus. Fault "only matters in the eyes of the law"; the only thing that matters is the outcome, which is under your control. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
