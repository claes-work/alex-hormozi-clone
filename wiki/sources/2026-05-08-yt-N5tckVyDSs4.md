# How To Actually Get Ahead of 99% of People

## Summary

Alex reads from an internal Acquisition.com sales onboarding note about becoming
exceptional, then argues that "more" is often the highest risk-adjusted return move.
When something already works, changing it has a fixed implementation cost and often
causes a temporary output drop; repetition, focus, and doing more of the working thing
compound better than constant optimization.

## Key claims (Alex's advice, dated 2026-05-08)

- Alex says wanting to be top 1%, 0.1%, or 0.01% has no bearing unless action matches
  the aspiration.
- He says becoming exceptional requires living an exceptional life, which may not be
  better, only different enough that many people reject it.
- He says the secret to success in the internal note is to get better and never stop.
- He says "more" has the highest risk-adjusted return when a business already has
  something that works.
- He contrasts maximizers, who ask how to get as much as possible, with optimizers, who
  ask how to get the most from the least.
- He says diminishing returns are still returns when the objective is winning.
- He self-reports learning this from high school: working less did not matter when
  another student got into Duke and he did not.
- He says change has a fixed cost and variable reward, and implementation often causes
  about a 20% performance drop in his observation.
- He says businesses are often imperfect, but repetition builds depth of understanding
  and skill.
- He says small businesses have limited resources for change, so they should choose one
  or two material bets rather than constant tweaks.
- He credits Sharran with the dinner-strategy analogy: decide what you want to eat,
  then get the ingredients, rather than only cooking from what is already in the
  fridge.
