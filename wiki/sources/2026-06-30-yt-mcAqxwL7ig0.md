# You're Too Cheap to Hire Anyone Good

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a trade-show-booth company doing about $10M with 30% margins that is constrained by skilled tradespeople such as welders and carpenters. Alex works through the gross-profit math per tradesperson and argues that if a $150K welder unlocks $200K+ in incremental gross profit, paying more is rational. He generalizes this into a talent-side version of LTGP:CAC: lifetime gross profit per employee compared with cost of acquiring talent.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Trade-show-booth founder** - the coachee. His business, revenue, margins, trade roles, and labor market are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If the founder has money and a missing role creates high gross profit, the constraint may be unwillingness to advertise/pay enough for qualified talent.
- The right question is how much gross profit a role creates after material costs, then how much compensation would reliably fill that role.
- If advertising at a higher wage attracts more qualified applicants and still leaves profit, the founder should do it.
- Alex says one of the biggest remaining arbitrages in business is paying 20-30% more for A+ talent because they are worth three or four times B talent.
- In supply-constrained trades, the supply side has the upper hand; owners must accept that acquiring and paying good tradespeople will cost more.
- Many trades businesses do not think this way, which creates an opportunity for owners who do the math.
- The talent-side equation mirrors customer acquisition: lifetime gross profit per employee vs. cost of acquiring talent.
