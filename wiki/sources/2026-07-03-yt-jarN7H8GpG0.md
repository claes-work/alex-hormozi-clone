# The Job of the Business Is Not to Satisfy Your Itches

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Jason, whose Austin med spa / wellness business has three locations and wants to grow from about $6.7M to $20M. Alex argues the next move is not another shiny object or location; it is fixing the existing locations' margins, talent, sales motion, membership model, utilization, and paid acquisition. He says the business should serve customers, not the founders' entrepreneurial itches.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Jason** - the coachee. His business, spouse/partner, locations, revenue, margins, and customer-acquisition mix are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The job of a business is not to satisfy the founder's entrepreneurial itches; it is to satisfy customers.
- If a founder has a model with strong returns on capital, the constraint may simply be unwillingness to keep doing the thing that works.
- Multi-location service businesses often overexpand: location one has founder magic, later locations dilute attention, revenue per location drops, and profit compresses.
- The med-spa business should fix current locations before opening more, aiming for materially higher margins before adding the fourth location.
- Organic/SEO-heavy demand can reflect a favorable supply-demand curve rather than a mature acquisition system.
- If the business learns PPC and paid social, Alex expects it to outperform local competitors who rely on reputation and walk-in demand.
- The first fixes are sales motion and membership model: more people should leave with a membership, producing revenue retention, better scheduling, higher sessions per hour, and higher revenue per square foot.
- Talent retention matters because injectors/providers can walk with customer relationships; compensation, career path, and model design should create loyalty to the brand rather than only to an individual provider.
