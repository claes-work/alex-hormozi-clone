# Helping a Software Consulting Firm Scale From $4M to $50M

## Summary

A short live coaching Q&A (workshop setting). The questioner (introduces himself as
**Alberto**) owns a **software consulting firm in Puerto Rico** doing software and
automation, mostly for **government agencies** ("in Puerto Rico that's the biggest
customer"), and has started exporting services to US companies that outsource for a
cheaper price — with reduced taxes on exports and R&D tax credits. He is at the **$4M
mark**, wrote $10M on the intake form, but after an exercise at the event now wants
**$20–50M** "so I can grow my multiple and maybe exit." He arrived thinking his problem
was marketing and sales; after two days he thinks it's "hiring and focus." Ad spend is
tiny (under $1–5k/month, self-reported), delivery is fine — he says he could reach ~$6M
with current staff — and he has only **two "unicorn" face-to-face closers**, no sales
training, no scripting; he used to sell himself but wanted out of that seat.

Alex's contribution is almost entirely **diagnostic questioning followed by a
theory-of-constraints prescription**. He walks Alberto down the funnel with a sequence of
sorting questions: do you have more business than you can handle? (No — delivery has ~$6M
of headroom, "so 6 [million] is sitting there.") Then manpower isn't the constraint —
"your demand is constrained." Getting leads seems to work (the small ad spend gets
appointments, which amuses Alex: government agencies actually responding to ads is a
first for him), so the choke point is **down-funnel: sales capacity and conversion**.
Alex reframes Alberto's self-diagnosis: it's not a focus issue, "it's a bigger business
issue" — the business is missing an **entire sales system** (training, scripting, setters
and closers, more than two reps). On the question of whether Alberto should post and
advertise more, Alex concedes that letting more people know about your stuff is
"unlikely [to] make you less money," but invokes the **theory of constraints**: focus on
the rate limiter. If leads already exceed sales capacity, then improving the landing
page, ad click-through, or page conversion yields nothing — "if you improve your landing
page, they're booked out. If you improve your ads, they're booked out." Every upstream
improvement only increases throughput when you are not supply constrained downstream.
The sequencing: **first increase sales capacity, then increase the market** and push more
into the front end. Alex closes by pitching Acquisition.com's help building the sales
team/system ("you should"), followed by the standard acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift
outro (10 growth stages, quiz, functions across product, marketing, sales, customer
success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance; thank-you page books a call, in-person Vegas
session if they can help).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-16. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Diagnose the constraint by walking the funnel with sorting questions.** Alex's
  sequence: (1) Do you have more business than you can handle? If delivery has headroom,
  manpower/supply is not the constraint — "your demand is constrained." (2) To get more
  customers you need leads and lead conversion; if lead-getting works (ads book
  appointments), the choke point is down-funnel in sales.
- **The owner's self-diagnosis gets reframed.** Alberto called it "hiring and focus";
  Alex: it's not a focus issue, "it's a bigger business issue" — there is no sales
  system. The prescription is to build an entire sales system (training, scripting,
  setter/closer structure, more than two reps), which Alex notes Alberto had effectively
  already learned at the event.
- **More awareness almost never loses you money — but constraint focus overrides it.**
  "If you let more people know about your stuff … it's unlikely that you'll make less
  money"; still, the point of the theory of constraints is to work the rate limiter, not
  whatever marketing activity is most visible.
- **Theory of constraints applied to a pipeline: upstream improvements only raise
  throughput if you are not supply constrained downstream.** Better landing-page
  conversion, better ad click-through — all of it yields more throughput *only* when the
  downstream stage has capacity. When sales is booked out, "if you improve your landing
  page, they're booked out. If you improve your ads, they're booked out. There's nothing
  you can do here." Sales is the rate limiter.
- **Sequencing the fix: first increase sales capacity, second increase the market.** Fix
  the sales bottleneck, then push more volume into the front end (more spend, more
  market).
- **A sales-trainer/consultant does not need to be a current practitioner of the
  client's trade for the diagnosis to hold** — implicit here; the whole exchange runs on
  funnel logic, not software-industry knowledge. _(Marked: editor's observation, not an
  Alex statement.)_
- _(Aside)_ Alex remarks that Alberto is the first person he's ever heard of for whom
  government-agency customers actually respond to advertising (Alberto clarifies it's
  Puerto Rico's government, "a little bit different" from federal).
- _(Pitch)_ Alex directly invites Alberto to work with Acquisition.com on building the
  sales team/system: "you should."
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz
  that locates a business and prescribes actions per function (product, marketing, sales,
  customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance); thank-you page books a call, with an
  in-person Vegas session if they can help.
