# Helping a Chiropractor Doing $2.4M/Year Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A chiropractor owner (100% owner, single location, 7,700 sq ft with ~4,700 occupied, ~$2.4M revenue at 30% margin ≈ $600k profit) has been stuck at $2.4M for 5 years and wants to reach $3.6M — to "get out of the swamp." He recently hired another doctor, flipping the practice from a supply constraint to a demand constraint. Lead sources: roughly half from referrals, ~20% paid Facebook/Meta ads, ~20% from Google (though he runs no Google ads, so Alex reframes that as word-of-mouth/SEO). He's afraid to spend more on Meta ads because he doesn't trust that it's working — he can't tell if a dollar in returns more than a dollar out. Alex's core diagnosis: the business lacks an **input-output equation** — it doesn't know which core actions increase revenue, so employees can't either. Because the practice is demand-constrained, **lead generation is the bottleneck**, and the levers are content, affiliates, and paid ads. The first fix is **attribution tracking** so a dollar in can be measured against $5/$10/$20 out. But the true order of operations, Alex says, starts even earlier: **pricing and packaging first** to free up cash flow (in the swamp, cash flow is the biggest need), then funnel that cash into ads (with attribution in place), then increase content cadence to build thought leadership. For a local business, sales is easy — existing local trust means you can one-call or two-conversation close even high-ticket, without elaborate funnels/indoctrination. The downside of local is a fixed-size market, so if you won't expand markets you must **dominate the one you're in**. Over the long term, strong brand/thought-leadership expands your radius so people travel and pay premium (he cites the Amen Clinics as a national-reputation example where people fly in). Finally, the owner's separate hiring problem (can't find high-quality doctors in Wyoming) "ladders up to" the same root cause: fix pricing → generate cash flow → pay doctors → reduce the business's reliance on the owner.
