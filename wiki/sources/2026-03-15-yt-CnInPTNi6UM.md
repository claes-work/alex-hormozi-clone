# Helping an Agency Fix Their Sales Process

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A founder who sells restaurant-operations consulting to restaurant owners (on track for ~$3.2M this year, wants to hit $10M by year-end) says his constraint is the sales process — from messaging through pricing — despite having more demand than he can currently unlock. He names four issues: messaging, the sales motion itself, pricing, and a "fast cash play." Alex works the problem live. First he models his **problem-diagnosis framework** — "logic, evidence, utility" (What does it mean? / How do you know that? / Why do I care?) — insisting on defining terms and answering "how do you know" with a quantitative measure (e.g. "80% of our calls are unqualified"). The client clarifies his calls are actually *qualified* but prospects don't know what's being offered, so the sales process gets harder. Alex's fix: an **indoctrination process before the call** (likely a video sales letter, which his team can help script), plus **questions that reverse-engineer the sale**. He illustrates **pre-framing** with the book-launch opt-in checkbox ("I want a VIP ticket") that changed nothing about the funnel yet lifted VIP sales ~16%, because the earlier question primes the later purchase. He then gives his **good-friction** rule: a filtering function should repel bad prospects and admit good ones; "the more good friction you add, the more money you make." His ROAS example: raising qualification bars roughly doubles cost-per-call (say $200 → $500) but return can go from 3x to 6x, and — the sneaky benefit — **sales efficiency doubles or triples**, so a smaller team closes more with less operational drag. On the fourth issue he explains a **fast cash play**: most owners sit on underutilized resources (dead contacts, old customers, a list, existing social following); a campaign that activates those resources lets the client "pay for yourself within the first 30 days" — Alex's "found money" principle (never take money you haven't already made the client). Finally, on clients who get the blueprint but don't take action, Alex prescribes **carrot-and-stick incentives**: unlockable bonuses/discounts for quasi-actions, and **guarantees made contingent on the client completing specific actions**, all reversed back to the front of the funnel; enforcement is "state the facts and tell the truth, be up front." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses problems with a three-question framework — "logic, evidence, utility": define the term (What does it mean?), quantify it (How do you know that? — answered with a measure like "80% of our calls are unqualified"), and test its importance (Why do I care? / is this actually the constraint?).
- As of March 2026, when prospects book calls but don't understand the offer, the fix is an indoctrination step *before* the call (often a video sales letter) plus sales questions that reverse-engineer the sale; the prospect should arrive already pre-framed toward buying.
- As of March 2026, pre-framing is powerful: a purely cosmetic opt-in checkbox ("I want a VIP ticket") that changed nothing else in the funnel raised VIP sales by ~16%, because an earlier question makes someone more likely to say yes when the offer is later presented.
- As of March 2026, high-traffic (especially organic) businesses need a filtering function that adds *good friction* — friction that repels bad prospects and admits good ones — as opposed to bad friction (arbitrary walls, slow pages) that repels everyone.
- As of March 2026, Alex's rule of thumb "yet to be proven wrong": the more good friction you add, the more money you make, and the more expensive each contact.
- As of March 2026, raising qualification bars raises cost-per-call (e.g. ~$200 → $500) but can raise ROAS more (e.g. 3x → 6x); the hidden win is that sales efficiency doubles or triples, so a smaller team closes more deals with less operational drag and scales easier.
- As of March 2026, a "fast cash play" works off a client's existing/underutilized resources (dead contacts, old customers, a list, an existing social following); Alex frames poor resource allocation as the fundamental reason businesses don't grow.
- As of March 2026, Alex's "found money" goal: structure the fast-cash campaign as the first part of onboarding so the client recoups the fee within the first 30 days — he never wants a client to pay him money he hasn't already made them.
- As of March 2026, to get clients to actually take action, use carrot-and-stick incentives: bonuses or discounts unlocked by taking a "quasi action," and guarantees made contingent on completing specific actions — all reversed back into the application and sales motion as the business's first activation point.
- As of March 2026, enforcing action-contingent terms is done by being up front: "state the facts and tell the truth."
