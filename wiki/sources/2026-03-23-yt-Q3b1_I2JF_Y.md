# To Anyone Who Wants to Be Exceptional

## Summary
A short motivational monologue in which Alex Hormozi argues that being exceptional requires being different, which necessarily brings social discomfort. He frames life as a long journey of self-acceptance and urges accepting one's own peculiarities early rather than conforming to a socially "acceptable" archetype. He notes that friends and family try to shove a changed person back into their old label, and that the awkward conversations this creates are the price of growth. The clip ends with a promo for the acquisition.com scaling roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues you cannot both fit in and be exceptional: fitting in produces internal conflict (not being 100% yourself), while being exceptional produces external conflict (others see you as different) — and you must pick one.
- As of March 2026, Alex believes most people have the potential to be exceptional because most people are peculiar in their own way, but they stifle it to be accepted and therefore never accomplish what they want.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames innovation as noticing a norm that never made sense to you and deciding to stop following it (he cites arbitrary norms like showering twice a day or wearing certain clothing).
- As of March 2026, Alex calls it fraud (a "micro rebellion against yourself") to keep performing an archetype — e.g. wearing cowboy boots you find less comfortable than New Balances just to signal an identity.
- As of March 2026, Alex cites a survey finding the main reason older people have less drama and are happier is that they "literally don't have time for it," and argues one should start living that way now rather than at 80.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he dislikes going home for the holidays because he must confront people who speak to him in a way he no longer accepts (whereas before he would let it roll off).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you want to be exceptional, you're going to be different from everyone else. That's what makes you exceptional. You can't fit in and also be exceptional."

> "When you fit in, you have internal conflict because you're not being 100% you. When you're exceptional, you have external conflict because everyone sees you as different. Pick one."

> "When your friends start to say, 'You've changed.' Remember, it's because they don't know how to say, 'You've grown.'"

> "I think most people have the potential to be exceptional because most people are peculiar in their own way. They just stifle that because they want to be accepted by most people, but in so doing never accomplish what they want to do because they conform."

> "If cowboy boots aren't as comfortable for you as New Balances are and you know that and you still wear cowboy boots, I would call you a fraud. Because that is like a micro rebellion against yourself."

> "If life is a long journey of self-acceptance, I think the earlier you can accept your own peculiarities as just part of you rather than trying to justify them or mold to the archetype that you think is acceptable within your social circle."

> "There was a survey where the number one reason that old people don't have as much drama and they're happier is they literally don't have time for it. And I was like, well, if I'm going to eventually be that way when I'm 80, I might as well just start being that way now."
