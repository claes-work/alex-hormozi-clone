# Building a $3,500,000 Business for Two Strangers in 52 Minutes

## Summary

In this live-build coaching episode, Alex Hormozi advises Calvin and Air, a husband-and-wife duo who own Basil & Co Thai Cuisine (Diamond Bar, CA) — a $3.5M/year restaurant at 19% net margin serving ~100,000 guests a year, now opening a second location. Alex diagnoses the business as fundamentally healthy (great product, word-of-mouth driven) and prescribes a stack of low-effort, high-leverage moves across five buckets: pricing, menu design, staff training, alcohol upsells, and second-location launch. The through-line: in a high-volume, thin-margin business, tiny changes (adding $0.99 to prices, a processing fee, a drink pairing) compound into large profit swings, and the product — not marketing — is what ultimately matters.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **On a long enough time horizon, the product is the only thing that matters.** Marketing only pulls your timeline forward — it introduces people to the product faster but cannot create a good business. Being good at sales/marketing accelerates decline if the product is bad.
- **Nail it then scale it.** Don't expand before the core model is solid; getting the 2nd location is faster than the 10th. Lock unit economics first: cost to open, time to profitability, payback period, grand-opening promo, staffing/culture.
- **Pricing move 1 — add a stated processing fee** (e.g. "+3% processing fee for credit cards") on the menu; it rarely affects order behavior, and a 3% top-line lift is ~15% more profit in a thin-margin business.
- **Pricing move 2 — charm pricing ($16 → $16.99).** ".99" isn't too "old school" unless you're serving $29–50 entrées as a luxury brand; most people judge price off the leading character. On a $16 average ticket, +$0.99 is ~6% revenue, which can move margins from 19% to ~25% — roughly +30% profit / ~$220K/year — with no expected drop in sales velocity.
- **Surge/weekend pricing.** Hand a separate weekend menu that adds ~$2 per dish (like Uber surge, or a dinner-vs-lunch menu) — customers don't feel it as an add-on.
- **Discounts: full price or free, never percentages off.** Free items over percentage discounts avoids training "discount seekers"; prefers a free item with a clear ascension path to full-paying customer. (Distinguishes this from prepayment or 24-hour buy incentives on sales calls, which he sees as legitimate.)
- **Menu design (3 priorities per section):** anchor with highest price at top; rank items within each box by gross profit (most profitable first); and surface "can't-miss for first-timers" highlight boxes — because nobody wants to decide "what's good." Balance high price, high gross margin, and the dishes that bring people back.
- **"Pairs well with" on the menu** next to each dish nudges alcohol attach — pure profit, gets you above the current 1% alcohol share.
- **Alcohol is the biggest lever here.** Going from 1% to ~6% of revenue on a high-margin line adds ~25% profitability. Run one rotating drink special ("made fresh here, we taste-test five and pick the best each week"), put it on a table tent so servers/customers never forget it, and keep the presentation upscale.
- **Cross-sell is one of eight ways to make a customer more valuable** (teased from his next book). Make it easy for the customer to say yes and easy for staff not to forget. "Do you want fries with that?" → "Do you want an alcoholic beverage with that?"
- **Reviews are the growth engine for a local/brick-and-mortar business.** People choose by searching online first; volume of reviews signals a destination. A 4.7 with 13,000 reviews beats a 5.0 with 50. Give a free item in exchange for a review — at point of sale, immediately, not "next time" (nobody cares about deferred). Can ask each person at a table to leave a review for their drinks; or the "manager ask" (review helps a named server hit a bonus) so you give nothing away.
- **Identify first-timers** (colored coaster/napkin so whole staff knows), give them a surprise free dessert at the end (peak-end bias — people remember the emotional peak and the ending), and hand a manager's business card with "free dessert" written on the back as a personalized comeback offer (better than a coupon).
- **AOV break points.** Set a free-gift threshold just above current average order ($60 avg → free item at $80, or $100 on weekends) to nudge spend up; works best on takeout/phone/online orders where servers aren't overloaded.
- **Training = behavior rules practiced as skits.** "When this, then that." Teach four rules, one per day, drilled ~20 reps in a pre-shift huddle: (1) greet & flag first-timers, (2) pitch the drink special, (3) suggest pairings/sides+alcohol, (4) end with review or comeback offer. Repetition ingrains it.
- **Servers are salespeople** having rapid sales conversations (a table of five = five sales interactions). Little nudges ("a lot of people do," "do you want to go ahead and take one of those?") phrase the question to get a yes. They're incentivized because tips rise.
- **Culture is reward and punishment.** ~1-in-3 business strategies succeed (McKinsey); the difference between 1/3 and 2/3 is culture — how people actually behave. Translate advice into "how will you get staff to do this every time," else it's just entertainment. Add a back-of-house leaderboard for most alcohol sales (cash bonus or schedule-pick priority).
- **Value vectors (from the Offers book, p.56):** improve any offer by making it less risky, faster, and easier. Applied here: reviews pay down risk; first-in-line access is the "faster" lever (not food speed); pre-paired drinks remove decision friction.
- **Second-location launch — influencer/affiliate night.** Run a VIP pre-opening for local influencers/affiliates (lead-getters with big megaphones); trade a free/discounted experience for their content, then reuse that creative in ads. An affiliate is an independent business that tells its audience to buy your stuff.
- **Loyalty programs — split verdict.** For a great-food restaurant, the food is the loyalty program; loyalty matters more for businesses like med spas. If doing one, use "running starts" (pre-fill 2–3 punches so people are 2–3x more likely to finish) and lead with real benefits: birthday freebies, a free first drink, and priority seating when there's a wait (that last one alone would sell him).
- **Fast, fun, and easy beats slow and hard** as an implementation standard.
