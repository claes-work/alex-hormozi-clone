# A Hard Learned Lesson (2020-11-24)

## Summary

In a solo podcast-style video, Hormozi shares one of his "hardest learned lessons":
as an entrepreneur levels up, the opportunities that appear become more attractive
("the woman in the red dress"), and the discipline to say no must be relearned at each
new scale. He argues that most entrepreneurs — unlike capital-unconstrained players like
Jeff Bezos — operate with limited money, time, and team bandwidth, so every bet carries a
real cost and should be filtered through three questions: what's my upside, what's my
downside, and can I live with the downside assuming it happens.

## Key claims

- (2020-11-24) Hormozi says he has recently been reading heavily from people who make much
  more money than he does, and that each step toward a "better ideal" in this direction
  makes him significantly more money. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) At every opportunity level, the opportunities become "sexier"; before you
  have money anything is attractive, but as you gain skills, bigger and better
  opportunities appear — so you must relearn to say no at each new level. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) He states he has made the mistake of saying yes to too-large opportunities
  "multiple times at different levels" over the past year and a half to two years, and is
  making the video partly for himself. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) He recommends the book *The Road Less Stupid* (Keith Cunningham), especially
  for entrepreneurs prone to getting excited about new ideas and constantly tweaking things.
  (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) He used to quote Jeff Bezos's baseball analogy: in baseball a home run scores
  at most four runs, but in business one big hit can score a thousand — and a 10% chance of
  a 100x payoff is mathematically worth taking every time. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) He adds a corrective sentence to Bezos: most entrepreneurs have constrained
  capital. If you enter the casino with only $30 and each bet is $10 for a 1-in-10 shot at
  a big payoff, you may only get three bets and thus roughly a 1-in-3 chance of winning
  anything — there is a real cost to each bet. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) The three-question decision filter from the book (as he uses it): What's my
  upside? What's my downside? Can I live with it assuming the downside happens? Assuming the
  loss will occur is what lets him say no. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) Business, at its base, is betting — more skill and less luck than poker, but
  still bets (e.g. hire a salesperson vs. spend on marketing, test a follow-up sequence vs.
  run SEO vs. cold outbound). (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) Citing study of Charlie Munger / Warren Buffett: the best bettors bet the
  least frequently but bet big on "no-brainers"; over ~40 years roughly 15 winning bets
  generated the bulk of their billions. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) An entrepreneur's greatest strength — optimism — can become their Achilles
  heel, causing them to overestimate the chance of success and underestimate the cost of
  the bet, especially when cash-constrained and making fast decisions on incomplete
  information. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-24) Applying the framework has let him say no to many small tests and tweaks and
  keep the team focused on fundamentals and compounding 1%-a-day improvement rather than
  changing everything every two weeks. (paraphrase)
