# My Process for Building Frameworks

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks how Alex retains and builds his frameworks; only Alex-attributed speech is used as voice data). Despite the title, Alex frames framework-building not as a memorization or acronym trick but as an epistemic discipline: the point of a framework is to **predict what happens next**, and better prediction requires "an accurate framework of how reality works." His core method is definitional precision. He argues most people "spend their time regurgitating and repeating things that they never thought about and saying words that they don't understand" — two people trading undefined terms then "roll a dice to see if they get what they want." His single greatest razor for defining reality accurately is **removing all sentiment, emotion, and psychology and looking only at what is observable** — a behaviorist frame. His test: imagine explaining a term to an alien or a toddler (would a court witness be able to confirm it happened?). He applies this to fuzzy words: "love me better" collapses into an observable behavior ("come give me a hug when I walk in the door"); the slogan "sales is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust" sounds great but is unusable because no one can act on it, whereas defining selling as "increasing the likelihood that someone makes a purchasing decision" is actionable and testable. He notes his content "seems both the same and different" from others' because he strips out the manifestation/energy/synchronicity layer — "maybe that stuff's true, but I can't see it, so how do I transfer it?" — and talks only about the observable world, which has improved both his communication and his ability to predict. He points out that every one of his books, like every legal document, opens with a recitation of definitions. Ends with the $100M scaling roadmap promo.
