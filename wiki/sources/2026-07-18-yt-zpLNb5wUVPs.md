# Don't Start a Software Company

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A clip. The questioner introduces himself as **Hector Quiroga**, owner
of a **US immigration law firm** that did **$24 million last year** and is at a
**$60 million run rate** now ("something happened in January"); his goal is **$100 million
in revenue**. He admits to "a little bit of a shiny object": in getting to $24M/$60M the
firm built substantial internal technology — processes, procedures, software, apps — and
he suspects "the tools are probably more valuable than the thing itself," so he wants to
sell the software to other immigration firms.

Alex's answer is immediate: **"Don't start a software company."** He frames it as a
pattern many service businesses hit: services become technologically enabled over time to
expand the service ratio (one person handling 100 customers instead of 10), which
dramatically improves margins — but the decision to then *sell* that tooling to
competitors is a mistake. True software is "such a different animal," and the firm can get
"almost the best of that world" as a **tech-enabled service**: above plain service revenue
and EBITDA multiples, tech-enabled services can fetch "13, 15, 18 times bottom line,"
while true SaaS multiples are unreachable anyway because Hector isn't going to "burn your
business to the ground." The alpha is in the **enterprise-value lift from having the
tools**, not from selling them — keep the software proprietary to "maintain and multiply
your competitive advantage," drive more EBITDA than everyone else, and force competitors
to copy the software to copy the firm.

Hector pivots: keep the software internal, but "lease my factory" — an affiliate/licensing
model where other lawyers (family, bankruptcy) get sales training and feed cases through
his machine, since opening his own locations costs ~$150,000 each and he can only open
two-three profitable firms a year. Alex calls this out as another **"do new" disguised as
"do more"** ("Do more so that I do new so that I could do more. I was like, 'I know what
you did there.'"). The arithmetic: Hector says roughly **four-five more offices** gets him
to $100M, and each office does something like **$15 million a year** — "So 24 months to go
from 24 million to 100 million? Then why are we talking about anything else?" Alex
generalizes the pattern: when people hit friction, "rather than deal with the constraint,
I'm going to start a new business." His prescription is **ruthless focus** — the reason
"the Zuckerbergs and Jobs get on stage and Bezos get on stage" talking about it — because
"at every level the opportunity that you have to say no to looks juicier." $150k per
location is "nothing" relative to the revenue; it's not a capital issue but "purely a
focus issue." If Hector put the software idea out of his mind and asked "How could I open
five this year?", he'd probably figure it out. The video ends with the standard
acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift CTA (10 stages, quiz, functions across product,
marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance; thank-you page books a
call, in-person Vegas session if they can help).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-18. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **(2026-07-18) Don't spin an internal tool into a software company.** Hormozi argues
  that a service business that built strong internal software should not start selling it
  (especially not to direct competitors); true software is "such a different animal," and
  the owner won't accept the burn required to earn true SaaS multiples anyway.
- **(2026-07-18) Tech-enabled service is "almost the best of that world."** Hormozi lays
  out a multiples ladder (paraphrase): plain service revenue/EBITDA multiples <
  tech-enabled service, which "sometimes can get 13, 15, 18 times bottom line" < true
  SaaS multiples (unreachable here). The enterprise-value lift from *having* the tools is
  a "huge amount of alpha" without pursuing SaaS.
- **(2026-07-18) Proprietary tooling is a moat, not a product.** Keep using the software
  to "maintain and multiply" competitive advantage: it yields market leadership and more
  EBITDA than competitors, and to copy the business they'd have to copy the software.
- **(2026-07-18) Why services add tech at all (paraphrase):** to expand the service
  ratio — one person serving ~100 customers instead of ~10 in the same time — which
  dramatically improves margins.
- **(2026-07-18) The constraint-avoidance pattern.** Hormozi says this "happens more
  times than it doesn't": faced with friction/a constraint, owners start a new business
  instead of dealing with the constraint. He also flags the questioner's licensing pivot
  as a new business dressed up as expansion ("This isn't a do more strategy").
- **(2026-07-18) The focus test:** "what's the one thing if it were true would make all
  my worries go away?" — here, five more offices = $100M, using a playbook the owner
  already knows, so "why are we talking about anything else?"
- **(2026-07-18) Ruthless focus scales with success.** Hormozi invokes Zuckerberg, Jobs,
  and Bezos on ruthless focus; "at every level the opportunity that you have to say no to
  looks juicier" precisely because you already have a base.
- **(2026-07-18) It's a focus issue, not a capital issue (paraphrase):** $150k per
  location is trivial relative to ~$15M/year per office; redirecting all the
  new-venture effort into "How could I open five this year?" would probably solve the
  speed constraint.
- _(Audience, self-reported)_ Hector Quiroga: US immigration law firm; $24M revenue last
  year, $60M current run rate, $100M goal; built internal software/apps/processes;
  new locations cost ~$150k and take too long (two-three profitable openings/year);
  estimates four-five more offices reach $100M; proposes licensing his "machine" plus
  sales training to family/bankruptcy/other lawyers.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours reviewing portfolio companies distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz
  that locates a business and prescribes actions per function (product, marketing, sales,
  customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance); thank-you page books a call, with an
  in-person Vegas session if they can help.
