# Sell Anything To Anyone With This Unusual Method (2021-03-16)

## Summary

In this early (2021) short-form sales/persuasion clip, Alex Hormozi teaches what he
calls his single favorite persuasion technique: the **"damaging admission"** — deliberately
stating true negatives about your offer (or yourself) so that the positive claim that
follows becomes more believable. The mechanical hinge is the word **"but"**, which he
frames as an attention amplifier: everything before "but" is diminished, everything after
is amplified. He argues buying is downstream of trust, and that owning your deficiencies
with integrity raises trust and therefore conversion.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-03-16)

- The degree to which a prospect buys is directly correlated with how much they trust you;
  in B2B, if a prospect truly believed a "$1 in, $10 out" promise, there would be no
  logical reason not to buy — so belief/trust is the real constraint. (2021)
- Trust decomposes into sub-beliefs: belief in themselves, in the system, and in the world;
  when those align, the prospect buys. (2021)
- Referrals close easily because the referrer has already "imbued you with trust" — the
  prospect arrives with high trust and you mainly need to get out of the way. (2021)
- The word "but" acts as an amplifier: everything after "but" is amplified, everything
  before it is diminished — so you can control where the prospect's attention lands. (2021)
- Reverse the usual order: instead of "positive, but negative," say "negative, but positive."
  The more negatives you stack first (and the more true and damaging they are), the more
  believable the positive claim that follows becomes. (2021)
- He says he learned the tactic from Eminem in the film *8 Mile*. (2021)
- Negative reviews lend credibility to positive ones: he'd rather buy from a 4.7-star /
  3,000-rating restaurant than a 5-star / 19-rating one; Gym Launch has many 1-star reviews
  alongside thousands of 5-stars. (2021)
- The Viagra "erection lasting longer than four hours" warning is cited as a damaging
  admission that amplified the product's appeal — a genuine risk that made the benefit seem
  assumed. (2021)
- The technique is grounded in integrity: you're only saying things the prospect would find
  out eventually, so you might as well use them to increase persuasiveness. (2021)
- Weave damaging admissions into copy, especially close to the call to action. (2021)
