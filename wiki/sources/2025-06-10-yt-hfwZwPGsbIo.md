# Why Doing More Is Actually Making You Less Money

## Summary

In a live in-person coaching session at the acquisition.com Las Vegas headquarters, Alex Hormozi coaches "Kira," a single mother of five running several overlapping businesses (a publishing house, book-writing luxury adventures, a TV show, a business school for women) who feels spread thin and wants clarity. Alex's core message: super-success requires concentrating your energy like a magnifying glass on one point, going "where the money's at" (the offer the market keeps buying), and trading novelty for loyalty to one business. He then delivers a tactical framework — every change to a business costs a guaranteed ~20% performance decrement, so entrepreneurs effectively get only a handful of "punch card" changes per department per year and should channel creative/ADD energy into marketing and content rather than constantly re-engineering the business.

## Key claims (focus / constraint argument)

- **Concentration beats potential.** Your potential/intelligence/work ethic is the *size* of the magnifying glass, but no matter how big it is, it does nothing unless focused on a single point. Talent just means more opportunities you must say *no* to.
- **The trade is novelty vs. scale.** Continuing to chase many things buys you novelty and excitement but caps your ultimate impact and business size. Concentrating gives up novelty to build something large with teams and systems.
- **Follow marketplace feedback ("go where the money's at").** When people repeatedly try to give you money for one specific thing, that is feedback from the marketplace; build the business around the door people keep knocking on, then remove the other doors and go all in.
- **Businesses rarely become what you first imagined.** In Alex's career, almost every business pivoted from his original idea to what the marketplace actually wanted — be "a bloodhound for value," proven by people paying.
- **You must say no to almost every opportunity** to make one opportunity worth pursuing; pursuing everything caused Alex multi-year plateaus.
- **The 20% cost-of-change rule.** Any change Alex makes to the business (sales process, onboarding, leadership, scripting) causes an immediate ~20% performance decrease from retraining and adjustment, even when the change is superior. It takes ~4–5 months to recover.
- **Risk-adjusted threshold for change.** Because of that cost, Alex will only make a change if he estimates a ~40% improvement with roughly a 50/50 chance of happening (a "risk-adjusted 20% improvement"). Estimating upside × probability improves with practice.
- **Left alone, people get better at their jobs** — you often capture the improvement anyway without paying the change cost.
- **Limited "shots."** Even automated tests (landing pages) are capped near ~52/year (one per week with enough traffic); people-side process changes are far fewer, maybe one per quarter (~4 per department per year). Treat it like a Warren Buffett-style punch card of limited lifetime investments.
- **Manage the idea itch.** Keep "Alex's big list of ideas" on your phone; write ideas down instead of calling emergency meetings, rank by estimated value × likelihood, and only act when bandwidth exists and the idea beats other ranked ideas.
- **Channel creative/ADD energy into marketing and content**, which is "accretive" to the business, instead of into re-engineering operations. Alex puts his ADD into content, ads, and writing books — the business does best when he is in writing mode because he interferes least.
- **Accept imperfection.** Every improvement he chases makes ~4 other things drop; some things stay broken and it isn't worth the cost of change. Most entrepreneurs change too many things too often and can never tell what broke.
- **Track real-time metrics.** Entrepreneurs miss the 20% decrement because they don't track; without fast, accurate, real-time metrics (NPS, churn, ascension, close rate) you can't see the cost of your own changes.
- **Tactical takeaways:** (1) channel ADD into creative work that benefits the business; (2) write crazy ideas down, tell no one, and sleep on them — if you still think about it a month later it "has legs"; (3) only pull an idea off the list if it's worth "detonating a bomb" in the business.
