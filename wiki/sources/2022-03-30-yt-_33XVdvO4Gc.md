# The day when everything changed for me...

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi recounts the moment his gym business first made
a sale without him — he got a text that a salesperson had closed deals while he
was driving, and pulled over because he "got emotional," realizing the business
could run without him. He uses this turning point as the springboard for his core
thesis on sales hiring: great salespeople are largely born, not made, so
selection beats training. He illustrates with his first "killer" hire (Mario
Negretti) closing a walk-in on pure rapport without knowing the pitch.

## Key claims

- The first time someone other than Alex closed a sale was an emotional
  turning point — proof the business "could actually work without you."
- When he started, he took every single sale himself out of fear someone would
  waste the opportunity.
- His first salesperson was not exceptional but "got the job done"; his first
  "killer" was Mario Negretti, who joined about a year into the business.
- Negretti closed a walk-in customer paid-in-full without knowing the pitch,
  winning purely on rapport — teaching Alex that selecting great talent beats
  training mediocre talent.
- Sales ability is "much more about selection than it is about training"; Alex
  says he has shifted over time toward "nature over nurture," especially for sales.
- Training's role is "to remind them how good they are, not necessarily to make
  them good."
- Analogy: Harvard doesn't produce the smartest people, it selects them; elite
  service firms (McKinsey, Bain, BCG) win by building a huge pool to skim the
  best talent.
- Cites Chick-fil-A founder Truett Cathy (transcript: "Estu Kathy"): "You win
  the championship in the draft."
- ROI argument: 10 hours moving someone from a 2 to a 4.5 is worse than 10 hours
  moving someone from a 6 to a 9; better base = more return on training time.
- If you pay well, the best salespeople are already employed — recruit them from
  businesses like yours to cut ramp-up time (only the product changes).
- Every "killer" he has hired closed a deal in their first sales cycle; if
  someone completes a full sales cycle without closing, he already knows they
  are not exceptional.
- Cutting the bottom performers raises the whole team: "whenever you cut the
  bottom, the top moves up." The longer he's in business, the shorter his
  tolerance for non-exceptional performers.
- Cites Jordan Belfort and Brad Lee as examples of sales trainers who were
  exceptional salespeople before they could teach; Alex says he had "already
  closed thousands of deals" before consuming his first sales training.

## Biography facts (dated, self-reported)

- **The turning point (self-reported):** While driving home, Alex received a text
  saying "we closed two sales today" (out of four people who had walked into the
  business). It was the first time anyone other than him had sold something. He
  pulled over because he got emotional / teary, realizing the business "could
  become a business that did not require me." This is framed as the day
  "everything changed."
- **First salesperson (self-reported):** Not exceptional, but able to get the job
  done; hired sometime after Alex had been taking every sale himself.
- **First "killer" salesperson (self-reported):** Mario Negretti, who came in
  "about a year into the business." On his first day, before Alex could train
  him, a walk-in ("some lady") came in; Negretti said "Go do your thing," took
  the customer with only "Fitness, nutrition, accountability" as guidance, and
  closed her paid-in-full without knowing the pitch. (Transcript renders the name
  variously as "Maru Negretti" / "Martin Negrety" / "Mario Integrity" — auto-caption
  noise; most likely Mario Negretti.)
- **Self-reported sales volume:** Had "already closed thousands of deals" before
  ever consuming a formal sales training.
- **Company scale (self-reported, as of recording):** "I own acquisition.com.
  It's a portfolio of companies that now does about $85 million a year."
