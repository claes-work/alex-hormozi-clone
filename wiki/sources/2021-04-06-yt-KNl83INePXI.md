# "I'm Broke... What Should I Do?" (2021-04-06)

## Summary

Responding to a viewer message from someone who is "dead broke," Hormozi argues (2021)
that the only thing worth doing when you have nothing is acquiring skills — because
skills can never be taken by a government, a divorce, or a financial crisis, and are
what lets entrepreneurs bounce back from zero. He walks through an
acquisition-entrepreneurship playbook (buy an existing profitable business instead of
buying a house or starting from scratch, using "agree on price, then agree on terms"
and seller financing) and illustrates it with a real deal — acquiring a fifth gym
location for roughly $40–50k on seller-financed terms with no money out of pocket. He
closes on his recurring thesis that the wealthiest people he knows have *lower* risk
tolerance than the poor, avoiding go-to-zero bets and instead buying upside "for
nothing."

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- The month after losing everything, he collected $110,000 cash in the next 30 days,
  which he attributes to skills alone. (2021)
- Skills are the one asset no government, person, divorce, revolution, or financial
  crisis can take from you — this is why entrepreneurs who hit zero can usually bounce
  back. (2021)
- As he moved up, the people around him had more money but *spent less* and worked
  harder to acquire things for less out of pocket. (2021)
- Buying a house with a mortgage just adds a monthly liability; the better use of, e.g.,
  $50k in savings is to acquire an existing profitable business. (2021)
- Small local businesses sell for roughly 2.5x earnings; a business doing $250k/yr in
  profit might sell for ~$625k — and you should not pay retail (broker) price. (2021)
- Contact business brokers not necessarily to buy, but to learn what businesses exist in
  your area and what price ranges look like. (2021)
- Negotiation rule from an early mentor: **agree on price first, then agree on terms.**
  (2021)
- Structure example: on a $625k business, get ~$437k seller-financed over several years,
  cover the remaining ~$200k with an SBA/bank loan, and put only ~$50k down — upgrading
  your income from ~$60k to ~$250k, most of it yours within ~24 months. (2021)
- Buying an existing business avoids the hidden startup costs: existing book of business,
  client lists, equipment, zoning permits, fees, licenses. (2021)
- "To the undisciplined, everything looks like a spending opportunity." Spending more
  doesn't buy more results — his $250k second gym made no more than his $40k first gym.
  (2021)
- There are motivated sellers everywhere — owners burned out on their business who would
  nearly give it away; even owners making $1–4M/yr have told him they'd take $200k to be
  done. These deals aren't listed; you have to look, and most people won't because it's
  work. (2021)
- The wealthy are more risk-averse because any number multiplied by zero is zero — one
  bad investment can reverse 30 years of good decisions. You should only have to get rich
  once. (2021)
- Poor people buy lottery tickets and things with near-guaranteed downside; rich people
  buy things that can't go to zero and buy them for nothing, preferring a guaranteed
  small return over a risky huge one. (2021)
- You must have the character traits of success (patience, work ethic) *before* you'll
  see success — a patient buyer can wait six months for the right deal. (2021)

## Biographical note

Self-references to his own broke era and gym operating history:

- **$1,100 rock bottom, and its timing.** He states his lowest point — "eleven hundred
  dollars in my bank account" — came **after he had six gyms**, i.e. *after* the
  multi-location gym-owning phase described here, not before it. This dates/orders the
  well-known $1,100 low point relative to the gym-chain collapse rather than to the
  earlier post-DUI period. (Worth flagging against the known timeline: confirm whether
  the "$1,100 after six gyms" low is the same event as, or distinct from, other
  rock-bottom framings in the corpus.)
- **$110k in 30 days after "losing everything."** He claims that the month after he
  "lost everything" he collected $110,000 cash in the following 30 days. Attribution of
  which collapse this refers to (gyms vs. earlier) is not specified in this video.
- **Gym build-out economics (new specific detail).** First gym location opened for
  ~$40,000 (an old gym with turf/flooring/paint already in place, so he "lucked out");
  the second location he spent ~$250,000 on and it "made no more money than the first."
  His next two locations were relatively cheap "corporate locations."
- **The fifth-location acquisition deal.** He bought a fifth gym from an owner facing a
  divorce/family crisis who wanted out. Agreed price ~$40–50k, seller-financed over 12
  months (he pushed for 24), **no money out of pocket**; the gym did $51,000 in sales in
  the first 30 days and "literally paid for itself." He later **met Russell** [Brunson],
  who told him he should teach others how he was doing this, and ~12 months later he
  **sold that gym for 1.5x what he bought it for** — "acquired a cash-flowing asset for
  nothing."
