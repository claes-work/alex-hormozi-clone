# How to Monetize as a Creator in 2026

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host poses questions; Alex answers — only Alex's words are used as voice data). Prompted by his 2024 Skool deal, Alex lays out his signature **creator-monetization framework: five paths that exist on an axis of control**, from least to most control (and least to most enterprise value): (1) **sponsorships** — people pay you, you're effectively an advertiser reading ads; (2) **affiliate / performance deals** — same as sponsorships but paid on performance, which shifts risk from the brand to you in exchange for a larger share of the upside ("winners always work on performance," because as the seller of attention no one can estimate its value better than you); (3) **promote + invest** — the middle path he took with Skool, putting work, brand, and capital into an existing company that has already proven product-market fit (the "sweet spot"); (4) **white-label / drop-ship** — slap your logo on an existing manufacturer's product; and (5) the "final boss," **build a product or service from scratch and market/sell it**. Enterprise value rises the further right you go, but there's "a right path for everyone." He frames the invest-path magic as **pulling the compounding curve forward** — taking a product that could 10x in a year in a market resembling your audience and "pulling year four forward," so both parties compound years sooner. He argues the pure-media path (sponsorship/affiliate revenue) can build real enterprise value only if you treat yourself as a **media business** (Barstool/Dave Portnoy, YMH, MKBHD as media), but the upside is capped and many media empires have failed (BuzzFeed). Digressions: a long riff that **you can't really get cancelled** unless you're deplatformed from distribution (Liver King, Chappelle, R. Kelly, Jake/Logan Paul — the news cycle lasts about a week, and most of the world doesn't know you exist); Prime = "identical to a Skool deal" because Logan Paul didn't build the product (Congo Brands did); a bet on MrBeast is a bet on Jimmy himself; and the wealthiest people rarely own 100% of what made them rich (Jensen Huang ~4% of Nvidia, Bezos ~9% of Amazon, Buffett <40% of Berkshire) — building something huge takes "multiple lifetimes," so you divvy up the pie to build bigger, faster. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames creator monetization as **five paths on an axis of control**, from least to most control: sponsorships → affiliate/performance deals → promote-and-invest → white-label/drop-ship → build-from-scratch; there is a right path for everyone.
- As of March 2026, sponsorships are the lowest-control path — the creator is effectively an advertiser being paid to read an ad or promote a brand; if you know your attention doesn't actually convert, doing straight sponsorships (paying for brand impression) may be the right call.
- As of March 2026, affiliate/performance deals are "one degree next to" sponsorships: you take a larger share of the upside by shifting risk from the brand to yourself — "winners always work on performance," because as the seller of attention you have an information advantage and should know its value better than anyone.
- As of March 2026, the middle "promote + invest" path (what he did with Skool) puts three buckets into a business — work, brand, and capital — and works best when the company has *already proven* it is good (revenue retention, churn, user acquisition, engagement, activation all check out).
- As of March 2026, the "nirvana" invest scenario: an unbelievable product already compounding on word of mouth for 12–24 months, in a market resembling your audience, that you know can 10x in a year — the deal essentially "pulls the compounding curve forward" several years for both parties.
- As of March 2026, Skool was "the only deal I've done since that time period" because it hit all of those criteria.
- As of March 2026, white-labeling/drop-shipping (find a manufacturer, put your logo on it, ship it) is the simplest higher-control path; building a product or service from scratch and marketing/selling it is the "final boss."
- As of March 2026, enterprise value increases the further right (more control) you go on the spectrum; sponsorship/affiliate revenue can still create enterprise value *if* you treat yourself as a media business ("taking an old business model and doing it in a new medium").
- As of March 2026, the pure-media path has limited upside and a high failure rate (BuzzFeed cited); it can be easier to grow a media network by selling ad space across a demo (Barstool/Portnoy's model) than to be the primary breadwinning creator carrying it.
- As of March 2026, Prime is "identical to a Skool deal" — Logan Paul promoted it but didn't build the product (Congo Brands did); for a huge audience the constraint becomes product distribution, not media distribution, so partnering with existing infrastructure beats spending five years learning the business yourself.
- As of March 2026, MrBeast/Feastables is a rare true build-from-scratch case; a bet on that business is really "a bet on Jimmy" (Chamath's framing) — with the world as your distribution base, "it's hard to lose."
- As of March 2026, on cancellation: you cannot truly be cancelled unless you're deplatformed from all distribution (R. Kelly in jail = effectively cancelled; Chappelle/Liver King/Jake & Logan Paul = "cancelled" but still going); the news cycle is ~a week, and most of the world doesn't know you exist — "no mistake ends your career unless you choose to end it."
- As of March 2026, there are two kinds of backlash — anger at betraying your stated brand/values (legitimate; don't do that) versus people who think everything should be free (ignore them); anything worth doing gets backlash, and every famous person has haters, so you "might as well accomplish your objectives."
- As of March 2026, the wealthiest people rarely own 100% of what made their wealth (Jensen Huang ~4% Nvidia, Bezos ~9% Amazon, Buffett <40% Berkshire); building something huge takes "multiple lifetimes," so combining several people's lifetimes lets you build bigger and faster — and the longer he's in business the more he values time and speed.
