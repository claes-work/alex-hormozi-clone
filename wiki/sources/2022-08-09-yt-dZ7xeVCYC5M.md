# How I Would Invest $1000 If I Were In My 20s

## Summary

Prompted by a young man at Brooks's house asking where to put an extra $1,000,
Hormozi argues that the highest-return investment when you're young is not the S&P
or real estate but your own earning capacity — courses, coaching, mentorships,
workshops. He says the moment you shift money into passive assets you're implicitly
deciding to stop growing your income, and instead you should live cheap and pour
100% of your excess into education that raises what the market will pay you.

## Key claims

- Your earning capacity — your ability to solve valuable problems for the market — is
  what actually generates wealth; the gap between the problem's value and what you
  charge is your take.
- Passive-asset investing (S&P 500, real estate, done young, long-horizon) is safe and
  will make money, but starting it early signals you've "ceased to want to make more
  income"; when young, invest excess into yourself instead.
- Illustration: a ~$500, 2-day certification can triple hourly earnings (minimum wage
  to ~$25/hr); reinvesting the surplus ($2K/month, ~$25K/year) into courses/coaching
  compounds far faster than ~10%/year.
- Real estate wealth is *active*, not passive — people who build wealth in it spend
  their time finding deals, walking buildings, working contractors; don't believe the
  "passive" framing.
- De-risk purchases by buying from whoever gives the most value for free (their paid
  product is unlikely to be worse than their free content); give yourself ~7 days to
  pick, then consider buying from everyone in a space. Replicate before you iterate —
  copy exactly first, earn the right to add "your sauce."
- Skills stack exponentially (video → editing → social/copy/branding → management →
  leadership), broadening someone from specialist toward CMO toward entrepreneur.
