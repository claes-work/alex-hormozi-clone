# How to Handle Price Objections

★ L3-candidate — introduces a concrete, teachable "price and terms" rule (never drop price without removing a component) plus the BAT objection-diagnosis taxonomy and the "price terrorists" metaphor — canonical sales material worth promoting into topics/persona.

## Summary
This is a short interview/coaching clip on the @MoreMozi channel in which an interviewer questions Alex Hormozi about handling objections in a sales setting. Alex's core argument is that a "no" is not monolithic: you must first diagnose the reason behind it — value, details, authority, or timing (he references the classic budget/authority/timing framework) — because some objections (e.g. a genuine mismatch of need) are not worth overcoming. On price specifically, he is a "firm believer in never negotiating with terrorists, including price terrorists": the quoted price is the price you hold, because the moment you negotiate once, every price becomes negotiable. His practical rule is "price and terms" — if he is going to change the price, he must also change the terms of the deal (remove a guarantee, reduce sessions per week) rather than simply selling the same thing for less, which he frames as both a business discipline (price standardization) and an ethical one (you can't sell one buyer the identical thing cheaper than another). The clip ends with a promotion for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-09. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-09) Whether a "no" can be turned around depends entirely on the reason the person said no; the follow-up should be a discovery question probing the variables behind the decision.
- (2026-02-09) There is a meaningful difference between a no based on value, a no based on details, a no based on authority, and a no based on timing — he ties these to the budget/authority/timing (BAT) components.
- (2026-02-09) Some objections should not be overcome because the need genuinely isn't there — e.g. selling a coat to someone in Panama, or a physical gym membership to someone who lives too far away to ever attend.
- (2026-02-09) He is a firm believer in "never negotiating with terrorists, including price terrorists."
- (2026-02-09) His teams train on responding to "can you do it cheaper?" by saying they can do it for more — and people often accept and buy at the quoted price anyway.
- (2026-02-09) The price you quote is the price you stick to; as soon as you negotiate once, every price you have becomes negotiable, which is bad for the business.
- (2026-02-09) Price standardization matters because you don't want different customers being sold the same thing at different prices — people talk.
- (2026-02-09) Before conceding anything on price, loop through discovery: why can't you afford it, how important is this to you, what would it cost you not to do this — quantify as much as possible.
- (2026-02-09) If a buyer truly only has $3,000 for a $5,000 offer, he won't sell the identical thing for less because he can't ethically sell one person the same thing cheaper than another paid.
- (2026-02-09) His rule is "price and terms": to change the price he changes the terms — e.g. remove the guarantee to knock $1,000 off, or drop from three gym sessions a week to two (with one workout done at home).
- (2026-02-09) He will not simply say "sure, I'll do it for less."
