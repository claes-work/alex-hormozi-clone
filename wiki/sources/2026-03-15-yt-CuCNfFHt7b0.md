# I Build and Sell Homes. But I'm Running Out of Land to Buy

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A home builder that sells new-construction homes to first-time buyers does ~$200M in revenue and wants to reach $300M, but is blocked by **land acquisition** — the constraint of their industry. On diagnosis it turns out the bottleneck is not finding raw land (the leads/names are readily available via MLS) but converting raw land into a **finished, build-ready lot**: development can take 2–10 years, and the builder doesn't want its cash "rotting" in a long timeline with no return, so it buys finished lots from third-party developers — of whom there aren't enough. Alex's answer is to **vertically integrate the developer step** rather than keep waiting on suppliers. Given the client's size and capital, he frames it as a classic **buy-vs-build** decision and leans toward *buy*: acquire an existing developer or, better, strip out the key talent. His concrete first move: pull the names of every developer they bought finished land from over the last 10 years, fly out to the older ones who may no longer want to run their business, and cut a deal. He generalizes with an OpenAI/AI-talent analogy — it can be more efficient to pay four key people $100M each than to buy the whole company for $4B, a "pure allocation of capital play" — as long as the relationships/inroads (zoning, local ties) can travel with the talent; if the inroads are too entrenched, buy the whole company instead. He notes older sellers often accept long earnouts and 15-year seller financing collateralized against the business (an annuity for them, a win-win structure). On the client's hesitation ("it's a distraction from home building, a completely different business"), Alex counters that **every business at large enough scale becomes a business of businesses** (Amazon = AWS + logistics + media + white-label, etc.), so integrating development may now fit. He closes on **constraint theory**: the constraint is the allocation of resources that yields the highest return, and de-constraining often produces order-of-magnitude gains, not 10–30% ones — "you can go to a billion because you opened up five times the land." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the builder's growth block as a single constraint — land acquisition — and treats de-constraining it as the highest-return move available to the business.
- As of March 2026, he distinguishes the real bottleneck: not sourcing raw land (names are readily available in MLS) but the 2–10 year **development of raw land into finished, build-ready lots**, which the builder avoids doing itself so cash isn't tied up with no return.
- As of March 2026, his prescription is to **vertically integrate** the developer step the builder currently outsources — "become the developer yourself" — because at their size and capital they can simply make the acquisition.
- As of March 2026, he frames it as **buy-vs-build** and favors buying: the fastest first step is to list every developer they bought finished lots from over the past 10 years and approach the older owners who may want out.
- As of March 2026, he argues stripping the key **talent** out of a target (if they can carry the relationships) and rebuilding is often more capital-efficient than buying the whole company — analogized to AI firms paying four key people $100M each rather than acquiring the company for $4B ("a pure allocation of capital play").
- As of March 2026, the buy-talent-vs-buy-company test is how **entrenched the inroads are**: if zoning ties and relationships are hard to transport, buy the whole company outright.
- As of March 2026, older sellers often accept long earnouts and ~15-year seller financing collateralized against the business — an annuity for them and a win-win deal structure, since both sides already hold assets.
- As of March 2026, he answers the "it's a distraction / different business" objection with the principle that **every business at large enough scale becomes a business of businesses** (Amazon example: AWS, logistics/distribution, media, white-label).
- As of March 2026, he defines a constraint quantitatively as "the allocation of resources that yields you the highest return," and notes that fixing a true constraint tends to deliver order-of-magnitude gains rather than 10–30% improvements.
