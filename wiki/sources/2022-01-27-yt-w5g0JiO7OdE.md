# I paid 0% tax on 56% of my income…here's how (2022-01-27)

> ⚠️ **Not tax advice; self-reported, US-specific, illustrative.** All figures and tax
> claims below are Alex Hormozi's own stated understanding as of Jan 2022, rounded for a
> slide presentation and not verified against tax code or registries. He explicitly frames
> the video as personal beliefs and rules of thumb, and jokingly "thanks the IRS" for
> sponsoring it because his core advice is *pay your taxes*.

## Summary

A ~19-minute slide presentation in which Hormozi argues **against** chasing tax shelters
and **for** simply paying taxes while building wealth through the tax-advantaged
appreciation of business equity. He states he paid ~$22.68M (also cited as "over $30M") in
taxes over the prior 5 years and spent ~$1.6M on legal/accounting to arrive at his current
structure, concluding that most shelters cost more than they save. The centerpiece is a
worked example showing that because most net-worth growth comes from the untaxed
appreciation of what you own, his effective tax rate on net-worth increase was ~21% and
~56% of that increase was tax-free — hence the title. He closes with six due-diligence
rules of thumb and seven personal beliefs (attention as the #1 asset, the "85-year-old
self" test, the "casino of life"/"taxed 100% at death" frame).

## Tax / wealth mechanisms

**Core thesis: equity appreciation is the real tax-free wealth engine, not shelters.**
- Wealth comes from the **appreciation of equities, not income**. He cites that "94% of the
  [Forbes] 100 growth came from appreciation, not income." (figure self-reported; likely a
  caption garble of "Forbes 100")
- Worked example (all illustrative, "move the zeros"):
  - Business earns $10M EBITDA, valued at 5x = **$50M**. Dividends taxed ~37% → ~$6.3M
    after-tax.
  - Next year EBITDA grows 20% to $12M; still 5x → value rises to **$60M**. Dividends
    12M × 63% = ~$7.6M after-tax.
  - Net-worth increase = $10M (equity value gain) + $7.6M (after-tax dividend) = **$17.6M**.
  - Pre-tax that increase was ~$22M, after-tax ~$17.6M → **effective tax ~21%**, because
    **~56% of the net-worth increase was tax-free** (the equity appreciation).
  - Leverage of the multiple: at a 40x public multiple, +$2M EBITDA = +$80M net worth.
- Takeaway he repeats: "What you build builds your net worth more than what you make." So
  live on a modest salary and **let the value of the business compound untaxed**, even in a
  high-tax state.

**On tax shelters — three unaccounted costs (why they "cost more than they save"):**
1. **Cost of discovery & conversion** — sifting swampland ("95% of solutions are swampland,
   5% legit"; "19 out of 20 are not legit"), then vetting, creating entities, transferring
   assets, re-contracting clients, new banking.
2. **Cost of upkeep** — ongoing attention to stay compliant (his captive-insurance example:
   quarterly allocations, claim decisions).
3. **Cost of unwinding & fines** — the IRS is "3 to 5 years behind"; when it closes a
   loophole it can claw back taxes + fines + interest for prior years, plus the legal cost
   to unwind. He says people close to him have been audited/fined.

**The tax code as an incentive system.** He frames the code as a system of penalties and
incentives designed to move the economy (e.g. real-estate incentives exist to make people
develop/maintain property). "What matters most is what your intention is when you use these
structures" — using a clause in a way it wasn't intended is what triggers unwinding.

**Where real, legitimate tax savings actually come from (his three scenarios):**
1. **Giving up ownership or control** — e.g. charitable giving and (properly done) trusts:
   you no longer own the money, so you don't owe tax on it.
2. **Depreciation** — the code lets you depreciate purchased assets (function like losses).
   Example: buy a $10M building, depreciate ~$4M in a year to offset ~$4M of income → zero
   tax. He claims real-estate fund GPs take a disproportionate share of depreciation
   ("which is how Grant Cardone doesn't pay taxes"). *(self-reported attribution)*
3. **Deferral over elimination** — many structures defer tax (often until death) rather than
   eliminate it.

**Specific structures he examined and declined:**
- **Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT)** — a "waterfall of trusts," ~$5M per trust
  (below the limit), 3-year wait to touch the money (past the IRS unwinding statute). He says
  it could have saved him ~$6M in taxes but he passed — not worth the worry, restrictions,
  and audit risk.
- **Puerto Rico / residency-day counting** — friends flying 104 days/yr, counting NYC days,
  one missing a child's birth "to get their days in." He argues they trade valuable attention
  for less-valuable money (money has decreasing marginal utility; time does not).
- **Captive insurance** — marginal savings, heavy upkeep; the relative who pitched him got
  audited.

**Six due-diligence rules of thumb (if you pursue a structure anyway):**
1. There is "hair" in almost every structure — find it; factor in discovery, conversion,
   upkeep, and fee costs before counting the savings.
2. "The wealthy have been doing this forever" is just a sales pitch — ignore it.
3. Don't pretend to understand; demand it be explained simply ("explain it to a golden
   retriever… who speaks Spanish. Pictures, simple words"). If they can't, they either don't
   understand it or want to confuse you — walk away.
4. Ask to speak with people who've run the strategy **10+ years** (through a full IRS cycle),
   and talk to several to rule out luck.
5. Background-check the person and their entities. (His example: a pitched entity was brand
   new, no reviews, and its owner had been indicted in a major tax-evasion case → reputational
   risk.)
6. Don't spend money you otherwise wouldn't just to expense it ("cutting off your nose to
   spite your face") — unless the spend is a genuine **asset** that builds company value.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2022-01-27)

- He paid ~$22.68M in taxes over the past 5 years (also stated as "over $30M") and ~$1.6M in
  legal/accounting to build his current structure. *(self-reported)*
- He recently sold a **66% stake in two companies at a ~$46.2M valuation** (invites viewers
  to verify). *(self-reported)*
- Stated current net worth ~$100M; he is 32, his wife Leila 29; expects to become a
  billionaire even at ~10%/yr with no new income. *(self-reported, dated)*
- His four anti-shelter theses: tax shelters (1) cost more than they save, (2) don't increase
  net worth, (3) don't improve your life ("inverted priorities" — sacrificing the goal for
  the means), (4) don't make you a better person.
- Fortunes are **made** by taking large risk with a little money, and **maintained** by taking
  small risk with a lot of money (attributed to Jack Butcher, loosely paraphrased).
- People obsessed with tax savings "think in increments, not orders of magnitude" — chasing
  30% savings instead of 30x growth. Saving 30% on taxes only raises net worth by ~30%; the
  few big wins drive net worth far more.
- His "freedom number" kept moving: $4M → $10M → $14M → $20M → $30M → $100M — hard-driving
  people move their own goalposts, making satisfaction hard.
- Two decision filters he actually uses: **reputational risk** ("you only get one name") — he'd
  rather be known as someone who "paid his dues and still killed it" than as someone who
  escaped taxes — and the **85-year-old self test**.
- At death "we will all be taxed at 100%" — you keep nothing — so don't sacrifice the quality
  of life/people around you to keep a bigger share of chips you must return.
