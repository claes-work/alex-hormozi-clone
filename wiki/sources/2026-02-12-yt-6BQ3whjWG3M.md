# How the Top 1% Actually Think About Money

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video on rethinking money goals to account for inflation and the time value of money. His core reframe: financial gurus who tell you to save $100/month ignore that a $1M nest egg at 67 will only buy ~$170K of today's goods, so you must set goals in *future* dollars (e.g. wanting "$4M in today's dollars" means targeting ~$24M). His four-part strategy — increase income, spend less, invest sooner/faster, and above all invest in income-earning skills — is illustrated with his own early frugality (six roommates, used car paid outright) and the "buying money" habit of trading cash for skills that immediately raised his income.

## Key claims

- Financial gurus telling people to save $100/month to reach ~$1M by 67 (at 9% compounding) ignore inflation: that $1M will only be worth ~$170K in today's purchasing power.
- $1 in 1975 has today's purchasing-power equivalent of ~$62 — a ~6x difference over 50 years, driven mainly by inflation; this is conservative because it's based on the past 50 years, not the next.
- To have "$4M in today's dollars" at retirement you'd actually need ~$24M in future dollars.
- Alex frames the problem as income, not savings: there's finite room between zero and you, infinite room above.
- Investing an extra $1,000/month can compound to ~$10M by retirement; $1,000 today at age 18 becomes ~$80,000 in 50 years (80x nominal), which is ~$13,000 in today's dollars — so every $1 saved today is worth ~$13 at retirement.
- Spending has the same reverse-compounding cost: a $500 belt ≈ $6,500 in today's dollars; a $500/month three-year car lease ($18K) ≈ $234,000.
- Starting at 18 vs 28 is the difference between roughly a 33x and an 80x on the last decade of compounding — youth's advantage is time.
- Three saving mechanics: (1) set a bank-account watermark and invest everything above it, or (2) invest a fixed amount every month first and live on the rest, or (3) invest a fixed amount and increase it over time; the richest people Alex knows think the "invest first / make more to hit the target" way, not the "take out what's left" way.
- Taking action (actually changing behavior) is the "big green flag indicator" of who succeeds — ~99% of it.
- Alex's own path: minimized living expenses (protein shakes, Chipotle, split a bedroom dorm-style in a six-roommate house), owned a used car paid outright (only car insurance, no payments), and invested surplus into learning to make more money.
- Skills give the highest ROI of any investment; example: $2,000 spent learning a skill that lifts income from $30K to $90K (~$75K post-tax) yields a permanent ~$35K/year of investable income; compounding ~$3,000/month reaches ~$31M in 50 years with zero raises.
- Alex paid $750/hour for 8 hours of one-on-one tutoring from an ads expert; that skill made him "hundreds of millions."
- He prefers tutoring over courses; $100K spent on coaching/tutoring (with due diligence — reputation, testimonials from people like you, no over-promising) beats $100K of higher education.
- Skill acquisition is like building a bridge: each purchase/course adds a "brick"; you can't cross until the missing links connect, so no single teacher "made you rich" — earlier steps were prerequisites (Spanish 6 requires Spanish 1–5).
- Inexperienced alternative-education teachers only know "Spanish 6," accept money regardless, and blame the ~4 of 5 who don't get results instead of recognizing missing prerequisite skills.
- Money ladder of where to find skills: free (Skool communities, forums, YouTube deep dives like his ~8hr sales trainings) → ~$10–200/month low-ticket communities → ~$500–3,000 DIY/DIY-plus-feedback → ~$5K–35K in-depth with in-person + feedback (where Alex spent almost all his money, buying "up the ladder" out of impatience).
- "Effort plus feedback is the loop" — an artificial environment where you can run 100 feedback loops a day beats one a week; Alex got good at sales doing 20+ in-person consults/day for years.
- The e-commerce entrepreneur ($35M/year) at an 8-figure event described forcing himself to spend a fixed learning budget (1–5–10% of income) on experiments expected to fail, to stay ahead.
- After hearing that, Alex (then ~$300–400K/month at Gym Launch, ~just married, working over a kitchen table with Leila and one assistant) took ~10% (~$30K) and raised ad spend from ~$400 to ~$1,400/day; Gym Launch then scaled month over month from $300K → $480K → $780K → $1M → $1.2M → $1.5M → ~$1.7M → $2M → $2.2M.
- Willingness to lose money unlocked willingness to spend more, which scaled the company — a self-reinforcing event that made him hunt for more places to "spend money like this."
- "The game of entrepreneurship is risk and time."
- Give first in communities: Alex paid ~$30K to enter rooms, then did hours of free work (reviewing others' sales scripts/calls) to be treated as a student and learn from everyone; "most people are too cheap with their time even though they're poor."
- People avoid spending on skills out of (a) fear of paying and getting nothing back, and (b) ego ("I can figure it out on my own") — you can, but not as fast; paying buys speed.
- Promo/gifts: all three of his hardback books free (entrepreneurs donated 3.6M books at his launch; he'll match/double), books also free on his podcast; a free scaling roadmap tool built from 10 stages of scaling across 8 functions (product, marketing, sales, customer service, IT, recruiting, HR, finance).
