# The shocking truth of Alex Hormozi's FIRST business!

## Summary

In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi recounts the origin story of his first business: a
gym in Huntington Beach, Southern California. He describes quitting a defense-contracting
consulting job (a subcontract under Booz Allen), driving across the country without
telling anyone, and apprenticing under a successful gym owner ("Seven Fig Sam") before
opening his own gym at age 22 with ~$50,000 saved. The core of the story is his
cash-conversion model: he pre-sold six-week weight-loss challenges via Facebook ads
(new in 2013), used the incoming cash to build out each gym, and opened new locations at
full capacity with no capital of his own. He explains the mechanics of the challenge
offer, the front-loaded cash economics that let him outspend competitors, and how the
model evolved iteratively from another gym owner's testimonial promotion into a
templated, one-to-many, Facebook-ad-driven system.

## Key claims

- His first business was a gym; he chose it because he was into fitness and "hated
  everything else." (self-reported)
- He ran Facebook ads to acquire members starting in 2013, learning them at a weekend
  workshop two weeks before opening. (self-reported)
- The gym grew revenue by ~$5,000/month every month for the first seven or eight months.
  (self-reported)
- He opened a new location roughly every six months, reaching five locations. (self-reported)
- His edge was opening gyms at full capacity on day one by signing the lease, running ads
  immediately, and building out with incoming cash over the 30-day pre-open window.
  (self-reported)
- The offer was a six-week weight-loss challenge, ~$500–600, framed as "free if they lost
  the weight" (10% of body weight), with meal plans, grocery lists, and eating-out guides.
  (self-reported)
- Unit economics: leads cost ~$5, he converted ~1 in 4 into a ~$600 sale, so ~$20–25 per
  customer acquired, plus $200–300 in supplements two days later. (self-reported)
- He claims ~30-to-1 return on ad spend and making ~$100k in a gym's first month; spending
  ~$300/day (elsewhere "$100 get $2,000 back"). (self-reported)
- He converted the $600 challenge fee into a membership by crediting it toward the client's
  long-term goal ($600 / 12 = ~$50/month off), landing them at ~$200/month subscriptions.
  (self-reported)
- His front-loaded cash conversion (vs. competitors' 3-months-up-front → 1-in-3 into
  $99/month) let him outspend everyone in his market and self-fund expansion. (self-reported)
- He did 20–25 one-on-one sales consults per day for years, attributing his close rate to
  sheer volume of reps. (self-reported)

## Biographical facts (dated)

For persona/biography. All self-reported in this source unless otherwise verified.

- **Before the gym**: Worked a consulting job in defense contracting / cyber intelligence,
  on a subcontract under Booz Allen. Hated it and quit. (self-reported)
- **Age 22**: Graduated a year early from a school in Tennessee, saved ~$50,000, and drove
  across the country to Southern California without telling anyone (specifically to avoid
  his father talking him out of it). (self-reported)
- **On arrival**: Showed up "with no plan" at a successful gym owner's gym and asked to
  learn; slept at the owner's place. He found the owner by Googling "successful gym owner"
  and finding a "crappy HTML site" branded "Seven Fig Sam." (self-reported)
- **Apprenticeship**: Spent 12 weeks with Sam, who worked a 4:00 a.m.–4:00 p.m. shift;
  Alex worked that shift and stayed after. Sam ran a small peer group of gym owners, which
  let Alex learn others' mistakes before opening his own gym. (self-reported)
- **First ad**: Ran his first Facebook ad for Sam's gym (personal-training side) before he
  knew Facebook ads even worked; it made money and sold the first ~10–12 people.
  (self-reported)
- **First own gym**: Opened in Huntington Beach; had money for only one rent, so he slept on
  the gym floor. Ran Facebook ads to get his first ~25 members. (self-reported, ~2013)
- **Scaling**: Grew ~$5,000/month for 7–8 months, brought in a manager, then opened a new
  location roughly every six months up to five locations. (self-reported)
- **Meeting Leila**: Met Leila ("Lea") around the time he had five locations. (self-reported)
- **Speaking**: Was asked to speak at an event about marketing for brick-and-mortar, despite
  not seeing himself as part of "that world." (self-reported)
