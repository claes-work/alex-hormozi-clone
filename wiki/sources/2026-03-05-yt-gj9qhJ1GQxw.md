# Balance Is a Myth

## Summary
Solo short. Alex argues that anyone who wants to achieve great things eventually realizes **balance is a myth** — and that realization is precisely what turns them into a high achiever. He reframes "balance" as mediocrity: to be balanced is to be in the *middle* (the "medium"), whereas anyone who is **imbalanced — more focused on one thing** — will beat you at that thing. The only real question is whether the thing they choose to master gives them **leverage over everything else later**: do work valuable enough to society and, he promises, you'll be able to buy the time, money, and life (he crudely jokes "a hotter wife") you want. He concedes the balanced life is a valid choice, but offers his key distinction: he sees **balance over a lifetime, not balance in every moment** — life comes in **seasons**. He took roughly a year off during the last year of Gym Launch (more time and money than anyone), but had earlier seasons where he took no time off at all. Beyond that "softer" framing, his "real talk" is that he works all the time simply because he **likes** it: once you build a **fast feedback loop** between work you enjoy and work that advances your goals, you won't want to do anything else. He debunks the idea that admired people have superhuman discipline — they just **like things that also bring them status**, and they pushed through the early, unrewarding bridge period (when they sucked and friends said so) until the thing started working. So you only need enough **gusto to start** and to survive the phase where you're bad. Everything new isn't fun because you suck at it, but **getting better is fun**; Alex says he gets *excited* seeing how bad he is at something new, because he has generalized reinforcement for improvement — he's learned **the meta-skill of getting better ("you learn how to learn")** and can apply it anywhere. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
