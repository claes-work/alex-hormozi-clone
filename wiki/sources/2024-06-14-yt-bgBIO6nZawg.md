# 7 Millionaires Asked Me How to Get Rich(er)

## Summary

Recorded after a 60-minute scaling talk, Alex Hormozi fields questions from seven
business owners each doing roughly $1M–$12M/year. He works case-by-case through a
neuropathy done-for-you scheduling business, a permanent holiday-lighting installer, a
Missouri real-estate flipping firm, a virtual CPA firm, an inbound-sales SaaS, and an
Indonesian DTC menswear brand. Recurring themes: the "ultimate version" of your
business as a compass, rolling up the niche customers you already serve, reoccurring vs.
recurring revenue, retention/onboarding as the real lever, and knowing your best
customers better than they know themselves.

## Key claims (Alex's advice)

- **Look at the "ultimate version" of your business.** Find the biggest company already
  in your space, study what they do, their model, whether they're vertically
  integrated, and treat the skills you lack to match them as your opportunity list.
- **Niche B2B agency/info businesses should consider rolling up the customers they
  serve.** If you already standardize your clients' marketing, pricing, packages and
  operations, you've done a rollup's integration work — the final step is to actually
  consolidate and sell the group. A grouped exit multiplies each practice's sale value
  (his example: a solo neuropathy clinic sells for ~$3M, but as part of a national group
  ~$10M), and the operator takes a 20–30% slice across many practices.
- **Do-it-yourself training only works when the skill is worth more than the customer's
  current earnings.** A $35k/year gym owner should learn to market and sell; a
  million-dollar earner is better off hiring it done because they can pay someone less
  than they make. That's why Gym Launch didn't sell done-for-you to high earners.
- **Rollups need a resale market — group-based service businesses have none**, which is
  why he never rolled up gyms; nobody was buying them.
- **Turn one-off sales into reoccurring revenue.** Recurring (subscriptions) isn't the
  only reliable revenue; like Coca-Cola, you can have "reoccurring" purchases. Run 2–3
  seasonal campaigns a year, create upsell/maintenance touchpoints, and simply have the
  data showing that half of customers buy again each year — that alone makes a business
  far more valuable.
- **Competition is a good sign** — it means there are buyers/demand. Being number one in
  a crowded market beats being alone in an empty one.
- **Expand by opening in new markets rather than changing your whole business.** Once
  you're driving past ~4 hours you're in flying range, so pick any city you want. Don't
  jump back down the learning curve by pivoting into an entirely new business.
- **On the real-estate flipper reliant on foreclosures:** start advertising (PPC,
  mailers, billboards) — the national leader already does this and it might double the
  business within the existing market. Then analyze the market leader exhaustively as
  the future version of your business.
- **Accounting/CPA firms are sticky, high-LTV, and roll up constantly** — the usual gap
  is that nobody there can market and sell, plus the difficulty of hiring good
  accountants.
- **Retention/"stick" complaints ("they already got the value") are usually bad
  onboarding, not real value delivery.** What people say on exit isn't the real reason
  they leave. Fix the Delta between what's sold and the early experience; communicate
  far more frequently and personally, with pushed updates (not asks), so clients feel
  the team is working hard for them.
- **On SaaS: know what business you're really in.** A software business is a *product*
  business, not a marketing/sales business — if people don't stick, nothing else
  matters. Front-end marketing/sales exists only to improve the product until it reaches
  passive (self-sustaining) growth, then you pour fuel on it.
- **To lower churn, either go up-market to people who don't churn, or help create the
  people who don't churn.** Find the flat part of your retention curve, profile that
  cohort (psychographics, demographics, geographics, activities), then retarget
  marketing to acquire more people like them — and get new users to do what successful
  users did.
- **The Vista Equity "secret":** they know their acquired companies' customers better
  than the companies do — turn off marketing that isn't attracting the best customers,
  crank the marketing that is. People stay poor because they lack the discipline to say
  no to money today (too many avatars) and so miss the big money tomorrow.
- **On the DTC menswear brand:** at $10M you don't need to add women's/sports/formal
  lines to grow; there are huge menswear-only companies. The real gap is going from
  media-arbitrage operator to *brand* — a brand (e.g., an endorser standing next to the
  product) is what raises conversion, lets you charge a premium, and makes the company
  sellable.
