# Warren Buffett's #1 Rule for Business Owners (2021-05-05)

## Summary

In this 2021 video, Hormozi argues that the single most important metric for a business
owner is **net free cash flow** — the money an owner can pull out of a business after it
reinvests enough to stay ahead of competitors and maintain its competitive advantage. He
attributes the underlying concept ("owner earnings") to Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger,
and recounts his own early mistake of being "asset rich and cash poor" by reinvesting all
gym profits into new locations. He recommends business owners stop banking on a mythical
future exit and instead extract profit every month.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- Net free cash flow is the amount an owner can take out of a business *after* the business
  reinvests to stay ahead of competition and maintain its competitive advantage; Hormozi
  calls it his #1 tracked metric. [[wiki/sources/2021-05-05-yt-EPEjYEihZ1s]]
- **Attributed to Buffett & Munger:** this is the "owner earnings" figure they calculate on
  companies they want to buy; they dislike businesses that require reinvesting all profit
  back in (e.g. manufacturing, capital-expense-heavy companies). *(Buffett/Munger position,
  as relayed by Hormozi.)*
- Two common owner failure modes (Alex's framing): (1) claiming to "reinvest" while actually
  making no profit, and (2) making profit but plowing all of it back into the business.
- Hormozi's own hard-learned mistake (2021 retrospective): in his first ~5 years running
  gyms and an online fitness coaching business, he opened a new location every time he made
  money — ending up asset rich and cash poor.
- He says he sold six gym locations for roughly $200K total (a "garage sale" of assets, not
  businesses), then lost that money in a failed partnership before Gym Launch — about $40K/yr
  over 4-5 years, far less than if he'd stacked cash the whole time.
- ~99% of businesses never sell; betting solely on a future exit is a ~99-to-1 gamble, and
  many exits that do happen are not lucrative.
- Owners are constantly exposed to **operational risk** (business could be outlawed, forced
  to move, blown up), so they should reward themselves for risk taken by pulling cash monthly.
- References *The Little Book That Beats the Market* (Joel Greenblatt): two metrics matter for
  above-average returns — **P/E ratio** (cheap price) and **return on capital** (business
  quality); combining low P/E with high return on capital reportedly beat the S&P 500 for ~25 years.
- Practical tactic: set a non-negotiable cash "line" for the business account and sweep
  everything above it into the owner's personal account every month.
- Reinvest only where return on capital beats alternatives — if a reinvestment only returns
  ~10%, you might as well put the money in the S&P 500.

> The number one metric that I track for our businesses is this one... it is the most
> important and Warren Buffett agrees with me. All right, it's net free cash flow.

> What net free cash flow is, is the amount of money that an owner of a business can take out
> of the business after the business reinvests in staying ahead of the competition and
> maintaining its competitive advantage.

> I was asset rich and cash poor... I had equipment, I had leases, I had flooring, I had
> signage, I had lobbies, I had front desks, I had glass walls — I had all of the things
> except for money in my bank account.

> This is owner earnings. This is what Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger calculate with the
> companies that they want to buy, because they're like, we hate companies where we buy them
> and then we take all the profit and have to reinvest all that money back into the same
> business.

> 99% of businesses don't get sold ever... this idea that we're going to have this mythical
> exit someday, for most businesses isn't true.

> Track it, stack it, and make sure that you empty your bank account every single month and
> stack the right account, which is your personal bank account.
