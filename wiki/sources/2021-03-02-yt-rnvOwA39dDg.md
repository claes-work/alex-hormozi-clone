# How To Retire Early (and be in the top 1%) — 2021-03-02

## Summary

In this early (2021) solo talk, Hormozi lays out a frugality-driven wealth
philosophy: the percentage of your income you *spend* — not how much you make —
dictates how fast you can retire. He walks through a "top 1% pyramid" of US net-worth
tiers to argue that becoming top 1% is mostly a matter of living far below your means
and saving into the S&P 500, not chasing a big income. He frames low spending as an
anxiety-reduction and freedom mechanism rather than deprivation, citing his own life
as a millionaire who drove a ~$6,000 Prius.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-03-02)

- Hormozi says he has always lived on **less than 5% of what he makes**, once he
  started making money; until he was earning $1M/year or more he lived on ~$30,000/year
  (≈$1,500/month rent, ~$3,000/month total). (paraphrase, 2021)
- He states that when Leila and he made their first $100,000, he told her it meant
  "three years of living" because they could live comfortably on ~$1,500/month.
  (paraphrase, 2021)
- He says he owns a Bentley and "totally regret[s]" it; he justifies it as costing
  less than one week's income and calls it probably the last expensive car he'll ever
  own. He argues the incremental life gain from a luxury car over a normal car is not
  worth the money. (paraphrase, 2021)
- His stated core mechanism: **more expenses = more anxiety**; lowering spending lowers
  his anxiety, so frugality is one of his "secret sauces of success." (paraphrase, 2021)
- "Top 1% pyramid" (US figures he read from notes, 2021):
  - High net worth = **$1–5M in assets**, ~1.5% of people, ~1 in 69.
  - Very high net worth = **$5–30M**, 0.3%, ~1 in 338.
  - Ultra high net worth = **$30M–$1B**, 0.07%, ~1 in 1,383.
  - Billionaires = ~1 in 468,000.
- He claims the top 1% *income* is ~$400,000/year (quotes $420k–$440k depending on
  source), while top 1% *assets* is only ~$1–1.5M — evidence, he says, that people
  spend all their money. (paraphrase, 2021)
- He claims that saving **$15,000/year for 30 years at 9% returns** (S&P 500, no
  crypto, no active picking) gets you above $5M — i.e. into the top ~0.3%.
  (paraphrase, 2021)
- Simplified pitch: saving **~$1,000–1,500/month into the S&P 500** gets you into the
  top 1%. (paraphrase, 2021)
- He says he had been reading heavily about **Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger**
  (Berkshire Hathaway) and their long-term thinking. (paraphrase, 2021)
- "World's simplest retirement calculator": the savings *rate* determines retirement
  speed. Example — earning $100k and saving 80% adds ~4 years of retirement per year
  worked; earning $400k but saving only 10% adds ~0.1 year per year, so that person
  "is never really going to be able to retire." (paraphrase, 2021)
- He frames the choice as ego-based: ego in how you look to others vs. ego in the
  freedom you feel. (paraphrase, 2021)

## Topic notes — early wealth philosophy (candidate for evolution timeline)

This 2021 talk is a useful **early anchor** for Hormozi's wealth philosophy on the
[[../topics/wealth/investing-and-wealth]] evolution timeline. Positions to date to
**2021-03-02**:

- **Extreme frugality / <5% spending rate.** In 2021 his headline wealth advice is
  personal-finance minimalism: live on $30–40k/year, save the rest into the S&P 500,
  and retire on a high savings rate. This is a *passive-index, personal-savings* frame.
- **Anxiety-reduction framing.** Frugality is justified emotionally (fear of losing
  everything, "dry powder," reserves) rather than as fuel for reinvestment.
- **Bentley regret.** He openly regrets the Bentley here — a datapoint on his 2021
  self-image vs. later comfort with visible wealth.
- **Passive index investing as the vehicle** (S&P 500, Buffett/Munger long-termism), not
  operating businesses / equity roll-ups.

> Evolution flag: This predates his later Acquisition.com era and the "seasons of
> spending" / reinvest-everything-into-the-business framing. The 2021 message ("spend
> less than you make, index the rest, retire early") sits in tension with his later
> emphasis on plowing capital into businesses and not optimizing for early retirement.
