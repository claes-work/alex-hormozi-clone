# "What a Privilege It Is to Suffer..."

## Summary

Short mindset clip in which Alex Hormozi reframes suffering as a privilege and an
inseparable part of the human experience. He opens with a story about a friend-of-a-
friend entrepreneur who got aggressive cancer and — *while going through it*, not only in
hindsight — felt grateful, reasoning that most people live and die never knowing what
that part of the human experience is like. Alex extends this to bankruptcy, jail, and
other depths of suffering (careful to say he is not justifying or minimizing anyone's
trauma), landing on a "you can't skip weather" metaphor: being human means both rainy and
sunny days, and embracing the *totality* of the experience produces gratitude even for
what feels locally like a low point. He argues most defining moments only reveal their
value when you expand the time horizon, and shares his recurring practice of talking to
his "85-year-old self," who counters his complaints by asking who he wants to become and
pointing out that that person necessarily goes through hard times — so complaining is
"wishing for the outcome but not willing to pay the price." A second speaker (the
interviewer) only offers brief affirmation and then a promo for the free
acquisition.com/roadmap; all substantive content is Alex's.

## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased, Alex's only)

- ★ Suffering is a privilege: getting to live even the painful parts (cancer, bankruptcy,
  jail) means experiencing dimensions of being human that many people live and die without
  ever knowing.
- The most powerful version of the cancer-survivor's gratitude was felt *while* going
  through the ordeal, not merely afterward — the frame "how cool is it that I get to live
  this part of the human experience?" was applied in real time.
- He is explicit that this is a *frame*, not a justification or minimization of anyone's
  trauma, especially for those still in it.
- ★ "You can't skip weather" — being human is both rainy and sunny days; you cannot have
  only the good ones.
- Embracing the *totality* of the experience creates gratitude for what feels locally like
  a low point.
- ★ Most defining moments in life only reveal their value when you expand the time horizon
  (his example: a broken leg that keeps someone out of war). The hard things are "the
  things that create us."
- He talks to his "85-year-old self" regularly as a decision/mindset tool; when he
  complains, that older self asks "Who do you want to be?"
- ★ Complaining about hard times is "wishing for the outcome but not willing to pay the
  price" — the person you want to become necessarily goes through hard times, and the
  hardship *is* what paying the price feels like.
- Reframe hardship as future narrative material: "embrace it because these will be the
  stories you tell."
- The entrepreneurial journey's "Rocky cut scene" (montage) is five years long, not 30
  seconds — so you need as many mindset tools in your tool belt as possible just to keep
  going.
