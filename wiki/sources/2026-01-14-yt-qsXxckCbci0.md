# How To Grow ANY Business FASTER (Masterclass)

## Summary

Solo masterclass in which Alex Hormozi argues that almost every business is one of
**four "shapes" (types)** — e-commerce, service, education/info, and software/SaaS —
and that each shape has its own characteristic growth curve, structural advantages, and
one "big hairy problem" that defines it. His core thesis: the thing you think is broken
about your business (cash-flow constraints, talent scarcity, churn, long unprofitable
runway) is usually a *feature* of your shape, not a bug — and whoever solves that
signature constraint unlocks disproportionate enterprise value. He draws credibility
from having owned businesses of all four types (Prestige Labs, gyms, gym-consulting,
Gym Launch, Skool, ACQ AI, the advisory practice, book launches) and closes by mapping
founder personality types to shapes and dissecting which shape each acquisition.com
revenue line is.

## Key claims (growth frameworks)

**The four business shapes (each with its curve, signature problem, and how to win):**

1. **E-commerce / physical products** — grows fast but hits stair-step breaking points
   (cash, traffic, distribution, supply-chain unlocks). Capital intensive (profit must
   recycle into inventory; growth rate is capped by free cash flow). Heavy dependence
   on external partners (manufacturing, logistics, raw materials) so redundancy matters.
   Big hairy problems: cash-flow forecasting, distribution, supply chain, and above all
   **brand** (the only thing a competitor can't copy "down to the thread count").
   - Best-in-class e-commerce runs a **70/30 spend split**: 70% top-of-funnel / brand,
     30% direct-to-purchase. Most beginners run ~100% direct-to-purchase and become a
     "drop-ship company with no competitive moat."
   - Brand delivers: higher click-through, higher repurchase rate, higher price premium
     → lower CAC, higher LTV, higher gross profit.
   - "Perfect" e-commerce co: owns manufacturing, decentralized/international logistics,
     lean performance-marketing team paired with brand-first team, brick-and-mortar
     partnerships, savage email marketing, and an exceptional product.
   - Retention signal: 60–70% of a cohort buying again after first purchase = strong
     product. Referral/word-of-mouth is the only compounding vehicle that scales against
     rising CAC.

2. **Service business** (~78% of US businesses) — slow-and-steady, people-heavy growth
   (people at attraction, conversion, and/or delivery). **Talent is the bottleneck**:
   easier to find more product than more exceptional people; higher-skill talent has
   multi-year gestation. Least risky model — you can always cut headcount and stay
   profitable; high cash flow, decent retention (customers stick because they don't want
   to do the work themselves).
   - Signature problem: **recruiting, onboarding, and training talent** — a service
     business is really a *teaching* business. Reduce dependence on the founder's
     technical expertise by productizing knowledge.
   - How to win: pick one avatar (usually decided in the $1–3M range), narrow to 1–3
     productized deliverables, systematize delivery → expand margins → build training
     → replace yourself operationally. Goal: reach the rare state where **demand is the
     constraint**.
   - **North-star metric of a service operator's progression = what they charge.** Good
     work → supply constraint → raise prices → higher margins → attract better ICPs.
   - Low-cost service provider is viable *only* if chosen day one (offshore, automate,
     cheapest USP). Alex prefers premium: "I'd rather make a million dollars per person"
     to justify the headache of headcount.

3. **Education / consulting / info-media** — **scales fastest** (near-vertical line)
   then hard-stops. Fast, easy, cheap to start (hence many competitors, and you *create*
   your competition by teaching it). Structurally low retention — success = graduation,
   not retention. Hard to scale past $1–3M, usually due to lack of promotional skill.
   - Signature problem: **create stickiness in a fundamentally unsticky model.** Tactics:
     split one-time vs. recurring value ("big head, long tail" — price the one-time
     transformation high, recurring low); add **consumption-based** components (creative,
     community, trending lists); collective/discount buying (ROI > subscription cost);
     **continued education** (nurse credits, doctor CME — cheap to *stay* certified vs.
     expensive to *become* certified).
   - Milk analogy: better to give a small amount of the best milk (dilute your access as
     you scale) than a large glass of watered-down milk (untrained coaches at full
     price). The rate you can dilute access is proportional to how good you are.
   - How to win: brand + **real-world proof.** People ignored Alex's content until the
     $46.2M exit; "the only thing you forgot to do was build Berkshire Hathaway" (on why
     a Buffett course from an unknown wouldn't sell). The long path — prove you're the
     best, *then* teach — pays down every risk in the model.

4. **Software / SaaS** — **slowest start, highest ceiling** (most scalable). Long,
   unprofitable, capital-heavy runway before product-market fit (hence the VC industry).
   Fewest businesses here; barriers highest. Vibe coding is lowering barriers but Alex
   has "yet to see a truly vibe-coded $100M/yr business" (cites Replit CEO Amjad).
   - Signature problem: **survive the pre-PMF years and achieve revenue retention.** A
     SaaS looks identical to an info business until it has retention. Marketers struggle
     at software because it requires people to *keep using* it, not just buy.
   - Growth-curve insight (attributed to Facebook head of growth): growth isn't a smooth
     line — users activate and *never churn*; a retained base compounds while you keep
     filling the top. Dropbox founder paid Craigslist users to test; 5/5 couldn't upload
     a file — you keep lubricating user-flow loops.
   - Targets: >60% logo retention by year-end, ideally >100% net revenue retention.
   - How to win: obsess over product quality/customer feedback (but don't feature-add
     into a "Frankenstein" — complexity is the antithesis of elegance); build a **viral
     loop** (bring 1 customer, they bring 1.1+) to compound down CAC (ChatGPT → 1M users
     in a week; Facebook's viral coefficient); pay 10x/100x for genuinely 10x/100x
     engineers; build scalable code + docs from day one.
   - Product-design principle: you don't make something "good" — you find an outcome
     everyone wants and **remove all friction** between the person and that outcome
     (Steve Jobs / iPhone).

**Meta-framework:** Every business's complaint *is* a core feature of its shape. Once
you appropriately value the hairy problem (e.g. "getting 2 people up to speed/month
might add $50M enterprise value"), you can justify dedicating serious resources
($100K–$500K) to solve it thoroughly rather than doubting your business.

**Personality → shape mapping:** engineer, creative, tolerant of long suffering →
software; promotional, dislikes detail → e-commerce or education; exceptionally skilled
+ great teacher + loves people/culture → service. "Culture is the internal brand" of a
service business.

**acquisition.com breakdown (bonus):** ACQ AI = software; books/media/bundle = a blend
of education + software (why the launch did 0→$100M in ~60 hours); advisory practice =
pure white-collar service (analyst → associate → director career paths, apprentice
model like consulting/legal); portfolio holds an insurance co (service), teeth-whitening
chain (Whiten, brick-and-mortar service), and a tech/media-enabled B2B consulting biz.
An investment firm "is a vehicle that contains businesses more than it's a business."
