# I've Never Been More Miserable. Help

## Summary

Alex responds to an owner distressed after acquisitions and exits by radically reducing
the perceived stakes. His method is to play the feared sequence forward, compare the worst
case with earlier survivable conditions, and remember that public attention is far smaller
and shorter-lived than anxiety suggests.

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, Alex says mortality makes most business events feel less significant
  to him because even his death would soon recede from most people's attention.
- He self-reports being about as happy when poor as he is now and imagines that losing
  everything could become a motivating comeback narrative, while still seeking to avoid it.
- His "play it out" exercise repeatedly asks what happens next until a catastrophized fear
  becomes a concrete sequence with tolerable or even beneficial elements.
- He attributes to Leila the image that fear is a mile wide and an inch deep: taking the
  first step often reveals less danger than imagined.
- His paralysis/well-being assertion is based on unnamed research and is not verified in
  this source; it should not be treated as medical or psychological guidance.
- He describes himself and Leila as opposite temperaments in business: he struggles to
  care about stakes, while she cares intensely.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Let's play it out. Like what happens next?"

> "The idea that everyone is watching, it just isn't."

> "For me, I just don't have really big stakes because I just don't think anyone cares."

## Notes

This is personal worldview commentary, not mental-health advice. Synthesis candidate: play-it-out catastrophizing tool and Alex/Leila stakes-temperament contrast.
