# How To Know If You're Actually Working Hard Enough

## Summary
In a conversation with co-hosts (one addressed as Chris), Alex Hormozi argues that people underestimate how much continuous, feedback-driven work greatness requires, and that "reality glasses" — an honest, granular list of everything that separates your current output from your desired output — reveal that hard work is a long, never-ending list rather than a single hard push. He reframes "hustle porn" as an empowering "treasure map" that shows anyone can travel from where a successful person started to where they are now, and warns that claiming success "came easily" is disempowering to others. He also shares rhetorical tactics for dismantling limiting beliefs, and reflects on how few things one can truly be great at in a lifetime.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims genuine expertise requires continuous, unending focus on one thing plus noticing the fine details that separate mediocrity from greatness — "100 hours with feedback," not the popular "10,000 hours," produces a granular understanding of what it takes to be great.
- As of March 2026, he frames someone recounting how hard they worked (a "David Goggins mode" account of setbacks) not as a humble brag or threat but as a "treasure map" showing others they aren't even as far behind as the successful person once was.
- As of March 2026, he argues that saying success "just came easily" or is "ineffable" is the more disempowering message because it makes the outcome feel unreachable; leaning into the "genetics / overnight" myth also (by his admission, his "ugly side") eliminates competitors by making success seem out of reach.
- As of March 2026, he says discontent about how others judge our success comes from an "unspoken demand" that strangers viscerally understand what we went through — an impossible demand, since fully understanding it would mean they already know how to do it themselves.
- As of March 2026, he describes "reality glasses" (vs. "woe is me glasses"): honestly listing everything you'd need to improve to reach your desired outcome; that long list *is* the hard work, and crossing off one item reveals ~10 more as you "start to win in the weeds" and see at higher resolution — "the never-ending cycle of excellence."
- As of March 2026, he claims becoming exceptional at one thing takes roughly five to seven years, so discipline means choosing only the two or three things you can be really good at in a life.
- As of March 2026, he recounts a limiting-belief tactic: agreeing with someone's excuse ("Then you'll fail forever") to provoke them into arguing their own way out; he references his "only one person can be in the angry/negative boat" idea.
- As of March 2026, he offers a self-example: a friend surfaced an interview filmed before Alex made videos; masking the face, the friend said the voice was the same but the clarity of thinking and articulation weren't there — a beginner sees no difference between old and current videos, but an expert can list a hundred granular improvements.
- Caller/co-host contribution (unverified as Alex's view): the co-hosts affirm that "back room" conversations among highly successful people share the observation that outsiders don't grasp how much actual work goes into things like a podcast (e.g., 100 hours of prep for a 1-hour event).
- The video closes with a promotion (not a belief claim) for the free "$100 million scaling roadmap" quiz at acquisition.com/roadmap, described as built from ~200 hours reviewing portfolio companies across 10 growth stages and business functions.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "People see that as a threat. People see that as a humble brag. But what it is is a treasure map."

> "I understand why hustle porn kind of gets a cringe pushback from the world. But what it actually should be is an unbelievably empowering message that anybody can get from wherever they are to wherever you are."

> "One of my favorite ways to help someone overcome an excuse or a limiting belief is to tell them I believe them."

> "They want the magic pill. They want to know the secret, but there is no secret. It's just hard work. But I think the thing that everyone who's listening lacks is the context on how hard hard work is — not in that it's complex, but just in that it's a continuous and unending focus on one thing and noticing the details that separate mediocrity from greatness."

> "Every one of them, and I love this description, is like a coat of paint. It's like you just put another coat of paint on the skill set or the achievement that you're working towards."

> "Once you actually give yourself the reality glasses, not the woe is me glasses... then you see how incredibly long that list is. And then you take this deep breath, and that list is the hard work."

> "For one that you cross off, you create 10 more, because you start to win in the weeds. You start to look at a higher resolution. And that's the never-ending cycle of excellence."

> "As soon as you get great at one thing, you realize just the tremendous amount of hours and work that it takes to be great at one thing... there's so few things that you can be great at. And then the discipline comes down to saying, what are the two or three things that I can be really good at in my life? Because it will take me five to seven years to be exceptional at this one thing."
