# How To Charge Exorbitant Prices (2021-06-16)

## Summary

In a short whiteboard talk, Hormozi argues that price is driven by the interaction of
supply and demand, and that a seller who deliberately **restricts supply** and
**stimulates demand through marketing** can charge far higher prices while often selling
*more* units. He opens with a story of a charity poker event at Arnold Schwarzenegger's
house that raised its ticket from $10k to $25k year-over-year and sold *more* seats, and
uses luxury brands (Chanel, Louis Vuitton) as models of engineered scarcity.

> ⚠️ This is a **proto-version of his scarcity/urgency pricing psychology** — the
> "artificially shift the demand curve" mechanics later formalized in *$100M Offers*
> (2021). Treat as an early, informal articulation dated to mid-2021.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- (2021) When demand increases, cutting supply "jacks prices through the roof" because
  more buyers now chase fewer available items — a lesson he attributes to a high-end
  jeweler at the charity event.
- (2021) Luxury houses (Chanel, Louis Vuitton) have "hacked" demand: stores stock only
  1–2 bags, corporate hides how many/which bags arrive and when production stops, and the
  goods can't be bought online — all by design to manufacture scarcity and lift prices.
- (2021) Most sellers make the mistake of cutting supply but *not* raising price to match
  — leaving "juice in the squeeze"; there is far more pricing power available than people
  take.
- (2021) Textbook microeconomics assumes a static, perfect-information market; in reality
  **marketing shifts the demand curve** — "marketing is artificially stimulating demand"
  — letting you move further up the curve and raise price.
- (2021) Selling fewer, higher-priced units beats selling more cheap ones on multiple
  axes: more total profit, less operational drag/easier fulfillment, and capacity to
  over-deliver (his example: 3 clients at $100k > 5 clients at $20k).
- (2021) "Full capacity" is whatever *you* declare it to be; you can cut supply to zero,
  build pressure like a pot, and periodically "let off steam" — that built-up pressure is
  your pricing power.
- (2021) A capped client list plus a waiting list both stimulates demand and signals
  quality ("they must be good — they have a waiting list").
- (2021) Tactic: pricing should feel like a "seductive dance" — tease, delay, drop hints,
  flirt; the rejection/"snapping point" is far further away than sellers assume.
- (2021) A limited-edition drop (e.g. a t-shirt) can sell *more* units, not because the
  demand curve is wrong, but because scarcity marketing shifted it — buyers who'd
  otherwise delay now feel forced to buy now.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You always have to remember, when demand increases... you cut supply. And what that
> does is it jacks prices through the roof, because now you have more people who wanted it
> than before and there are fewer items for sale."

> "Marketing is artificially stimulating demand. You're purposely letting more people know
> about what you do so that you can generate demand."

> "There's so much extra juice in the squeeze that's available for you if you just know
> this."

> "As soon as you cut your supply to zero, your price shoots that way... and then this
> pressure builds and it's like a pot. And then you want to periodically let off the
> steam... and that pressure is your pricing power."

> "One of the biggest mistakes that I see with most entrepreneurs is that they spend too
> much time trying to supply and not enough time trying to generate demand."

> "You want to think about it like a seductive dance... someone has this desire, but you
> want to delay. You don't want to satisfy."
