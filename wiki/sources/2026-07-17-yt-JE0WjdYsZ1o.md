# The Ketchup-Packet Customer Problem

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Chad, who runs a $1.3M/year indoor air quality business serving customers who are highly sensitive to their indoor environment. Chad's core problem is that his customers are "single serving ketchup packets" — the business solves their problem so well that they inherently shouldn't come back — and he wants to know how to raise LTV. He aims for $5M ("10 would be better"), and when Alex prods "Why not 100?", Chad answers "at least 100 million. I read the book."

Alex responds by enumerating the generic levers any business has to increase revenue (raise prices, decrease costs, cross-sell additional services, upsell better quality, upsell more units, downsell an inferior unit — "there's an eighth one, but there's seven"), then zeroes in on two: pricing headroom (most people are underpriced) and a maintenance package, since the company is functionally a home services business. When Chad reveals he already has an annual "superior air plan" checkup but only a quarter of front-end customers buy it, Alex reframes the whole thing as a sales problem, not an LTV-model problem. He splits sales into two buckets — front-end sales (where a ~35% close rate is normal KPI for an appropriately priced offer) and upsells (where a cheap bolt-on to the core product should be an assumed close positioned as what "everybody always does", hitting 70–80% take rates). Chad therefore has two sales problems: the maintenance take rate, and the sales scripting for upselling once customers come back for maintenance.

On acquisition, Chad asks whether to pivot focus to an "affiliate army" of holistic doctors as the first foot in the door. Alex learns Chad's only current channel is Google Ads and that it isn't maxed out, so he prescribes spending more on the existing channel before adding a second one: more of the same probably gets him to $3M, at which point costs rise and the maintenance plan must start paying dividends — potentially freeing cash flow toward $1M/month — and only if that fails would he explore alternative channels. The video closes with Hormozi's standard CTA for the free $100 million scaling road map at acquisition.com/roadmap (10 stages, built from ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies).
