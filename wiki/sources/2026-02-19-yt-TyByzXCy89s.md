# What Is Mental Toughness?

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi monologue that **defines** mental toughness and lays out a visual framework for measuring it. It shares a title with [[2026-02-23-yt-Ui6pE7qfmUI]] and is clearly a segment of the same larger talk, but the **content is distinct**: where the 2026-02-23 video walks through the four *components* (reactivity, negative snowball, fortitude, adaptability) and their fixes, this video does the upstream conceptual work — the probabilistic **definition** of mental toughness and a **6-stage response-curve diagram** of how you behave when bad things happen. Treat as a companion, not a duplicate.

His definition: mental toughness is **the percentage likelihood that when something bad happens, you change how you act in a way that's against your goals.** Reframing it as a probability turns it from a binary ("do you have it or not?") into a scalar ("how much do you have?"). High mental toughness = a low chance a bad event knocks your behavior off course; low mental toughness = a high chance it does.

He then draws a diagram — "how we act when bad stuff happens" — with six numbered stages:
1. Your normal baseline behavior (how you act every day).
2. A bad thing (or enough bad things) forces you to change how you behave.
3. How low you go / how upset you get — the depth of the dip.
4. The recovery period: time elapses, the bad behavior continues, then you start coming back up.
5. A bidirectional fork: as a result of the hard thing you end up either better or worse than before.
6. The magnitude of #5 — how much better or how much worse you got.

The point of the framework: strip mental toughness of "rah-rah, beat-your-chest, alpha-masculinity" connotations and treat it as plain **resilience** — a measurable, exercisable response pattern. He also clarifies his working definition of "bad": simply **against your preferences** — something happened that you wish hadn't.
