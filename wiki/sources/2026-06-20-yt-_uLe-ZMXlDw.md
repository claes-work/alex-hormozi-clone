# How to Scale a $3M Tennis Business to $10M

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a tennis and pickleball facility constrained by space. Alex distinguishes between an immediate profit lift and a bigger strategic expansion path. Immediately, he suggests pricing, offer, and sales-process improvements because demand exceeds supply. Strategically, he recommends analyzing revenue per square foot by service category, identifying the hidden core economic engine, and replicating a smaller, more efficient version rather than assuming the next unit must resemble the current 45,000-square-foot facility.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A space-constrained brick-and-mortar operator faces a strategic choice between tuning the current model and building a larger replicable concept.
- If demand exceeds capacity, price increases, offer changes, and sales-process improvements are the immediate profit levers.
- Revenue per square foot should be analyzed by service category.
- The current business may contain a smaller hidden economic engine that is easier to replicate.
- Alex again uses Rick Mayo's Alloy example: identify the service with the best revenue per square foot and LTV:CAC, then duplicate a smaller unit.
- A smaller-footprint model may be easier to expand across markets than a very large facility.
- The model should be nailed before scaling.
