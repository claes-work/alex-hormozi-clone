# Learn These 28 Ways To Be Rich

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi monologue framed via Charlie Munger's inversion thinking: rather
than list rules for getting rich, he lists 28 ways to *stay poor*, then reverses them
all in a rapid-fire close. The throughline is that success is largely about not making
the predictable, self-inflicted mistakes most people make — start now, keep promises to
yourself, prioritize being rich over looking rich, and spend less than you make. He
notes the list originated as a Twitter thread that got over 5,000 shares.

## Key claims

All claims are Alex-attributed (solo video). Each "way to stay poor" is the inversion of
his actual advice.

- **Start tomorrow** — procrastination is the single best way to stay poor; the fix is to
  start today, whatever the goal (weight, savings, business).
- **Read lots of books and do nothing** — better to read one book and act on it than
  read 52 and apply none.
- **Take advice from poor people on how to be rich** — and let them judge your ideas as
  stupid; "what would they know?" Don't seek approval from the unsuccessful.
- **Pick a spouse who makes you feel guilty for working** — puts the power over your
  destiny in someone else's hands and breeds resentment; choose a partner who supports
  your dreams even when they disagree.
- **Fail once, quit forever** — rests on a wrong view of failure; "either you win or you
  learn."
- **Think the world is fair / blame your circumstances and complain** — complaining
  produces zero activity; assume the world is unfair and act accordingly. "Your mess is
  your message"; strong character is forged through hard times.
- **Expect the government (or anyone) to save you** — "no one's going to save you except
  for you."
- **Value others' opinions over your own** — "you are successful the moment you say you
  are." Setting a number just moves the goalpost; success is a process of living your
  values, not an outcome.
- **Avoid discomfort** — the discomfort is just self-imposed judgment about being bad at
  something you've never done; avoiding it guarantees the #1 discomfort of not
  accomplishing what you want.
- **Tolerate mediocrity from yourself and others** — great leaders hold a high standard
  they themselves stay above; most people don't need to be led, they need a model.
- **Make promises, break promises** — the hardest reputation to repair is the one with
  yourself; self-respect is the hardest to earn because you know how hard you could have
  pushed. He frames self-respect as a form of happiness.
- **Wait for perfect conditions** — they never come; if you need perfect conditions,
  you'll fail the moment they change. Better to start at the worst.
- **Prioritize looking rich over being rich** — "be willing to look poor"; you must be
  willing to die to the opinion of others rather than the opinion of yourself.
- **Avoid working on what matters most** — speed isn't about activity; the rich do the
  right stuff (usually the thing you're procrastinating on), not the most stuff.
- **Do what everyone else is doing** — you can't be in the 1% doing what 99% do.
- **Do your best, not what it takes** — the marketplace doesn't care about your best; it
  cares about what's required. Become good enough that your best exceeds the requirement.
- **Talk more, do less** — posting goals/affirmations and prepping routines instead of
  doing; "start with the doing."
- **Start something new, repeat** — leaving half-built bridges; jumping ship at the
  "valley of despair" of the change cycle (uninformed optimism → informed pessimism →
  valley of despair).
- **Repeat the same mistake again and again** — same inputs, same outputs. "Poor can be
  whatever you want it to be" — even a billionaire can be poor if it's less than he wants.
- **Be replaceable** (credited to Naval Ravikant) — if a degree qualifies you, anyone can
  replace you; learn things others can't do.
- **Find something that works, then stop doing it** — most people already know what works
  (they make money last-minute to pay others' bills) but won't do it for themselves;
  "most people know what to do to achieve their goals."
- **Hire dumb people** — a great way to have a bad business.
- **Assume you are always right** — possibly a top one; people who are far ahead have a
  voracious desire to learn and the humility that everyone knows something they don't.
  "You'd rather be right than be rich."
- **Spend more than you make** — the simplest: no matter how much you earn, outspending it
  keeps you poor. He notes the bottom ~50% of America has no savings, yet lifestyle is
  controllable.
