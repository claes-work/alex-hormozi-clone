# Helping an Agency That Serves Wholesalers Scale

## Summary

Short coaching Q&A. The questioner ("JT") runs a done-for-you real-estate business — a
"closer" service that handles marketing and sales for new wholesalers so they can scale
and "earn while they learn." He reports his sales team keeps closing deals where the
customer agrees on price but can't afford the first payment or qualify for financing, and
that his close rate is very low despite a healthy ~7:1 LTV:CAC. Alex Hormozi's diagnosis:
this is a lead-quality problem, not a sales problem. His prescription is to add
qualification friction to the front end and to define the actual customer avatar, using
the process from the first chapter of the "Lost Chapters" book (look at your
highest-spending, longest-staying customers; extract demographics, quantifiables, and
geographics), then weave that avatar through every asset — ads, landing pages, funnel
drop-down questions, and the VSL. Along the way he distinguishes a real qualification
question from a "do you have a pulse and a credit card" question, and reframes the common
fear that narrowing the audience raises cost per lead.

## Key claims (2026-02-18, paraphrased; Alex-attributed unless noted)

- The problem the caller describes (customers agree on price but can't afford the first
  payment / can't finance) is a lead-quality problem, not a sales problem — the fix is
  qualification, not more closing pressure.
- ★ Adding qualification friction to a funnel almost always makes you *more* money;
  Hormozi says he can barely recall a time adding qualification friction made him less
  money.
- Adding qualification changes the metrics predictably: upstream metrics (cost per call /
  cost per click / cost per appointment) will likely go *up*, but CAC may go *down* and
  LTV will "certainly" go up.
- Qualification also protects the sales team's morale — reps stop getting "beat down from
  hearing no all day," so they close more in general, beyond just removing disqualified
  prospects.
- Accepting unqualified buyers ("minnows") is bad on multiple axes: it's someone's "last
  dollar" (an ethics problem), and it's bad for the business, the sales team, and
  reputation. Fundamentally, "when you accept everyone, you ward off people who can
  actually afford stuff."
- Ethics arguments rarely persuade an operator who's making money — so he argues it on
  business grounds too.
- ★ A lead magnet (here, the caller's in-depth SOP/training guide) can carry the
  qualifier: keep the free value, but add a box/field that captures the traits a good
  customer must have, then only route qualified leads to sales.
- Front-end routing pattern: only call the qualified leads; have the thank-you page sort
  the best leads to the sales team and send the worst leads to an automated flow that can
  self-liquidate some of the ad spend.
- "Are you willing to invest in yourself?" is not a qualification question — it's a "do
  you have a pulse and a credit card" question. Real qualification identifies who the
  avatar actually is.
- ★ Avatar-finding process (attributed to the first chapter of the "Lost Chapters"
  book): look at the customers who have spent the most and stayed the longest, then
  characterize them along three axes — demographics (who they are), quantifiables (income
  level, number of kids, family situation), and geographics (where they live). Bonus axis:
  sales-process behavior (did the best customers consume other assets during the sales
  process that lower-value customers didn't?).
- Worked example he gives from his own data: his best avatar turned out to be
  "conservative Christian males 35-plus who are married" — so that's who the ads are built
  for. (Illustrative of the method.)
- The fear that "more qualifications = higher cost" is misplaced, because you're now
  advertising to the *right* customer, who opts in and then buys more expensive things —
  "everyone's fear is going from general to specific, but when you go specific, you get
  the right people."
- Full sequence he prescribes: (1) determine the avatar from existing customer data;
  (2) weave the avatar into ad callouts; (3) into the landing pages; (4) into the funnel
  drop-down questions / added friction; (5) into the VSL, which should open with the pains
  and hook of that specific avatar. Do all of it and you "will for sure convert more."
- Aside: he suggests the caller could raise money and just do wholesaling directly rather
  than run the done-for-you model — offered as a side note, not pursued.

### Caller's self-reported numbers (audience, uncertain — NOT Alex)

- LTV ≈ $35,000, realized within ~90 days (mostly during onboarding, not from long
  retention).
- CAC ≈ $5,000 → roughly a 7:1 LTV:CAC ratio (Hormozi confirms "you're seven to one…
  that inherently is okay").
- Retention is low; the main thing that keeps customers past onboarding is the CRM (a
  white-labeled GoHighLevel).
- Reported constraint: "our close rate is just super low."
