# Helping a Local Business Owner With Google Ads

## Summary
In this live coaching clip, Alex Hormozi advises Stephen, a podiatrist who sells custom insoles through a local business (in the Netherlands, ~£54K/month revenue, ~£3K/month ad spend, ~£325 AOV). Stephen's stated constraint is that his Google Ads specialist won't spend more than £3K/month. Alex reframes the real problem: the business's binding constraint is sitting under someone else's control, which he calls a tough position to be in. His core prescription is that the owner should learn PPC himself because "it's really not that hard," treat education as a venture-style investment (a personal learning budget where 9 of 10 bets return nothing and the 10th pays off many times over), and run his own £3K/month budget in parallel to compete with the specialist. He also gives a concrete tactic for extracting skill from agencies: pay them to do the work *live on two calls a week while the owner controls the mouse and asks why*, rather than having them work the account privately — the same method Alex says he used to learn national Facebook ads in about six weeks. Throughout he stresses not letting ego attach to one or two months of profit, since profit is always available if you choose not to reinvest, and treating reinvestment (especially when the business is small, where "you are the reinvestment") as the priority.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-10. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-10) [guest — Stephen, self-reported] His business does ~£54K/month revenue with ~£3K/month ad spend; the offer is custom insoles at ~£300 with an AOV of ~£325; he is a podiatrist running a local business (Netherlands), roughly a 10km radius.
- (2026-02-10) [guest — Stephen, self-reported] A Google Ads specialist runs his ads; Stephen never learned it himself, and the specialist says the budget can't exceed £3K/month; Stephen suspects the specialist may just lack the skill to scale the campaign, and his marketing agency is already checking another specialist to duplicate campaigns.
- (2026-02-10) Alex questions the premise that the business "just can't spend more money," saying he doesn't believe it and probing the city size / 10-mile radius to test whether the market is really saturated.
- (2026-02-10) Alex reframes the situation: right now the constraint of the business is under someone else's control, which is a tough position to be in; the owner should want to own this himself.
- (2026-02-10) Alex argues learning PPC / Google Ads is "really not that hard," partly because the platforms make trillions and work hard to make it easy for advertisers.
- (2026-02-10) Alex recounts a formative story: at a meetup of eight-figure earners he sneaked into, a man making ~$30–35M/year advised writing down a percentage of income as a monthly learning budget, spending it every month, and expecting zero ROI on nine of ten things while the tenth returns ~10x — framed as a venture bet on yourself.
- (2026-02-10) Alex's tactical advice: Stephen should run his own £3K/month budget that he controls (e.g. ~$100/day), and try to beat the specialist — predicting the owner will beat him because it's his own business, after an inevitably rough start.
- (2026-02-10) Alex claims that spending more on ads reliably makes more money: even if inefficient, more ad spend increases topline revenue.
- (2026-02-10) Alex advises against letting ego get wrapped up in one or two months of profit, because that profit is always there if you choose not to spend it; he prefers taking as much profit as possible and reinvesting it.
- (2026-02-10) Alex's principle: when a business is small, "you are the reinvestment" — invest in your own skills.
- (2026-02-10) Alex's agency-learning method: don't just get weekly update calls; instead pay the agency to do *all* the work live on two calls a week while the owner controls the mouse and the agency explains what to do and why. He says this is exactly how he learned to run national-level Facebook ads — he hired an agency, controlled the mouse, recorded his screen, asked "why are we doing this?", and within about six weeks no longer needed them.
