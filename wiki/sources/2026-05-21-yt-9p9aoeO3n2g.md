# Should You Do What You Love or What Pays the Bills

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says "follow your passion" does not serve most people. He argues that
passions change, some passions are not valued by markets, and the emotional expectation
that work should always feel good can cause people to quit on the right path. He reframes
unpleasant work as the price tag for becoming the person someone wants to become.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex treats "follow your passion" as a common myth.
- He thinks successful commencement speakers say "follow your passion" partly because it
  is socially acceptable advice.
- Passions and interests often change across a decade, so building a long-term career on
  current passion alone is risky.
- Some passions are not valued by other people enough to produce income.
- The follow-your-passion frame can make business too emotional: when it stops feeling
  good, people assume they should stop.
- Business contains parts someone loves and parts someone hates; both may be required.
- Pain from doing disliked tasks can be reframed as the price tag required to become the
  desired person.
- Traits such as patience, endurance, and wisdom require conditions that train those
  traits.
- Someone may be good at something the market values and use that work to fund the rest
  of life.
