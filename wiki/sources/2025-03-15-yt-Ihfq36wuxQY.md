# Sell Anything To Anyone With This Unusual Method

## Summary

Alex Hormozi teaches a single persuasion tactic he calls the **"damaging admission"** — deliberately stating a true negative first, then using the word **"but"** to amplify the positive that follows. Because the word "but" diminishes whatever precedes it and amplifies whatever follows, reversing the usual order ("negative but positive" instead of "positive but negative") makes the closing claim far more believable. He argues the tactic works because it is rooted in integrity: you own your deficiencies honestly, which makes you stand out from "charlatans" and increases prospect trust — and trust is what ultimately drives the buy decision.

## Key claims (the sales method)

- **The degree to which someone buys is directly correlated with how much they trust you** — especially in B2B. If a prospect truly believed "for every dollar you give me you'll make 10 back," everyone would buy; the only thing standing in the way is whether they believe you (and whether they believe in themselves vs. the system vs. the world).
- A **referral closes easily** because the referrer has already imbued you with trust and vouched for you — the prospect arrives with sky-high trust and you just need to get out of their way.
- The core technique is the **"damaging admission"**, built on the word **"but"**: everything after "but" is **amplified**, everything before "but" is **diminished**.
- Most people phrase it as **positive-but-negative** ("you'll make a ton of money, but it'll be a ton of work"), which directs attention to the negatives. **Reverse it** to negative-but-positive ("you'll have to go through hundreds of hours of videos and spend five hours a day, but you'll make more money than you've ever made in your life").
- **The more negatives you admit up front — and the more true and damaging they are — the more believable the positive claim that follows.** The admissions must be truthful.
- This lets you **control where the prospect's attention goes** — directing it to the point after "but."
- Applies to **reviews/social proof**: negative reviews give credence to positive ones. He'd rather eat at a restaurant with 4.7 stars and 3,000 ratings than 5 stars with 19; gym launch has one-star reviews *but also* thousands of five-star reviews.
- The **Viagra "erection lasting longer than 4 hours" warning** is the archetype: stating a genuine risk in extreme fashion amplified the product's perceived power — every guy heard it and thought "sign me up."
- Warning of the **extreme adverse effect of a positive result** (e.g. "working with us will massively increase your tax burden from all the money you'll make") makes the underlying result seem assumed, boosting the believability of the whole argument.
- **Weave it into copy, especially near the call to action** — and use it in sales presentations.
- He notes **"because"** works similarly in a different way (to be covered in another video).
