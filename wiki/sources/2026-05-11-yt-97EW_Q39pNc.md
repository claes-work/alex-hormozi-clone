# I Was Just as Happy When I Was Poor

## Summary

Alex reflects that he once wanted his current life, but his contentment is roughly the
same as when he was poor. He argues that wants, whether past, present, or future, have
less power over present perception than people assume. He uses hedonic adaptation,
including a paraphrased example about disability and subjective well-being stabilizing,
to argue that good things are not as good and bad things are not as bad as expected.

## Key claims (Alex's self-report and opinion, dated 2026-05-11)

- Alex says he knows he wanted the life he currently has.
- He says that when he was poor, he was pretty happy and okay with being poor.
- He says that now that he is rich "in degrees," his contentment is about the same.
- He concludes that richness did not determine his level of contentedness.
- He says past, present, and future wants are largely irrelevant to present perception.
- He says anxiety often attaches to future events, good or bad, whose emotional impact
  will likely normalize.
- He references research-like claims that people who become disabled often experience a
  dip in subjective well-being and then stabilize after several months.
- He concludes that good things are not as good and bad things are not as bad as people
  think.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "When I was poor, I was pretty happy."

> "The richness had nothing to do with the level of contentedness."

> "Good things aren't as good as you think they are. Bad things aren't as bad as you think they are."
