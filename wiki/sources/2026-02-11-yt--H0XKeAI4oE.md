# I Own a Recruiting Company. What Do I Sell After I Get Someone a Job?

## Summary
In a live Q&A on @MoreMozi, a member running a $39/month paid community that helps students land corporate jobs (average: ideal job in 60 days) asks how to retain members after they've achieved the desired outcome. Alex reframes retention as a next-problem question: once you solve someone's current problem, sell the solution to the problem that solving it creates. For job placement, the next problem after getting the role is upward trajectory — raises, skill upleveling, negotiation, and re-bidding for better roles. He argues the whole recruiting/placement industry (with billion-dollar players) is built on "foot in the door," but durable continuity requires niching down to one avatar (engineers, tax lawyers, etc.) so you deeply know their problems and can deliver high-value solutions — e.g., a lower-priced professional association around career advancement and continued education. The strategic move is to sell a larger five-year career vision on the front end so members stay for the full journey, not just the initial placement.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) The questioner runs a paid community helping students get into corporate companies, priced at $39/month, averaging landing the ideal job in 60 days. [guest-attributed] [self-reported figures]
- (2026-02-11) The core retention move is to ask: what's the next problem the customer will want to solve after you've solved their current one? Sell the solution to that next problem.
- (2026-02-11) You could build an entire business off just getting people into roles — that's fundamentally what the whole recruiting and placement industry is based on, and there are billion-dollar companies that do only that.
- (2026-02-11) Most successful placement companies niche down to one particular role type and, over time, build a large network within that niche (engineers, salespeople, customer success, legal interns, etc.).
- (2026-02-11) For an ongoing/continuity offer, a good fit is a professional-association-type product priced at a big fraction (a fraction) of the front-end price, focused on career advancement and continued education.
- (2026-02-11) Continuity like this is really only viable if you have one specific niche, because it lets you know the avatar's problems and provide high-value solutions.
- (2026-02-11) After someone gets the role, their next problem is their upward trajectory: getting raises, upleveling skills to earn more, and negotiating their annual raise.
- (2026-02-11) You can also help members use a new/competing offer to bid out other roles at other companies to move up.
- (2026-02-11) What you sell on the front end determines the retention ceiling: you can sell just "the foot in the door," or you can paint a larger vision — "this is a career for you" with a five-year playbook — which keeps people much longer.
- (2026-02-11) Example structure: step one is a "foot in the door" program that gets someone from not making money as an engineer to being an engineer.
- (2026-02-11) Engineers can make millions of dollars a year if they're really good, so the follow-on program teaches the three highest-demand, highest-paying languages over the next 12 months.
- (2026-02-11) After that skill-building period, you run another job cycle: take the member's new resume and skill set and bid it out.
- (2026-02-11) That job cycle has two outcomes — (1) they get a job they like, or (2) they get an offer they can take back to their current employer to negotiate a matching raise within the existing role.
- (2026-02-11) The same model transfers to other professions: e.g., in law, teaching a more valuable specialization like tax law beats generic law.
- (2026-02-11) All of this is enabled by niching down enough to truly know the specific avatar's problems and deliver very high-value solutions.
