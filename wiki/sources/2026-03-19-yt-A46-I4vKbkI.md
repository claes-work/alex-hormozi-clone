# Helping a B2B SAAS Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a B2B SaaS/service business selling a "sustainable growth platform for gyms" runs $4M top-line revenue at ~50% profit ($2.2M) and wants to reach $10M in two years. He can't name a single hard blocker; his real question is "what's the right question to ask" to ease the path. Alex runs the diagnostic himself: CAC ~$1,000; ~70% of acquisition is Meta ads (so ~20% of the mix is paid, rest referral/organic — organic carried early but hit "net zero return," so ads were added per the original plan); LTV ~$12k top line per client at ~20% cost (so high gross margin); ~$500/month with many annual plans; churn roughly 3–3.5% (data admittedly messy because "Stripe sucks our data"); sales velocity 35 units/month; ad spend ~$30k/month. Alex computes the **hypothetical max**: 35 units × $12k LTV ≈ $4.8M, meaning at current velocity the business is near its ceiling — "given we changed nothing." His verdict: with ~12:1 LTV:CAC and two-year annual stick, it's already a valuable business, and the hard problems are solved. To grow, spend must scale from ~$30k to ~$300k/month in ads — but the owner admits the fulfillment side (a web team building client websites) is "starting to scream." Alex's core coaching move: the owner is **pulling his punches** — profitable but hesitating. Rather than "build the plane as we fly," Alex says **build capacity first**: you must "either choose to sacrifice profit or reputation," so when capacity-constrained you either reach into your bottom line to build capacity or you juice demand and let metrics/reputation take the hit. Since he's not in a rush, build capacity (e.g. double the VAs supporting the one site-builder — "cost you 17 cents"), get everything tuned and the team happy, and *then* spend bullishly. Second half is a personal Q&A: the owner, who also serves gym owners, asks how Alex knew to "cross the bridge" and move on from Gym Launch. Alex's answer: the turning point was falling out of love with the avatar — he grew **resentful** ("what have you done for me lately"), not bored; he was ~10 years younger and more immature; and he couldn't *see* how to make Gym Launch bigger, which is why he sold. In hindsight he says he could have exited for $400M (or a half-billion to a billion) "with changing very little" — "the most expensive things in life are the things that we don't know we should know." He closes by telling the owner the metrics are strong and there's "a nine-figure-plus opportunity with the business as it currently stands." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses scaling readiness by reconstructing the unit economics live: CAC, primary acquisition channel and its share, LTV/gross profit per client, monthly price, churn, and sales velocity — then multiplies sales velocity × LTV to find the "hypothetical max" the current machine can reach unchanged.
- As of March 2026, this client's numbers: $4M revenue at ~50% profit, CAC ~$1,000, ~70% Meta ads, LTV ~$12k/client at ~20% cost, ~$500/month with many annuals, churn ~3–3.5%, 35 sales/month, ~$30k/month ad spend — yielding ~12:1 LTV:CAC, which Alex calls "pretty good" and "a valuable business."
- As of March 2026, at 35 units/month × $12k LTV the business tops out near $4.8M "given we changed nothing," so meaningful growth requires scaling ad spend roughly 10x (from ~$30k to ~$300k/month) to buy more volume.
- As of March 2026, when scaling volume "the thing breaks" somewhere — here the constraint surfaced first in fulfillment (a web team building client sites "starting to scream" for bandwidth).
- As of March 2026, Alex's core principle: a capacity-constrained business must "either choose to sacrifice profit or reputation" — you either reach into your bottom line to build capacity, or you add demand and let your metrics/reputation take the hit.
- As of March 2026, for a profitable owner who is "not in a rush" and "pulling his punches," Alex prescribes building capacity FIRST (rather than "build the plane as we fly") because "building capacity will keep your ethics high"; once tuned and the team is happy, the owner will "feel really bullish" about spending.
- As of March 2026, adding capacity can be cheap relative to its payoff — doubling the VAs supporting a single site-builder is "not a hard task" and would "cost you 17 cents."
- As of March 2026 (personal/reflection): Alex says the signal to exit a business is when you're "no longer in love with your avatar"; his Gym Launch turning point was growing **resentful** ("what have you done for me lately"), not bored, compounded by being younger and more immature.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he sold Gym Launch because he "couldn't see a way to make it bigger," but in hindsight could have exited for $400M — or a half-billion to a billion — "with changing very little," framing it as "I only know what I know now because I went through what I didn't know."
- As of March 2026, Alex tells the client there is "a nine-figure-plus opportunity with the business as it currently stands," needing only a few pieces and possibly a couple of strategic investments to make it acquisition-attractive.
