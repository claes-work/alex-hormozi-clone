# Cash vs. Equity: How to Pay Employees Without Losing Your Company

## Summary

In this Q&A, Alex Hormozi gives a simple equity-versus-cash compensation equation to a chess-improvement entrepreneur: compare the equity cost of giving employees ownership to the equity cost of selling shares to investors and using the cash to pay market compensation. The caller then asks what "winning" means to Alex. Alex answers that he enjoys the game itself and defines a good day as working out, eating with people he likes, and writing something.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach and personal respondent. The advice and self-reports below are Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - chess-improvement founder. His revenue, investor access, hiring concern, and question about winning are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Employee equity should be compared against the dilution required to raise investor cash and pay employees full cash compensation.
- If raising money creates less dilution than equity compensation would, the raise can be mathematically preferable.
- Alex says his "win" is the game itself; he enjoys playing the process.
- His optimization process is high volume, then reviewing the last 100 or 1,000 units of output, isolating what the top 10% did differently, and repeating those elements.
- Alex self-reports that at age 21 a boss's comment helped him operationalize happiness as living as many good days in a row as possible.
- Alex defines a good day as working out with something he likes, eating food with people he likes, and writing something.
