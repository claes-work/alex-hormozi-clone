# The Only Two Real Tax Shelters That Actually Hold Up

## Summary

Alex and Leila answer a new founder's question about legal/tax structure. They say
making money matters more than optimizing future investor structure because acquirers
and investors often restructure companies anyway. Alex then warns against aggressive tax
schemes, naming real-estate professional depreciation and charitable foundations as the
main durable shelter categories, while emphasizing by-the-book compliance.

## Key claims

- Alex and Leila say the founder's entity structure can usually be changed later during
  investment or sale processes.
- Alex says acquirers may prefer asset purchases for liability reasons, while founders
  often prefer entity sales for capital-gains treatment.
- Alex self-reports that Gym Launch was sold as an entity after the original LOI was an
  asset purchase.
- Alex says Acquisition.com, Sequoia, and Andreessen Horowitz may prefer different
  entity structures, so optimizing for one future buyer too early is not useful.
- Alex says the two major durable tax shelter buckets are real-estate professional
  depreciation and charitable foundations.
- Alex says location/jurisdiction can be a major tax lever, and that he and Leila have
  tried to stay by the book to remove risks that could kill the business.
- Leila says they saw multiple tax-scheme promoters who later proved untrustworthy, and
  Alex cites Charlie Munger's view that many tax schemes later get unwound.
