# Giving Away Free Stuff Will Make You Rich

## Summary

A solo Alex Hormozi video framed as a sneak preview of one chapter of his forthcoming
book *$100M Leads*. He argues that most businesses are starved for leads because they
don't give away enough free stuff, and lays out his definition of a lead magnet — a
*complete* solution to a narrowly defined problem that naturally opens up the next
(paid) problem. He works through the unit economics of why a paid or costly lead magnet
still lowers total customer-acquisition cost, and closes with a seven-step checklist for
building lead magnets. The video also doubles as an ad for his August 19, 2023 book
launch event at acquisition.com/leads.

## Key claims

- People buy based on a **prediction of future value**; the easiest way to make someone
  predict they'll get value after buying is to give them value *before* they buy.
- There are two fundamental ways to get leads: (1) just make your offer directly, or
  (2) put a lead magnet in front of it.
- **Core offer** = the thing you sell that makes you the most money. **Lead magnet** =
  "the thing before the thing." If a lead magnet starts making you the most money, it
  has actually become your core offer.
- A lead magnet is **not** a partial solution. Giving away the first 6 minutes of a
  12-minute video and gating the rest loses trust and is the opposite of the objective.
- A lead magnet is a **complete solution to a narrowly defined problem** — one of the
  many micro-steps in a larger solution, solved fully at a massive discount to attract
  people and build trust ("one foot in the door").
- Free (or discounted) offers generate demand: if you give something away and it's good,
  demand becomes exponential.
- **Unit economics example:** offering a $100 massage to 100 people might yield 1 sale;
  offering a $10 massage might yield 20 takers, of whom ~30% (6) later buy the $100 core
  offer. Even if delivering the lead magnet costs money (e.g. losing $10 on 20 people =
  $200 spend), you acquire 6 customers worth ~$2,000.
- **The core principle:** even if your lead magnet costs money to deliver, it should
  still *lower* your total cost to acquire a new customer. That's what separates big
  business owners (who incur calculated upfront cost for a bigger back end) from small
  ones.
- Something that costs you money to give away is usually valued higher by the market than
  a free PDF or video series — unless the digital freebie is exceptional, people won't
  value it.
- Solving one problem well creates the next problem (before / during / after the customer
  journey), which becomes the next paid step — illustrated with a weight-loss example
  (grocery list → food prep → storage/portioning).
- Your **free stuff should be better than your competitors' paid stuff**; over-deliver
  even to people who will never pay you, because they refer the people who will. A bad
  ("sucky fluff") lead magnet actively creates negative word of mouth.
- **Seven-step lead magnet checklist:** (1) figure out the problem and who you solve it
  for; (2) figure out how to solve it (assessment / trial / and combinations); (3) figure
  out how to deliver it (info, service, physical product); (4) name and package it — the
  most important step (cites A/B testing the *$100M Leads* title and cover); (5) make it
  fast and easy to consume across formats (audio, written, video — *$100M Offers*
  consumption split roughly evenly across formats); (6) make it genuinely good — put 100
  in, not 10; (7) make it easy to take the next step: "don't be clever, be clear," and
  offer multiple CTA paths.
