# watch this if you want to find your purpose in life

## Summary

A long-form conversation (Alex interviewed by an unnamed host/team member) in which
Hormozi lays out his philosophy of purpose, happiness, and meaning. His central move is
to reject happiness and meaning as goals — they are external, so wanting them guarantees
you don't have them — and to replace them with **usefulness** and **work itself as the
goal**. He connects this to skill mastery, the "input-output equation," sacrifice/focus,
and his own biography (writing scholarship, quitting pre-med, selling Gym Launch,
"Solomon" self-dialogue). Recurring frame: you create passion by being willing to suck
for a long time until you get good; effort is the universal currency of respect.

## Key claims (his view on purpose / meaning)

- He does **not** optimize for subjective well-being / happiness; he says he gave that up
  around age 19–20 and it "has never been a goal."
- Happiness treated as a goal is self-defeating: making it a goal puts it *outside* you,
  creating a gap that means you are not happy *now*. Same logic applies to meaning.
- Life does not *need* to be meaningful and the universe does not owe you happiness — you
  can choose meaning, but it isn't a requirement; the only reason you're "here" is that
  you're alive and might procreate.
- His replacement goal is **being useful** — useful to other people, which forces both a
  service element and self-improvement. "No one casts aside a man who is useful."
- Men (he explicitly addresses men) want **respect** more than love, and respect comes
  from being useful; skill mastery is therefore attractive and status-conferring.
- Reframe "value" as "usefulness" for clarity. Because he has skills, he isn't afraid of
  losing it all — he lost it before and got it back; luck without skill is what should
  scare you.
- His tactical happiness method: over 3–4 years he logged good days vs. bad days, found
  what the good days had in common (workouts, writing, doing things he likes with people
  he likes), then maximized those and minimized/outsourced the rest.
- **"Doing things you like with people you like"** is his single simplest formula.
- Purpose/skill: find the **input-output equation** of a skill (e.g. sales = 100 calls a
  day; ~10,000 hours) and dump as much input in as fast as possible; remove everything
  (including people) that interferes.
- Confidence = a stack of undeniable proof/evidence that you can do the thing (100 reps of
  the book-launch talk), not affirmations. Do it until you're bored of it.
- Mastery = the transition from external motivators to the **work itself becoming
  intrinsically rewarding**; masters enjoy the work more than novices, so it gets easier.
- **The goal is the work**, not what the work gets you. You set new goalposts to create
  challenges, not to "achieve goals." Winning the lottery would be the ultimate loss
  because you'd never get to prove yourself.
- "Getting it right is where all the money is" — most people want to get it *done*. He
  rewrites books 5–10x end-to-end; quality is his own standard, aimed at being a
  bestseller "when I'm dead."
- To be the *best* you must sacrifice everything not aligned with the goal; you can be
  "really good" without that, but not #1. Focus follows from how long "getting it right"
  takes — you can only do one thing.
- You **create** passion, you don't find it — by being willing to suck for a long time.
  Reject the "fallacy of the perfect pick": get directional, iterate, and every prior
  skill carries over uniquely (Steve Jobs' calligraphy; his own consulting → gyms).
- Reframe hardship as "the story you will someday tell"; the worst that can happen is you
  die, and you'll die anyway, which lowers the stakes.
- **"Solomon"** = his future/wiser self. He opens a Google Doc, sets a date, and types a
  back-and-forth dialogue ("me/him") — a self-therapy tool with perfectly aligned
  incentives and full context, unlike a paid therapist.
