# My $100M Leads Affiliate Marketing Guide

## Summary
Alex Hormozi walks through the affiliate-marketing strategy he used to promote the free virtual launch event for his book *$100M Leads*, framing the whole campaign as a live, meta demonstration of the concepts inside the book. He explains that an affiliate strategy means serving two customers at once — the affiliate and the affiliate's audience — and that success comes from aligning the incentives of all three parties (you, the affiliate, and their audience) so that the ask costs the affiliate no goodwill. He details a two-tier affiliate structure: super affiliates competing for a scarce top-10 prize (a live AMA with him for their audience) and regular affiliates who unlock two unreleased bonus chapters simply by bringing 10 people to the event. He introduces a "whisper, tease, shout" three-phase promotion timeline layered on top of constant, unchanging value delivery, and covers affiliate selection, onboarding, opt-in mechanics, and ongoing reminders. He reports getting roughly 9,000–10,000 people to promote the book for free, mostly driven by two emails to his existing list. Throughout he stresses building "missionaries, not mercenaries" and making every advertisement itself valuable.

## Key claims
- He set out to get 10,000 people to promote *$100M Leads* for free, likening it to "an entire stadium full of people."
- His first book, *$100M Offers*, was priced at 99 cents but bundled a course most would charge thousands for, as a meta-demonstration of the value equation; he used *$100M Leads* to demonstrate lead generation via a free virtual launch event.
- An advanced advertising insight: with affiliates you have two customers — an "affiliate avatar" and a "customer avatar" — and must deliver to both.
- An affiliate strategy involves two layers of offers: the offer you make to the affiliate, and the offer they make on your behalf to their audience.
- Brick-and-mortar example: a massage therapist offering two free massages to a company's new hires, or to a chiropractor's patients — often needing no monetary incentive because it serves the partner's interest.
- Three types of affiliate/influencer arrangements: brick-and-mortar partnerships, influencer discount-code endorsements, and internet-marketing affiliate lists (the third being his main method for the book).
- He claims he had zero financial incentive built into his campaign, which is uncommon, and affiliates could tell their audience they had no financial incentive either.
- Two-tier structure: super affiliates (big, competitive, status-driven audiences) compete for a top-10 prize of a live AMA/Q&A with him for their audience; regular affiliates who bring 10+ attendees get two unreleased bonus chapters.
- The top-10 prize was engineered as a live AMA *for the affiliate's audience* specifically so the ask benefits the audience rather than withdrawing goodwill — aligning all three incentives. He notes he hadn't explained this publicly before.
- He limits his supply of public speaking; he claims that in the past year, of companies he doesn't own, he did only one speech.
- Content volume claim: he puts out ~250 pieces of content a week, ~1,000 a month, ~12,000 a year; and emailed his list only ~three or four times in the past year.
- "Whisper, tease, shout" three-phase promotion: whisper = 6+ weeks out (building anticipation without details); tease = 6 weeks to 7 days (revealing snippets); shout = last 7 days (mostly reminders). He heard the phrase at an event and doesn't know who coined it.
- Common marketer mistake: shouting for six weeks so audiences get bored before the event; shout is effective but burns out fast.
- Value delivery stays constant and unchanged throughout all three phases; promotion is layered on top, not substituted for value. He and his brand director Caleb decided to keep providing the same volume of value plus the book content.
- A well-designed advertisement is itself valuable — like movie trailers people share — informing people about the thing while entertaining them.
- Mentor lesson: "the key to making it a big deal is to make it a big deal"; showing you worked on something for two years makes people believe it is worth more (2-years vs. 2-hours video analogy).
- Paid vs. unpaid affiliates: paid requires tax forms (he thinks a 1099), which adds friction that also qualifies more committed affiliates; unpaid requires other incentives (his bonuses). He chose low-friction/unpaid for a mass-market book.
- Opt-in mechanics: affiliate form collected first name, last name, email, phone; thank-you page and a follow-up email both delivered the unique tracked link. Adding the extra email / thank-you-page link reportedly made a ~25% difference in promotion rates.
- Changing the banner on top of the submit form reportedly increased affiliate sign-ups by ~60%.
- The value equation (dream outcome, perceived likelihood, time delay, effort/sacrifice) applies to every step; he made affiliate sign-up take ~30 seconds.
- Two emails a week to affiliates: one with fresh content (to keep their ads from going stale), one with their standings/tracking; expectations set up front.
- He gave affiliates a "black book" up front and another big content packet ~3 weeks in to keep their ads fresh.
- Two emails to his main event list generated 9,000 people registering to promote; he estimates a third of the 9,000 just wanted to watch his behind-the-scenes method and didn't plan to promote at all.
- He advises only two (max three) promotional messages to the audience meant to *attend*, to avoid burning their goodwill.
- "Missionaries, not mercenaries": getting affiliates for the cause rather than money avoids a "race to the top" bidding war; mercenaries defect to the highest bidder.
- Affiliates hold higher standards for how you treat *their* customers than their own, which pressures him to raise his messaging/advertising standards.
- Win-win-win: you win, the affiliate wins, and most importantly their audience wins — the audience winning unlocks unlimited promotion.
- He mentions making late wording tweaks to the promotion only after close friends said they'd push it to their whole audience, giving him the pressure to refine the messaging.
