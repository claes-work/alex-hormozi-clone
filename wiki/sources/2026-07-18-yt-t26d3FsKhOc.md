# Should You Work Harder or Just Play More?

## Summary

In this short conversational clip, an interviewer challenges Alex Hormozi on his rhetoric: since his podcast is called "The Game" and business can be framed as fun, why tell people to "work harder" instead of "play more"? Hormozi answers that the wording is largely irrelevant to him — rhetoric matters little as long as people actually keep doing the work; whatever "flavor of doing" motivates someone is fine with him.

He then gives two concrete reasons he still defaults to "work harder." First, if he told his team to "play," there would be a high likelihood of misunderstanding because of the connotations that word carries from people's history of learning. Second, his experience of work is asymmetric across timescales: in the micro, many things are uncomfortable, while in the macro you love looking back at what you accomplished in a year. He extends the game metaphor: real life doesn't hand you swords, treasure, and new powers, but in a way it does — the rewards are skills, and instead of a scoreboard you see more money, a rising conversion rate, or a salesperson successfully duplicating something you taught him. He closes the exchange saying he is genuinely fine with either framing — "whatever gets you going."

The clip ends with his standard promotional segment for the "$100 million scaling road map" at acquisition.com/roadmap — a free diagnostic built from roughly 200 hours of reviewing acquisition.com portfolio companies, broken into 10 stages of growth across eight business functions (product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance), with a call-booking offer on the thank-you page (not persona content beyond the factual description of the asset).
