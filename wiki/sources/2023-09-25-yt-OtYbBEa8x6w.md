# Alex Hormozi Teaches You How to Write a #1 Best Seller [9 TIPS]

## Summary

Alex Hormozi shares the nine writing rules he applies across books, ads, and outbound copy. His core thesis: writing that "sounds smart" reads that way because it is simpler, not more complex — simplicity takes more effort. He frames the tips as general rules for good writing rather than proprietary rules, and names the Hemingway app as his single most valuable writing tool for fast, color-coded feedback.

## Key claims (the writing tips)

- **Be simple, not complex.** People who imitate him add complex words and sentence structures thinking it sounds smart; writing reads as smart precisely because it is simpler, which takes more effort.
- **Use present voice/tense.** Avoid mixing tenses; default to present tense whenever possible, or otherwise simple tenses.
- **Be active, not passive.** "The body was carried out of the room" (passive) vs. "we carried the body" (active).
- **Cut adverbs.** Adverbs are usually placeholders for weak verbs — "we shut the door really hard" becomes "we slam the door."
- **Write short sentences, almost always max one comma.** When a sentence has two commas, you can usually rip the middle clause out, reframe it into one sentence, or split it into two.
- **Keep vocabulary under third grade, ideally fifth grade tops.**
- **Use positive language.** People can't perceive negatives (the "pink elephant" effect), so say what the reader should do, not what they shouldn't. Note: with very common sayings like "don't stop," the brain already shorthands the meaning, so the effect matters most with atypical phrasing ("don't leave the facility" vs. "stay inside").
- **Remove redundant words.** "We simultaneously walked out the big door at the same time" — cut the redundancy.
- **Eliminate anything that, if removed, leaves the sentence meaning more or less the same** — "killing your darlings," analogous to cutting a part from a video edit and not missing it. Applies to individual words too.
- **Tooling: use Hemingway.** He calls it the greatest writing tool ever created because it gives fast feedback cycles, changing color as you type to show which mistake you made; several of his rules are built into it.
