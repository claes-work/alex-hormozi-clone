# Never Quit...

## Summary
Solo motivational monologue on persistence and insulating yourself from other people's opinions. Alex's core persistence argument: **remove "quitting" from your menu of options** and then simply try your next idea — nothing counts as quitting unless you stop entirely. He reframes the grind with a "Rocky cut scene" metaphor: the training montage lasts seconds on screen but "might last a few years" in real life, and the low point where you ask "why do I even bother" is precisely "where most people stop and this is why they don't win." The second, larger half pivots to why other people's opinions are a trap. His claim: people "don't want the best version of you, they want the version of you who best serves them," and the closer they are, the more your success threatens them — noting he has "more people who root for me now that I've already won than people who rooted for me along the way." He argues no one is doing as well as it appears (the perceived median is ~3x the real median), so most people are "way above average" versus what they imagine, and everyone still has "innings left" / "quarters left to play" until they die. He recounts his own status collapse — Vanderbilt in three years, fraternity president, powerlifting VP, management consultant → minimum-wage employee — and how the implied "why are you here?" question hurt until he realized his reaction was "a me problem": the statement doesn't change whether he takes it as insult or compliment. He warns that building your existence "in opposition to someone" (proving them wrong) hands them "complete control over your life," so he chooses to accept insults rather than let his goals rank below changing someone's mind. Death recurs as his real behavior-change motivator — realizing when he'd achieved everything meant to impress others that "if this is what winning is, then I don't want to play" forced him to change the game to one with rules he set and things he could control. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames persistence as a menu choice: remove "quitting" as an option, then just try your next idea — nothing is quitting unless you stop, so you keep trying ideas.
- As of March 2026, he uses a "Rocky cut scene" metaphor: the montage lasts seconds on film but the real-life version "might last a few years," and the low point where you ask why you bother is exactly where most people stop and lose.
- As of March 2026, he argues people don't want your best version, they want "the version of you who best serves them," and the closer someone is to you, the more your success makes them feel bad about themselves.
- As of March 2026, he claims more people root for him now that he has "already won" than rooted for him on the way up.
- As of March 2026, he argues no one is doing as well as they appear — the perceived (cumulative) median is roughly three times the actual median — so someone who feels average or slightly below is probably "way above average," and it doesn't matter anyway because you have "innings left" and "hope" until you die.
- As of March 2026, he reframes unsolicited advice: someone telling you how to live your life is really stating their own preference for their life; his response is "live your life that way and I'll live my life this way" because your life has no effect on theirs.
- As of March 2026, he attributes such opinions to the other person's discomfort ("that's their problem not yours"), and says he learned frames for not caring out of necessity, having been "crippled by the opinions of other people" for a long time.
- As of March 2026, he cites his own status drop — Vanderbilt in three years, fraternity president, powerlifting-team VP, management consultant → minimum-wage employee — as "about as high status as you can get" at 22 to "about as low status as you can get."
- As of March 2026, his key reframe on being questioned/judged: the statement is identical whether he takes it as insult or compliment, so his emotional reaction is "a me problem," and most people asking "did you go to college?" simply lacked context and meant no insult.
- As of March 2026, he warns that creating your existence "in opposition to someone" (proving them wrong) gives that person complete control over your life; he chooses to accept insults rather than put changing someone's mind above his goals — "my goals are more important to me than changing your mind."
- As of March 2026, he says he talks about death a lot because facing his death is one of the few things that actually motivates him to change his behavior; realizing "if this is what winning is, then I don't want to play" forced him to change games to one with rules he set and things he could control.
