# 'How Do You Sell When Your Value Is Invisible?'

## Summary

A caller who runs a 20-year-old managed service provider (MSP) — IT consulting,
cybersecurity, and support for ~500 managed users — asks how to build a Grand Slam
Offer and risk reversal for a prevention business. His problem: when the job is done
perfectly, nothing happens (no outage, breach, or email fraud), so prospects compare
providers purely on price per user because the value stays invisible until something
breaks. He also can't responsibly guarantee zero breaches because employees, vendors,
and third parties remain outside his control.

Alex reframes the situation. The MSP is not selling "nothing bad happened"; it is
selling a good outcome — the equivalent of a plane landing on time. Using the airline
analogy, he argues that even when two providers deliver the same binary result, the
value lives in the other variables of the journey (speed, service quality, delays,
convenience), which is why people still have favorite and hated airlines. He routes
this back to the value equation and urges decoupling the elements of value in the
service.

On the guarantee objection, Alex says an unconditional guarantee would be tough, but a
scoped, conditional guarantee is viable — functionally "reverse insurance" for the
business itself. Example: if service goes down, the client gets that month free. The
math works if closing twice as many customers outweighs the occasional refunded month;
it's a controlled, deliberate risk, and more aggressive terms win more business.
Conditions can be attached (client follows best practices), the same way insurance has
conditions. He closes the framework with the levers to raise value: name the big risks,
guarantee around them responsibly, maximize speed (and reduce delays), and improve ease
and reduce sacrifice — all aggregated to escape commoditized pricing.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-18. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-18) A prevention business (like an MSP) is not selling "nothing bad
  happened" — it is selling that something good happens (the plane lands on time), and
  should reframe the pitch accordingly.
- (2026-07-18) The MSP's stated problem — prospects compare on price per user because
  value is invisible until something breaks — is a symptom of selling a commodity; Alex
  notes this is why the first chapter of the book (offers) is about the fact that you
  are selling a commodity.
- (2026-07-18) When two providers deliver the same binary outcome, value differentiates
  on the other variables of the journey — service quality, timeliness, delays,
  convenience — which is why people have favorite and hated airlines that both reach the
  destination.
- (2026-07-18) You must decouple the elements of value in the business; this maps to the
  value equation.
- (2026-07-18) Even without the ability to guarantee no outages, a guarantee is still
  possible if you manage risk with conditions; an unconditional guarantee is tough, but
  a conditionally scoped one works.
- (2026-07-18) A scoped guarantee (e.g. "if we go down in service, you get that month
  free") is functionally reverse insurance for the provider — a controlled, deliberate
  risk the business chooses to take on.
- (2026-07-18) The math works: if you close twice as many customers and lose one month
  from roughly every third customer, "the math maths"; the more aggressive the
  guarantee, the more business you win, given your risk tolerance.
- (2026-07-18) Guarantees can carry conditions the same way insurance does (e.g. client
  follows best practices) — the analogy: life insurance doesn't pay if you kill
  yourself; there are conditions for both the risk and the guarantee.
- (2026-07-18) To make the offer more valuable: (1) name the big risks, (2) guarantee
  around them responsibly, (3) maximize speed and reduce speed delays, and (4) improve
  ease and reduce sacrifice — the things the client has to do that they don't want to,
  and the things they get to do that they otherwise couldn't.
- (2026-07-18) The single most important perspective shift for this caller: he is not
  selling that nothing happened — he is selling a good outcome, guaranteeing around the
  bad responsibly, and improving every other component of the service.
