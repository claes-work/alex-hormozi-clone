# Why Am I Making No Profit?

## Summary
Alex Hormozi advises a caller (Zach Levine), owner of a New York integrated pain-management practice (chiropractic/PT) doing $4.2M revenue across three offices at roughly zero profit. The caller's out-of-network insurance model is dying as reimbursements decline and out-of-network benefits dwindle, so he wants to transition to a cash-pay model. Hormozi frames the choice as two distinct games — in-network (operational efficiency, no pricing power) versus cash-pay premium (marketing and sales) — and walks through the concrete acquisition playbook for the cash-pay path: local paid ads, a discounted front-end assessment to capture a credit card, and back-end cross-sell/upsell.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the strategic decision as two different businesses: going in-network makes the business "all about operational efficiency," returns on capital, and low cost because "you have no pricing power," while going cash-pay premium makes it all about learning to market and sell customers.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises treating a model switch as "starting a business over, but with resources already" — the service and packaging are solved; only acquisition is new.
- As of March 2026, Alex ranks the four acquisition channels for this local business as: paid ads (best bet, because local ads are operationally simple — "drop a pin, do a radius"), outreach, content (probably not best), and affiliates (rejected here because injury attorneys route to low-paying workers'-comp/network cases).
- As of March 2026, Alex predicts the business will find only one to three offers that convert profitably on the front end, with the remainder of revenue coming from cross-selling and upselling once customers walk in the door — rather than advertising all ~15 services.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends a highly discounted front-end assessment (e.g., a ~$200 X-ray offered for $19–20) whose real purpose is to get a credit card on file, not to profit — enabling no-show fees, a smoother back-end sale, and a 70–80% show rate.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises selling at "point of greatest need, not point of greatest satisfaction" — do the assessment, then run the sales appointment before treating, so the offer lands when need is highest.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends a speed-to-lead process: ad leads flow to a CRM (or a Google Sheet if low-tech) and the front desk calls within 60 seconds, calling again if they don't pick up.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises the caller to keep the existing $4.2M business running but direct all discretionary resources to "build a bridge to tomorrow," and to first remove the lowest-reimbursing patients (e.g., Medicare) to lower the cost structure and free up cash flow.
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses the caller as at the "optimized" stage (20–49 headcount), where the common problems are inability to hire higher-level talent due to low cash, spending to grow without growing, and having too many products with no real acquisition engine. His prescribed fixes: customer segmentation by cohort, an activation process, sales training, an ad-assembly process, and headhunting senior roles once cash flow increases.
- (Unverified caller claims) Zach Levine states his business sells pain-management services to New Yorkers, is brick-and-mortar and mostly out-of-network insurance, does $4.2M revenue across three offices, wants to reach $20M, and runs at essentially zero profit; he says friends who are in-network are "murdering it" but that in-network is a commoditized "race to the bottom" with heavy administrative burden.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You can either go in network, which is you have guaranteed customers, but you're getting in a lot of ways like lower quality customers ... or you can go cash pay."

> "If you want to be in network, then it's going to be a business all about operational efficiency. It's about returns on capital, and basically how efficiently you can staff up, and how low you can keep costs across the business because you have no pricing power."

> "Fundamentally, I think you just have to think about this as basically starting a business over, but with resources already."

> "You will run advertisements, and you will make offers to people, and then they will come in, and then they will take their credit card out, and they will buy stuff from you."

> "You're probably going to end up finding that you have one to three offers that actually convert, and then the remainder of your business will be upselling and cross-selling when they walk in the door."

> "You just want a credit card on file so that when they walk in the door ... you can charge a no-show fee if you want to. But typically, if you're getting even a low ticket amount, you'll be in the 70 to 80% show rate, so you won't waste practitioner time."

> "You sell at point of greatest need, not point of greatest satisfaction."

> "Because your time is valuable, you'll probably want to consider running some sort of highly discounted assessment on the front end. So that'd be like a $200 X-ray or something like that that they can get for 19 or 20. You don't care about that."

> "Once you have a cash business, it's all about efficiency of acquisition. You're trying to acquire customers as profitably as possible, and then ascend them to the highest LTV."

> "You're going to keep that business, but all of your discretionary resources are going to go towards this if you want to build a bridge to tomorrow."
