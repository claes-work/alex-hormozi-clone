# Unlock Your Potential: Maximize Instead of Optimize

## Summary

In this @TheSkoolSchool conversation, Alex Hormozi reprises his "maximize, don't optimize" theme and extends it into a defense of deliberate imbalance: he argues you lose more life optimizing than living, that greatness comes from being willing to be unbalanced and to sacrifice what others won't for an extended "season of no." He reframes insults like "you're unbalanced" or "you changed" as true statements you already chose, recounts telling Leila for the first three years of marriage that "the business comes before our marriage," and shares his decision-making frame — "which problems would we prefer?" — as a tool for regret minimization by weighing the downsides of the path unchosen, not just the upside.

## Key claims

- **Optimizing vs. maximizing:** people get obsessed with "optimal" (most bang for your buck), but maximizing is just getting the most bucks; over-optimizing costs you more life than it saves.
- **Deliberate imbalance is the price of outsized returns:** a balanced outcome forecloses an outsized return on one thing, so greatness requires being unbalanced for a stretch.
- **"Season of no":** he periodically enters extended stretches where he says no to almost everything; others call this unbalanced, which is the point.
- **Reframe insults as chosen truths:** "you're unbalanced," "you changed," etc., are meant as insults but are usually *true* — and they describe the choice you already made, so you can take them as compliments.
- **Balance-as-programming:** he was raised to prize "balance" (which really meant "be awesome at everything"), so "unbalanced" felt like an insult for years until he rejected it.
- **"Nothing great was ever achieved by people who tried to be great at all things."**
- **Business-before-marriage period (self-reported):** for the first three years, even while married, he told Leila the business came before the marriage; they later flipped it to "if we're good, the business will be fine."
- **Now-isn't-forever / periodize:** the thing you're doing now doesn't have to be forever; dedicate hard to one thing for a season, then pivot back — gains compound multiplicatively, not additively.
- **"Which problems would we prefer?"** decision frame: rather than comparing upsides, spell out the concrete problems each path would create, then choose the set of problems you'd rather have and are best equipped to handle.
- **Regret minimization:** most regret comes from the path unchosen because we imagine it only with its upside; remembering a path's downsides makes you glad you avoided them.
