# Take Responsibility

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera interviewer asks the questions; the entire substantive answer is Alex's). Prompted with "why does saying 'my fault' and taking accountability begin to set someone down the path of making more money?", Alex frames responsibility as the **foundation layer** of success. Using a construction metaphor — the hole, the poured concrete, the base of the whole building — he argues the base layer is the question "who is in power? who is source?" If anything but *you* sits in that base layer, everything built on top topples: it's "a foundation of sand," and "you can't build on top of blaming other people." His worked example: someone who says "I can't learn sales because I didn't get hugged enough as a kid" keeps sand as their foundation; the unlock is saying "it's my fault that I don't know it and because of that, it's within my power to change it" — so the leverage isn't the sales books or the offers, it's the ownership. He issues a standing challenge to blame: "give me one example of when you blamed something else and it made you better" — you can't find one, because it's fundamentally not true, and therefore "you cannot both desire to get better and also maintain blame. You have to pick." He notes ~90% of people never even reach that base layer (the girlfriend left, the boss sucked, the economy). Asked when this hit home, Alex gives an origin story: at 19, in an intro-to-psych class, he had to write an essay about depression *from someone else's perspective*. He wrote about his mother — an immigrant who was beaten as a kid in the (US) '50s for not speaking the language — and reframing her life ("maybe she's actually a massive success given where she started") shifted him. On the next trip home, a line that would normally start a fight instead drew "I understand you. I'm sorry you went through the stuff you went through," and she broke down crying; the dynamic changed from then on. He extends the lesson to his romantic life: realizing he'd blamed his mother-dynamic for failed relationships, he decided "I am giving her this power over my love life. That's gross," and took ownership — pushing the excuse ("commitment issues," "I've been hurt before") to its natural extreme ("You'll never have a relationship that will last. You're right. Now what?"). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
