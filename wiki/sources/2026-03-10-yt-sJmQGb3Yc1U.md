# Goals Aren't Finish Lines, They're Mile Markers

## Summary
Solo short on why goals are directional markers, not endpoints. Alex opens with a Haitian proverb — "behind mountains are more mountains" — to argue that hitting a goal (whether $10k, $100k, $1M, or $10M a month) never delivers a finish line: "the mist lifts when you get there and you realize there's another mountain top," and you simply move the target again. He rejects the common critique from "people at the bottom of the mountain" who say you weren't happy at the last peak so you won't be happy at the next one — that objection assumes you climbed to *reach* the summit, when in fact you did it *for the climb*, a distinction he says such people (viewing life "in their poverty lens") will never understand and shouldn't be expected to. He extends this into a broader point about not wasting energy decoding illogical people, citing advice from his father: "You're trying to apply logic to someone who's illogical, and that itself is illogical" — so if something doesn't make sense, ignore it, because those people disappear anyway. He frames the psychological spectrum with a maxim: "the lazy don't know how to start, the losers don't know how to finish, and the legends don't know how to stop," noting he says this as someone who *doesn't* believe in legacy or an afterlife — so his drive is tactical, not legacy-driven. His tactical loop: find what works, do more of what works, find what's preventing more of what works, solve that, and repeat. He closes with the thesis that in health, marriage, business, or any game worth playing, "you don't win by winning — you win by staying alive long enough that you outlast everyone else," before the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
