# Make Them Regret Doubting You — Alex Hormozi

## Summary

In this clip from @TheSkoolSchool, Alex Hormozi unpacks his maxim that "success is
the only revenge," tracing it to a high-school teacher (Mr. Given / "John") who
worked out with him and dismantled his teenage revenge fantasy. He argues that
revenge-driven ambition is a trap: chasing a goal to spite someone means they've
already won because they set the game you're playing. He illustrates this with his
central life story — spending years trying to out-earn his Iran-born father, only to
realize at ~28 that he'd "won at his game" without ever asking what game was his own.
A co-host contributes parallel material about childhood bullying and a UK club-
promoting career (not Alex — see Notes).

## Key claims

- Success is the *only* revenge, not the "best" revenge — every other form of revenge
  is petty and proves you were thinking about the person all day, which means they win
  by default. (Alex, attributing the framing to his teacher.)
- The only winning move is to "win so big" that others constantly compare themselves
  to you, until you forget they even exist and cast a shadow they can't be seen behind.
- Copycats stay behind because they copy the *what* without the *why* — when it breaks
  they can't fix it, because they didn't build it.
- Revenge-motivated goals let the other person "ventriloquize" you: you hijack your own
  direction to spite them and call it taking control of your life, when you're acting in
  pure reaction — they've won because they changed the game you're playing.
- Alex's long-running drive was to out-earn his father (first match him, then exceed
  him, then exceed his father's lifetime earnings); achieving it exposed that he'd been
  playing a game he never chose. Money-as-status was explicit in his upbringing.
- After ~5 years of little contact following Alex leaving, his father called to
  apologize ("I'm sorry... for everything"); Alex rebuffed the olive branch, and the
  father replied "we'll see how long your success lasts." (They are on good terms now.)
- Reframe: if the father who raised him made him into someone he likes, the father may
  have been "the perfect father" — which unsettles the whole revenge narrative.
- Traumatic/negative events happen "for us, not to us" — micro-level negatives, if
  doubled down on, can become macro-level wins unavailable to a mere micro-win.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "As you expand, they shrink into irrelevance. As you get louder, no one can hear
  them. You don't beat them — you cast a shadow so big no one can see them to begin
  with."
- "When people copy, they copy the wrong stuff, because they don't know why it worked
  to begin with. And when it breaks, they don't know how to fix it, because they didn't
  build it. So don't sweat it — copycats will always be behind."
- "It's not the best revenge — it's the only one. There's no other revenge, 'cause
  everything else is petty. Everything else does show that you were thinking about these
  people all day long, which means they win by default."
- "I'm allowing them to ventriloquize me through pain that they didn't even mean to
  give me."
- "In the beginning it was make as much as my father, then it was make more than my
  father, and then it was make more than my father had ever made in his entire life.
  And once I had achieved that... I was trying to beat him at his game."
- "It was only when I realized that I had won at his game that I realized I never even
  asked the question of, like, what game am I trying to win. And I don't know how many
  people are actually trying to win at a game that they didn't even set the rules up
  for."
- "I said, I didn't care about your opinion five years ago when I left, and I don't
  care now. And he was like, well, we'll see how long your success lasts."
- "He raised me — so doesn't that mean that he might have been the perfect father? And
  then that really messes with me."
- "The most traumatic events that happen in our life — they happen for us, not to us."
