# Why "Following Your Dreams" is Keeping You Poor

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that "follow your passion" is bad advice
that keeps people poorer than they should be, because passion follows competence
rather than preceding it. He lays out nine reasons the advice fails (backwards
cause/effect, too vague, liking ≠ skill, skips the hard work, false expectations,
ignores money realities, misses other life factors, no flexibility, self-focused)
and then flips them into a practical framework: start with curiosity, get good
first, build from your circle of competence, commit to deliberate practice, take
money seriously, fix your environment, let passions evolve, and connect the work
to serving others.

## Key claims

- "Follow your passion" gets cause and effect backwards — people usually become
  passionate about things *after* they get good at them, not before. Passion is a
  consequence, not a requirement.
- The advice is too vague; many people have no single clear passion. Preferences
  ("do you like pizza or tacos?") are hard to rank, but skills ("are you better at
  English or math?") are easier to A/B decide — skills give a clearer next-step.
- Liking something doesn't mean you're good at it or can monetize it. Understanding
  fitness is ~5% of running a fitness business; the passion (cooking, training) has
  almost nothing to do with monetizing the skill.
- Deliberate practice — required for competence, which is required to make money in
  any career — is, per the research, *not* an enjoyable process. It's tedious,
  repetitive iteration on feedback loops.
- Cites Warren Buffett's "circle of competence": focus on what you understand best,
  not what excites you; passion follows competence.
- Cites Bill Gates trying law, mathematics, and programming before finding his
  direction — exploring abilities/opportunities, not following a clear passion.
- Money realities: supply and demand punish popular passions (acting, singing,
  painting) with heavy competition. Money can't buy happiness but can help you avoid
  pain; happiness rises with money up to a point — and Alex's theory is that
  "spending money is a skill," so with better spending it can keep making you happier.
- The "martyrdom effect": people endure far more pain in service of others than for
  themselves (shock-machine allegory; he can take more pain for Leila). So he prefers
  "follow your purpose" over "follow your passion."
- How you approach work changes the work itself — illustrated by the Japanese
  floor-sweeper parable, the "do better" waiter, and the three stonecutters (same
  work = a job, a career, or a calling depending on perspective).
- Practical framework: (1) start with curiosity not passion, (2) embrace the learning
  process / get good first, (3) value existing abilities (circle of competence),
  (4) commit to deliberate practice — "burn the boats," (5) set realistic
  expectations (all work has overhead), (6) take money seriously / follow the money
  when roughly even, (7) prioritize environment, (8) let passions evolve, (9) connect
  work to serving others.
- The Gym Launch origin: "The mission of Gym Launch when I started was Alex not being
  broke." The larger mission (taking the industry from its knees to its feet) came
  later, after he'd lost everything for the second time.
