# Launch A Physical Product Brand The Right Way

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex walks through, largely off the cuff, how he would build a
physical-product (CPG) brand from scratch — using nose strips (which he wears to stop
snoring) as the worked example. He reasons from product-selection criteria (high margin,
recurring, no-willpower usage) through pricing, upsells, unit economics, a
personal-development branding angle, a custom-word manufacturing moat, and an influencer
distribution model, arriving at a hypothetical 3–4M EBITDA business worth 40–60M. Draws
on his direct CPG experience from Prestige Labs (supplements).

## Key claims (physical-product / CPG launch method)

- **Product-selection criteria** — pick products that (1) cost pennies to make and sell
  for ~100x cost (huge gross margin), (2) are recurring/consumable so people rebuy, and
  (3) require no willpower to use — tie the product to something people already do daily
  without having to remember. Nose strips beat supplements on the willpower axis because
  "most people sleep every day, most people don't work out every day." Same reasoning is
  why he finds beauty products more interesting than fitness products.
- **Auto-ship / subscription** — build recurring auto-ship into a consumable to make it
  convenient and lock in repeat purchase.
- **Differentiate via branding on the product itself** — print a motivational word
  (e.g. "persist," "endure," "patience") across the bridge of the nose strip; ideally a
  clear strip where only the word shows. Turns the wearer's face into a "walking
  billboard" and a daily subconscious reminder (he cites sales-room anecdote: a single
  word on the wall changed team performance). He'd have it spelled backwards so the
  wearer reads it in the mirror.
- **Pricing** — competitor strips run ~30 cents/strip; he'd sell 30/60/90-count packs,
  premium-positioned at ~19.99–24.99, split-testing price points.
- **Unit economics (worked example)** — COGS dominated by shipping (~$2.50–4 via 3PL),
  strips themselves minimal; ~$16 gross margin on a $20 product ("really good for
  physical products").
- **Upsell ladder** — five options (1/3/6/12 months) with cascading discounts up to
  ~15% off for buying 12 months.
- **Growth math to equilibrium** — 10k page-clicks/month → 5% conversion → 500 buyers/mo;
  at 10% monthly churn the base grows until signups equal cancellations at 5,000 users
  (500 ÷ 0.10). At $20 that's ~$100k/mo revenue, ~$80k/mo profit ≈ ~$1M/yr profit from
  the core product.
- **Brand > product (the "wedge")** — position the brand around personal development /
  "becoming the person you want to be," not around nose strips. The nose strip is the
  wedge ("we help people breathe better") but the promise is "we help people be better."
  Make it mass-market by showing different walks of life working on different words
  (moms → "patience," a released prisoner → "forgiveness"). "You want it to be about the
  prospect… the problem… the person they want to become and the promises we're
  fulfilling."
- **Custom-word manufacturing moat** — build capacity to print any custom word on demand.
  Offer 5 preset words free, or a custom word for a ~$30 premium (2–3x the base price).
  Users text in their word for the month; it auto-enters the system and ships. On-demand
  personalization is the competitive moat that justifies premium pricing.
- **Influencer distribution** — ship strips printed with influencers' community terms
  (e.g. "mozination"), give them ~40% margin to sell to their own audiences. With high
  gross margin he can afford this; 100 influencers each doing a tenth of his volume could
  10x total volume (~$10M), at ~20% net to him after their 40% and ~20% cost.
- **Outcome / valuation** — nets a ~3–4M EBITDA business that is recurring, not dependent
  on a single face, high-margin, with a moat and premium pricing headroom. A physical-
  products business at 4M EBITDA would be worth ~40–60M.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "No strip costs pennies to manufacture, you can sell them for a hundred times that price."
- "I'd rather associate a product with something that people are doing on a regular basis without willpower."
- "It's the reason beauty products to me are more interesting than fitness products by and large."
- "The problem with supplements is that people have to remember to take them."
- "The wedge product is the idea that we are helping people breathe better, but the brand and the promise is about we're helping people be better."
- "If you can help someone feel better about who they are, people will pay anything for that feeling."
- "You want it to be about the prospect and more specifically about the problem that we're solving and the person that they want to become and the promises that we're fulfilling."
- "Because I have such high gross margins I could say, hey man, I'll give you 40 [percent]."
- "It was mostly on the fly."
