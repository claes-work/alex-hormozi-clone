# How To Make BUCKETS of Money In SERVICE Businesses (2021-09-24)

## Summary

In this early (2021) short video, Hormozi lays out a taxonomy of what a business can
sell — products, services, and access — each in a physical or digital variant — and
argues that margins and scalability differ sharply across them. His central practical
tip: to raise cash fast, a service business should sell a small number of high-touch,
guaranteed "access" slots (one-on-one or small group) prepaid for a full period. He
frames access as his favorite category because of its near-100% margins, and warns
against liquidating all audience goodwill at once, advocating a controlled "burn" that
preserves pent-up demand for future offers.

## Key claims

- (2021) Hormozi says at the time of recording that acquisition.com does about
  $85 million a year in revenue. (self-reported)
- (2021) He lists businesses in his portfolio: a publishing (digital) business, a
  brick-and-mortar chain of photography studios, a certification business, an online
  business for trainers, Gym Launch (gym licensing), Prestige Labs (supplements), and
  ALAN (software helping brick-and-mortar agencies work leads). He notes he only names
  businesses he owns 100% of; for minority stakes in influencer/personality-driven
  brands he names only the category. (self-reported)
- (2021) He claims everything a business sells falls into three categories: products
  (widgets/stuff), services (people doing things for others), and access — and access
  is the one most people miss. (paraphrase)
- (2021) Each category has a physical and a digital version, giving six types, e.g.
  physical product = supplements in the mail; digital product = a Netflix subscription;
  physical service = a massage; digital service = a marketing agency; physical access =
  concert tickets; digital access = access to him via Slack or a virtual event.
  (paraphrase)
- (2021) He claims digital access and digital products have almost 100% margins, while
  digital services have lower margins. (paraphrase)
- (2021) He argues service businesses are hard to scale because scaling people means
  scaling culture, training, and onboarding, and things with hard costs are hard to
  scale — which is why buyers dislike acquiring service businesses. (paraphrase)
- (2021) He advises pairing categories (e.g. adding a digital component or an access
  component to a physical product/service) to differentiate, avoid being a commodity,
  and command "monopoly prices." (paraphrase)
- (2021) His fast-cash "hack": sell a more individualized, higher-touch version of your
  own existing solution as access — e.g. "I can only take five people for one-on-one
  (or small group) for one year for this outcome," backed by a guarantee that you keep
  working for free until they get it, and require full prepayment of the period.
  (paraphrase)
- (2021) He says an offer structured this way needs no discount, because the value comes
  from access plus service plus expertise. (paraphrase)
- (2021) He advises against selling out all demand at once: if you could sell 100, sell
  20; keep ~80% of demand pent up so future offers sell out again, and grow the demand
  pool over time (100 -> 200 in the base, 20 -> 40 sold). (paraphrase)
- (2021) He claims high-ticket access buyers are often the best, least-needy customers
  and produce great testimonials and outcomes. (paraphrase)

## Topic notes

- **Core service-business principle**: the fastest, highest-margin way for a service
  business to make cash is to package a small number of high-touch, guaranteed,
  fully-prepaid **access** slots on top of the existing service — access is
  near-100% margin, needs no discount, and attracts the best customers.
- **Products / services / access taxonomy** (x physical/digital = six types) is an early
  2021 mental model; connects to his later offer/value-stacking work. Candidate for a
  [[topics/business/pricing]] or offers topic page if promoted to L3.
- **Anti-commodity / monopoly pricing**: differentiate by pairing categories so you are
  evaluated as a standalone business and can charge premium prices.
- **Controlled-burn demand management**: deliberately underselling to preserve pent-up
  goodwill so future launches sell out — a scarcity/demand-pool principle relevant to
  [[topics/marketing]] and launch strategy.
- **Scaling constraint**: services scale poorly (people = culture/training/onboarding;
  hard costs), motivating the shift toward digital/access components — connects to his
  gym/agency roots and enterprise-value thinking.
