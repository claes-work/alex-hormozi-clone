# Helping a $35M Private Equity Firm Reach $50M

## Summary
In this short consulting clip, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a private equity firm holding 14 disparate portfolio companies (roughly $35M combined revenue) whose goal is a minimum $50M enterprise value. Hormozi diagnoses the firm as being in "PE no man's land" — more companies than most funds hold, but without a synergistic (vertical roll-up) strategy. He lays out two paths to $50M: concentrate on a few well-understood businesses and double down, or go vertical and roll up same-type companies to capture synergies. He ties the dilemma to the "commit to the niche" lesson from his Offers book (page 38).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the caller's situation as a classic private-equity "no man's land": having far more portfolio companies than a typical fund while never adopting a synergistic strategy to justify that volume.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues the caller can reach $50M enterprise value on either of two paths, so the deciding question is which day-to-day life the owner actually wants ("who do I want to be when I grow up").
- Path A (concentrated): with a ~5-person team, preside over roughly six of the better portfolio companies, inject capital, recruit higher-level talent, improve strategy, and grow them.
- Path B (vertical roll-up): acquire many same-type companies to capture synergies — centralize costs to add profit across all, and distribute best practices learned from each founder — then sell the group together (e.g., buy 10 companies for $1M each and sell all 10 for $50M).
- As of March 2026, Alex prescribes an 80/20 pass when the owner has no strong conviction: identify the 20% of companies driving 80% of enterprise value, and trim the rest by going passive, returning equity, or doing a deal to reclaim attention.
- As of March 2026, Alex maintains the caller's core problem is failure to commit to a niche — the lesson on page 38 of the Offers book.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims the bigger the goal, the fewer the paths to it: a trillion-dollar company likely requires new technology, but a $50M exit is achievable with almost any business (including brick-and-mortar chains and online) in any niche, no matter how small, just by scaling it to a decent size.
- Caller-reported (unverified): the firm requires/holds companies in the UK and Dubai, has ~$35M portfolio revenue across 14 companies, targets a minimum $50M enterprise value, does not own all outright, and the companies are dissimilar.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You're in a very classic kind of PE no man's land."

> "You have more companies than most funds would ever have, and you didn't pursue a synergistic strategy in order to accommodate volume."

> "First thing I would do is probably ask myself who do I want to be when I grow up, in terms of what you want the actual day-to-day to be, because you can hit 50 on either of those paths."

> "What's the 20% that's driving 80% of the enterprise value? And trim. Honestly, I would just go completely passive on those ones, or just return equity, or do some sort of deal so I can get my attention back, and then double down on the ones that you are good at."

> "If you have 10 HVAC companies started by 10 different founders, all of them are going to have a few things they do well. So you take the best practices from each of them individually, and then you implement them across all of them."

> "You get this cost savings off the bottom line by centralizing costs, and then you distribute out best practices. And so that is a typical kind of private equity play, which is a roll-up play, where you can buy 10 things for a million dollars, and sell all 10 together for 50 million."

> "There's a lot of ways up the mountain. The bigger the goal is, the fewer the ways up the mountain there are."

> "If you want to have a 50 million dollar exit, you can do that with just about any business by just getting it to a decent amount of size. And yes, that includes chains of brick and mortar, that includes online — you can absolutely do it in any niche, no matter how small it is."
