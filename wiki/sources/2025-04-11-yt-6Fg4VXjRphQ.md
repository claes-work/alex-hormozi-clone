# I Confronted a Stranger With a Failing Business...

## Summary

A live coaching teardown in which Alex diagnoses and re-architects the failing
business of Alexi Omar, a legal attorney serving artists in the Latin/Spanish music
industry. The business ran three units — services (profitable), education (roughly
break-even), and software (burning ~$150K/year) — off a single YouTube audience,
splitting one traffic source across three offers and losing ~$100K/year. Alex's
prescription: kill the software, treat education as a charity/lead-nurture layer,
and funnel all three traffic sources (YouTube, Instagram, community) into the single
most profitable vehicle — high-ticket legal services sold on the phone — while raising
prices dramatically and adding light qualification. Format is Alex's recurring
"stranger with a failing business" live-build.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The two most important concepts in business are **supply and demand** and **leverage**.
  If supply is low and demand is high, price should go up — the correct response to
  demand you can't fulfill is to raise price, not to spin up a cheaper second product.
- A business losing money over 12 months means the owner "could have done nothing and
  had zero" instead of working all year to end up worse off.
- Diagnosis of the root problem: one source of eyeballs (YouTube) was being split across
  three different monetization paths. Fix: create three traffic sources (YouTube,
  Instagram, community) and force all of them through the single most efficient
  monetization vehicle.
- "Don't break what already works." All sales already happen via Instagram DMs, so make
  every call-to-action "DM me on Instagram" rather than pushing to a website.
- Embed CTAs in **four places** on YouTube: (1) a ~30-second spoken CTA at the 30% mark
  of every video, (2) the description above the fold, (3) a pinned comment, (4) the
  channel profile.
- Sell the **outcome**, not the hour. Hourly billing makes the provider's incentives
  adverse to the client (incentivized to take as long as possible). Charge for the
  outcome instead.
- Qualify leads on **budget, authority, need, timing** (BANT). For this business,
  qualify primarily on income, not on vanity metrics like Spotify listeners.
- Raise prices: the service was averaging ~$1,500/ticket; Alex pushes to charge $5,800
  as a retainer (anchored: $25,000 flat, or $5,800 + 5% of the deal).
- A checkout-page product can typically be **tripled in price when sold on a phone call**
  — the $500 education offer becomes a ~$2–3K downsell for unqualified callers.
- Add a 5–7 minute **VSL (video sales letter)** before the call to explain process,
  show results/reviews, and set expectations.
- The "free consultation" is not a consultation — it's a sales call; be clear about that.
- DM **every new follower**: "Are you here for free content/entertainment, or do you
  want help?" Everyone who says "help" is a lead (~800 new followers/month = 800 leads).
- Target **80% gross margins minimum** on services; keep delivery cost under ~$1,100 on
  a $5,800 sale. Rule of thumb: revenue must be ~5x the cost of the delivering lawyer.
- Prefers Skool over Kajabi for community (more active users; free onboarding calls
  become setting calls to triage to services).
- The six big mistakes small business owners make: too many avatars, lack of focus
  (more than one business) / overexpansion, compensation (overpaying), underpricing /
  mispricing, and single-product (no front/back end).
- Speaking on stages generates more demand you can't fulfill when you're supply- not
  demand-constrained; only accept if paid enough to cover the hard cost (lost sales)
  and soft cost (focus/distraction) — e.g., a $36,000 speaking fee plus travel.
- On the sunk-cost software: he'd rather sell/give it to interested B2B firms at cost
  ("pay me $100–200K, I'll give you the whole thing") than keep funding development.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "The two most important concepts in business [are] supply and demand and leverage."
- "A long enough time horizon and you'll have no one if we don't make this business
  profitable."
- "The place that you don't intend people to go is where you make your money. And you
  don't tell people to go there. They just find you."
- "Why are we doing that? Because it already works. So I'm not trying to break it."
- "A lot of lawyers like to charge by the hour. The reason that we don't do that model
  is because it disincentivizes us... we're incentivized to take as long as humanly
  possible. So it means it costs you more and it takes longer. That sucks. So what
  we're going to do is we're just going to charge you for the outcome. Period."
- "Our job isn't to try and figure out who's going to make it. Our job is to enable
  the opportunity."
- "You can't help people if you're broke."
- "The way I see my YouTube [is] I have... each of them are basically like little mini
  ads that are always running."
- "We don't have to build anything. We just have to take what we already had, redirect
  it through a better monetization vehicle."
- "All the work that you spent doing over the last however many years was not wasted.
  You just didn't monetize it well."
