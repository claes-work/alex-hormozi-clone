# Missionaries vs Mercenaries: Who Makes More Money

## Summary

Alex contrasts people who work hard for a contest or short-term win with people who keep
working because they believe in the mission. He says missionaries make more over the long
haul because a mission bigger than personal reward keeps them pushing after personal
needs are met. He also reframes workload sustainability: the founder must transfer the
skill and input to other people rather than assume the founder must personally do four
people's work forever.

## Key claims

- Some Skool Games winners stop pushing because they were working to win rather than to
  help the community.
- Alex says the effort level used to win is how business should be done for an extended
  period.
- Missionaries can outlast mercenaries because their goal is bigger than their own
  personal reward bucket.
- Alex cites Bezos and Elon as examples of people driven by a mission bigger than wealth.
- Sustained business input should be viewed at the team level, not just the founder's own
  work ethic.
- After learning the skill, the founder must transfer it to others; sometimes multiple
  people are needed to replicate one person's work.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "That effort is how you do business for an extended period of time."

> "If you really believe in your mission then you don't stop."

> "Missionaries versus mercenaries make the most money over the long haul."
