# Helping a $1M/Month Bootcamp Get to $10M

## Summary
A live coaching-clinic video in which Alex Hormozi takes rapid-fire questions from three founders in a row. The first, Chris, runs a data-engineering bootcamp doing $1M/month and wants to reach $10M/month; he isn't sure whether his bottleneck is marketing, sales, or the sales process as he tries to lower CAC (his LTV:CAC is currently ~3.2). Alex zeroes in on ad-creative volume: Chris is producing 5-6 pieces of creative a week on roughly $200-250K/month of spend, far below the benchmark Alex cites (about 50 pieces/day at $500K/day spend, or a floor of 25/week at Chris's scale). Alex then walks through his own ad-production method in some detail — 50 hooks, 3-5 "meat" segments, and a settled call-to-action, recombined into ~150 ad variants per recording session — and describes how he mines competitor and cross-industry ads for reusable hooks instead of paying for any ad-intelligence subscription.

The second caller, Luke, runs an Australian holding company with three small acquired businesses (SaaS, early-stage education, a $3.5M food e-commerce brand) and wants to scale to ~30 businesses with a defined org chart. Alex reframes the question as a strategy problem, not an org-chart problem: the businesses are too small and under-resourced to be "deal-ready," most private-equity firms only write checks above $5M EBITDA for exactly this reason, and Luke's current path — buying many small, undercapitalized businesses and expecting exits — is a foundational flaw. Alex offers two paths: raise significant capital to buy real, scorecard-passing businesses, or pick one existing business as a platform and bolt on sub-brands/product lines to it. He grounds this in his own early acquisition.com history of doing many small deals in the first 24 months and calls it a costly lesson.

The third caller, Chris, sells prescription glasses through 10 brick-and-mortar locations (personally) but is starting a new, separate $0-revenue glasses brand aimed at task-specific eyewear (driving, office) and is deciding between a doctor/affiliate distribution model versus a direct-to-consumer, media-heavy model. Alex applies a framework he attributes to Leila: pick the business based on "which problem would you rather have," since both paths carry a solvable problem with a large payoff attached. He walks through the operating demands of each path (constant ad/CRO output and volatility risk for DTC vs. a slower-building but more sellable affiliate/referral network for the brick-and-mortar path) and recommends the affiliate/physician path given the caller's medical background and lack of enthusiasm for direct-response media. The video closes with a direct-to-camera pitch for the free "$100 million scaling roadmap" quiz at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-20. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ Chris runs a data-engineering bootcamp for current tech professionals doing $1M/month, targeting $10M/month; his LTV:CAC is about 3.2, ad spend is ~70% Meta / 20% YouTube / 10% TikTok, and he currently produces 5-6 pieces of ad creative per week on roughly $200-250K/month spend.
- (2026-07-20) Creative-volume benchmark: businesses spending around $500K/day in ad spend typically run about 50 pieces of creative a day; at Chris's spend level he should be producing a minimum of 25 pieces of creative per week.
- (2026-07-20) His ad-production method: write ~50 hooks (short pattern-interrupt openers), pair them with 3-5 "meat" segments (the core pitch/proof, e.g. market-growth stats, credibility claims, program length), and a single settled call-to-action tested once and then reused — combinatorially yielding roughly 150 ad variants from one recording session, so only 3-5 actual "media angles" need to be conceived per session.
- (2026-07-20) Hooks generalize across unrelated businesses — he has reused the same hook across four completely different businesses (B2B, B2C, tech, and service) and it still worked.
- (2026-07-20) He does not pay for any ad-intelligence/ad-spy subscription; instead he watches competitor and adjacent-market ads directly and writes down hooks that appear to be running heavily, on the assumption that persistent ad spend signals a winning hook.
- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ Luke runs an Australian holding company that buys majority stakes in businesses (three currently: a SaaS company, an early-stage education business, and a food e-commerce brand doing $3.5M); the firm has completed multiple prior exits and wants to grow to operating/contributing to ~30 businesses, and sees talent/org-chart design as its core constraint.
- (2026-07-20) Diagnosis of Luke's model: the underlying strategy, not the org chart, is the problem — the acquired businesses are too small and lack operating leverage, and most would fail a standard deal-readiness scorecard; this is why most private-equity institutions set a minimum check size tied to roughly $5M+ in EBITDA.
- (2026-07-20) Small businesses are often small because they lack strong entrepreneurial leadership; removing a mediocre founder from an acquired small business commonly leaves the acquirer having effectively "bought a job," not a scalable asset.
- (2026-07-20) Two viable paths from Luke's position: (1) raise substantial capital to buy legitimate, scorecard-passing businesses, or (2) pick one existing business as the core platform and use M&A skill to bolt on sub-brands/product lines to it, rather than running several disparate small businesses in parallel.
- (2026-07-20) Biographical: in the first 24 months of acquisition.com, Alex did many (garbled in transcript as "20 40") smaller deals, taking minority stakes in emerging entrepreneurs' businesses on the theory that his brand alone could add value; he now frames this as a costly early mistake tied to the "small business, weak entrepreneur" problem described above.
- (2026-07-20) Roll-up-style PE firms with a single-industry thesis (e.g. "we only buy plumbing companies") are a legitimate, more boring, ops-heavy alternative that requires less capital per deal and rewards founder entrepreneurialism more directly than a diversified minority-stake holding company model.
- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The second Chris runs a $0-revenue new eyewear brand focused on task-specific glasses (driving, office), separate from a 10-location brick-and-mortar optical business he already operates; he is a physician (his grandfather was an ophthalmologist) and is deciding between selling through doctor affiliates versus direct-to-consumer.
- (2026-07-20) Framework (attributed to Leila): when choosing between two viable business models, ask "which problem would you rather have," since every path has a hard, solvable problem attached, and successfully solving that specific problem is what earns the payoff — reframing hard periods as evidence you're "solving the priced-in problem" rather than failing.
- (2026-07-20) Comparison of the two glasses paths: the affiliate/doctor-distribution path centers on recruiting, training, motivating and quality-controlling affiliates; the DTC path is a high-volume, direct-response, media- and CRO-driven business (compared to Snow Teeth Whitening) requiring constant ad output and eventually an influencer/affiliate layer to become a brand rather than a pure ROAS-arbitrage business.
- (2026-07-20) Recommendation for the eyewear caller: the physician/affiliate-distribution path, both because of his personal fit (physician background, no enthusiasm for direct-response media work) and because it likely produces a more sellable business — DTC/media-heavy businesses are vulnerable to a single volatile month killing a sale in the run-up to an exit, while a referral network built over time is comparatively durable.
- (2026-07-20) Closing offer: a free "$100 million scaling roadmap" — described as roughly 200 hours of analysis of acquisition.com portfolio companies' growth stages and where they got stuck — delivered as a quiz at acquisition.com/roadmap covering product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, and finance, with an optional follow-on call and an in-person Vegas session for qualifying businesses.
