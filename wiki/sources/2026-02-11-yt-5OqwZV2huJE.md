# Don't Make This Mistake When You Record Content

## Summary
Alex Hormozi reflects on a mistake he believes he made recording content: because his material is prompt-driven rather than scripted, it drifts toward the people physically in the room. His camera crew (young men in their 20s-30s) became his de facto audience, since he talks to whoever is nodding and reacting in front of him, and their real-time reactions steer which topics he expands on. He argues this skewed his early content toward a "hardcore" tone — partly because he was covertly coaching his own team through the camera (e.g. telling them to try harder or leave an unsupportive partner) — which the podcast circuit then read as his brand and reinforced through the questions they asked him, creating a self-fulfilling loop. He says he now does live streams specifically to see the chat and diversify who he's actually addressing, and that if he could start over he'd engineer a broader in-room audience. He adds that he expects to do fewer red-pill/masculinity-adjacent podcasts (naming Logan Paul as an example) because that positioning isn't an accurate reflection of who he is.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) The person behind the camera becomes who he actually ends up talking to, not the notional audience — so the crew's demographics shape the content.
- (2026-02-11) His crew were 20-to-30-year-old men; had they been, e.g., five women aged 45+, his content would be different.
- (2026-02-11) A mistake he's probably made is talking too much to his own team, using examples relevant to them because he wants to help them and sees them nodding/taking notes.
- (2026-02-11) The crew is told to react and take notes because it gives him a live signal of what's interesting, which is how his content "moves" while he makes it.
- (2026-02-11) His content is not heavily scripted — it's "prompt driven."
- (2026-02-11) Crew reactions create reinforcing feedback: seeing them respond makes him decide a topic is good and talk more about it.
- (2026-02-11) He does live streams now to diversify the audience and read the chat in real time, giving broader context on who the real audience is versus the people in the room.
- (2026-02-11) His earlier content was more "hardcore" because he was effectively yelling at his team through the camera — telling them to try harder or break up with an unsupportive partner — without addressing them directly.
- (2026-02-11) The podcast circuit interpreted that hardcore tone as "what Hormozi is about" and asked matching questions (e.g. "what do you say to somebody who isn't trying hard?"), creating a self-fulfilling loop.
- (2026-02-11) He expects to do fewer podcasts centered on red-pill masculinity; he says he's "on the edge" of but not "in that world," and cites Logan Paul as an example of that kind of appearance.
- (2026-02-11) He plans to do more content "on the other side" because the red-pill positioning isn't an accurate reflection of who he is.
