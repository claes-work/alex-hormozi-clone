# Don't Ask These 2 Questions

## Summary
Short clip on decision-making. Alex argues there are two types of questions you should *not* ask other people for advice on: (1) questions that can be **solved with a spreadsheet** (i.e. math), and (2) questions that can be **solved with testing**. Roughly 30% of the questions he gets are of the first kind — e.g. "should I sell this product or that one?" — which is really just an LTV-to-CAC comparison: work out lifetime value and cost to acquire for each, allocate resources to the higher ratio. Anyone can do that math; no outside opinion is needed. For the second kind, the answer is to run a cheap test rather than debate it. His example: he didn't know what to title the *Leads* book, so he ran story-ad tests for a week or two pitting candidate titles/covers/sub-headlines against each other ("$100M Advertising" vs "$100M Marketing" vs "$100M Leads"), and "Leads" kept winning — even though the book is about advertising, people want *leads* (the hole, not the drill; the book is just the vehicle to getting leads). A second speaker (interviewer) adds a market-expansion hack in the same testing spirit: instead of spending $250k on studies and a build-out, take ~$1,000 and run your best-winning promotion across all candidate markets, measure lead cost in each, and open where lead cost is lowest. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
