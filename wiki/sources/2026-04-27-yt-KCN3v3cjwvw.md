# Helping a Med Spa Sell High-Ticket Treatments

## Summary

Alex coaches a med spa whose most popular services are the least profitable. He frames
the front-end versus upsell decision as a math problem: compare CAC, front-end gross
profit, upsell conversion, and upsell gross profit against direct marketing for the
higher-ticket service. In the example, he suspects the issue is not CAC but the size of
the high-ticket bundle and the sales process. He recommends starting with the ideal plan,
then using a menu close and financing to make it fit the buyer's budget.

## Key claims

- Med spa acquisition strategy should be solved with unit economics, not preference.
- Compare the front-end service's gross profit plus upsell expected value against the CAC
  of directly marketing the higher-margin service.
- In the example, Alex sees a roughly $202 CAC and a 40% upsell into about $1,300 gross
  profit as workable but improvable.
- He suspects the higher-ticket bundle could be expanded to $5,000-$6,000.
- Alex recommends offering the ideal transformation first, then removing items or using
  financing to fit budget.
- A menu close can frame each crossed-off treatment as accepting a worse aesthetic result.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I'd rather start at ideal."

> "Everything they cross off, they just look uglier and older."
