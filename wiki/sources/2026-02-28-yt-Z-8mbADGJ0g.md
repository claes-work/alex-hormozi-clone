# Why Businesses Get Stuck at $1-3M/Year (And How to Get Past It)

## Summary
Solo teaching clip. Alex diagnoses the hardest stretch in a company's life — the **$1-3M/year "swamp"** (his term at acquisition.com) — and explains why it's so painful with a **numbers argument**. Take a $1M business at 20% margins = $200k profit. Hiring a real **A-player** roughly costs 100% of that profit, so the founder faces an "impossible choice": sacrifice basically all profit to bring the person in, or work another ~6 hours a day to do that job themselves. Both paths work, both are painful/risky — the overwork path can push you to $3-4M (where 20% is now $800k and you can spend $200k on the hire and still live), while the hiring path risks picking wrong. Alex frames this as the essence of entrepreneurship: outsized returns come from taking outsized risk, and the founder's job is to make impossible choices between two relatively-bad scenarios (the obvious choices — call leads faster, follow up more, onboard faster — "don't even feel like choices"). He invokes Elon Musk's line that running a business is "staring out to the abyss and chewing glass": existential risk that may never resolve, plus getting funneled the worst problems no one else can or wants to solve, so the founder becomes "a filter for the worst things." Key mindset point: **it never stops being hard — the currency of the hardness just changes.** At each level there are new sacrifices you didn't know you'd have to make (not just long hours, but hard conversations, risk-taking, bets, legal issues, lawsuits — "new levels of glass"). That's why most entrepreneurs **don't quit, they fizzle**: they get content, stop seeking/striving, coast, and fade — or they refuse to "chew the new glass" (learn the new platform, e.g. blogs → TikTok/YouTube), citing a nine-figure founder still trying to "crack YouTube" in 2024 who needed to a decade earlier. Entering a new domain means transferring generalized knowledge, expecting near-certain immediate failure, getting "directionally correct" on the first shot, then doing more of what works. Ends promoting his free **10-stage roadmap from $0 to $100M+** (broken down by 8 business functions — constraint, symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate; tested across software, physical products, services, brick-and-mortar) at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, Alex calls the **$1-3M/year band "the swamp"** (acquisition.com's internal term) and considers it the hardest, most painful period for entrepreneurs — and argues there's a concrete numbers reason why.
- As of February 2026, the math of the swamp: a $1M business at 20% margins earns ~$200k profit; an A-player hire costs roughly 100% of that profit, forcing an "impossible choice" between (a) sacrificing nearly all profit to hire, or (b) personally working ~6 more hours/day to cover the role.
- As of February 2026, both paths work and both are risky: overworking can push you to $3-4M, where 20% margins = ~$800k, so a ~$200k hire is affordable with cash left over; the hiring path's downside is picking the wrong person.
- As of February 2026, entrepreneurs earn outsized returns because they take on outsized risk; the founder's job is making impossible choices between two relatively-bad scenarios — the obvious choices (call leads faster, follow up more, onboard faster) "don't even feel like choices."
- As of February 2026, Alex endorses Elon Musk's framing that running a business is "staring out to the abyss and chewing glass": constant existential risk plus being funneled the worst, hardest problems no one else can or wants to solve, making the founder a "filter for the worst things."
