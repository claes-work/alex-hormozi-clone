# "Can I Scale Without a Sales Team?"

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A (final question of the session). The questioner — name garbled in the
captions (rendered "Twin Bird") — runs an IT managed-services company (MSP) selling fully
managed IT to SMEs of 30–100 staff, at an average ticket of **$7,500/month**, doing
**$2M/yr** and targeting **$10M minimum by 2030**. His self-diagnosis: he never took sales
and marketing seriously — no sales department, an almost non-existent marketing function
(a new agency doing SEO is the first one that hasn't "sucked"), and he sits in too many
seats himself (closes every sale, plus finance and operations). Leads today come from word
of mouth, referrals and early SEO: roughly 10 first-time appointments since the agency
started, ~4 of them qualified, none closed yet (long sales cycles).

Alex runs his standard constraint drill — "there's always a rate limiter, not many rate
limiters" — and narrows the constraint under "demand" to **lead gen, specifically getting
more of it**. His verdict on the SEO: keep doing it; it's "a slow grow to snowball," and a
40% qualified rate on calls is good for that customer level. Quick tactic: add a pre-call
qualification form (e.g. employee count) so unqualified calls can be cancelled. Beyond
that, he lays out the channel choice from the **Core Four** (pointing him to the *Leads*
book): affiliate partners (people who already have your customers — "centers of
influence," an affiliate strategy on the supply side rather than the demand side), paid
ads, or content. For this business he leans **PPC over social** to start — less creative
required and warmer leads, at the cost of higher lead prices, which demands a more
dialed-in sales motion. Social's value is the opposite: "you sell in a vacuum" — nobody
wakes up wanting to buy IT services, so social *creates* demand while PPC *channels*
existing demand. With PPC (and, he says, for the home-services guys in the room),
**speed to lead matters more than anything**: double-dial and text within **60 seconds**
or lose to a competitor; on Meta you can get away with about **5 minutes**. On strategy
discipline: "There are many ways up the mountain. All of them will work. The thing is we
just have to pick one and then stick to it." The fix sequence he prescribes: **offer →
sales motion → traffic, in that order** — because the current offer is a warm-traffic
offer ("just buy our stuff"), which collapses on cold audiences; scaling means
transforming a warm acquisition process into a cold one. His counterintuitive image: build
a "**microwave** to turn your cold into hot" — you actually *add* steps and friction to
the sales process for cold traffic to create the heat. (He notes most of the room —
referral- or content-based businesses — has the same warm-motion-on-cold-traffic failure
mode.)

On whether to hire two salespeople now (advice from one of the event's "coaches" — Alex
interrupts to insist, deadpan, "We have no coaches here… Advisors"): no. He is "a big
advocate of the founder knowing how to sell" — "if you cannot collect money, you do not
have a business." He **never outsources acquisition** — traffic, conversion, delivery —
while happily outsourcing legal, accounting and insurance, because the things that touch
the customer are what make a business a business. The founder will always close better
than any hired salesperson — more conviction, more knowledge, and the ability to adapt on
the fly — and getting good at sales makes the marketing work better. Hiring rule (echoing
his earlier "two news" point in the session): don't pair a **new process with a new
person** — dial the process yourself first so it becomes an *old* process, then introduce
the new person; that removes the founder's time constraint and lets him consolidate the
other seats. Standard acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift outro (10 stages, quiz across
product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance; thank-you page
books a call, in-person Vegas session if they can help).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **(2026-07-17) There's always one rate limiter, not many.** Hormozi opens diagnosis by
  forcing the constraint to a single point: under "demand," is it lead gen or conversion?
  Here: lead gen.
- **(2026-07-17) SEO is a "slow grow to snowball" — keep it running.** A 40% qualified
  rate on inbound calls is good for a ~300-employee-ceiling customer profile; the agency
  is "doing it right."
- **(2026-07-17) Cheap fix for bad calls: pre-call qualification form.** Ask employee
  count (etc.) before the call; cancel calls that fail the filter. Framed as an "easy
  tactic" that just saves time.
- **(2026-07-17) Channel selection comes from the Core Four; he points the questioner to
  the *Leads* book** ("It solves this particular issue"). Options offered: affiliate
  partners, PPC, social content (e.g. LinkedIn).
- **(2026-07-17) PPC vs social trade-off:** PPC is easier to start (less creative, warmer
  leads) but lead costs are higher, so it requires a more dialed-in sales motion. Social
  "sells in a vacuum" — it *creates* demand for things nobody wakes up wanting (IT
  services), whereas PPC *channels* demand that already exists and you take your piece.
- **(2026-07-17) Speed to lead is the dominant variable in PPC** — reach the lead
  (double-dial + text) within **60 seconds** or lose to a competitor; **Meta allows about
  5 minutes**. Explicitly extended to home-services businesses.
- **(2026-07-17) "Many ways up the mountain — all of them work; pick one and stick to
  it."** Channel choice matters less than commitment to a single channel.
- **(2026-07-17) Centers of influence = affiliate strategy on the supply side.** Outbound
  to people who already own the customer base and cutting a deal works the same way as a
  demand-side affiliate play.
- **(2026-07-17) Fix order for scaling this business: offer → sales motion → traffic.**
  Stated as the explicit sequence ("That'll be the order").
- **(2026-07-17) Warm offers die on cold traffic; scaling = converting a warm acquisition
  process into a cold one.** A "just buy our stuff" offer only works on people who already
  know you or know a buyer. Most referral- and content-based businesses stall when scaling
  to cold because the motion is wrong for the traffic, not because the channel failed.
- **(2026-07-17) The "microwave" model (counterintuitive): add steps and friction for cold
  traffic.** You build a microwave to turn cold into hot — you end up *adding* steps to
  the sales process with cold traffic to make it warm before running the motion.
- **(2026-07-17) "If you cannot collect money, you do not have a business."** Core of his
  founder-must-sell advocacy.
- **(2026-07-17) He never outsources acquisition — traffic, conversion, delivery** — while
  happily outsourcing legal, accounting, insurance. The customer-touching functions are
  "what makes a business a business," so he keeps control of messaging, conversion events,
  and delivery.
- **(2026-07-17) "Knowing how to sell will never make you less money."** The founder also
  sells the team and recruits; getting better at sales makes the marketing work better.
- **(2026-07-17) The founder always closes better than a hired salesperson** — two massive
  advantages: more conviction and more knowledge — plus the ability to improvise while the
  process is still being figured out.
- **(2026-07-17) Hiring rule: "new process, old person" — never new process, new person.**
  Founder dials the sales process until it's an old (proven) process, then introduces the
  new person; only then does the founder's time constraint lift and seat consolidation
  begin.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours reviewing portfolio companies distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz
  that locates a business and prescribes actions per function; thank-you page books a
  call, with an in-person Vegas session if they can help.
