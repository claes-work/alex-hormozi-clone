# A Simple Way to Make Better Personal Decisions

★ **L3-candidate** — contains a named, concrete decision heuristic (the "Solomon paradox" / running conversation with your older self, plus a repeatable self-interrogation drill).

## Summary

Short clip in which Alex describes his personal decision-making method: the **Solomon paradox** — reasoning about your own life as if you were a wiser, older version of yourself. He personifies this as "old man Mosie" / "older me," an imagined future self he holds a running (and often self-mocking) internal conversation with. To work a problem, he adopts that older self's perspective, states his current situation, and then runs a self-interrogation drill: he keeps asking himself why he can't do more, meets each answer with "why is that limiting you?", and drills each excuse until it either dissolves ("that's not a real issue") or is confirmed as a genuine constraint. He illustrates the method with his own current constraint — needing four more C-level executives before the company can take on his next idea — and shows how he then reorganizes his calendar around that single constraint (near-zero recurring meetings; all malleable time poured into recruiting).

A second speaker (interviewer) prompts him with questions ("What was the last thing old man Mosie said to you?"), but all substantive content is Alex-attributed.

## Key claims

_All dated 2026-02-22, paraphrased unless quoted, Alex-attributed._

- The **Solomon paradox** is framed as holding a running conversation with an older, wiser version of yourself, and using that perspective to reason about your own decisions.
- Alex personifies this future self as "old man Mosie" / "older me"; most of these internal exchanges are him "getting mocked by older me" (self-deprecating humor is part of it).
- His self-coaching drill: adopt the older-self perspective, lay out the situation ("here's what's going on"), then repeatedly interrogate it — "why don't you do more?" → "I can't because of X" → "why is that limiting you?" — drilling each excuse until it either collapses as "not a real issue" or is confirmed as a real constraint. You "keep going" through the chain.
- He references walking himself through "the six M" as part of this process (caption unclear; framework name not fully specified in the transcript) and mentions having done an "L3 [growth] day" exercise together that trained this perspective. _[attribution: Alex; term garbled in captions]_
- His **current constraint** for the business: he needs four more C-level (C-suite) executives to do what he wants to do next. Until he has them, that limit governs what the company can take on.
- His calendar is deliberately the most **malleable** of anyone in the companies: it starts essentially empty, with almost no recurring meetings.
- The only recurring commitment is Mondays — and marketing, sales, and media have all been **combined into a single one-hour meeting**. The rest of his time is 100% malleable.
- Post-launch, once the launch cleared off his calendar, he reallocated all that freed time to the current constraint — recruiting calls, interviews, interview notes, and introductions to the next people who will be vetted by the team.
- The payoff of solving the constraint: once he has the four executives, the company gains capacity, and he can then go to "big Alex's big list of ideas," pick the best one, and have the capacity to actually push it through.
