# Building a brand, but it's on easy mode instead

## Summary

A ~11-minute pull from an 8-hour workshop with a room of business owners, where Alex Hormozi answers questions on organic content and branding. He argues the fortune is in marrying an audience (traffic) with a way to monetize (a product), that content should be pure "give" while ads carry the "ask," and offers his working definition of a brand as an association between things, measured by influence, directionality, and reach. He also walks through his content-idea sourcing buckets, his "framework for making frameworks," and his take on cancellation and controversy.

## Key claims

- Two worlds exist: businesses that make money but need traffic, and audiences with traffic but no way to monetize. Marrying them produces outsized returns (examples cited: Logan Paul + Prime, MrBeast + chocolate, Hormozi + Skool as the product for his distribution).
- Starting organic makes existing paid ads more profitable: people see the ad, check the organic, then take the next step, show up to booked calls, and close at a higher rate. Early organic acts as lead nurture; at scale it becomes acquisition.
- He starts every new platform/medium with an agency to "buy lessons and shrink timelines" — his first YouTube guy cost ~$5k/month, "did nothing" special, but kept him accountable to 3 videos/week. He says he still doesn't really know how to post a video on YouTube.
- Do not put CTAs in the content — they kill reach and feel weird. Put the ask in descriptions and link-in-bio. Content is 100% give; ads are the ask. This removes the need to balance a give:ask ratio and "protects the brand."
- The 3:1 give-to-ask ratio still applies, but at the feed level: three pieces of free content to one ad, not within a single piece.
- Bigger creators post less often because power laws exist: spending 10x more time on one video can yield 100x the views. Make quantity early to learn quality; platforms now reward quality heavily. (Attributes the "20% better ABD/CTR → 100x views" idea to MrBeast.)
- His intro framework, derived from rewatching the first 3 minutes of his top 35 videos: the three Ps — Promise, Proof, Path. Promise and proof answer "why should I stay / why believe you"; path sets and meets expectations (watch time). For educators, "proof" is the equivalent of the entertainer's thumbnail payoff (the "Vegas lights of why should I listen to you").
- Brand definition: a brand is an association between two things — something people know linked to something they don't. Branding is a word for teaching (the hat example: wear it every video, people associate it with you and your value).
- Three elements of a brand: (1) strength of influence — does seeing you/the logo change behavior; (2) directionality — toward or away; (3) reach — how many people hold the association. The hypothetical max: as many people as possible all moving toward you when they see your thing (Taylor Swift cited as near-max).
- Rejects "all publicity is good publicity" — "all publicity is publicity." It raises reach but not necessarily influence or direction.
- Because brands are built through association, he is deliberately careful about who he appears next to: he no longer speaks on stages he doesn't own and has become strict about podcasts, because the audience associates him with the other speakers.
- Align your objective with the platform's objective. Platforms optimize for three things: lots of people click, lots watch all the way through, ideally watch something else from you. Hacks (hashtags, etc.) die when the loop closes, so don't build on them.
- Long-form has only three formats: lists, steps, and stories — and you can intermix them. Think of a long as several shorts strung together along the "path."
- Volume: his team puts out ~350 pieces a week (~50/day) across platforms. Start with what you can commit to, then ramp. Key leverage is "double/triple/quintuple dips" from one source.
- His highest-leverage activity is writing tweets — they're the "life source" of everything. Ideas get tweeted in seconds; the team turns tweets into recordings, reels, Instagram captions, and spoken videos. This replaced an old habit from the Gym Launch days: a private email thread to himself logging lessons and failures.
- Four content-idea buckets: real-time (tweet/note dumps), recent past (scan your calendar/meetings), far past (beliefs that shifted), and manufactured (e.g. "I lived on $100 for a month" — closer to entertainment).
- Framework for making frameworks: do a thing repeatedly, want a template so you don't re-decide; drink in a ton of data, write down what each instance had, then delete everything that isn't common. Same method produced the "Five Horsemen of retention" at Gym Launch (interviewed ~20 gyms with <3% monthly churn; all were doing the same five things). Naming: same first letter or an acronym.
- On cancellation: he doesn't think you can actually be canceled unless every channel to your audience is removed and you stop making content (cites R. Kelly as canceled because he stopped; Kanye/"Ye" as still making content and keeping followers). "You become uncan[cellable] as long as you don't accept that you can be canceled."
- Controversy behaves like a trending topic — it drives reach/CTR but not necessarily the other two brand elements. He personally avoids controversies because he doesn't want the associations and "doesn't care enough."
- Agency vs. in-house hires: he did both. Started with agencies, then (after selling Gym Launch) brought Caleb in to manage the agencies and hire in-house replacements. Expect a period paying twice — agency and team overlap until your team beats theirs, then the agency goes away.
- Finding a good agency/creative director: talk to 10, you'll quickly see who has it together; use referrals; the best agencies don't need to run ads because they have more customers than they can handle.
- On going niche vs. wide: keep making content that serves your avatar. Roughly a 4:1 ratio of on-topic to wider content. Niche content gets fewer views but brings the customers who actually buy; wide content is further top-of-funnel.
