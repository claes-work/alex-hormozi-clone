# How to train sales people

## Summary

In this recorded mentoring exchange, Alex Hormozi coaches the owner of a local
lawn-fertilization/weed-control business (Indianapolis, ~90% residential homeowners,
tracking to $1.5–2M this year with a goal of $5M) whose stated constraint is sales and
marketing. The owner has one experienced rep who closes 40–45% but "doesn't follow
anything" and isn't good with change, and the owner wants to build a standardized,
repeatable sales motion before the spring peak season (March–May). Hormozi lays out his
method for extracting a script from a star rep by observation, memorizing it via a
word-blackout drill, drilling it through high-interruption role play, and enforcing
process adherence to reduce dependence on any single person (key-man risk). He argues
sales is a highly trainable skill, that the volume of training required is far higher
than most businesses assume, and that a team of "all eights" running a great process beats
a lone star with no process. He also urges the owner — even though he is "not a sales guy"
— to know the sales process himself, because leads generation, conversion, and delivery
are the core elements every owner should understand.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-13. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-13) The best salespeople are typically terrible at everything except selling; having an "all-star" rep who is bad at process is normal and to be expected.
- (2026-02-13) To extract what a star rep does, observe them dispassionately — like a court reporter — recording exactly what they say and do (word choice, order, where the voice rises, where they pause) with no emotion or judgment; the more you quantify it, the more you can drill and train it.
- (2026-02-13) A star rep who "doesn't follow a script" is in fact following one — he just doesn't know what it is; there are specific words said in a specific order that get people to buy.
- (2026-02-13) Observing ~10 of the star rep's conversations captures roughly 80% of his effective script.
- (2026-02-13) Most businesses drastically underestimate how much training is required to build a truly world-class sales team; the amount acquisition.com trains is often a "belief breaker" for people to comprehend.
- (2026-02-13) At acquisition.com the sales team trains and role-plays every single day, and each rep has a weekly one-on-one with their manager reviewing "game tape" of what they specifically need to work on.
- (2026-02-13) You train salespeople through role play "until your eyes bleed" because it is the fastest feedback loop available for training any skill.
- (2026-02-13) Script memorization method: put the script in front of the rep, they read it aloud, black out one word, read again, black out another word, read again — repeating until the whole page is blacked out; by then they've said the script as many times as it has words and know it cold ("breathe the script").
- (2026-02-13) Only after a rep can "breathe the script" do you move to the role-play component.
- (2026-02-13) The first time a rep runs the script in role play, tell them up front you'll interrupt them ~50–60 times before the end, and that this is okay.
- (2026-02-13) The worst way to do role play is to let someone deliver the whole script uninterrupted; the second worst is to give all feedback at the end — no one remembers it and it's too late.
- (2026-02-13) Feedback must be immediate: interrupt the moment they err ("Hey, stop. Try it like this. Do it again."). The reason immediate feedback works is the same mechanism behind superstition — you change something and instantly get feedback.
- (2026-02-13) "Locking it in": once a rep does something right, have them repeat it again and again to cement it, so every rep delivers the script the same way.
- (2026-02-13) Most sales teams have a loose script no one follows, and recruit by hiring a bunch, seeing who sticks, and cutting the rest.
- (2026-02-13) An exceptional sales organization can take almost anyone and make them a "savage"; it's only a question of how much effort you're willing to invest, because sales is so trainable as a skill.
- (2026-02-13) Hormozi always wants adherence above everything — "the process is above everyone," analogized to the New England Patriots; no one is better than the process.
- (2026-02-13) He positions adherence to reps by framing the script as hard-won: thousands of calls and hours went into it, so "you say the script because we know these words close people."
- (2026-02-13) A star salesperson tends to become a "cowboy"/primadonna; if he does whatever he wants, that is a cultural issue and becomes "a cancer long term."
- (2026-02-13) He'd rather have a team of "all eights" closing on a "10 out of 10 process" than one star with no process; the process-driven team always outperforms.
- (2026-02-13) When creating a script, eliminate as many words as humanly possible; star reps "get cute" and add too many words.
- (2026-02-13) When acquisition.com fixes an organization's sales process, they typically cut the script in half or more because of accumulated "superstition" — extra steps kept because they worked once — that makes scripts longer but not more effective.
- (2026-02-13) The ACQ closer sales handbook provides a model script with defined pieces — intro, discovery ("disco"), recaps, offer, and the rest — and the owner should model each piece.
- (2026-02-13) Sequence to build the system: observe the salesman → document his words and actions → use that as the basis for memorization → use role play to ensure execution.
- (2026-02-13) An owner not being a salesperson doesn't prevent them from documenting the sales process well — it may actually make them better at it.
- (2026-02-13) The core elements of every business are how leads are generated, how they are converted, and how the product is delivered; the owner should know how to do all of these.
- (2026-02-13) Key-man risk: if the owner doesn't know the sales process, the star rep can leave and start his own competing business, leaving the owner "screwed"; knowing the process is how the business sustains and thrives.
- (2026-02-13) On the owner's fear that a future rep won't replicate the star: the individual reps won't replicate him, but the business needs to operate — "the business is the model."
- (2026-02-13) The owner's takeaway, restated by Hormozi as the action item: "just develop a standardized sales motion."
