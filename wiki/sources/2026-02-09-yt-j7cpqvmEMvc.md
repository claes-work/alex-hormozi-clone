# How to Beat Competitors Bigger Than You

## Summary
In a @MoreMozi Q&A, a new event-company owner (two months in business) asks how to
win client trust when prospects keep comparing them to competitors with 15 years of
track record. Alex Hormozi answers with what he calls the "small dog frame": comparison
only bites in the vague, not in the specific, so a young business should reframe the
comparison away from company-vs-company and toward the actual person the client will
work with. Rather than arguing his tiny company is better than the incumbent's whole
company, the small player should concede the incumbent's longer history and deeper
track record, then point out that at the big firm the client is "just a number" who
gets handed to a low-ranking account rep who doesn't care — whereas the founder is more
motivated and will do everything to make the client's event succeed. Alex grounds it in
his own experience: when he entered the gym space he was the small dog, "not the 800 lb
gorilla," and every sales call was a comparison to someone else. Framed as founder-
motivation vs. a clock-in/clock-out salaried employee, he says he wins that bet 10 out
of 10.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-09. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-09) [guest-attributed] A questioner runs an event company established only two months ago, while competitors have been in the market 15 years, and asks how to get clients to trust him when they compare the two.
- (2026-02-09) Alex frames the answer as the "classic small dog frame" — his standard reframe for competing when you are the smaller, newer player.
- (2026-02-09) Alex was himself the small dog, not the "800 lb gorilla," when he entered the gym space; every sales call involved prospects comparing him to someone else ("Well, what about this person?").
- (2026-02-09) Core principle: "comparison only works in the vague, not in the specific" — a broad company-to-company comparison favors the incumbent, so you must make the comparison specific.
- (2026-02-09) The reframe: shift the comparison from "their company vs. my company" to "me vs. employee number 86 (or account rep number 6) at that company."
- (2026-02-09) Tactically, lean into the incumbent's advantage rather than deny it — concede they have been around longer and have more track record and experience.
- (2026-02-09) Then flip it: at the big competitor the client will "just be a number," and if a problem arises they get routed to a low-ranking account rep who doesn't really care.
- (2026-02-09) The real question to pose isn't "is my company better than their company," but whether the founder — a new business owner motivated to do everything to succeed — is better and more motivated to deliver an amazing event than the incumbent's hourly/salaried employee who clocks in, clocks out, and doesn't care.
- (2026-02-09) On that specific bet — motivated founder vs. indifferent salaried employee — Alex says he wins "10 times out of 10." This is the reframe he recommends when starting out.
