# I Make YouTube Videos. How Do I Get More Sponsors?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A travel-food YouTuber (~$4.3M revenue last year, ~70%+ profit margins, ~21-person media team based in Vietnam, 2 videos/week) frames his ceiling as two problems: a **key-man issue** (he's involved in most of production) and **under-monetized sponsorships** (not selling a sponsor on every video). Alex quickly reframes the sponsorship lever as small: maxing out sponsorship would add only ~$800k, roughly a **20% lift** — the creator is already ~80% monetized, so "that's not a huge lift." The real stated goal is getting the owner's time back so he's no longer the bottleneck. Alex reframes the business as a **talent business**: like Taylor Swift, at some point you get "tremendous operating leverage from you just being exceptional," and being in the videos is fine and part of the game (he notes he does essentially no pre-/post-production himself, he just shows up). The fix Alex prescribes is a **manpower / willingness-to-pay** move: headhunt a high-leverage YouTube strategist (he floats a ~$200k+/year person with experience building a bigger channel in the same travel niche), poached from another channel, which could pull 50%+ of the workload off the owner and buy back mental bandwidth — accepting slightly lower profit (e.g. 3.15 instead of 3.3) for a happier life. He warns that in Vietnam the owner likely defaults to cheap labor, but "some roles are worth paying more for." The creator reveals he's already hired a COO (big media-ops background) starting in a month. Alex layers on hiring/onboarding advice: don't "solve the same problem twice" (Leila's principle — give existing solutions time to work rather than starting a second parallel fix); the senior hire should be your **intellectual equal or superior** in the domain, and if they're not better than you at it, don't hire them; use the pre-start month as a de facto trial by engaging them now ("maniacs don't stop being maniacs when they switch jobs"), citing Zuckerberg dining with Sheryl Sandberg weekly for a year before she joined as COO — the more senior the person, the more "surface area" you want with them across different conditions before they start. He's mildly wary of the hire needing a full month off ("if you think that was stressful… you're going to die here") but fine with someone honoring prior obligations. Bottom line: it's a manpower issue, the new hire gives operational leverage, but **the only output that matters is quality**, and quality reverse-engineers to talent. Closes with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (ignored for claims).
