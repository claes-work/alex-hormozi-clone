# How I learned to SELL...[mindset training]

## Summary

An interview clip (reposted to Alex Hormozi's channel) in which two interviewers
question Alex about his drive, his work habits, and — for the final and longest
stretch — how he thinks about selling. Alex frames sales as fundamentally a transfer
of conviction via trust, argues top salespeople are largely born with a proclivity for
it, and lays out his questions-based, "childlike curiosity" approach to closing without
confrontation. He also touches on his original drive (insecurity, needing approval),
his working rhythm with wife Leila, and thinking in multi-year "seasons."

## Key claims (sales mindset)

- The number one predictor of good sales is **conviction**. Selling is one person who
  believes in something transferring that belief to a person who does not yet believe
  it, and **trust is the thing that transfers the conviction**. You need both trust
  and conviction. (paraphrase)
- Conviction is **not binary** — it's not "I believe / I don't believe," it's *to
  what extent* do I believe. Most salespeople don't have 100% conviction. (paraphrase)
- **Trust comes from expertise plus some level of rapport.** (paraphrase)
- Big sales-team gains come less from drills ("blocking and tackling") and more from
  **hearing testimonials** of the people they sold last week and how those lives have
  changed — it juices conviction. (paraphrase)
- Alex observed near-100% close rates on in-person "weigh-out days" (end of fitness
  challenges, clients crying and excited) by stacking sales appointments during them —
  proof was right in front of prospects. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- If you truly believe in the product you talk about it differently; it's easier to
  **trick yourself into the right tone by genuinely believing** than to train the tone.
  (paraphrase)
- You should help someone sell by **asking the right questions so they reach the
  conclusion on their own.** (paraphrase)
- Most sales conversations follow the same framework: why are they there, what's the
  problem, what have they done so far, where did they fail, why our product differs from
  what failed, ask permission to explain the product, explain it **only via the
  experiences they'll have (never features)** using analogies, then close. (paraphrase)
- The salesperson has the advantage because they've had the conversation a hundred
  times and the prospect has had it once — you should know how it's supposed to go.
  (paraphrase)
- People fear **confrontation**; you can sell with zero confrontation using
  **"childlike curiosity"** — meet objections with genuine interest ("huh, that's
  interesting, tell me more") instead of arguing, because in arguments no one wins.
  (paraphrase)
- Sales is like fighting: adrenaline/fight-or-flight makes you breathe fast; trained
  people slow their breathing down and stay curious under intensity. (paraphrase)
- Alex has a **very short "allowing people to fail at sales" cycle** — shorter than most
  — because he's never had a killer salesperson who didn't do pretty well in the first
  week; turning through fast leaves a team of killers. (paraphrase)
- Some people naturally, based on upbringing, have a higher proclivity for selling
  ("gift of gab and empathy"); big sales trainers (Belfort, Cardone, etc.) almost all
  share the origin story of being the best on the team before knowing why. (paraphrase)

## Biography facts (self-reported)

- His **wife (Leila) works in the business as a true 50/50 partner** and is the
  operator — builds infrastructure, recruiting, HR, culture; he says from a work-output
  standpoint she does more than he does. He "occasionally comes up with a good idea and
  sticks with it long enough to see it come true." (self-reported)
- He describes his workday (at the time): no kids, working roughly **5-ish AM to 4 PM**,
  gym for an hour or two mid-day, then back to work and out to dinner at night.
  (self-reported)
- His **original drive came from "crippling insecurity and needing approval"**; positive
  feedback conditioned him to keep going, and he now continues the actions without the
  original catalyst. Estimates he's "probably 30% better" on the insecurity than at the
  start. (self-reported)
- **Took the prior year largely off / didn't work much**, traveled (Cabo, Scottsdale,
  Sedona, Flagstaff); found it "pretty empty," concluding he loves working and won't
  judge himself for it. (self-reported)
- Thinks in **entrepreneurial "seasons" of roughly five-year chunks** — ~3 years to see
  something through, ~2 years to transition; says this is his fourth season.
  (self-reported)
- Learned/derived his sales understanding by **retroactively analyzing what he did
  differently** ("why is this different"), and built a lot of sales teams. (self-reported)
