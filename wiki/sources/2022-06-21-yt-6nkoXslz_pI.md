# Why I don't follow my feelings..

## Summary

An unusually philosophical, introspective clip in which Hormozi lays out his personal worldview on feelings, meaning, and belief. He argues feelings exist but are not useful for editing behavior, that there is no capital-M "Meaning" (a nihilism he frames as freeing), and that reframing beliefs as "assumptions" and accepting all emotions removes self-imposed suffering.

## Key claims

- Only observable stimulus→outcome→feedback loops are useful for driving action; internal feeling-states, while real, are not helpful for editing what activities you do (paraphrase).
- He rejects a single capital-M Meaning of life; because humans are "meaning-making machines," you can create and destroy little-m meaning as you choose — more difficult than "religion off the shelf" but more rewarding (paraphrase).
- Beliefs are just "assumptions"; renaming them that way detaches them from identity and ego, making them flexible and updatable when new evidence arrives (paraphrase).
- Dropping required meaning eliminates "should / must / need / have to," removing the buried expectations that weigh people down and letting him take business actions others would judge (paraphrase).
- All emotions are like weather — neither good nor bad; the labels create more suffering than the experiences. His core mental refrain is "and that's okay." He states he doesn't try to not be sad and doesn't identify as a sad person (paraphrase).

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "The feelings, although I recognize that they exist, they're not helpful in trying to edit what our activities are."
- "We have meaning-making machines in our brain and so we get to create and destroy meaning as we see fit."
- "Beliefs are just assumptions, but when you call them that they become less tied to your identity."
- "So many people make themselves miserable because they think that they shouldn't be who they are and they shouldn't feel how they feel."
- "The labels create more suffering than anything that we ever actually experience."
- "The biggest refrain that I have in my head for like my own mental illness is just like... and that's okay, period."
