## Summary

End-of-year "couples edition" Q&A in which Alex and Leila Hormozi answer audience
questions about their marriage and working relationship. They cover date-night habits,
balancing ambition against marriage time, how they resolve conflict, their
non-negotiables/agreements, and how respect (over love) anchors the relationship. Alex
recounts several early-relationship hardships and frames marriage priorities in
terms of ROI, seasons, and shifting expectations to match reality.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed only)

- No one works effectively 24 hours a day; the goal is **effective work**, not sitting
  at a computer "productively procrastinating." When your measurable output rate drops,
  quality drops too, so he "calls it" and stops. (self-reported working style)
- Frames marriage time as **ROI / minimum effective dose**: of ~16 waking hours, the
  first hour of "marriage time" yields far higher return than the last hour of work, so
  it's worth trading the last work hour for it. Index heavily on goals (they usually
  need more time than the marriage does), then dedicate rest/recharge time to the spouse.
- Big goals require an understanding partner; a relationship "should propel you faster
  to your goals," not distract from them — spouses should make it *easier*, not harder,
  to hit goals.
- A spouse who is highly ambitious comes with a **price tag**: they work more, have a
  less flexible schedule, and can't get away on a whim. It's unfair to hold "dual
  desires of things that conflict" — you must pick the one that matters more.
- Change your expectations to match reality; if you feel you're falling short of what a
  husband "should" do, either change the expectation or accept it as the new normal for
  the season.
- On respect vs. love: **respect is the bedrock, not love.** Feelings (including being
  in love) are fleeting; respect — admiring the person's character on paper — is more
  unchanging and makes it easier to be "proud to be married to this person" during the
  ordinary, unromantic majority of life.
- On conflict resolution (shared practice, Alex-stated): **they text each other when
  angry** and physically change environments (she to the living room, he to his office).
  Writing creates space, lets you reread, and decreases the emotionality of resolving.
- "Highest success marriages are people who have **low expectations and high
  commitment**" — commit even expecting the person won't change (or may get worse),
  provided core values hold. Low expectations lower the pressure in conversations and
  stop people catastrophizing small things (the "socks on the floor" example).
- **Daily habit:** the first ~6 hours of every day go to the single most important /
  highest-leverage thing; he spends a long time deciding what that one thing is. Says
  he's done this "since I started working — a decade plus."
- On whether he could have succeeded without Leila: **"could I do it on my own?
  Absolutely not."** Finding the right business partner is one of the hardest things;
  cites Charlie Munger & Warren Buffett as two partners who found each other. Notes on
  Acquisition.com applications that businesses are often held back because the
  partnership isn't right.
- On desire: "**desire comes from a place of lack**" — not always getting to see your
  spouse makes the time you do get more cherished. Paraphrases Esther Perel (*Mating in
  Captivity*, which Leila read and relayed to him): "you need to **create space to be
  missed**."

- **[Both, self-reported]** At about the **three-year point** both were miserable. They
  sat on a bench outside their house, each admitted they didn't like it, and **flipped
  the priority to marriage-first**. Alex joked they "3x'd" (tripled) their marriage that
  year after the change.
- **[Both]** They have had **only one real big argument** in their careers — over a
  business decision about their **software business**; they locked themselves in a room
  for ~2 days over the disagreement. *(The detailed account of that fight is
  **Leila-attributed / context** — see Notes.)*
- **[Both]** They go out to eat most nights (business dinners ~3 nights/week at five-star
  restaurants; also Jimmy John's, Chick-fil-A, Chipotle). A "date" is delineated by Alex
  dressing up (showering, deodorant, no hat) vs. a work dinner.
