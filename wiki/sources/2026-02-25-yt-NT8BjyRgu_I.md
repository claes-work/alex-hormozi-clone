# "'I'm a Video Game Coach. How Do I Scale?'"

## Summary

A short coaching-format clip in which an audience member — a video-game coach — describes his group-coaching business and Hormozi diagnoses a pricing/billing mismatch. Hormozi's core teaching: education is not inherently a recurring product, so billing structure must match how value is actually delivered over time. He argues one-time (education) value should be priced and captured upfront, with a smaller recurring backend for genuinely consumable elements (community, ongoing access, calls). The questioner's business figures are self-reported and unverified.

## Key claims

All claims dated 2026-02-25. Hormozi statements are paraphrase unless quoted below.

- Education alone is not a recurring product; community and services are the recurring products. Billing should be matched to how value is delivered. (Hormozi)
- Because most educational value is delivered upfront, he suggests raising the price or moving to an annual plan to properly capture the value of the information/education, then keeping a much smaller backend for community access and other ongoing services or calls. (Hormozi)
- A common mistake in the education/services world: assuming that because someone says yes today (when they don't yet know how to do something), they will keep paying that same amount three months later once they already know how to do it. (Hormozi)
- Framework — split the product into consumable elements vs. one-time elements. One-time things typically carry significantly more value; consumable things carry less per-unit value but recur month over month. (Hormozi)
- Skill acquisition is worth a lot exactly once — valuable today when you can't do something, worth nothing once you can. What remains valuable on a recurring basis are the consumable inputs. (Hormozi, via the sandwich analogy)
- Don't take a one-time-valuable skill and amortize it into a long monthly subscription (e.g. billing "$200/month for a year" for something worth learning once); bill what the customer wants/needs upfront, then bill appropriately (often lower) for their different, ongoing needs later. (Hormozi)
- Self-reported by the questioner (audience, unverified): video-game coach earning ~$45,000/month, ~57,000 YouTube subscribers, charging $30/month, with ~560 students via group coaching. (attribution: audience member, not Hormozi)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Hormozi (persona/voice data):

> "Education alone is not a recurring product. Community is a recurring product. Services are recurring products. And so we want to make sure that we're matching how we're billing to how we deliver value."

> "I think it's one of the biggest mistakes that people make in kind of the education services world is that they think that because someone would say yes today when they don't know how to do something that three months from now when they still — they now know how to do it — that they still want to pay that."

> "We have to break apart our product offering into what are things that are consumable and what are things that are one-time. And so the one-time things typically have significantly more value, and then you have the consumable elements that are typically less value but they recur month over month over month."

> "I am a reoccurring customer of the sandwich shop because it is consumable. I eat it and I still need another one tomorrow. Whereas if I learn how to make sandwiches for myself, then the skill of making the sandwich will no longer be required, but then I'll need a subscription to bread and meat and lettuce and whatever."

> "Bill what they want, what they need upfront, and then when they have different needs, which might be lower, you then bill appropriately to that."
