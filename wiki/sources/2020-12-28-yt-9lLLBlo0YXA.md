# Say THIS When Your Competition is CHEAPER

## Summary

In a sales-coaching clip, Hormozi role-plays how to answer a prospect who is leaving for
a cheaper competitor (e.g. a $200/month provider vs. his higher-ticket offer). He reframes
the objection from price to price-vs-value and expected outcome, teaches the "comparison
close," and argues a truly superior provider would never charge the same as a budget
option. He also dismisses the cheap competitor as not real competition because they lack a
way to acquire customers profitably.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2020-12-28)

- **Cheaper-competition rebuttal (core):** analogize to personal training — an $80/hour
  trainer vs. a kid down the street who does it for $10. The cheap one has to charge $10
  *because* his training is worse; "you get what you pay for." (paraphrase)
- **Price vs. value framing:** spending $200 and getting nothing is a worse investment
  than spending $1,000 and getting 10x back — so cheaper is not actually cheaper. Uses the
  stock analogy: Amazon costing more than Bank of America is irrelevant; "price is what you
  pay, value is what you get." The stock's price is irrelevant vs. what you get for it.
  (paraphrase)
- **"He'll be back":** a prospect who leaves for a rock-bottom competitor tends to return.
  (paraphrase)
- **The cheap provider isn't real competition:** at $200/month even a full year of
  retention is only ~$2,400 LTV, and they have no scalable way to acquire customers.
  (paraphrase)
- **The comparison close (named framework):** say "let's pretend price isn't an option —
  we're both priced exactly the same. Which would you rather do?" The prospect picks you;
  ask "why," then keep asking "anything else?" (prompting ROI, risk, track record) so the
  prospect sells *themselves* on your superiority. Then: "for that reason you can
  understand why we're not $200." (paraphrase)
- **Outcome + time reframe:** the real question isn't price but how likely you are to hit
  your goal and how valuable your time is — do you want to waste three more months on
  another "Mickey Mouse thing"? (paraphrase)
- If someone were truly the best in the industry, they wouldn't charge the same as a
  budget bootcamp client. (paraphrase)
