# What Is My Fitness Routine?

## Summary
Interview clip in which a host asks Alex to lay out his current fitness and diet routine (as of early March 2026). Alex frames his answer around **simplification after long experience**: he is "crossing 19 years of training" (elsewhere he rounds to ~20 years / "2 decades") and says he went ~16 years without missing more than a week of working out, though at the moment of recording he candidly admits he "hasn't worked out in like 3 or 4 weeks" — he goes "through seasons." His current program is **progressive-overload resistance training** (weights, machines, plate-loaded machines) for almost two decades, which he calls the number-one thing for longevity and maintaining bone mass. His **diet is deliberately minimal**: he eats **2 lb of meat a day to hit ~200 g of protein**, then eats **dessert in excess of his calories** to reach his goal — cutting dessert to lose weight, keeping it steady to maintain, and adding other calories (meat plus pasta or rice) to gain. He rattles off the many styles he cycled through in his "first decade" — kettlebell-only, barefoot, swim-sprint, HIIT, strongman, five years as a competitive powerlifter (now training/looking "bodybuilder-esque") — and the many diets (warrior diet, intermittent fasting, alternate-day fasting, keto, high-carb/low-fat/high-protein, zone). His thesis: a few fundamentals grounded in **thermodynamics** (calorie deficit → fat loss / catabolic; surplus → anabolic; protein and hormones build/maintain muscle; progressive overload builds muscle) do almost all the work, and everything else — cold plunge, infrared sauna, blue-light blocking, MRI scans, keto vs. vegan — is preference or marketing "magic." He warns that fitness/supplement clinics and blood-work "spit-test" gimmicks are also **businesses** with "games behind these things." He debunks "starvation mode" via the Minnesota Starvation Study (BMR drops at most ~30%), argues perceived metabolic slowdown with age is really an **activity (NEAT) decline**, not a metabolic one, and notes people badly mis-measure both intake and output. He closes on why he rarely talks about fitness anymore: he did it professionally, "wrote the plans that 4,000 gyms use," but his passion has waned after two decades because "bodies haven't changed" while business "changes all the time." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
