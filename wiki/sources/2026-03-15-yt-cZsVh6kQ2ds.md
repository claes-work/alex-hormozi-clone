# Helping a Junk Removal Company Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A junk-removal and demolition company selling to commercial property managers does ~$1M/year and wants $10M/year. The owner's question: which sales channel to focus on, and how much time/volume to invest before deciding whether to keep or abandon it. Alex first diagnoses the current state — the business runs almost entirely on **referrals** (nothing to "do more of" there except good work), while cold email, LinkedIn outreach, social-DM outreach, and BNI networking are only half-started. His core move is to **reject the premise** that you must find the one channel that "works": you will *make* one work, and any repeatable channel (social, LinkedIn, cold outreach) can be made to work, so the real selection criterion is **highest overlap with your existing skill set** (yours and the team's) — "why don't we just send more emails" rather than splitting effort. When the owner admits his own strongest skill is in-person **networking**, Alex reframes the whole plan around it: spend hours finding every local community event of ~20–50 people matching the avatar, and scale attendance from a few a week to **several a day**. He calls this the "risk-adjusted move" because it doubles down on a proven, already-profitable channel rather than betting on channels the business has never validated. He then explains the **sub-$3M "swamp"**: below roughly $3M/year the founder has to learn *everything* because the unit economics don't yet allow hiring people who are genuinely good — a $2M business at 30% margins nets ~$600k, and after reinvestment and living costs there may be ~$200k left, which you either bet entirely on one key hire or cover by working double-time (both fine, just different risk preferences). Scaling, he says, is repeated **pattern recognition**: second businesses grow faster than first ones because the founder now knows what good hires look like; and you must relearn that at every level and department (SDR → salesperson → sales manager → closer → closer manager → director → VP), failing repeatedly until it clicks — so "the further along a business you'll get, the more you will feel like you are assembling businesses than you are really building them." His prescription: do far more of the thing already working (networking) to get from ~$1M toward ~$3M cheaply and profitably, then reinvest that cash flow into learning one additional channel. Ends with the $100 scaling-roadmap promo.
