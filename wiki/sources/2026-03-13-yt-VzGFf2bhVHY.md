# How I Think About Making Content

## Summary
Interview clip (Alex is questioned by an unnamed host; only Alex's words are treated as voice/persona data). Alex lays out the **mental frames** that let him keep producing content through the discouraging early phase, when few people are watching. Frame one: **treat content as practice, not the game** — a podcast you record and post is "practice for future me getting better," so it's still "preseason" where the scores don't matter yet; this deliberately *lowers the stakes* of hitting publish. Frame two, the "equal-opposite" problem of not wanting to start because no one's watching, he solves by **gamifying the metrics**: "the more ways you track, the more ways you can win" — track ~100 stats and you only need one to tick up to feel progress. He reframes tiny numbers generously: an impression count is a room of real people ("100 impressions… if there was a room of 100 people I'd be stoked"; "13 views… that's a small room, that works"), and he tracks both **absolute and relative growth** (going from 10 to 15 followers is "only 5" *or* "up 50%"). As a self-described "Excel projection guy," he projects growth forward — compounding "13% a month" on one platform — predicts where they'll be in 6–12 months, "and we usually hit it," treating unexpected viral hits ("white swan events") as upside *padding* he never bakes into the projection. He grounds the gamification in his friend "Dr. Cash" (behavioral): successful people don't have more willpower, they "find other ways to reward themselves" — more skills = more ways to reward yourself = a longer time horizon you can sustain. He generalizes across domains: cardio done as "5×5 to get out of here," and Olympic-lifting coach Travis Mash having lifters log every set/rep/weight so they can always find an old record to beat and thus **PR (win) every workout**. Micro-wins inside the long game are what keep you playing. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames content as **practice rather than the game** — a recorded/posted podcast is "practice for future me getting better," still "preseason," which mentally decreases the stakes of publishing (he says this helped most in the early days).
- As of March 2026, the antidote to "no one's watching, what's the point" is **gamifying tracking**: "the more ways you track, the more ways you can win" — track ~100 stats and you only need one to go up to feel you made progress.
- As of March 2026, he reframes small metrics generously by looking at the **biggest available number** (e.g. impressions) and treating it as a room of real people — 100 impressions is a room of 100 people he'd be "stoked" to speak to; 13 views is "a small room" that still "works."
- As of March 2026, he tracks both **absolute and relative growth** — 10→15 followers is "only 5" *or* "up 50%," and the relative framing is the exciting one.
- As of March 2026, as an "Excel projection guy" he projects growth forward, compounding "13% a month" on one platform, predicts 6–12 months out, "and we usually hit it."
- As of March 2026, he projects **with no "white swan" (unexpected viral) events**; a surprise multi-million-view video is treated as upside *padding*, never assumed in the forecast.
- As of March 2026 (via his friend "Dr. Cash," behavioral), more successful people don't have more willpower — they **find more ways to reward themselves**; more skills = more ways to reward yourself = a longer sustainable time horizon.
- As of March 2026, he generalizes the principle across domains — cardio run as "5×5 to get out of here," and coach Travis Mash having lifters log every set/rep at every weight so they can always find an old record to beat and thus win (PR) every workout — building **micro-games inside the long game** to keep going.
