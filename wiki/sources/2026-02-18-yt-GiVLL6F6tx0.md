# What Has Value in the Age of AI?

## Summary

A live Q&A / workshop clip (Skool context — "we're talking school here") in which an
unnamed co-host relays a community member's question — *when information is free and
infinite via ChatGPT/AI, what actually has value?* (her example: making pretty ebooks /
graphics used to be a point of differentiation for women in their community, but now
anyone can generate graphics) — and Alex Hormozi answers. Only Alex's answer is persona
training data; the co-host contributes the setup question only.

Alex's core move is to reframe the premise: information has been effectively free for a
long time (the internet), so AI is not a new phenomenon in that respect — and the answer
is the same as it has always been. When information/execution is infinite, value migrates
to **selection and curation** (nobody has time to consume everything to find what's good),
to **compressing time and removing friction** (perennially what humans move toward), and —
distinctively for the AI era — to **authentic human realness and community**, because "in
a world where everything is fake" people will crave realness more, not less. He then turns
practical for community/course operators: separate **one-time value** (a course/webinar,
charged at relative value) from the **consumable value** (the fresh "inputs" of your system
that get re-consumed month over month), and price the consumable portion appropriately to
improve retention. Illustrated with the recurring "consumable input" of several communities:
Gym Launch delivered tested winning ads; a 3D-printing community delivered the week's
trending product; a wholesaler delivered vetted home deals; financial guys deliver stock
picks — the method is taught once, but the fresh inputs are consumed forever.

Ties into his broader AI thread (execution commoditizing → taste/strategy/authenticity
retain value). What is distinct here is the explicit mapping of *where* value goes as AI
commoditizes information — selection/curation + friction-removal + authentic community —
and the operator-level split between one-time vs. consumable value for pricing and retention.

## Key claims

_All claims dated 2026-02-18. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section._

- ★ **Where value goes as AI commoditizes information (his AI-era value thesis).** When
  information/production is infinite and free, value migrates to: (1) **selection and
  curation** — nobody has time to consume it all to figure out what's good; (2) **time
  compression and friction removal** — making things faster/easier is what humans always
  aggregate toward; and (3) **authentic human realness and community** — the thing AI
  cannot replace, which becomes *more* valuable, not less.
- **AI is not a new phenomenon here — information has been free for a long time.** The
  internet already made information "readily available," so the panic about how to put
  free information behind a paywall is not new. The answer to "what has value" is the
  same as it has always been.
- **Value is in curation, not the raw information** — recalls a favorite college
  professor's image of consulting as "sitting next to a river with a cup, taking a cup
  out, handing it to someone, and charging them for it." With infinite information, the
  value is in the selection.
- **You are almost always selling compressed time.** A "year of dates in 10 minutes" is
  time compression; people judge it as a good deal *for their time*. Things that make
  things faster/easier win; **removing friction from people's lives will always be
  something humans go toward.** AI has done a lot of that, but there is still value in
  *your specific way* of doing something.
- **AI replacing human tasks over time is uncontested** — the shrinking question is "what
  can humans do that AI can't?" But **in a world where everything is fake, people will
  crave realness and authenticity more than anything.** A community of real people going
  through real struggles and sharing them "will always be valuable and, if anything, more
  valuable in the future." (He notes his company is making a very big bet on exactly this.)
- ★ **One-time value vs. consumable value — the retention/pricing split (operator-level).**
  Whatever you teach has a **one-time component** (your method/way of thinking — a course
  or webinar, charged at whatever the relative value is to the person) and a **consumable
  component** that gets re-consumed month over month. **Community itself is consumable** —
  you don't stop wanting community next month. Separate the two, and price the consumable
  portion appropriately; the consumable value is typically *smaller* (based only on what's
  actually consumed), and getting that price right "will help a lot of people get better
  retention in their communities."
- ★ **The "consumable input" of a system drives retention.** After someone learns your
  method, ask: *what are the ongoing inputs of that system?* Fresh inputs that change each
  week/month are what get consumed. Examples: **Gym Launch** delivered tested winning **ads**
  (method taught once; new winning ads every cycle); a **3D-printing community** delivered
  the week's/month's **trending product**; a **wholesale real-estate** operator's team
  sourced **vetted home deals** (numbers penciled out; member just walks in and buys);
  **financial guys** deliver **stock picks**. The teaching is one-time; the inputs are the
  recurring, consumable value.
- **Loneliness / anti-authenticity tailwind.** "People have never been more lonely in the
  age of social media" — what was supposed to connect us has separated us — so the desire
  for "true human-ness" will only go up. Plenty still has value that AI hasn't replaced
  (friends, spouses, real relationships).
- **Prior differentiators are eroding (the questioner's premise, accepted).** Skills like
  making a pretty ebook or nice graphics that once gave an edge no longer do, because
  anyone can now generate graphics/PDFs with AI — which is *why* value shifts to curation,
  friction-removal, and authenticity.
