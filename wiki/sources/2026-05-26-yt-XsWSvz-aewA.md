# The New Way of Making Content In The Age of AI

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out a framework for building a personal brand in 2026 in a world where AI can duplicate content. He argues AI will not disrupt all creators equally: creators sit on a continuum from low-risk (entertainers, B2C educators) to high-risk (B2B), and AI will disrupt the low-risk end first because higher-stakes advice requires credibility and proof that AI cannot yet fake. His prescription is to compete on "proof" and "demonstration at scale" — engineering proof-generating loops into your existing business so you capture content while you deliver rather than manufacturing it.

## Key claims (Alex's AI-era content views)

- **AI will not equally disrupt all creators.** Creators sit on a risk continuum, and AI disrupts the lowest-risk content first, moving up toward higher-risk content over time.
- **The continuum runs from entertainers → B2C educators → B2C/prosumer (money) → B2B.** Risk is defined by what is asked of the consumer: an entertainer's product is self-contained (you laugh, value delivered), while higher tiers ask the consumer to take real action with real stakes.
- **Entertainment's objective is to be consumed;** education's objective is to change behavior. A "perfect retention" video (100% watch 100%) goes ultra-viral but leaves nothing after consumption.
- **The higher the stakes, the more the audience requires third-party, demonstrable proof** before acting on advice — money advice needs more proof than a makeup tutorial; business advice needs more still.
- **Credibility is why real practitioners beat AI/GPT content even when the words are identical** — people act on advice from someone who has done it in the real world (his example: Elon is the #1 business influencer because he's the richest man in the world).
- **AI will struggle to manufacture the proof required for B2B / high-risk content** until there are AIs that have built giant companies with no human involved; that keeps the high-risk end safer "in the short-to-medium term."
- **The defense is proof and demonstration at scale, in real time.** Stated accomplishments (exits, revenue, headcount) are third-party proof you can mention once; real-time demonstration is far harder to fake.
- **"Do epic, document it" is the content-creation method;** better is to mine real material you already generate — e.g., transcribe your virtual meetings and have AI surface the interesting moments so you tell real examples instead of making up stories.
- **Best of all: engineer proof-generating loops into your existing business motion** — a "self-licking ice cream cone" where selling generates marketing that generates customers (his hair-extension example: sweepstakes inside the product → call winners → install in-person → document).
- **His own strategy is "live and interactive"** — capturing content while delivering (Q&As, event floor questions, calls with his community) so it adds no extra time. "I'm usually not making content. We're just capturing it."
- **If you can't figure out how to engineer demonstration into your business, you won't make it** — worst case, do free audits/work for customers, document them, and give it away.
- **Two kinds of proof:** third-party (things that have happened — exits, revenue, headcount) and demonstration (seeing the person use their expertise in real life); demonstration skews toward services but products can be physically demonstrated.
- **Predicts AI taking sales calls is "probably a year or two away," maybe by end of year** — so the move today is to "win with what you got."
