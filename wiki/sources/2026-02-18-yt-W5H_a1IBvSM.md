# Advice for Hard Days...

## Summary

A short clip in which Alex Hormozi shares the self-talk and borrowed quotes he
leans on to get through genuinely hard days — the kind where he asks himself why
he even bothers. His core moves: reframe the moment of wanting to quit as exactly
the point where most people stop (and therefore why they don't win), and treat
the fact that you're still alive as proof that you can withstand whatever you're
going through. He cites a line from his editor and closest friend Trevor, and a
Morpheus quote from *The Matrix*, to make the same point: having survived life up
to this moment is all the evidence you need that you can keep going. A brief
interviewer interjection appears near the end, after which Alex pivots to a promo
for his free acquisition.com scaling roadmap.

## Key claims (2026-02-18, paraphrased)

- When things get really hard and he wonders why he keeps going, he reminds himself
  that this is the exact point where most people stop — and that is why they don't
  win. (paraphrase)
- He resolves the moment by asking whether he wants to win more than he wants to
  fail; the answer is yes, so he continues. (paraphrase)
- He credits his editor and closest friend Trevor with the reframe: if you say you
  can't stand it anymore, being alive is proof you can stand it — and if you die,
  you won't have to stand it anymore, so you can always stand it. (paraphrase)
- Going through something hard doesn't stop being real just because you reframe it:
  you may be uncomfortable, in pain, and hate it — but you're still here. (paraphrase)
- He links this to a Morpheus line from *The Matrix* about standing unafraid because
  of the path that lies behind, not ahead. (paraphrase)
- ★ The "gift" everyone already has is that they have withstood all of life up to
  this point, and that track record is itself all the proof needed that you can keep
  standing. (paraphrase) — resilience-as-evidence framing built on your own survival record
- He tweeted the "this is where most people stop" line during a writing process and
  it got a lot of engagement. (paraphrase)
