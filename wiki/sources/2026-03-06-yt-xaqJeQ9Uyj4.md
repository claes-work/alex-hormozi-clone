# Why I Started Making Content

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host asks the questions; only Alex's turns are used as voice data). Alex recounts, self-reported, why and when he began creating content. He frames himself as **not a "traditional creator"** — he had (and still has) businesses that aren't built on his face — but says he saw "the monster amount of impressions that social media was getting and giving away for free" and found it "the most insane thing I've ever seen," a view he still holds: that a business can earn a hundred million to a billion impressions a quarter for free. On the dated origin, he says he **started making YouTube videos and wrote a book at basically the same time**, and each — the book and the videos — then took on its own growth path. He offers a taxonomy of creators: "words guys," "video guys," and audio as the crossover, placing himself as "a little bit of both." The through-line of his content and business is that he is "all about business process and system": people followed him because the systems were good, and because the systems were good they didn't need him personally for the systems to work — a deliberately more duplicable draw than the typical creator's. He ties content directly to his firm's thesis: acquisition.com's entire premise was **"something old (private equity) with something new (social media)"** and how to merge the two — entrepreneurs realizing the amount of attention available and using content as a way to advertise the business. He cites Warren Buffett (legacy-media influencer), Ray Dalio, and Naval as figures who get deal flow because they are "out there" talking about business, and says he "just took what they were doing and did a lot more of" it. He contrasts the old Casey-Neistat conception of a creator (make content → brand deals/ads support you) with today's creators "becoming entrepreneurs" who use content as how they market themselves. He also notes that three years earlier his acquisition model was running the **Gym Launch playbook** — take a digital-media-driven, vertically-integrated-education business in a specific sector, wrap in business services, package it on the strength of revenue retention, and sell it — and that transitioning a face-driven business off its face is a hard operational challenge (his analogy: "Taylor Swift isn't looking for somebody to step in and sing for her"). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
