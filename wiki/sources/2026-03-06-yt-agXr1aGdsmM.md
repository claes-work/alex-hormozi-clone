# Seasons of Learning vs. Earning

## Summary
Solo monologue on career strategy for young people: **life has seasons of earning and seasons of learning, and most people trade to earning too soon.** Alex opens with a story about an 18-year-old who reached out wanting to edit videos for him but demanded $100,000/year as a starting salary because another company had offered it. Alex calls the reasoning "logical and also stupid": optimizing for earning potential in your 20s "misses the point." That editor could have joined a globally recognized media brand, learned to be far better than he was, and become a creative director at another company or one of Alex's portfolio companies — instead he'd earn six figures as the only person in a department where no one knew anything about media, so he would *earn* but not *learn*. Ten years later, "I made social media content for a real estate firm" opens no doors, whereas "I worked for one of the biggest media companies out there" gets you every job offer — **and that is the point at which you have earned the right to choose earning over learning.** People make the trade too early because they compare themselves to the 0.0001% they see on social media and mistake it for the norm. Choosing to earn over learn is "right hooking life" — declaring you're done developing and that "this is good enough" (fine if $100k truly meets your long-term goals, but he doubts it usually does; the number is just a placeholder). Alex grounds the ask in his own story: he left a white-collar defense-contracting consulting job with top-secret clearance (space, cyber, intelligence) and a clear two-to-three-year path to an Ivy League MBA to work for **$13/hour at a gym** after graduating magna cum laude in three years from Vanderbilt — so he's not asking anyone to do something he wasn't willing to do, arguably a more extreme version. He closes on **passion as a language error**: young people are paralyzed saying "I can't figure out what I want" and wanting to *find* their passion, when passion is something you *build, create, or learn* — "if you're good at something, you'll like doing it, and the only way you get good at something is by doing things that you suck at, and doing a lot of it." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap scaling-roadmap promo.
