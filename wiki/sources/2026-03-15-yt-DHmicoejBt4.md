# Helping a Tax Advisory Firm Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a UAE-based tax-advisory firm selling to rich investors and entrepreneurs who want to avoid taxes: ~$6M revenue, ~$4M net profit (tax-free), and wants to reach $24M. He personally does 68% of revenue despite having seven agents (two are young, ~21 and 23, doing a few hundred thousand each). Alex runs his standard constraint-elimination pass and quickly rules out every bottleneck except one: the funnel/metrics are fine, margins are fine, the market is fine, talent quality is fine — **the constraint is manpower / recruiting**, and the client admits he is doing "nothing" to acquire agents. Alex's math: seven people ≈ $2M (subtract the owner's own $4M), so ~$300k/rep; to hit $24M you need ~80 reps, i.e. ~73 more than today. A side-lesson on the office lease the client is over-negotiating: at $500k/month in personal billings, the difference between a good and mediocre lease is a rounding error ("two and a half days of work") — don't burn a month nickel-and-diming a $50k saving; take ~10% of profit, reinvest, and grow. The core diagnosis is an **identity shift**: from "sales god" to "sales-training god" / "sales-recruiting god." The client's ego and lower personal risk keep him billing rather than building; Alex argues no one will remember what he personally made — they'll remember whether he built an infrastructure of 80–100 reps doing $30M/year. He pushes the client to codify what makes him win (Michael Jordan: great player, not automatically a great coach — winners often don't know why they win), script every customer interaction, document the sale step-by-step into a repeatable "black box," and use the recruiting-machine playbook of ACQ president Sharod (ex-president of Real Brokerage) who scaled by doing nothing but recruiting agents into a known input→output system. Alex references the four empty desks the client already has as immediate ~50% growth he should fill "tomorrow." Long-term vision: build a brand big enough that already-good agents (doing $1–3M) are attracted to it, fed leads they'll close — "take heroes and make them superheroes" instead of taking people from zero to hero. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses scaling by elimination — checking funnel/metrics, margins, market, and talent quality in turn — and here concludes the sole binding constraint is manpower/recruiting, since the owner is doing essentially nothing to acquire agents.
- As of March 2026, his rough capacity math: ~7 reps produce ~$2M (excluding the owner's own ~$4M), so ~$300k per rep; reaching $24M requires ~80 reps, i.e. ~73 more than the firm currently has.
- As of March 2026, over-negotiating a fixed cost is a mistake when your time is worth far more: for an owner billing ~$500k/month, the good-vs-mediocre lease gap is "two and a half days of work," so spending an extra month to save ~$50k is not worth it.
- As of March 2026, he advises taking ~10% of profit and reinvesting it to grow rather than optimizing to preserve profit (e.g. making $3.6M instead of $4M but unlocking scaling).
- As of March 2026, the founder-level unlock is an identity shift from "sales god" to "sales-training god" / "sales-recruiting god" — success stops being how good you are at closing and becomes how good you can make anyone at closing.
- As of March 2026, winners often don't know why they win (Michael Jordan was a far better player than coach), so you must codify your winning behaviors into an explicit "big laundry list" that someone else can execute.
- As of March 2026, everything a customer might say should be scripted, and the whole sale should be documented step-by-step into a repeatable "game plan"/"black box" — right now the process only lives in the owner's head.
- As of March 2026, the recruiting-machine model (per ACQ president Sharod, ex-president of Real Brokerage): once you know the input→output (a rep in your system sells X per year), scaling is just "how many people can we put through this black box."
- As of March 2026, he reframes ego: no one will care what the owner personally earned; they'll care that he built an infrastructure of 80–100 reps doing ~$30M/year, next to which his personal four deals "don't matter."
- As of March 2026, he estimates hard-locking ~25% of the owner's time (≈$1M/year of forgone personal billings) into reinvesting in the team yields disproportionate returns — more than $1M back by getting each other rep one extra deal a year.
- As of March 2026, he judges $24M in ~24 months achievable given the identity shift, and stages it: grow from 7 to ~20–30 reps (~$12–15M) while the owner keeps billing $1M+/month, reinvesting a portion of profit into consistently reinforcing the brand.
- As of March 2026, the end-state business attracts already-good agents (doing $1–3M) to the brand because it's big enough to feed them leads they'll close — "take heroes and make them superheroes" rather than taking people from zero to hero — with a big brand reinforcing high status so people would rather get mentorship from you.
- As of March 2026, the owner keeping his own "chops" as the aspirational person still has value, especially in a status-driven space, but must be paired with the shift to building infrastructure.
