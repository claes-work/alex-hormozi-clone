# How to Set Up Career Paths

## Summary
Short clip (a brief interviewer aside aside, this is essentially solo Alex) on how to design career ladders that keep employees invested and reduce turnover. Alex frames career pathing as **two parallel columns: title/perks and money**, with progression happening in small, frequent increments up both. He works a worked example with a video editor: someone comes in as a *junior editor* at (made-up) $10/hour; after the first 30 days (or hitting a defined milestone) they move to $11; hitting the next milestone promotes *junior editor → editor* (and editors are relieved of a menial task junior editors do, like cleaning camera lenses); after ~100 videos and 6 months another pay bump; another 6 months and they become a *senior editor*. His core mechanic: stack many small rungs so each role has junior / standard / senior variants. He notes that with roughly **six such role-tiers you get about 12 career progressions**, and that mirroring the same pattern across a function (sales: junior setter → setter → senior setter → junior closer → closer → senior closer) multiplies the ladder. Alex cites real-world models he admires: **med spas and nail salons** that tier aestheticians (junior/standard/senior at levels 1, 2, 3), gating each step behind **in-house mini-certifications** that let staff perform more expensive, complex procedures and thereby raise their hourly billables — so employees "always felt like they were learning" and "making progress." His flagship anecdote: a nail-salon chain his team put an offer on, whose owner extended average tenure from an industry-standard ~6 months to ~4 years and took staff with no high-school diploma to ~$70k/year (high for her markets) by systematically teaching gels → fancy nails → embellishments. He closes that the same structure applies to "salespeople, editors, anything that's a repeated role," then the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex models career pathing as two parallel columns advanced together: **title/perks** and **money** (pay).
- As of March 2026, progression should happen in **many small, frequent increments** — small pay bumps plus title changes tied to milestones (e.g., first 30 days, a video/procedure count, tenure thresholds) rather than a few big jumps.
- As of March 2026, most repeated roles can be split into **junior / standard / senior** variants, and higher tiers should shed a menial duty the lower tier still does (his example: editors no longer clean camera lenses).
- As of March 2026, roughly **six tiered role-steps yield about 12 career progressions**, and layering the pattern across a function (junior setter → setter → senior setter → junior closer → closer → senior closer) multiplies the ladder further.
- As of March 2026, more rungs keep people "invested much longer" — the ladder is a **retention mechanism**, not just an org chart.
- As of March 2026, med spas and nail salons execute this well by tiering staff across levels 1/2/3 (junior/standard/senior each) and gating each rung behind **in-house mini-certifications** that unlock more expensive, complex procedures and higher hourly billables.
- As of March 2026, the felt experience matters: because staff were continually certifying up, they "always felt like they were learning" and "making progress," which drove retention.
- As of March 2026 (anecdote), a nail-salon chain his team put an offer to acquire had lifted average tenure from an industry-standard ~6 months to ~4 years and paid staff with no high-school diploma ~$70k/year (high for her markets) via a systematic skill-teaching progression (gels → fancy nails → embellishments).
- As of March 2026, this structure generalizes to "salespeople, editors, anything that's a repeated role."
