# I Build Websites For Clients. Is AI Going to Put Me Out of Business?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a "website-as-a-service" (WAS) agency — builds websites and does digital marketing for small/medium businesses, ~$450/month average revenue per customer, ~$20M revenue, 100% of growth from outbound cold calling — asks whether AI will put them out of business. Their narrative: AI is "constantly degrading and decaying" the product because customers can increasingly build their own websites, plus a one-channel risk (cold calling is getting harder). Alex reframes the whole premise. His core move is his **"solving problems that don't exist"** rant: the owner has a story about AI killing the business, but "all I hear is that you have customers and your job just got way easier." If churn isn't actually moving in any meaningful way (it's only "slightly ticking up"), there is no real problem to solve. He argues the AI-savvy customer was never the buyer — "the person who is super into AI right now wasn't hiring [you] anyways… they built their own website before AI made it easy" — so plenty of laggards remain. On the two paths the owner poses (innovate the product vs. build an inbound engine), Alex says don't touch the product unless a business metric is way off; instead **double down on acquisition (paid ads / inbound)** to de-risk the single channel. He validates the unit economics — 29-month average lifetime is right in the sweet spot (he's seen 30–40, tops ~38 for this business type) — so it "all works out." Tactically he suggests trying to get customers to prepay for the quarter to offset CAC, but only for *new* customers, never changing billing on existing ones. The sharpest turn: the owner is worried about AI hurting them while not using AI themselves ("you're worried about them doing it, you're not even doing it"). With only $3.6M EBITDA (a "lowish" margin) on a people-heavy operation, Alex's actual fix is to spend ~6 months reorganizing the workflow, **cut headcount ~50% using AI workflows** to do the same work, roughly doubling margin from $3.6M to $7M+, then use that added cash flow to fund aggressive acquisition — going cash-negative for a quarter knowing 29 months of back-end revenue follows. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's reframe: the agency's "AI is decaying our business" narrative describes no actual problem — "you have customers and your job just got way easier"; without a business metric moving meaningfully (churn is only slightly up), there's nothing to fix.
- As of March 2026, the AI-savvy customer was never the buyer: someone "super into AI right now wasn't hiring [you] anyways… they built their own website before AI made it easy," so laggards who won't vibe-code remain the market.
- As of March 2026, Alex would not change/innovate the product absent a business metric that's "way off"; he'd double down on the **acquisition side** to reduce the single-channel (cold-calling) risk.
- As of March 2026, the business's unit economics are sound: ~29-month average customer lifetime is "right in the sweet spot" (he's seen 30–40, high ~38 for this business type), and a slightly higher price "all works out… same thing."
- As of March 2026, to build inbound Alex would run paid ads and try to get customers to **prepay for the quarter to offset CAC** — but change billing only for *new* customers; changing it for existing customers is what causes churn ("why would the people who are with you leave you for how you treat your new customers?").
- As of March 2026, doubling down on inbound raises CAC (media spend on top of sales commission), so it must be funded from cash flow; a business owner should be willing to go cash-negative for a quarter knowing ~29 months of revenue follows on the back end.
- As of March 2026, the deeper irony: the owner fears AI disrupting customers while not using AI internally — the people-heavy operation ran only $3.6M EBITDA ("lowish" margin).
- As of March 2026, Alex's actual fix: spend ~6 months reorganizing the workflow and **reduce headcount ~50% via AI workflows** to do the same work, lifting margin from $3.6M to ~$7M+, without changing the front-end price — then deploy the added cash flow into acquisition.
