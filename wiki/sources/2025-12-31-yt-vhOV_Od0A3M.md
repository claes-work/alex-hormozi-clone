# If you want 2026 to be the best year of your life, please watch this video

## Summary

A year-end compilation of Alex Hormozi's core mindset-for-success material, stitched together from prior podcast/interview segments (an off-camera interviewer occasionally prompts him with questions). The spine is his "how to get out of poverty" doctrine — a layered foundation of radical self-blame ("it's my fault"), using what you already have, and enduring near-term pain to get ahead — expanded into 18 tactical lessons on early success: work volume, high-volume sales apprenticeship, cutting friends who lower your odds, betting excess income on skills, decision speed, and paying down "ignorance debt." Alex is the dominant speaker throughout; the content is heavily autobiographical (gym years, first paid consult, six gyms by 26, selling Gym Launch).

## Key claims (Alex-attributed)

- **The foundation of getting out of poverty is two words: "it's my fault."** Roughly 90% of people can't get to this base layer; anything built on blame is "a foundation of sand." Whatever you cast blame to, you cast power to.
- **Layer 2: use what you have, not what you wish you had** — circumstances, connections, passion may be absent, so use whatever you do have (anger, shame, pain) as fuel. He credits sheer anger, not passion, for driving him out of the gym years.
- **Delayed pain, not delayed gratification, is the poor person's default** — they take pleasure now and pain later, then act surprised "when the bill comes due." "Today is yesterday's tomorrow."
- **The first $100K is the hardest and took him ~5 years**; you can't save your way out on minimum wage, so after spending less than you make, take the excess and spend it on making more (skills, experiments, bets).
- **Failed bets aren't a return to zero — they're experience points.** Enough experience points level you up. Losing is reframed as learning; "an expert is just somebody who's made all the mistakes you can make in a very narrow field."
- **Skills generalize across domains** (sales helps with dating, negotiation, etc.), and **skill development = volume × feedback between reps.** Posting/selling without reviewing your top and bottom performers wastes the volume.
- **Every self-made millionaire/billionaire friend of his started in a high-volume sales environment** (door-knocking, cold calling, in-person) for 3–5 years as an anonymous rep.
- **Cut friends who lower your probability of hitting your goal.** A supportive friend deliberately decreases your chance of failure; anyone who does the opposite is functionally an enemy. "There are no social obligations, only social consequences."
- **Money loves speed** — deciding fast then correcting a mistake beats deliberating; unmade decisions are the heaviest weight in life. You can move ~7x faster by shrinking your decision horizon from end-of-week to end-of-day to now.
- **Pay off "ignorance debt"** — the most expensive debt is the skills/knowledge you should have but don't; the price of inaction is usually underestimated.
- Advocates **normalizing a 6-day, 12-hour work week for men roughly 16–28**; young people have little utility but lots of energy, which they should trade for experience points. "Patient with your outputs, impatient with your inputs" — then wait ~36 months.
- **Name the voice** driving your inaction: he nearly didn't sell Gym Launch (for $46.2M) because he feared a respected colleague wouldn't be impressed. Ask whose judgment you're handing your life to.
- **"Get over yourself"** — everyone's childhood was hard; disadvantages, taken to the extreme, are the *better* story ("no one cares when the silver-spoon kid succeeds").
- Concrete "very broke" playbook: stop eating out (discount groceries), buy clothes at Goodwill, attend only free friend events, apply to higher-paying jobs / side hustles / higher-rate skills, and out-work the hardest worker in any room by 2x.
