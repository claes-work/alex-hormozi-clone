# The Difference Between Heroes and Villains

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex builds on a frame he credits to **Donald Miller**: heroes and villains share the same backstory — pain (the orphan, the disfigurement showing they "went through hard times"). What separates them is what the character *chooses to do* with that pain. "Villains say, 'The world hurt me. I'm going to hurt it back.' Heroes say, 'The world hurt me. I'm not going to let anyone else hurt this way.'" The formulation he lands on: **heroes use pain; villains are used by it.** Pain is a constant; the *reaction* to it dictates the path of a life, and everyone has the choice to become their own hero or their own villain. From there he moves into two practical "rules of the game" that have served him: (1) the phrase **"And that's okay"** — his most-used frame for untangling old stories about things that weren't his fault or that he treated as problems ("the easiest way I found to solve problems is to decide they're not problems to begin with"), e.g. not being close with a relative; and (2) **document your life when it sucks, not when it's great.** He says he keeps a handful of pictures from his first five years of "sucking" in business and uses them daily, while barely touching the "zillions" of pictures from after he became successful — because the struggle is the story you'll tell and the proof of who you really are. He quotes Morpheus from *The Matrix* ("I stand here truthfully unafraid… not because of the path that lies before me, but because of the path that lies behind me") and reframes the viewer's current struggle as the "path behind" their future self — the evidence that lets that future man or woman look themselves in the mirror unafraid. His advice to his younger self: document more, because "the details are what make it worth remembering" — the floor-sleeping, the missed seventh sale, the lowest bank balance, the car breaking down. Those ignored, look-away moments are exactly what to capture, because "no heroes are heroes without epic monsters, and the bigger the monster, the greater the hero." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
