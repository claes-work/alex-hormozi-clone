# Your Failures Can Make You Rich (if you write them down)

## Summary

Solo mindset talk in which Hormozi argues that systematically documenting your business mistakes AND successes — so you extract the *correct* lesson from each — is one of the largest drivers of compounding wealth. He frames entrepreneurship as a video game where documented lessons let you "skip past" early levels quickly and reach "Virgin Territory," and dates the start of his own compounding growth to the podcast he began 90 days after losing everything the second time. Recurring metaphors: the video game, the "woman in the red dress" (repeated mistakes in new disguises), and "time travel" (writing to future/past self).

## Key claims

- **The core method — "lessons and failures" email threads.** Every year he starts a new email thread to himself titled "lessons and failures [year]"; it grows to hundreds of one-to-two-phrase entries logging what he thought would happen vs. what actually happened ("open-looped stories"), which later crystallizes decision-making.
- **Documenting began after his second wipeout.** He started his podcast ~90 days (3 months) after losing everything the second time, explicitly to learn from mistakes and stop repeating them. He dates the *beginning of compounding wealth growth* to that point.
- **Learn the right lesson, or you repeat the mistake.** "The only thing worse than the mistake is learning the wrong lesson from the mistake." He cites a wisdom continuum: fool never learns → dumb person learns eventually → smart person learns one lesson → very smart person learns multiple lessons → wise man learns from others' mistakes → wisest also learns from successes.
- **Success needs auditing as much as failure.** Paraphrasing "Dr. Burgman": it's worse to be successful and not know why than to fail and know why. People attribute failure to outside circumstances but success to themselves — he took years to realize early Gym Launch success was largely cheap Facebook-ad arbitrage, not just his marketing skill.
- **Outcomes usually require multiple things at once.** Success is rarely one variable (the "spot" vs. the "arrow") — it's a combination (e.g., a sales director must be charismatic AND have systems AND care about people). Better operators have high "predictive power."
- **The "woman in the red dress"** = the recurring mistake that reappears in new forms ("now she's black, now she's Asian... sometimes it's maroon"), which he's had to learn from repeatedly; its inverse is over-committing to too many things (failing to say no).
- **Documenting publicly adds three benefits:** (1) it crystallizes the lesson for you (telling the story makes it stick — Elon Musk wrench/engine analogy), (2) it makes you accountable to the lessons you claimed, (3) it makes money because people identify with your values — treat that money as "gravy."
- **Content-idea fix: "talk about your news, not the news."** He tells creators who can't find content ideas to discuss the problems they're currently solving (optionally "in rear"/retrospect to avoid risking relationships), which doubles as documentation.
- **Two things he most attributes success to:** (1) maker/manager time-blocking (maximize the raw work unit), (2) crystallizing lessons (get the most out of that unit). Documenting is the "second most valuable thing" he's done in his career.
- **The Alien (software co.) lessons:** software is hard; don't get into a features war (competitors copy features — build a moat); and biggest of all, don't steamroll your operator — he overrode Leila's objection that they lacked bandwidth, and a business did suffer.
- **"Solomon sessions" / time travel frame:** he keeps a chat thread with an imagined 80-year-old version of himself; asks "if I were coaching/mentoring myself, what would I tell me?" — you always have full context on your own situation; most people lack execution, not information.
- **Success also must be studied.** Prestige Labs supplement launch ($1.7M recurring in month 1) was his cleanest because operator was on board, team trained, alpha/beta phases done, inventory ready — he logged the checklist to repeat it. Leila: "nothing feels like success" — remember how long/costly it was.
- **Meta-lesson: learn how long things take → learn how few you can do → say no to almost everything** (cites Steve Jobs: "what have you said no to lately?" — focus is everything you turn away from).
