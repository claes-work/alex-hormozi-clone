# If I Wanted To Become a Millionaire in 2026, This Is What I'd Do [FULL BLUEPRINT]

## Summary

A long-form solo blueprint in which Alex Hormozi walks step-by-step from "first
dollar" to first million across three levels. Level 1 (fundamentals) covers who you
are — knowledge, skills, motivation, environment — plus who to sell to (the three P's:
pain, passion, profession) and what to sell (unique, expensive, sticky, air). Level 2
covers getting customers to buy (Core Four + four lead getters, the CLOSER sales
framework) and buy more (eight ways to increase customer value, the 5-to-5-to-5 pricing
model), plus recruiting help (the management diamond, the three business pillars). Level
3 covers keeping the advantage (leverage), sticking with it (compounding, focus, the
woman in the red dress), and getting better (common factors analysis, one's relationship
with goals, infinite games). This is a comprehensive synthesis of his core business
catalog rather than new material.

## Key claims (the millionaire blueprint)

**Level 1 — Fundamentals**
- A millionaire = over $1M in liquid investable assets, **excluding your primary
  residence**. Alex claims **1 in 9 Americans** is a millionaire; what separates them is
  "what they know exists" — rich kids get told which opportunities exist.
- "Who you are" rests on four building blocks: **knowledge, skills, motivation,
  environment**.
- Traits (confident, charismatic, good on camera) aren't real — each is a **bundle of
  sub-skills**. Sales' sub-skills: active listening, repeating back, eye contact, firm
  handshake, projecting voice, reasoned argument.
- Skills stack and make each other retroactively more valuable: **sales → marketing
  (sales one-to-many) → acquisition** (sales + marketing). Learning to pack boxes takes
  as long as learning sales but has far lower returns — knowledge tells you where to aim
  effort for the highest return.
- **Motivation comes from deprivation** (you're motivated to become a millionaire
  because you lack a million). Boost money-motivation by surrounding yourself with (or
  building a reference group of) people who earn much more. At some point **high agency /
  self-belief** must outweigh what others say.
- **Environment is friction or lubricant on your goals** — change behavior by changing
  environment. Filter everything through "does this increase or decrease the likelihood
  I hit my goals?" and cut what decreases it. The **"season of no"**: say no to social
  gatherings, distractions, entertainment. Odd results require being odd.
- **Who to sell to:** it's easier to sell 100 people something for $10,000 than 10,000
  people something for $100 (both = $1M). Split over 4 years = ~$200/month; ~2 sales/week
  gets you 100 customers.
- **The three P's — pain, passion, profession.** Start with the smallest, most specific
  version of the market (Tesla Roadster → Model S → Model 3 analogy: start expensive/top
  of market, work down). When split between options, **serve whoever you can provide the
  most value to** (dollar value = proxy for value delivered).
- Filters for the "who": they must have the **problem**, the **money**, the **urgency**
  to solve it now, and the **authority** to spend. A16Z / Y Combinator now only fund
  founders who've **lived with the problem 5+ years** — that intimacy is a "time warp."
- **Warning:** only enter a market that is flat or growing (retirement homes yes, print
  newspapers no).
- **What to sell — four traits:** unique (no one else can sell it), expensive (more
  money per unit), sticky (bought again and again), and "air" (cheap to deliver → high
  gross margin). Coca-Cola hits all four. Add **scale** to go beyond the first million.
- **Scalability isn't binary — it's a degree**, governed by the **incremental cost of
  adding a customer** (cell networks, Facebook, Skool ≈ near-zero). Highly scalable
  businesses tend to be more demand-constrained; high-service businesses (accounting, law)
  are harder to scale and often use partnership structures.

**Level 2 — Get them to buy, buy more, and get help**
- **Two steps to a sale: find out, then buy.** Getting strangers to find out = the
  subject of *$100M Leads*.
- **The Core Four** (only things one person can do to spread word): **warm outreach**
  (people who know you, 1-1), **cold outreach** (strangers, 1-1), **content** (people who
  know you, 1-many), **paid ads** (strangers, 1-many).
- **Four lead getters** (others acting on your behalf): **customers, employees,
  affiliates** (reciprocal referral partners who already have your customers, e.g. gym +
  chiropractor), **agencies** (content/paid-ads/outbound specialists).
- To hit ~25 customers, start with **warm outbound** (offer 3–5 hours of free help, then
  make a paid offer) paired with **content**. Until $1M, you can run just one channel; if
  two, do content + warm outbound (cheapest, easiest). Ads are most expensive but scale
  fastest.
- **CLOSER sales framework:** **C**larify why they're there · **L**abel them with the
  problem (establish current state, desired state, and the gap/obstacle — you're the
  sword that kills the monster) · **O**verview past pain (the "pain cycle" expands the
  gap = deprivation = motivation) · **S**ell the vacation (a **three-pillar pitch**, each
  pillar one-two statements + an analogy/metaphor; sell Maui/Hawaii, not TSA — talk about
  their outcome, not your product) · **E**xplain away concerns (blame goes to
  circumstances / other people / self; six objection buckets: time, money, decision
  authority, fit, others' past products ["Bernie twice"], and the stall/avoid) ·
  **R**einforce the decision (begins the onboarding handoff).
- **Onboarding:** make and keep many promises in the first 24–48 hours. **BAMFAM** = book
  a meeting from a meeting (baton pass so no one falls through the cracks). Pass notes so
  a customer never repeats themselves — small change, big lift in satisfaction and
  ascension.
- **Eight ways to make a customer worth more:** (1) raise price, (2) decrease costs,
  (3) buy more often (subscription), (4) buy something new/complementary (cross-sell),
  (5) buy more quantity now (bulk), (6) higher quality (upsell), (7) decrease quantity
  (downsell — turns a no into a small yes), (8) lower quality (economy version). Easiest
  for beginners: raise price, get them to buy more stuff, get them to buy more often.
- **5-to-5-to-5 pricing:** for every 5 sales, raise price 20%; keep going until people
  stop buying, then optimize gross profit × conversion. **Never discount to close** — you
  can test a price but must honor it.
- **Leaky-bucket / LTV:** fix retention before pouring in more customers.
- **Recruiting help:** a company's size is capped by the **cumulative knowledge (brains)**
  inside it — hire people whose lifetimes of experience raise the ceiling (Steve Jobs
  built the team that built the iPhone, not the iPhone). The most valuable owner skill is
  getting others to do things for you.
- **The management diamond** — if someone doesn't do what you want, it's one of: didn't
  know **what** (fix with clear comms + deadlines), didn't know **how** (fix with the
  **three D's: document, demonstrate, duplicate**), didn't know **when** (durations +
  ask their estimate), didn't know **why** (tie their work to the mission), or a **bonus
  fifth — something blocking them** ("he ain't got no eggs"; the Google Fiber story).
- **Three pillars of every business (the tripod):** **acquisition** (marketing + sales),
  **delivery** (product/fulfillment/customer success), **operations** (IT, legal,
  finance, payroll, HR, recruiting). A partner must bring a **skill, time, or capital you
  lack** — don't partner with someone strong in your own pillar or the tripod tips over.

**Level 3 — Keeping your advantage, sticking with it, getting better**
- **Leverage = how much you get for what you put in** (neutral term). Forms: **brand**
  (higher click-through, conversion, and price — the branded t-shirt sells at $30 vs a
  blank at $5), **people** (borrow others' lifetimes), **code/media** (write once, used
  millions of times — a book keeps selling), **skill**.
- **Work = volume × leverage = output.** It's not how hard you work but how much you get
  per unit of work. Early on you have low leverage, so **compensate with volume** (1,000
  calls/emails/pieces of content); volume builds skill, skill is leverage — the virtuous
  cycle. "You have to work hard in order to work smart."
- **Sticking with it — compounding.** Munger (paraphrased): compounding is the eighth
  wonder; the objective is to never interrupt it. **Focus = the quality and quantity of
  what you say no to** (Steve Jobs: "What have you said no to lately?"). The **woman in
  the red dress** = shiny new opportunity that is an agent in disguise sent to destroy
  your focus.
- **Five stages entrepreneurs go through:** uninformed optimism → informed pessimism →
  valley of despair (where most quit) → informed optimism → achieve the goal. Most quit
  and restart the cycle chasing the next "quick and easy" thing (drop shipping, crypto,
  wholesaling) — none are quick or easy.
- **The "seven income streams" myth confuses sequence** — tall people play basketball,
  playing basketball doesn't make you tall. Concentrate all eggs in one basket, then
  diversify only after success (and returns fall). **Bill Gates** (49% at IPO) would be a
  ~$1.5T trillionaire had he never sold Microsoft; he diversified down to ~$100–150B,
  while number-two **Steve Ballmer** (~2–3%) stayed concentrated and is nearly as wealthy.
- **Getting better — common factors analysis:** compare your top 10% to your bottom 10%
  (content, ads, sales calls, customers) and the discrepancies are the skill nuggets.
  Volume generates the data; a pain + volume + feedback loop creates skill. This is how
  Alex learns things no course teaches.
- **Relationship with goals:** Alex defines love as what you're willing to give up to
  keep something. He filters people by "is my goal achievement more/less likely with this
  person?" — neutral drains resources, so he says no. "You can't want to be exceptional
  and not be willing to be the exception."
- Alex says he's hit his $1M and $100M goals and is publicly documenting the path to a
  billion — not for the money (he can't consume it) but to **meet the man he'd become**
  who could achieve it. "Either your business is growing or you are." Growing pains, not
  growing joys.
- **Infinite games:** the point of business is to stay in business (of marriage, to stay
  married; of health, to stay healthy). No one "wins" — richest men held the top briefly.
  You are the asset; to stop playing is to stop becoming.
