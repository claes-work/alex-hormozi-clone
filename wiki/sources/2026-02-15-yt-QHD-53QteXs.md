# How Making More Money Affects Your Life

## Summary

In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi walks through the "tiers of wealth" — how life materially changes as income rises, and where the returns start to diminish. He cites Felix Dennis's book (*How to Get Rich* / *I'll Teach You to Be Rich*), whose first chapter lays out ~11 levels of wealth and pegs "truly wealthy" at ~$400M liquid. Alex's own view: the meaningful jumps are biggest early (anything → six figures/year; $10k → $20k → $40–50k/month), because those changes hit the everyday variables of living — where you live, your kids' schools, vacations, food, clothes — and almost all of those top out around the top-1% earning level of ~$500k/year. Above that it's "nicer and nicer" houses/vacations, private travel (~a couple million/year), then boats and houses you can't really consume.

He then engages the famous money-and-happiness research. He argues the well-known ~$70–75k/year plateau (inflation-adjusted closer to ~$100k) actually keeps rising beyond that, but most people have a **skill deficiency in how to spend money** to improve their lives above ~$100k/year — and that spending well is a learnable skill. For himself, the things that improve his life are buying time back and paying to remove annoyances/frustrations, plus devoting real resources to something meaningful. The interviewer counters with an updated US study (peak dollar-to-value ~$150k, diminishing returns tapering ~$650–700k, and *lower* quality of life above ~$1M/year), which Alex finds "super believable," speculating the >$1M dip comes from having more problems, fake friends, and money to manage. A personal aside: by percentage of income he spends very little (he says he's "a lot like Graham" — likely Graham Stephan — and only spends because Leila spends); he has no need for a mega-yacht ("I think Leila wants one though").

## Key claims (2026-02-15, paraphrased; Alex-attributed unless noted)

- ★ The biggest life-changing income jumps happen **early**; going from anything to ~six figures/year is a material change, as is $10k→$20k→$40–50k/month. The jumps get smaller as income rises. *(durable belief — wealth tiers)*
- ★ "Change" is best measured by the **variables that affect daily living** — where you live, your kids' schools, vacations, where you stay, the food you eat, the clothes you wear — and almost all of those are maxed out by roughly the **top-1% earning level (~$500k/year)**. *(durable belief — needs-met threshold)*
- At ~$500k/year you can live a "baller lifestyle" but not really save; whether that income lets you save is entirely personal and comes down to your appetite for saving vs. spending, which is really a question of how much **risk** you're willing to take.
- Above the daily-living threshold, extra money buys nicer houses/vacations, then private travel (~"a couple million bucks a year"), then boats and houses — and at some point **you literally can't consume any more of it**.
- ★ (Controversial, per Alex) Money's contribution to well-being **keeps rising past the ~$70–75k / ~$100k plateau**; the real bottleneck is that **most people don't know how to spend money effectively above ~$100k/year** to actually improve their lives — but spending well is a **learnable skill**. *(durable belief — money & happiness / spending as a skill)*
- ★ For Alex, money that genuinely improves life is money that **buys time back** and pays to make **annoyances and frustrations go away** — those "net enhance"; longer-term, resources let you make a real impact on something meaningful. *(durable belief — how he spends to improve life)*
- Felix Dennis's book breaks wealth into ~11 levels and puts "truly wealthy" (do-whatever-you-want) at **~$400M liquid**. *(Alex citing Dennis, who was worth roughly that.)*
- **Interviewer-attributed (NOT Alex):** an updated US study within the last ~10 years found peak dollar-to-value around **$150k/year**, diminishing returns tapering around **$650–700k/year** (~top 1%), and **lower quality of life above ~$1M/year**.
- Alex finds the >$1M-lower-quality-of-life finding "super believable," guessing it stems from **more problems** — career issues, fake/non-genuine friends, and the burden of **managing money**.
- Above ~$5–10M/year income the next strata is ~$25–40M/year where you "can do crazier things," but past the point where you can buy the hotels you stay at, it stops mattering.
