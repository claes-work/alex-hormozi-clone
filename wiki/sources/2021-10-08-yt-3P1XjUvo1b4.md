# I cracked Starbucks RECURRING SALES model and built a FORTUNE (2021-10-08)

## Summary

In this early (2021) short talk, Hormozi argues that fortunes are built by selling
things people *do not stop buying* — recurring, sticky products rather than one-off
sales. Framed shortly after he and Leila sold their eight-figure software company, he
introduces the notion of **customer surplus** (value delivered far exceeding price) as
the primary driver of retention, contrasts selling **models vs. methods**, and lays out
a three-stage company-growth sequence (promotion → product → scale) that he attributes
to a former HubSpot executive. Note: despite the title's framing, the transcript does
**not** describe a Starbucks gift-card/prepay-float mechanism; "Starbucks" appears only
as a one-line example of a brand people never stop buying.

## The Starbucks mechanism

> ⚠️ Title/content mismatch. The clickbait title promises a decoded "Starbucks
> recurring sales model" (typically the gift-card prepayment / stored-value float).
> **The transcript contains no such mechanism.** Starbucks is invoked once, only as an
> illustrative example of consumer stickiness:

> "Some of you probably drink Starbucks. You probably will not stop drinking Starbucks.
> Some of you drink Coca-Cola and will probably not stop drinking Coca-Cola."

The actual "recurring sales model" Hormozi teaches here is **churn-minimization via
customer surplus**, not gift-card float or prepayment. His retention examples are
subscription-style utilities (Wi-Fi, phone service, Netflix) where a customer will
immediately re-enter their card if the service is cut off — because they value the
service far above its price. There is no prepaid-balance, gift-card, or float mechanism
described anywhere in the source.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-10-08)

- The single "one-way direction" to a fortune in business is to only sell things people
  do not stop buying. (paraphrase)
- Most of his own business success came not from novel invention but from improving
  something until people keep buying and keep paying for it. (paraphrase)
- Thought experiment for small-business owners: total lifetime customers × current
  average monthly price = the revenue you'd have if you never lost anyone — usually far
  more than current revenue, and it also funds a larger customer-acquisition budget.
  (paraphrase)
- **Customer surplus** — value received far exceeding price paid — is the biggest single
  driver of "stick"/retention; it makes customers feel they're getting a "crazy deal"
  and never want to leave. (paraphrase)
- Most people chase surplus by *cutting price*; the better route is *increasing value*,
  because cutting price leaves no profit and no real business (unless reproduction cost
  is ~zero, e.g. Dropbox storage). (paraphrase)
- Prefer selling **models over methods**: a method is one-and-done (people buy it, learn
  it, then no longer need it); a model continually evolves and remains needed.
  Franchises sell models — the marketing channel changes (Groupon → email → Facebook/
  Instagram/YouTube ads) but the model stays. (paraphrase)
- SaaS retention metrics apply fully to *services*, because service is just software
  doing a job manually; study SaaS/software business writers. (paraphrase)
- Three-stage growth sequence (attributed to Mark Roberge, early HubSpot exec who he
  says took them from 0 to ~$100M, now a Harvard lecturer): (1) **Promotion** (~$0–1M):
  get enough promotion to acquire customers — priced high enough to matter but not to
  maximize profit; (2) **Product** (~$1–10M): optimize the product until people don't
  leave — don't scale before this; (3) **Scale**: once retention is solved, marketing
  compounds because customers accumulate. (paraphrase)
- "You make sales to get customers. You're not making customers to get sales." — the
  goal of the promotion phase is customer acquisition, not profit. (paraphrase)
- If you never lose a customer, revenue compounds year-over-year forever; over a 20-year
  horizon even a weak marketer builds a huge business simply by not churning. (paraphrase)
- Target churn of ~1–2% monthly at most, versus the 10–20% he says most businesses have;
  get there by interviewing/surveying customers, studying usage patterns, and increasing
  "collateral" within the community. (paraphrase)
- Anecdote: a consulting client sold the same number of clients as his company but made
  $300K/month vs. his $4M/month — ~100× the profit — purely from better monetization,
  product, and value delivery. (self-reported)
- "High ticket" is a silly concept; the real concept is **high value**. Price is what you
  pay, value is what you get; the discrepancy is the customer surplus. (paraphrase)
- The business game is to build a "$500,000 house" for $5–10K and sell it for $50K — i.e.
  engineer a large value-to-cost gap. (paraphrase/metaphor)

2021, roughly one month after selling his software company and before his money-model
material was formalized.

- Relation to [[../topics/business/continuity-offers]]: This is an *early* articulation of

  **customer surplus** rather than the offer-structure and payment-mechanics vocabulary
  of the later *$100M Money Models* era. The **prepaid/float/gift-card** continuity
  mechanisms the task hypothesized are **absent** here — the title's Starbucks framing is
  not delivered on. Flag as an early, retention-first version.
- Relation to [[../topics/business/money-model]]: Contains embryonic money-model ideas —
  LTV via never-churning customers, acquisition-budget headroom created by retention, and
  the promotion→product→scale sequencing (credited to Mark Roberge / HubSpot). These
  predate and foreshadow his systematized money-model frameworks; treat as historical
  antecedent, not current doctrine.
- Recurring themes also seen elsewhere: **models > methods**, **high value > high ticket**,
