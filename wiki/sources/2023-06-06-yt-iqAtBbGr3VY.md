# Selling with Logic To Make Lots of Money [Alex Hormozi]

## Summary

A recorded live sales training Alex Hormozi delivered to gym owners, focused entirely
on **closing** — which he defines as everything that happens after you present the
offer (the "red zone" / "last five yards"). He positions himself as a *logical closer*
who believes logic produces better long-term outcomes than emotional pressure, because
the emotion fades but the logic remains. The core mental model is the **onion of blame**:
prospects (and people generally) cast their power to three sources of emotional
disturbance (borrowed from Albert Ellis / CBT) — circumstances, other people, and self —
which manifest as five obstacle/objection types: time, value (money), fit, authority,
and avoidance. He walks through concrete frameworks and one-liners to dismantle each,
peeling layers until the prospect is "in power" and able to make a decision. The
throughline is that sales is really about power and helping people make empowered
decisions to help themselves — regardless of whether they buy.

## Key claims (sales/logic frameworks)

- **Closing definition**: selling happens before you ask; closing happens after. Closing
  = the moment you present the offer onward. This talk covers only the "red zone."
- **Sales ethics = intention.** The difference between help and manipulation is intention.
  Test: if the prospect knew everything you knew and would still choose *not* to buy, then
  selling them is deceiving them.
- **Logical vs. emotional closing.** Alex leans logical: emotions fade in two weeks, so
  the logic must still be there for the decision to hold.
- **Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust.** Belief and trust are
  *continuums, not binaries* — ask "how much" not "do they / don't they."
- **Belief is the #1 sales tool.** Salespeople who learn more about a weak product lose
  belief and unconsciously stop closing ("commission breath" — humans sniff out intention).
  Fix belief (and the product) first. (Mortgage-company "how good are the leads?" story.)
- **Prospect breakdown**: ~10% never buy, ~10% buy regardless ("scarecrow" sale), ~80%
  need help deciding — that 80% is what you train for.
- **Goal of closing is to get someone to *decide*, not to buy** — reward the deciding
  activity, not the outcome, because the team can only control what they do.
- **Obstacles vs. objections**: obstacles come before the offer (you're on offense —
  destroy them); objections come after. Easier to handle obstacles than objections.
- **Onion of blame** (Albert Ellis's 3 causes of emotional disturbance → 3 layers):
  circumstances, other people, self. A circumstance objection is the outermost layer;
  expect two or three more layers underneath.
- **Five obstacle types + counters:**
  - **Time** — macro ("busy is the best time," because you'll always be busy and need to
    learn it now); micro ("everyone has the same 24 hours" / phone-screen-time story);
    the **"when-then" fallacy** ("I'll start when…" reverses the required order).
  - **Value/money** — "can't afford it" means not valuable enough (the $5k Ferrari
    thought experiment: enough value makes resources a non-issue). Four angles: why it
    being a lot *for them* is good ("take the bait"); why it's actually not a lot (split
    price from belief); "what's money good for anyway" (you'll buy the lessons either
    way; you spend the thing you value least); "you don't need money — be resourceful,
    not resourced" (Shoe Dog / Phil Knight; tax-bill resourcefulness).
  - **Fit ("special snowflake")** — new identity → new priorities ("we vote with our
    dollars"); pain of change (Tony Robbins — you change when the pain of change < pain
    of staying same; "reset the thermostat"); the **hypothetical isolate** ("if the
    program had everything you needed, would you do it?").
  - **Authority ("I have to talk to my spouse")** — hardest to overcome; 3-step: isolate
    & collapse the foil ("what part wouldn't they approve of?"), realize they've already
    given permission (do they approve of your current struggle?), realize you never needed
    permission — "you're looking for permission when you really need support."
  - **Avoidance ("I need to think about it")** = "I want to avoid this decision for fear
    of a mistake / let the universe decide." Frame across past / present / future:
    stack past actions ("six inches from gold," "another year of *almost*"); present =
    "rocking chair close" ("you don't need time, you need information — I'm your source");
    future-pace both positive and negative outcomes.
- **Best-case / worst-case (risk-free) close**: both options are risk-free, but only one
  moves you toward your goal — walking out is *guaranteed* to not get what you want.
- **Bonus close**: whatever reason the prospect gives is usually the exact reason they
  should do it ("I don't have time" → perfect reason).
- **Decision-making = product + trust + access to money**: do you believe it works, do
  you trust us, will it work for *you*, do you have/know access to the money. If yes → decide.
- **Master frame**: don't ask "will this get me what I want?" (needs high predictive
  accuracy) — ask "will this get me *closer or further*?" Lowers the action threshold;
  "the perfect decision maker makes none, the directional decision maker makes many and
  course-corrects faster."
- **Education as the best asset / "ignorance is the most expensive debt"**: the gap
  between current income and target income is what ignorance costs you per year; education
  can't be taxed, lost in divorce, and appreciates.
