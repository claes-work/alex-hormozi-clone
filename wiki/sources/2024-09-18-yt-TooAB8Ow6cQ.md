# 3 Hours of Money Making Advice You Needed To Know Yesterday

## Summary

A ~3-hour compilation stitched together from an 8-hour Skool "school day" mastermind Alex Hormozi ran with the top-10 winners of the Skool Games, intercut with his own to-camera intros for each clip. Despite the packaging it is **not a solo video**: it is a multi-speaker coaching room in which Alex is the primary teacher but Skool CEO **Sam Ovens** and ~10 community owners (Goose, Kirby, Justin, "Shark," David, and others) speak extensively. The material re-runs many of Alex's established frameworks — the four levers (traffic · conversion · price · churn), LTV = price ÷ churn, "more, better, new" prioritization, niching/avatar selection, and community retention — but adds distinctive fresh anecdotes (Leila taking his income from $3M to $17M, deliberately re-narrowing his content back to 100% business) and one framework he states unusually cleanly here: **"big head, long tail"** pricing (separating one-time value from ongoing consumable value).

## Key claims

Alex-attributed (persona-eligible):

- **Pricing is the strongest lever on profit** — more than acquiring more customers, more than retaining them longer. "Nailing pricing will make you more money than any other skill."
- **The four levers of any business are traffic, conversion, price, and churn.** LTV = price ÷ churn; you grow by increasing traffic, conversion, and price while decreasing churn — but price and churn and conversion are bidirectionally linked, so you optimize for *conversion rate × LTV* (earnings-per-click over the longest time).
- **His pricing philosophy has swung away from the old Dan Kennedy "charge as high as you can without cracking a smile" model** toward "make them smile at how blown away they are by the value" — because word-of-mouth and retention (which he says he massively underestimated) compound harder than a high one-off ticket.
- **Gym Launch regret / retention thesis:** if he restarted Gym Launch he'd price it ~$10k/year and obsess over keeping 80–90% of customers renewing. "If all 5,500 of those locations were still paying me, I'd be richer than I am today." As an investor, the #1 thing he looks at is *how many customers from last year are still paying.*
- **"Big head, long tail":** split your offer into **one-time value** (education/skills/tools/blueprints — value spikes once then decays to zero) and **ongoing consumable value** (community, calls, ads, done-for-you). Charge a real upfront price for the one-time value and a lower ongoing price for the consumable value — instead of the industry default of quoting a big ticket ($30k) and spreading it over a year while delivering all the value up front (which is exactly where churn hits). "Whatever you claim versus what you make are two very different things" — a $30k ticket at 25%/mo churn is really ~$10k collected.
- **"More, better, new" prioritization:** before starting anything new, ask (1) can we just do *more* of what already works, (2) if not, can we do it *better*, (3) only then do something *new*. Most small businesses stall because "they find something that works and they stop doing it," then get spread thin across six new things. The entrepreneur has the least leverage and the most direct time-to-income link, so a new project can literally shrink the business.
- **Whatever you work on must ladder up to one of the four levers** — pick the biggest current constraint; scale until something breaks, fix it, scale again.
- **Delegate your personal life first.** Laundry, cleaning, and food average ~96 hours/month (before switching costs); if you can earn more per hour than it costs to hire it out, outsource it — "it has nothing to do with luxury, it has everything to do with return on time." (The specific $1,500/mo private-chef math was a community member's; Alex endorsed the principle.)
- **Maker time vs. manager time.** Makers (coders, editors, and the entrepreneur doing deep work like a YouTube video) get only ~14 usable time-blocks a week; managers work in 15-min increments. A manager interrupting a maker costs a disproportionate 1/14 of the week. His single greatest return-on-time habit: the first 6 hours of every day, since he was poor, have zero meetings; he now batches all coordination into one day (Mondays) and keeps the rest of the week's calendar empty because "the team knows that if I have an empty calendar we make more money."
- **Niching lets you productize delivery.** Generic consulting can't deliver a consistent outcome; a narrow niche can ("here is the ad, the landing page, the price, the scripts, the meal plans"). His Gym Launch sales training was **under one hour**, and his nutrition module was a **~13-minute video** — built to produce a result, not to impress.
- **Avatar selection = LTV/CAC × TAM;** almost always, going up-market makes more money (higher-quality prospects pay more and are lower-maintenance). Stop running two front-ends — focus everything on the segment with the biggest arbitrage, then cross-sell/ascend. "Everyone fails because they can't say no to money."
- **Hire for the customer-funnel in reverse and value "vibe" over raw skill** for close-contact roles. Recruiting *is* the acquisition funnel (generate applications → nurture → interview = the sale → onboard → retain/ascend), and "brand counts double" (front-end for customers, back-end for talent). For his videographer, the deciding trait was "you like me" — "it's so much harder to find someone you like than someone who has the base skill."
- **The hardest, highest-value skill in entrepreneurship is recognizing "what good looks like" at each level** — both in general and per specific role. He reset his entire talent bar after watching a private-equity team across a boardroom table out-operate his own $100M+ team.
- **Value post vs. "valuable" post:** a real valuable post says *I did work for you / took risk for you / spent money for you* (a bargain on time or money) — not "here are six tips no one cares about." Community "culture" = *the rules that govern reward and punishment in an organization.*
- **Refund freely; don't create enemies.** The people who get FTC trouble are almost always the ones who refuse refunds → disputes rise → attorney-general complaints → they get "kicked off Stripe." "You don't want to become someone's project." (He credits the "project" line to Sam.)
