## Summary

In this short solo clip (responding to an off-camera interviewer's question about his 10-year vision),
Alex Hormozi describes a shift in priorities going into 2026. He states that 2026 is the first year of his
life — since roughly age 18 — in which making more money is **not** his primary goal, because he now sees
almost no incremental life improvement from additional money. He reframes business as one game inside the
larger "game of life," and says he'd rather "win at life" than only win at business. He argues people should
be allowed to change their minds after accomplishing something without it invalidating the path that got
them there.

He gives forward-looking company targets (Skool reaching ~$6–8B within 24 months; acquisition.com reaching
~$2–3B in 24–36 months) and predicts he'll make more money in the next five years than in his entire life
before, "times a lot," largely because things are already in motion. He introduces "knowing when enough is
enough" as a core value — 2026 is the year he hit his personal number for what "financially free" means to
him — and gives a tactical example: he now gives himself permission to work out 2–2.5 hours almost every
day purely because he enjoys it, not for productivity. He ties the reprioritization to mortality ("death
creates clarity"). Solo Alex throughout; the opening question comes from an unnamed off-camera interviewer.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-24, paraphrased unless quoted below._

- Going into 2026 is the first year of his life — since about age 18 — in which making more money is not
  his goal.
- The shift happened because at his current level he gets very little or almost no incremental life
  improvement from additional money; other things now improve his life more.
- He frames business as a game within the larger game of life; he wanted to "win" business for himself but
  now prefers to win at life rather than only at business.
- He defends the right to change one's mind after an accomplishment without it reversing or invalidating the
  path taken to get there.
- He predicts he will make more money in the next five years than in his entire life put together, "times a
  lot," because much is already in motion.
- Company target (paraphrased): Skool will "probably" be a $6–8B company within 24 months; he says they
  already have the metrics and it's a straightforward math equation. _(Caption renders it "school"; Skool is
  the intended company.)_
- Company target (paraphrased): acquisition.com will "probably" be ~$2–3B in 24–36 months.
- Being the richest man in the world has never been his goal; he optimizes only around the few things that
  positively affect his life.
- Tactical example: he now permits himself to work out ~2–2.5 hours almost every day, acknowledging it's not
  good for his joints and isn't productive/biohacking — he does it because he likes it.
- His argument to high earners: work exists to make money, money buys freedom to do what you want, so
  sacrificing that freedom to keep making money is self-defeating.
- 2026 is the year he hit his personal number for what "financially free" means to him; that number is
  individual (he names examples: $4M, $100M, or $1B for different people).
- "Knowing when enough is enough" is a valuable skill; he cites having stopped at 245 lbs as a jacked
  bodybuilder as a prior example of recognizing enough.
- He describes his goals as bigger than most people's but attainable, not unbounded.
- He says "death creates clarity" around the priorities that now matter more to him.
