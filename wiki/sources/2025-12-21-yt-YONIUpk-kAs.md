# 26 Harsh Lessons I Learned in 2025

## Summary

Alex Hormozi's annual year-end lessons video: 26 lessons compiled from notes he texts
himself throughout the year (an 8-year habit). 2025 was his biggest financial year ever
(companies did over $250M in revenue), he broke the Guinness World Record for
fastest-selling non-fiction book with the `$100M Money Models` launch ($106M in sales in
under three days), and his mother died suddenly. The lessons span mindset (fear, mental
toughness, grief, agency), business scaling (talent, A-players, delegation,
decentralization, leadership), and marketing (offers, ads, brand). A recurring throughline:
he has reached his "enough" number and is now shifting to plan his personal life first and
let business fill the cracks.

## Key claims

- **Fear lives in the fog.** Write down in excruciating, step-by-step detail what you're
  actually afraid of; specificity dissolves the fear because "it only exists when it's
  hazy, not when it's crisp." Most fear is a stranger's disapproval, and "no one really
  cares that much about anyone besides themselves."
- **Mental toughness > motivation, and it's measurable** via four variables: (1) how much
  bad can happen before your behavior changes, (2) how much your behavior changes when it
  does, (3) how fast you rebound (resiliency), (4) how far you rebound — below, back to, or
  above baseline (adaptation). He defines **trauma** neutrally as "a permanent change in
  behavior from an aversive stimulus" — not inherently bad.
- **Your power follows the direction you point blame.** Blaming outward hands control to
  others; owning it ("I wasn't resilient/adaptive enough") is what lets you fix it.
- **Record-breaking outcomes take record-breaking work.** He wrote 400+ pages of email
  copy (more words than the book), 6 VSLs, and 1,700 webinar slides by hand (zero AI for
  the slides; AI only for slide visuals). "No one will get credit for the work they do" —
  you have to be the one who cheers for you.
- **Take care of your team's personal life so they can succeed professionally.** A
  neutral-or-unsupportive spouse caps a person's potential; he now actively looks at whose
  home environment is supportive to unlock discretionary effort.
- **Think for yourself or the world will think for you (high agency).** Most people pick
  their identity, beliefs, and apparel "off the shelf." Interrogate every component — "What
  do I want, and does this thing help me get it?" Unique people combine self-reasoned
  pieces that don't obviously go together. Ties directly to building a unique brand.
- **A hard conversation up front saves millions and years.** All eight of his lawsuits this
  year were with friends/friendly parties. Put everything in writing; front-load the
  discomfort. Cites Rockefeller: "a friendship founded on business is better than a
  business founded on friendship." Uses a five-levels-of-friends test (past → other people
  → ideas → doing things → making/losing/spending money together).
- **Do more than what is required / know the rules.** Ignorance of the law is no defense;
  he essentially "got a law degree this year." Knowing the rules removes the stress that
  otherwise eats your shower/dinner/driving time.
- **Shifting to judgment over volume of work.** Past a certain scale "you will be
  compensated for the quality of your judgment, not the quantity of your work." He's now
  planning life first, prioritizing sleep, travel, and unplugged time to improve
  decision quality — put together a deal worth more than the year's revenue in ~6.5 hours
  of calls on a day after his best-ever sleep score.
- **Two razors for hard decisions:** (1) What do I want to have happen? (2) What increases
  the odds of that happening? Your gut counter-reaction rarely helps. "Doing nothing at all
  is often the most productive thing" — the "heavyweight champion of the world" is
  do-nothing; physically efficient but emotionally painful.
- **More > Better > New.** New is the riskiest and most resource-hungry move. The taller
  your working "stack," the higher the odds the next new move is the wrong move. He allows
  himself **one new thing per year** (this year: the book launch). The real risk to a
  business isn't dumb decisions — it's doing the 2nd/3rd/4th most valuable thing instead of
  the most valuable. Give solutions time to bear fruit ("give time time").
- **It's okay to just make money — the "third marshmallow fallacy."** Endless delayed
  gratification means you die with a stack of crumbs. Winners must learn the harder skill:
  when to reap. Risk-adjust future payoffs; money today is worth more because you have more
  years to compound it.
- **Clear beats clever in all things.** You'll repeat yourself until you die; no one is
  listening. He ran ~5,000 near-identical ads. "One big idea with 10 reasons" beats "10
  ideas with one reason each."
- **The offer is still king.** "It's the offer, stupid." A book-shipping offer he threw
  together (pay shipping on 3 books, he loses money) posted the best funnel metrics of his
  career. Each bonus should be worth more than the whole thing you sell; if a bonus needs
  explaining, delete it. Razor: the offer should be **textable** — fits in a text, gets a
  "yeah, I'm in."
- **Ads = volume of creative + hyper-personalization.** 2026 will be personalization at
  scale via AI. Build the "self-licking ice cream cone": customers make creative that gets
  more customers.
- **Side quests can protect the main quest** — but only if they drain no resources from it
  (for him, writing books gives thinking space and lets his team improve without him
  blowing things up).
- **Delegation is the price of scaling = relinquishing control.** "If it requires me, it
  doesn't work." His internal term: **scale zero** — scale himself down to zero so the
  business can scale to infinity. At equilibrium, increase **supply** first, not demand.
- **Real estate** (ACQ Real Estate / ACQRE with Shiron) was a big win; will talk about it
  more now that he has credibility.
- **Best ROI in business is still talent.** Frame the cost of acquiring talent against the
  profit it generates (dentist example: pay $150K headhunter for a $700K/yr gross-profit
  hire). "Excellence attracts excellence"; no one better than you at *everything* will work
  for you, so your growth is capped by your ability to learn.
- **A-players make you rich; fire the sixes.** Five 8s + five 6s become a team of 6.7s;
  cut the sixes and the eights become nines. Cut a 14-person sales team to 6 and closed
  more. He knows an A-player within 14 days. "The best talent is always in the future"
  (Shiron).
- **Money attracts talent; culture keeps talent.** "You must be this tall to ride the
  ride" (pay), but financial incentives are too infrequent to drive daily performance —
  latency beats intensity. A-cultures self-reinforce. "Winners want to win more than they
  want to make money."
- **Decentralization wins (team of teams).** Independent leaders owning their own P&Ls;
  think "business of businesses" (Amazon/AWS model). His unlock for going from hundreds of
  millions to $1B/year. If a leader needs you to drive the revenue, they're not the driver.
- **0-for-12 on EAs, hoping for 1-for-13.** His new world-class EA is "a psychopath" with
  high general intelligence; speed non-negotiable (responds within 60 seconds). You should
  own decisions about your own time; recommends the maker/manager framework.
- **The leader is ALWAYS the problem** — not sometimes. A mediocre function = a mediocre
  leader. A great leader can turn a mediocre team great; a mediocre leader turns a great
  team mediocre. Fix the leader before chasing tactical fixes.
- **In the age of AI, your brand is your moat.** Products/services get commoditized; brand
  = who and what you associate with. "Give away the secrets, sell the implementation" —
  people won't do the work, or won't do it alone. Brand must be constantly reinforced;
  "something is only a big deal if you make it one" (candidly admits the launch PR push
  "fell flat" — a self-described miss).
- **The greatest risk to the business is that you stop feeling like doing it.** "Most
  businesses don't run out of money. The founder runs out of will." Plateaus trap people
  longest (painful but not painful enough to quit). You need something outside yourself the
  business drives toward — for him, the person he wants to become.
