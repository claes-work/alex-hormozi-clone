# Learn to Love the Pain

## Summary
Solo advice clip answering a written question from a broke 21-year-old who supports his mom with cold calls he hates and wants to escape into "AI and creative." Alex's core move is a **reframe of pain and unglamorous work**. First correction: stop saying you hate the work — realize you're doing it to provide for people you love, and there's "no higher honor than that." He tells the **parable of the stone cutter**: three men do identical stone-hammering work; the first calls it backbreaking misery, the second says he's providing for his family, the third says he's building a cathedral that will last generations. The work was the same; the only difference was their outlook — and changing your outlook makes the work more bearable. He debunks "follow your passion": drawing on his own arc (management-consulting "make money" track → quit to open a gym following his fitness passion), he found that 80–95% of the day was selling memberships, mopping floors, cleaning equipment, billing, and picking playlists — not the thing he loved. Two reasons passion-chasing disappoints: (1) most of the day won't be the beloved activity anyway, and (2) doing the same beloved thing all day every day kills the liking, because you adapt — you like it now precisely because it's rare, on your terms, and unpaid. His concrete plan for the young man: **double down on sales** to escape poverty through the sales cycle, fall in love with the craft and "sharpen your sword" (a skill that stays core to everything regardless of whether AI takes over selling); then use the off-hours (he sketches 5–9am and 5–9pm plus weekends, ~8 hours) to learn the AI/creative skills and actually go get business with the cold-calling skills he's building. And expect it to still be miserable — because work is hard — then reframe it like the third stone cutter: maybe it's not miserable; maybe it's meaningful because you *choose* to make it meaningful. ## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues you should stop framing hated work as hatred and instead see it as providing for people you care about — in his words there's no higher honor than providing for your family.
- As of March 2026, per his stone-cutter parable, identical work is experienced completely differently based on outlook alone; changing your outlook on the work makes the work itself significantly more bearable.
- As of March 2026, "follow your passion" misleads people because the vast majority of any workday is not the thing you love — he cites his own gym: following his fitness passion still meant selling memberships, mopping, cleaning, marketing, billing, and picking playlists 80–95% of the time.
- As of March 2026, doing the one thing you love all day every day makes you stop liking it, because humans adapt; you enjoy it now only because it's rare, done on your terms and time, and for no money.
- As of March 2026, if there were one secret activity that stayed enjoyable forever, everyone would already do it; the real point is that things change while we wrongly expect them to stay the same, then feel wronged when life doesn't meet that expectation.
- As of March 2026, his advice to the broke 21-year-old: double down and get really good at sales, because you can absolutely escape poverty through the sales cycle.
- As of March 2026, he recommends going all-in on sales for 8–10 focused hours with nothing else on the calendar, falling in love with the process and craft ("sharpening your sword"); learning to sell stays core to everything in life whether or not AI takes over selling.
- As of March 2026, he prescribes using off-hours (roughly 5–9am and 5–9pm plus weekends) to learn the desired AI/creative skills, and to use the cold-calling skills already being practiced to go get actual business rather than sitting on the sidelines.
- As of March 2026, he says the work will still be miserable because work is hard; the only remedy is to reframe it like the third stone cutter — deciding it's meaningful because you choose to make it meaningful.
