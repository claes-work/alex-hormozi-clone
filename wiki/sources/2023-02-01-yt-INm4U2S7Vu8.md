# Get RICH With These 24 Investing Rules

## Summary
A solo, rapid-fire talk in which Hormozi lays out 24 investing "rules," each framed as an if-then principle he claims to reuse across decisions. The through-line is extreme risk-aversion and disqualification: he argues you should say no far more than yes, insist on diligence, models, contracts and track records, minimize the unknowable, and only make a move when he and Leila both agree. He also draws sharp lines between investing and trading/business, and stresses reputation and peace of mind as returns that compound. This is Acquisition.com investing content aimed at owners of $3M–$100M companies as a lead-in to being invested in.

## Key claims
- **If you can't buy it twice, don't buy it once** — realistically he wants to be able to buy 3–6 times; when he started he thought he'd put a third into one thing, but his biggest allocation might be 10%.
- **You can buy insurance for anything** — for a specific worry (e.g. fear of running out of money) buy the corresponding insurance; annuities are "buying money for the rest of your life," a guaranteed income until death.
- **If a deal feels like a grand slam, get a second opinion** — when something looks amazing he assumes he must be missing something and sends it to others for fresh eyes.
- **If you don't know how you can lose money, don't do it until you do** — find *all* the ways you can lose money in a deal; the goal is minimizing the unknown/unknowable to the highest degree possible.
- **If it feels rushed, don't do it; FOMO means slow down** — pressure and the fear of missing out are signals to pause, not act.
- **If you only have one deal on the table, don't do the deal** — always have backup deals; with only one option you talk yourself into it because there's nothing to compare it to (dating analogy).
- **Buying with intent to sell is trading, not investing** — investing runs on very long time horizons; trouble comes from applying business rules to investing (or vice versa) — they're different skills.
- **If we don't agree, we don't do it** — even when he has decision-making authority, he won't move until he and Leila agree; he's usually the "yes man" who over-sees upside, so a partner who genuinely balances that adds value (yin/yang).
- **If it's complicated, pass** — if it feels above your head it probably is; complete understanding lowers risk, barely-catching-on is gambling.
- **No diligence, no deal; no model, no deal** — he won't act on hype/an email/a phone call; he wants the math, projections and assumptions, and asks what has to be true for it to work.
- **If someone's non-responsive, no deal** — he judges people fast on responsiveness and expectation-meeting: "do what you're going to say, say what you're going to do."
- **If it costs peace of mind, don't buy it** — anything that costs him sleep or worry is too expensive.
- **If it doesn't build your reputation, don't do it** — no move is neutral to your brand; reputation is the one thing you keep forever, can lose, and compounds.
- **No track record, no deal — performance over charisma** — best predictor of future performance is past performance (cites Napoleon: "I'd rather have lucky generals").
- **If it's the same thing you've always done well with, do more of it** — compounding comes from repeating what works (e.g. a fourth hard-money loan) rather than chasing variety like crypto day-trading; your luck improves the more you do it.
- **Always under-borrow** — any number times zero is still zero; debt introduces risk, so borrow less than you could even though it caps upside.
- **Play the fool** — ask stupid questions, define terms, draw pictures, see examples; if you can't draw it back you don't get it. No advantage to seeming smarter than you are; advantage in knowing more than they think.
- **If we're not in the GP, we're not in the deal** — once you have ~$10–30M investable, ask for part of the general partner (the people running it), not just the limited-partner position.
- **If it's a bad person, it's a bad deal** — bad partners sink a deal no matter what the paper says; you should say no way more than yes.
- **No contract, no deal** — contracts set clear expectations for every failure scenario; anyone who "only does handshake deals" is a red flag to run from.
- **Negotiate as if the contract is everything; deliver as if it doesn't exist** — be exacting when writing terms, then live week-to-week on the offensive during delivery (defensive contracting + offensive delivery = best of both).
