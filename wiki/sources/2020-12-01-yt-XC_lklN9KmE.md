# Your Inflow Is Your Bottleneck (2020-12-01)

## Summary

Hormozi argues that most service businesses that fail to hit their growth goals are
capped by their *inflow* — their ability to acquire and sell new clients week in,
week out — not by retention or product. Working backward from a target member count
and churn rate, he lays out the "money math" and "time math" required to grow, and
insists owners must operationally prepare (and emotionally accept) the relentless
selling that big goals demand.

## Key claims (paraphrase)

- (2020) **Central argument — inflow is the bottleneck:** owners set big goals
  (300/400/500 members) but don't do "the simple math of getting there," and most
  times the *inflow* (new-client acquisition) is the cap, not retention.
- (2020) Growth requires diagnosing the true constraint and working *backward* from
  the goal: target members × churn = signups needed just to break even, then
  gross up for close rate, show rate, and lost back-end efficiency.
- (2020) Worked math: to hold 300 members at 10% churn you must sign ~30/month;
  at a 50% trial close that's 60 front-end sales; at 1-in-2 booked-to-close that's
  ~120 conversations/month (~30/week); factoring show-up rate pushes it to ~60
  appointments/week — *just to maintain 300, before growing.* To grow you roughly
  double it.
- (2020) Backing further into ad cost: ~15 leads/day × $10/lead = $150/day ≈
  **$4,500/month ad spend** to feed that pipeline.
- (2020) Service businesses are "easy in, easy out" — low switching cost means
  higher back-end churn than sticky software, but the compensating advantage is you
  can scale inflow fast; failing to maximize inflow wastes a service provider's
  primary edge.
- (2020) To be "massive" you must grow off everything at once — organic referrals,
  outbound, *and* paid acquisition — not just organic.
- (2020) Inflow must never stop: turning ads on for a few weeks then off to service
  clients is a growth-killer; you must sell and fulfill *concurrently*, week after
  week.
- (2020) "Acceptable metrics": accept realistic industry numbers (service churn
  rarely below ~1–2%; ~35% industry trial close) and extrapolate off them rather
  than waiting for perfect performance.
- (2020) Scaling is fundamentally *time-blocking* math: compute hours per activity,
  count team members, block the calendar (e.g. onboarding, conversion, retention
  outreach, sales into distinct blocks). "How long does it take, how many do we
  have, block the time — that is how you scale."
- (2020) Emotional prerequisite: high-volume selling means many no's; "champions
  have short memories," don't take rejection personally (it's about the prospect,
  not the salesperson).
- (2020) Priorities never truly conflict in importance, only in nature — if you
  won't do the math/work, the goal simply isn't your real priority.
- (2020) People *think* they want a full gym until they see what a full gym
  requires — recalls Gym Launch flying out to take gyms from ~80 to ~280 clients in
  21 days and owners panicking.

## Notes — proto-constraint / diagnostic framework

⚠️ This video is an early, explicit proto-version of Hormozi's later **constraint /
bottleneck diagnostic** thinking (the "find the one constraint" logic he later
formalizes, e.g. the Theory-of-Constraints-style framing in his growth content).
Here he: (1) names a single limiting factor ("your inflow is your bottleneck"),
(2) reasons backward from the goal through churn/close/show rates to required
inputs, and (3) converts it into concrete time-blocking and ad-spend numbers. It
also foreshadows his standard "leads / sales / retention" lens and the recurring
"do the math backward from your goal" method. Worth promoting into a topics/ page
on constraints/diagnosis during a later synthesis pass.
