# Billionaire Recession Advice: Stop Investing in THIS (2023)

> ⚠️ DATED POSITION (per fidelity rule 3): This is **October 2022** recession-era
> commentary. Alex describes a specific point-in-time cash/investment strategy shaped by
> the 2022 macro environment (inflation, crashed crypto/stocks, shaky real estate,
> then-current bank vs. Treasury interest-rate spreads). The interest-rate figures and
> the "take cash out of banks" move are tied to that moment and should NOT be presented
> as his standing or current advice. The durable, less time-bound idea is the "invest in
> what you know" thesis at the end.

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex explains why, given the 2022 economic shift, he pulled his cash
out of banks and parked it in U.S. Treasuries — framing Treasuries not as an investment
but as a safer, higher-yielding "bank account." He then walks through how banks profit
off depositors' money, and pivots to his larger realization (prompted by a Dave Ramsey
clip): the reason he couldn't deploy capital for two years was that his planned "barbell"
strategy (stocks + real estate) didn't match what he actually knows. His conclusion:
invest concentrated in the game you understand, which for him means service-based
businesses — a mirror of his singular-focus business philosophy.

## Key claims (his recession / investing advice)

- **Cash strategy — Treasuries as a bank account.** He took his cash out of banks and
  bought U.S. Treasuries, reasoning the U.S. government is less likely to default than a
  bank (e.g. Bank of America) and pays more. He frames Treasuries as a cash-equivalent
  "bank account," not an investment vehicle — and says this is what Buffett means when
  he's reported to hold "cash."
- **How banks profit off you.** A depositor is actually a *creditor* of the bank. The
  bank pays you ~0.1–1% annually, then lends your money to the government (bonds) or to
  borrowers (mortgages) at ~4–5%, keeping the spread (~40x what they pay you).
- **Two factors in any loan/debt:** the return (interest rate) and the likelihood of
  repayment (risk). Above the FDIC-insured amount, if a bank fails you can lose the
  excess; he claims the FDIC is effectively bankrupt.
- **Borrow against Treasuries.** You can take a loan of up to ~80% of Treasury value,
  keep earning the interest, and deploy the borrowed cash elsewhere.
- **His investing evolution (over ~5–10 years):** (1) invest in *yourself* — skills,
  access, coaching, mentorships (his highest-return investment ever); (2) once he had
  more cash than he could reinvest in himself, index into the S&P 500 ("what Uncle Warren
  told me to do"); (3) post-sale of three companies, a wealthy friend outlined a
  "barbell" strategy — stocks on one end, real estate on the other, little in between,
  plus private equity in companies he owns.
- **The core lesson — invest in what you know.** From a Dave Ramsey clip (Ramsey advising
  Graham Stephan): your portfolio should reflect your *knowledge base*. Alex realized the
  barbell was his *friend's* strategy, not his. He knows service-based businesses, not
  real estate — so that's where he can write large checks confidently.
- **Concentration over diversification for outsized returns.** Billionaires "don't do
  many things"; they pick one or two games they understand and do a lot of them. Outsized
  returns come from playing where you have an unfair advantage / know more than everyone
  else. The S&P 500 is the right advice only *because* most people don't know what they're
  doing.
- **Risk = ignorance.** Echoing Buffett, "it's only risky if you don't know what you're
  doing." He calls himself risk-averse but can name and locate the risks in deals he
  understands, making sizable private-deal checks "appropriately" risky.
- **His 3-step framework:** (1) invest in you; (2) invest excess into indexes; (3) once
  you're good enough at your main game, deploy your stockpile there and start the
  compounding machine.
