# What Should I Sell?

## Summary
Q&A clip (an interviewer sets up the question; only Alex's answers are voice/belief data). The prompt: a beginner has read Hormozi, accepts that they need to "sell a thing," but has no idea what to sell and doubts they have any skills. Alex — who says he covers this more in the forthcoming Leads book (~6 months out) — argues that **everyone already knows something**: being alive means your eyes and ears have taken in inputs, giving you "unique depths of knowledge" in certain areas. To find sellable knowledge he offers a **three-bucket sourcing model**: (1) your parents' jobs/industries (you absorb an industry by osmosis — "if your dad was a mechanic, you know so much about cars"), (2) your own past jobs, and (3) your current personal interests. Ask of those three: which bucket lets me help someone *do a thing better*? He frames this via Y Combinator, which prizes prior industry experience because of **"ignorance debt"** — the huge cost of entering an industry you know nothing about. On *what* to sell, the goal is the most valuable problem you can solve: in B2B, the problem where you can make someone the most money (and charge a percentage of it); in B2C, the problem people perceive as most viable/desirable (e.g. learning music, or editing songs for musicians who hate that part). He attacks two beginner fallacies: expecting a **perfect/novel first business** (nearly every entrepreneur has "a graveyard of failures behind them" — only freaks like Bezos hit it first try), and the **employee fallacy** that your first pick is what you'll do forever. Instead you just need something you're good enough at to "learn the game" — take a step and the next step gets illuminated; planning 100 steps ahead is pointless because chaos breaks the plan. He recommends **service businesses** to start (mowing lawns, cleaning windows/houses) as lowest-risk because you only invest your time. No novel idea required: **copy what others do and do it a little better**, exploiting the obvious holes (slow dry cleaner, sloppy lawn-care edges, poor communication). Finally, he reframes early income: you're still trading time for dollars, but the point is to **learn, not earn** — most of your "income" is really education, paying down ignorance debt. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
