# How I Think About Investing in a Business

## Summary
Interview clip (one host question mid-way; everything else is Alex). Alex lays out how he evaluates business investments and, more broadly, how he prioritizes which opportunities to pursue. His starting premise: **nothing is risk-free** and "there are always reasons to say no to a deal" — you can build a compelling scare-argument against anything (even US treasuries, if you assume the economy collapses), and influencers profit from doing exactly that. The productive move is therefore to **compare risks rather than try to eliminate them**, which is why he prizes **risk-adjusted return** as a tool to normalize different opportunities against each other. He frames entrepreneurship itself as betting: "we bet with our time, we bet with our money" — limited resources against unlimited opportunities — and calls the skill of making these bets well "if not *the* most important skill set as an entrepreneur." Using a "women in the red dress" analogy, he says you must score both the **opportunity** (how good, a 4 vs. 6 vs. 8) and its **risk** (how "crazy"), which is what the diligence process is for. He then shares a prioritization ladder he says he just wrote up as a chapter in a forthcoming book: rank opportunities by highest likelihood of success, and **do first the ones that need no new skills and no (or least) new effort**; then the ones needing more effort but still no new skills; only then start learning new skills, choosing the skill with the **highest leverage** (most output per unit input). These "cost basically nothing" wins have the lowest likelihood of *not* happening. A host asks whether this transfers to personal-life decisions (salsa vs. coding); Alex says yes — the "investor frame" is valuable precisely because investors are people whose decision-making has been **scored and quantified**, giving a verifiable scoreboard (why Ray Dalio's *Principles* became a bestseller for non-investors), so world-class investors with "a stack of undeniable proof" are an unusually trustworthy source of decision-making advice. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues there is no risk-free investment — even US treasuries carry risk (they'd be worthless if the US economy collapsed) — so you can always construct a reason to say no to any deal.
- As of March 2026, he says the right approach is to **compare** risks against each other rather than trying to **eliminate** risk, since risk can never be zeroed out.
- As of March 2026, he identifies **risk-adjusted return** as a core investor tool: a way to normalize different opportunities so they can be compared on a like-for-like basis.
- As of March 2026, he calls the skill of making good bets — allocating limited time and money against unlimited opportunities — "if not the most important skill set as an entrepreneur," because entrepreneurship is fundamentally betting.
- As of March 2026, evaluating an opportunity requires scoring two things separately: the quality of the opportunity itself and its risk level; the diligence process exists to assess the risk side.
- As of March 2026, Alex's opportunity-prioritization ladder (which he says he wrote as a chapter in a forthcoming book): (1) pursue first the opportunities needing no new skills and no/least new effort; (2) then those needing more effort but still no new skills; (3) only then learn a new skill, choosing the one with the highest leverage (most output per least input).
- As of March 2026, he prioritizes the low-effort/no-new-skill opportunities first because they have the lowest likelihood of *not* happening (highest likelihood of success).
- As of March 2026, he says the "investor frame" is valuable in personal decisions too, because investors are among the few people whose decision-making has been objectively scored and quantified — a real scoreboard most advice-givers lack.
- As of March 2026, he cites Ray Dalio's *Principles* selling to a mostly non-investor audience as evidence that good decision-making principles generalize, and argues world-class investors with "a stack of undeniable proof" are an unusually validatable source of advice.
