# I'm Buying Courses but Not Getting Results. What Should I Do?

## Summary
In a live Q&A clip, a caller who left their job to build a personal-training business reports buying a fitness-business course but getting no ROI. Alex Hormozi reframes the situation: education is cumulative, not a single silver bullet, and a lack of results reflects a skill gap (the student's or the teacher's), not proof that the method doesn't work. He advises learning from everyone rather than betting on one source, and closes with a plug for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that a course producing no immediate ROI is not a waste; the buyer may need to take several more courses before it clicks. He uses a "bridge with bricks" analogy: each learning experience adds a few bricks, and only once the bridge is complete can dollars cross it.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames failure to get results as a skill problem — either the student lacked the skill to execute or the teacher lacked the skill to teach — not evidence that getting customers for fitness is impossible.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that most educators genuinely try to help and are limited by their teaching ability, not nefarious; he acknowledges charlatans exist but says they are the minority.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the core problem in the "alternative education" world is not the products but the promises — if a course promised only to teach a subset of the required skills, buyers wouldn't feel scammed.
- As of March 2026, Alex illustrates with a "10 skills" model: a teacher may only convey skills 1, 3, and 7; students who already happened to have the other skills succeed and leave glowing testimonials, which misleads new buyers about what the course alone delivers.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises learning from every available teacher rather than loyalty to one, reasoning that combining everyone's knowledge could let you outperform each of them.
- As of March 2026, Alex warns against drawing the "wrong lesson" from a failed attempt ("I tried this, it didn't work, therefore it doesn't work") and urges persistence.
- Caller claim (unverified): the caller left their job, is funding living costs with creative-agency skills, and paid for a fitness-business course with no return so far.
