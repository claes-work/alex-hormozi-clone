# How to Scale a Dental Business From $15M to $50M

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A in which Alex Hormozi advises a dental group owner running four offices (a fifth opening next month) doing ~$15M/yr who, after a two-day workshop, has raised his ambition from doubling (~$25M) to tripling (~$35M, and framed as up to $50M). The owner's constraint is manpower — trained doctors, especially orthodontists — plus leads. Alex reframes the business as demand-constrained on the talent side and walks the owner through applying an LTV:CAC-style ratio to talent acquisition, showing that spending real money (headhunters, agencies, communities) to acquire high-gross-profit doctors is a hugely favorable trade the owner has been failing to make. Only Alex's advice and frameworks are persona-relevant; the owner's business details are context.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **Solve the current binding constraint first.** Alex insists on isolating one constraint ("you can't solve doctor time you don't have — so let's assume it's doctors"), because manpower (doctors) gates growth before leads do.
- **All businesses are demand-constrained — including on the talent side.** A more advanced way to think about business: it's not just customer demand; supply-constrained businesses (home services, HVAC, dental — "specialized labor") are demand-constrained on talent.
- **There's a talent-side equivalent of LTV:CAC.** Just as you have LTV:CAC on the customer side, you have lifetime gross profit per employee divided by your cost of acquiring that talent. For a dentist making $400K gross profit/yr with a ~6-year average tenure, lifetime value is ~$2.4M against a small acquisition cost.
- **Paying to acquire talent is one of the best trades available.** An orthodontist generating $400K/yr in gross profit is worth paying far more than $0 to acquire; even $50K–$100K–$200K is an 8:1, 4:1 or 2:1 return, "and then next year I'm also going to get 400 grand too." Refusing to pay is why owners stay stuck.
- **"It cost me nothing up to this point" is the trap.** Owners who acquired talent for free via referrals cap their growth: "Yeah, and that's why you are stuck." You must be willing to spend to break the constraint.
- **Bomb the constraint.** Once identified, attack the constraint aggressively — headhunters, 10 recruiting agencies, forums for the specialty, paying community owners to post recruitment. Alex's portfolio example: an outsourced sales team brought in-house in 90 days (hired 80, kept 50), paid ~$500K to 10 agencies at ~$10K/head, and it added ~$5M/month — "great trade."
- **You pay for growth in either profit or growth rate.** "You either pay in profit or in growth rate. So if you want it to happen slower, pay less." Pulling the future forward (2 years instead of 7) means investing a big number up front and accepting lower near-term profit.
- **The enterprise-value multiplier is where it gets "sexy."** A dental group likely trades at ~10x. So a ~$250K investment (5 orthodontists × $50K) that yields ~$1M extra EBITDA produces ~$10M in enterprise value — "the cash is just gravy."
- **Willingness-to-pay test.** Alex closes by pressure-testing commitment: with $4.5M profit on $15M, "are you willing to make $4.25M to pull the future forward?" — quantifying the trade of near-term profit for accelerated scaling.
