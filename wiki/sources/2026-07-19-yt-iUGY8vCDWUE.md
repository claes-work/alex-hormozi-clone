# 'Is My New Offer Genius or Just Escape?'

## Summary

In this short coaching clip, a caller named Nate Friedman describes his business selling marketing to MSPs (local IT support companies), currently doing ~$300k/month in recurring revenue and aiming for $10M annual revenue. The core business is structured as an agency, which grew well until roughly $2–2.5M, after which growth slowed and delivery became human-resource-heavy and prone to breaking. About a year ago he launched a lower-ticket offer (~$500/month) aimed at emerging MSPs, building their website in HighLevel; it has added ~60 customers and is now signing ~5/week. His question: is the new offer genuinely better, or is he just chasing something new to escape friction in the agency model?

Alex Hormozi interrogates the unit economics rather than the emotion. He surfaces that the low-ticket offer has lower churn (~1%/month vs ~2.5% for the agency), ~75% gross margins after setup (delivery is largely outsourced — a partner handles live chat at ~$5/chat plus a $35/month base fee), and a huge, easily-reached avatar. Because the offer is set-up-then-hand-off, it mainly needs a sales machine to scale. Alex concludes — explicitly noting this is not his usual advice — that the low-ticket business is actually the better business and worth strongly considering as the pivot.

The refinement of the "am I just escaping?" question is the substance: Alex does not treat the new-shiny-thing instinct as automatically wrong. Instead he tests it against metrics (churn, margin, TAM, scalability). When the "escape" candidate has better retention, better margins, a larger and stickier avatar, and cleaner delivery than the core business, the pivot is justified on the numbers. He sizes the upside: even signing 500/year at $500/month adds ~$3M/year to ARR, and sticky recurring revenue at prosumer scale is a high enterprise-value business.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) The right way to decide whether a new offer is "genius or escape" is to compare its unit economics to the core business — churn, gross margin, avatar size, and scalability — not to judge the emotional impulse behind it.
- (2026-07-19) Lower churn on the new offer than on the core service is a strong signal that it is the better business, not merely a distraction.
- (2026-07-19) An offer that is "set up once, then hand off" (delivery largely outsourced) mainly requires a sales machine to scale, because the constraint becomes acquisition rather than delivery capacity.
- (2026-07-19) A ~1%/month churn translates to roughly 85%+ annual retention, which Alex calls good — "especially with an avatar that there's so many of."
- (2026-07-19) A large, easily-reachable "prosumer" avatar (here: IT guys starting an MSP) makes strong retention numbers especially valuable because there is abundant volume to acquire against.
- (2026-07-19) Sizing the upside: signing ~500 customers/year at $500/month (~$250/month net framing in his phrasing) adds roughly $3M/year to ARR, and sticky recurring revenue creates a high enterprise-value business.
- (2026-07-19) Alex explicitly flags that recommending the pivot is against his usual advice ("I don't normally say this"), but the metrics for this specific business justify it.
- (2026-07-19) He probes scalability by asking whether the offer could be 100X'd and how many customers/week the caller could handle at max — testing capacity before endorsing a "10X spend" move.
- (2026-07-19, attribution: audience) Caller: the core business is an agency that grew well year-over-year until ~$2–2.5M, after which growth slowed, more things broke, and it required a lot of human resources.
- (2026-07-19, attribution: audience) Caller: the ~$500/month low-ticket offer has ~75% gross margin after setup; the remaining ~25% covers extras like a live-chat agent (partner-provided at ~$5/chat + $35/month base) and unlimited website tickets.
- (2026-07-19, attribution: audience) Caller: agency churn is ~2.5%/month; low-ticket churn is ~1%/month; the low-ticket offer added ~60 customers in year one and now signs ~5/week, with acquisition via word of mouth, Google Ads, SEO, and newly-successful Meta ads.
