# Every Business Is Hard

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A CFO-advisory owner (bricks-and-mortar in Texas, billboard, but most clients out-of-state) will do ~$2.9M this year, up from $2.2M last year (~30-35% annual growth), running a monthly recurring service with ~190 clients at ~20-25% margins, all grown organically off content and referrals. He wants to reach $20M, and — because fulfilling the service is "hard" — he's tempted to pivot into selling the books and courses he's already made (*Fire Your CPA*, *Tax-Free Millionaire*), which he's never marketed. Critically, he's **closed sales entirely** ("goose egg" on new clients) while he figures things out. Alex reframes the whole problem: the business isn't demand-constrained, it's **supply-constrained** — "you can't even take people," so marketing courses solves nothing. His counsel is to not abandon a valuable, sticky service to start a course business, because "hard things are hard" and the course business "will suck too, you just don't know it yet" (course customers aren't sticky, expect everything, and churn on a $17 product). The fix is **operating leverage**: use offshore/overseas talent to give each existing team member 2-3x capacity without growing internal headcount, then layer AI on top — but only after becoming a **data-first company**, because "if you don't have data, then there's no AI." He frames this AI-plus-data operating-leverage shift as a **once-in-a-generation move** (analogizing to cloud computing 25 years ago, which minted the last wave of software billionaires), where being early means the pieces don't fit together perfectly yet — and that friction is exactly where the money is made. The sequence: fix the supply constraint via offshoring → add a data layer → add the AI layer → **re-open sales** (make the new-clients number "more than zero") → and only if still capacity-rich, use the books/courses as marketing assets "in your back pocket" to drive demand later. Ends with the acquire.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that a business already doing ~$3M/year organically off content and referrals *is* marketing — the owner's belief that he "isn't marketing" is wrong; the constraint is elsewhere.
- As of March 2026, he argues this business is **supply-constrained, not demand-constrained**: "you can't even take people," so more marketing (courses, books) won't grow it — the delivery capacity is the bottleneck.
- As of March 2026, he warns against abandoning a valuable, sticky service to start a course business: low churn is the signal your service is better than you think, and "no one who has bad churn stops selling."
- As of March 2026, his recurring theme: "hard things are hard" — it never stops being hard, every business always sucks in some way; the course business will suck too (unsticky customers, high expectations, churn on cheap products) — "you just don't know it yet."
- As of March 2026, the fix for a supply constraint is **operating leverage via offshoring**: give each existing team member 2-3x leverage with external (overseas) headcount instead of increasing internal headcount, which also tends to increase margin.
- As of March 2026, to be an "AI-first" company you must first be a **data-first** company — using ChatGPT for emails is not being AI-first; AI works on data, and "if you don't have data, then there's no AI."
- As of March 2026, the architecture is sequential: complete data + architecture first, then layer AI on top with reinforcement training to actually do work.
- As of March 2026, he frames this AI/data operating-leverage shift as a **once-in-a-generation move**, analogous to cloud computing 25 years ago (CDs → cloud) that made the last wave of software billionaires; being early means friction, and that friction is where the money is.
- As of March 2026, stopping new sales ("closed down for new sales") will not grow the business — he urges re-opening sales and making the new-client number "more than zero," promising faster growth if so.
- As of March 2026, books and pre-made courses should be kept "in your back pocket" as **marketing assets** to increase demand once capacity exists — not spun up into a separate course business "cuz you just happened to have recorded it."
- As of March 2026, his full sequence for this owner: increase operating leverage through offshoring → add data layer → add AI component → re-open sales → and only if still under capacity, use the back-pocket assets for more lead gen.
