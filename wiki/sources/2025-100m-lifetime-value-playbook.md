# $100M Lifetime Value Playbook (2025)

**What it is.** A short Acquisition.com playbook by Alex Hormozi, titled "$100M Playbook: Lifetime Value" with the subtitle "Eight Ways to Get Customers to Buy More Stuff" (`PDF p. 1`). ~24 numbered content pages (27 PDF pages incl. front/back matter); raw text at `raw/books/raw/books/2025-100m-lifetime-value-playbook.md`. It defines LTV, shows how to calculate it, and lays out eight levers ("The Crazy Eight") to make each customer worth more (`PDF p. 3`).

## Thesis

Advertising is the machine that makes a business grow; LTV is the fuel (`PDF p. 6`). The business owner who can make a customer worth more to his business than to his competition wins, because he can outbid everyone in any auction for attention and effectively holds "a veritable monopoly of attention" (`PDF p. 6`). Since customer acquisition cost can only fall to zero but revenue per customer can go infinitely high, the durable edge is monetization (`PDF p. 9`). Hormozi offers eight repeatable levers so anyone can become "fluent in monetization" for any product or service (`PDF p. 10`).

## Structure and content

- **Opening story (`PDF p. 4–6`):** A May 2020 acquisition of a volatile business with one "one and done" front-end product and one marketing channel. Hormozi and the founders disagreed on which back-end product to add; they surveyed customers, Hormozi's "more of what they just bought" upsell won decisively. The upsell 2.2x'd LTV per customer and let them 5x advertising, growing revenue "from a few million per month to a few million per week" (`PDF p. 5–6`).
- **What Is LTV? / Why It Matters (`PDF p. 6`):** LTV defined as gross profit over a customer's lifespan; also called LTGP or CLV. Fuel-for-the-machine argument.
- **How To Calculate LTV (`PDF p. 7–9`):** Three-step money-math method (gross profit → transactions/churn → multiply or divide), drawn from the "lost chapters" of a prior book launch. Includes "Rich People Know How To Count Their Money" pro tip.
- **The Crazy Eight intro (`PDF p. 10`):** The eight levers listed; promise to illustrate each with local service, physical product, and digital product examples.

- **Lever #3 Increase # of Purchases — add recurring / decrease churn / follow up (`PDF p. 15–16`).**

## Key claims

- LTV is the gross profit collected over the lifespan of a customer — total money made minus everything it costs to deliver; Hormozi also calls it lifetime gross profit (LTGP), and notes CLV is the same idea (`PDF p. 6`, 2025).
- LTV calculation, Step 1: compute gross profit (revenue minus cost of delivering the good/service — not net profit). Worked product example: a $100 widget costing $20 to make/ship = $80 gross profit (80%) (`PDF p. 7`, 2025).
- LTV Step 2: find average transactions per customer, or (for recurring businesses) churn. Churn = customers lost between periods ÷ original count; e.g. losing 5 of 100 = 5% churn — and signing up new customers does not change that period's churn (`PDF p. 8–9`, 2025).
- LTV Step 3: for transactional businesses, gross profit × average transactions (e.g. $80 × 4 = $360 LTGP); for recurring businesses, gross profit ÷ churn (e.g. $2,400 ÷ 5% = $48,000 LTGP) (`PDF p. 9`, 2025).
- The Crazy Eight levers: (1) raise prices, (2) lower delivery cost, (3) upsell frequency (buy again later), (4) upsell quantity (buy more now), (5) upsell quality (premium version), (6) downsell quantity, (7) downsell quality, (8) cross-sell a different thing (`PDF p. 10`, 2025).
- Pricing affects gross profit more than any other lever, so Hormozi starts with it and runs pricing tests more than anything else; taking a 10%-profit business, raising prices 20% with sales held flat, triples the business (`PDF p. 11`, 2025).
- Practical pricing method: maximize sales-conversion-rate × LTGP; test prices every quarter (cites a Profitwell study linking profitability to pricing-test frequency); "start low then go up," nudging price ~20% every ~10 sales until sales drop sharply, then return to the sweet spot (`PDF p. 12`, 2025).
- Cost-reduction tactics: raise employee-to-customer ratio, offshore talent (can cut costs 80%+), productize delivery, shift done-for-you → done-with-you, cap usage, lifetime → annual access, in-person → remote, cut meetings, buy in bulk / prepay for 10–20% vendor discounts (`PDF p. 13–14`, 2025).
- To increase number of purchases there are three ways: add recurring, decrease churn, or run follow-up/reactivation; a quarterly promotion is his preferred long-term follow-up, balancing the give:ask ratio (`PDF p. 15–16`, 2025).
- Decreasing churn directly multiplies LTV: going from 10% to 5% monthly churn doubles LTV, since Price ÷ Churn = lifetime revenue (`PDF p. 15`, 2025).
- Cross-sell math: add the upsell's conversion rate × gross profit to existing LTV — e.g. $100 LTV plus 20% taking a $100 all-profit upsell adds $20 → $120 new LTV (`PDF p. 17`, 2025).
- Quantity upsells come in three forms — bulk (buy more at once), more often (increased delivery frequency), and bigger (more per package); offering these first, then downselling the standard offer, can produce 20%+ lifts in cash collected overnight (`PDF p. 18`, 2025).
- Downsells should be offered only to prospects who don't qualify for the main offer; Hormozi forbids his sales team from selling a qualified person a downsell to avoid cannibalizing the main offer while still capturing extra cash (`PDF p. 21–22`, 2025).

## Notable verbatim quotes

> If advertising is the machine that makes a business grow, LTV is the fuel. (`PDF p. 6`)

> If customers like your stuff, they want to buy more stuff like it! (`PDF p. 4`)

> My favorite upsell of all time: More of - or more help with - what they just bought… but with faster results, less risk, less effort, less hassle - for more money. (`PDF p. 6`)

> After all, the cost of getting a customer can only hit zero… but how much money you make from each customer can go infinitely high. (`PDF p. 9`)

> If you take a 10% profit business, raise the prices by 20%, and keep sales the same…you don't grow the business by 20%...you triple it. (`PDF p. 11`)

> Remember, the goal isn't to sell the most stuff, it's to make the most money. (`PDF p. 12`)

> if it means this or nothing, then this beats nothing! (`PDF p. 21`)

> I forbid my sales team from selling a qualified person a downsell. (`PDF p. 22`)

> If you make more than anyone else does from the same customer then you can spend more than anyone else to get them. Simple as that. (`PDF p. 25`)
