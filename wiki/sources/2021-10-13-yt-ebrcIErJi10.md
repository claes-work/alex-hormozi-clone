# Why PRETENDING to be RICH will make you POOR

## Summary
Responding to a viewer whose mentor told them to "fake it till you make it," Hormozi argues against pretending to be rich. He tells the origin story of his mustache — shaved nervously the night before an early licensee event — and recounts a licensee pulling up to see him in a dented Prius with a cracked windshield and asking "I thought you were supposed to be rich," to which he replied "you should see my bank account not my car." His core thesis: wealth is a ratio (spending less than you make), not a number; faking success costs you your own self-respect and conviction, and over-extension wipes people out in downturns like 2008.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021)
- "Fake it till you make it" is a path to being broke; at night in the mirror you know you're a fraud, and that lost integrity is very hard to recapture (2021).
- The hardest person's respect to earn is your own; once you have it, others' respect comes easily (2021).
- Entrepreneurship is a journey of beliefs and character; businesses are only a reflection/manifestation of internal change — he calls it the single greatest personal-development path (2021).
- Honesty about what you are and aren't good at wins more followers, customers, and loyal employees than pretending; people can feel a lack of conviction (2021).
- He is against buying the Ferrari or the unaffordable house to look the part; he cites Jay-Z's "if you can't buy it twice, don't buy it once" (2021).
- Fame should follow accomplishment, not precede it — Bezos and Musk are famous for what they did, not famous first and then building (2021).
- Wealth is a ratio, not a number: nearly anyone can become wealthy by choosing to live on less than they make (2021).
- Many who "faked it till they made it" before 2008 "faked it until they lost it all" because they were over-extended (2021).
