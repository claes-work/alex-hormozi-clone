# Helping a Home Services Company Build a Sales Team

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The questioner is the "right-hand woman" (and daughter) of the founder of a family-owned residential fence company (~$20M revenue, Minnesota) that wants to reach $50M. She frames the block as leadership, A-players in sales/management, and lack of SOPs/standardization. Alex first diagnoses the actual constraint: they can already fulfill demand and have plenty of paid leads coming in, so it's a **sales (throughput) constraint**, not a demand one — they have five reps and want to double to ten. He probes the hesitation to hire: it's either an irrational fear ("between your ears," just get over it) or a real cash-flow problem (then walk that decision tree) — and since the CEO doesn't report cash-flow issues, the answer is simply to hire five more reps (which they've already started). The core teaching is that a home-services business at their scale needs to build an **internal sales academy / recruiting machine** — a **parallel "supply-gen" structure** that mirrors the demand-gen funnel: app gen → app nurture → interviewing → onboarding → retention & ascension of salespeople, just as they run lead gen → nurture → sales → onboarding → retention & ascension of customers. And you must track supply-side metrics (recruiting funnel conversion) with the same rigor you track demand-side metrics (CTRs, opt-in, show and close rates), so you can "spend money reliably" on hiring talent the way you spend reliably on demand. He then runs the hiring economics: a rep generates ~$2M in revenue → ~$1M gross profit (COGS about half); the best reps are paid up to ~$200k → a **5:1 return**, which he says is one of the best returns in business (cost to acquire talent vs. lifetime gross profit per employee). Framed against the S&P: $200k into the "talent machine" yields ~$1M gross profit plus ~8x enterprise-value lift ("8 million tax-free for 200k"). The clip closes with a "softer" answer to the room: what really limits most (small-business) entrepreneurs is that world-class talent doesn't yet want to work for them — so you must **win on character**, punch above your weight class to attract talent that then pulls the business up, build the talent funnel, and "not be mean." He anchors this with the Gym Launch sale story: seeing the private-equity team where "every single person on his team was better than every single person on my team" made him vow that his next business needed a bigger vision to attract a higher tier of talent. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, before hiring or fixing anything, diagnose the binding constraint: if you can fulfill current demand and have leads flowing, the constraint is sales throughput (people to answer leads and close), not demand.
- As of March 2026, hesitation to hire reduces to one of two causes: an irrational fear "between your ears" (just get over it) or a genuine cash-flow problem (then follow the cash-flow decision tree); if the CEO isn't reporting cash-flow issues, hire the reps.
- As of March 2026, a home-services business at this scale (~five reps, all paid leads) needs to build an internal "sales academy" / recruiting machine rather than hire ad hoc.
- As of March 2026, you should build a **parallel supply-gen structure** that mirrors the demand-gen funnel: app gen → app nurture → interviewing → onboarding → retention & ascension of salespeople, matching lead gen → nurture → sales → onboarding → retention & ascension of customers.
- As of March 2026, you must track recruiting/supply-side metrics with the same rigor as demand-side metrics (CTR, opt-in, show %, close %), so you can spend money reliably on acquiring talent the way you spend reliably on demand gen.
- As of March 2026, the hiring economics in this case: a rep drives ~$2M revenue and ~$1M gross profit (COGS ≈ half); paying the best reps up to ~$200k is roughly a 5:1 return.
- As of March 2026, cost to acquire talent versus lifetime gross profit per employee is "some of the best returns you get in the business"; $200k into the talent machine beats $200k in the S&P — ~$1M gross profit plus ~8x enterprise value ("8 million tax-free for 200k").
- As of March 2026, the deeper limiter for most (small-business) entrepreneurs is that they "aren't good enough as entrepreneurs" yet — not enough achieved, not sustained long enough — so world-class talent doesn't believe in their vision or want to work for them.
- As of March 2026, business at the highest level reduces to "find world-class people and get out of their way" (citing Branson and Steve Jobs); the problem is that such talent (making ~$20M bonuses) won't work for a small business unless you win on character.
- As of March 2026, to attract talent you must "punch above your weight class" on character — don't be a hothead, don't turn on a dime, if you have a bad day no one knows — because that talent then pulls the business up.
- As of March 2026, seeing the private-equity team's every member out-class his own during the Gym Launch sale made him resolve that his next venture needed a significantly bigger vision to attract a higher level of talent.
