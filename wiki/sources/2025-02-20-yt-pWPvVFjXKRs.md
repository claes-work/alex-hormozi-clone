# I Tried Elon Musk's Productivity Techniques

## Summary

Alex Hormozi walks through a five-step process for allocating time, attention, and resources that he says he "stole ruthlessly from Elon Musk" (Musk's engineering "algorithm"): question the requirement, delete, optimize, accelerate timelines, and automate. He illustrates each step with his own operating stories — killing a podcast manager's "make shorter podcasts" idea by demanding it be tied to platform growth, reallocating his 20-person media team toward Instagram and YouTube (87% of his traffic) and away from TikTok, and pulling deadlines forward to get "two weeks worth of work" done in a day. His core thesis: the number-one job of a CEO/entrepreneur is prioritization of scarce resources, and beginners stall because they try to do everything instead of ruthlessly cutting.

## Key claims

The five steps are **Musk's algorithm** (Alex explicitly credits Elon Musk); the applications, examples, and the "rule of 300" reference are **Alex's**.

- **Step 1 — Question the requirement (Musk's step; Alex's sub-framework).** Ask "why are we doing this?" Alex adds three sub-questions to interrogate any requirement: (1) *what does that mean* (define the term / quantify it), (2) *how do you know that* (demand the evidence/stat), (3) *why do I care* (force a link to business/platform growth). If someone can't explain how a task translates to growth, it doesn't matter.
- **Step 2 — Delete (Musk's step; Alex's application).** After questioning, hack away — Alex cites Musk walking into Twitter asking "why do we need this?" and deleting. He claims most people "can probably get away with deleting 90% of the work" because it doesn't drive the business. Deleting also **eliminates switching costs** — you recover both the time spent on the task and the time lost switching between tasks (one of the biggest inefficiencies for human brains).
- **Step 3 — Optimize (Musk's step; Alex's application).** Once you can see the activities that generate output, get more throughput — this works the same for sales, editing, and advertising. Alex extends it to the *platform* level, not just the activity level: allocate resources where returns are highest.
- **Step 4 — Accelerate timelines (Musk's step; Alex's application).** Pull deadlines forward — question the estimate ("it takes three hours, it's 2:30, why not by 5:30?"). Two levers to make things happen faster: **move up deadlines** and **increase communication frequency** (meeting daily instead of weekly extracts more output because people default to the gap between check-ins). Claims this is "how in real time people can move 14 times faster."
- **Step 5 — Automate (Musk's step; Alex's application).** Last, not first — either have someone else do it or use software/AI/robots. Alex stresses automate comes *last* so you don't automate work that should have been deleted.
- **Alex's framing claims:** The number-one job of the CEO/entrepreneur is **prioritization** — "strategy is prioritization of resources," and the fewer resources you have, the more sparing you must be. Beginners stall because they try to figure out everything at once instead of sequencing (his "about page → drive traffic → fix conversion/VSL" example). Reject the **sunk-cost fallacy**: when new data says you're wrong, the only thing worse than not knowing is knowing and doing nothing.
- **Alex's data point (as of recording):** 87% of his traffic comes from two platforms (Instagram and YouTube), yet editors were evenly distributed across all platforms; Instagram now drives more traffic to acquisition.com than YouTube. TikTok produced 1 opt-in in 4 days off ~1M followers vs. 187 opt-ins from Instagram in the same window — despite more posts on TikTok (3M followers) than Instagram (1M). Fix: reallocate a 20-person team toward Instagram/YouTube (e.g., 8/8, then 1 each on the other four) instead of evenly.
- **Prioritization-in-crisis example:** Fixing a broken department by itemizing three problems and refusing to discuss #2 or #3 until #1 was fixed (each took ~3 days).
