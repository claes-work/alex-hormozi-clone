# Niche So Boring Nobody Competes (80 Deals/Yr)

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a local architecture firm that specializes in assisted-living conversions and gets about 80 referral deals per year. Alex says the firm should win on one of three value vectors: speed, risk reduction, or ease. Because the sale is logical and price-sensitive, he recommends using statistics about costly mistakes to reframe the fee as insurance against larger downstream losses. He also reframes the business around centers of influence: consultants and referral partners are the recurring acquisition nodes, even if the end customer buys one project at a time.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Zack / coachee** - architecture-firm owner. His niche, referral volume, customer profile, and service details are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- In a price-driven service, the firm should lead with speed, risk reduction, or ease.
- For this architecture niche, speed matters because time affects returns on capital.
- Risk-reduction messaging should quantify costly mistakes and delays that can happen if the plan process is mishandled.
- The sale should be framed as dollars-and-cents logic rather than broad emotional branding.
- Statistics about common downstream problems can create fear, uncertainty, and doubt around the cheaper alternative.
- Recurring revenue may not be natural in this business, but reoccurring referral flow can create similar stability.
- Referral partners and consultants should be treated as the real acquisition customers.
- The firm should build a customer journey for consultants so they introduce the firm with the right pricing and value frame.
- Affiliate structures can include selling a first step, percentage commission on the whole service, or lead-gen/kickback arrangements, subject to legal limits.
