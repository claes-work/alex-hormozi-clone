# Your New Ideas Are Killing Your Business

## Summary

A short interview clip (a host/interviewer prompts; Alex carries the substance — only
Alex-attributed material below) on why an entrepreneur's own new ideas are the main
threat to their business, and the personal discipline system Alex uses to contain them.
He opens with two borrowed anchors: a billionaire friend who runs a family office told
him "sometimes the most profitable thing you can do is wait," and a recent Jeff Bezos
talk in Italy where Bezos said he could fill a whiteboard with 100 ideas in 30 minutes
that would destroy his company. Alex's takeaway: every entrepreneur "absolutely" has
enough ideas to kill their business, so the job is to prevent that.

His mechanic is a personal document — "Alex's big list of ideas" — where he fully
fleshes out each idea (with a linked memo covering how it works, what it'd make, etc.),
then keeps living his life and only revisits the list when he sees the team is rested and
energized. Distance reveals which ideas were bad: you get "emotionally charged and sold"
on an idea while in the throes of it, but space lets you hear it the way outsiders would.
He pairs this with a Blind Side analogy (the "if you still want the shirt a week later,
go back and buy it" rule): if you forget an idea it wasn't good; the ones that gnaw at
you and keep you up at night are the ones worth fleshing out — but he still releases work
only "at a rate the business can handle," which builds respect for the org's output
capacity.

The clip's second half pivots from idea-restraint to *why* capacity is the real limiter:
manpower/talent is almost always the constraint, but A-level talent doesn't want to work
for you yet — so much of the entrepreneurial journey is building proof that earns you the
right to attract them. He closes on pattern recognition (why you scale faster the second
and third time), the sales-org "vertical stack" of archetypes, the "one missing brick"
that can block a business for years, and hard conversations as the true bottleneck.

**Distinctness vs. the shiny-object / focus / "more before new" thread (core existing
thread):** heavy conceptual overlap — this is another restate of idea-discipline. What's
distinct: (1) the concrete *"big list of ideas"* document-with-linked-memos mechanic;
(2) the *Blind Side shirt test* as a keep/discard heuristic; (3) framing idea-restraint
as respecting the business's *output capacity* / releasing work at a survivable rate; and
(4) the Bezos "100 ideas that would destroy my company" and "most profitable thing is to
wait" anchors. The talent-limiter / pattern-recognition / hard-conversations material is
adjacent to existing scaling and hiring threads rather than to shiny-object per se.

## Key claims

All paraphrased from Alex unless quoted, dated 2026-02-17.

- **Your own ideas are the threat.** Every entrepreneur has more than enough new ideas to
  kill their business; the job is to prevent that from happening. Anchored to Jeff Bezos
  (talk in Italy) saying he could write 100 ideas in 30 minutes that would destroy his
  company, and to a family-office friend's line that "sometimes the most profitable thing
  you can do is wait" — doing nothing is itself an action.
- ★ **The "big list of ideas" document.** Alex keeps a personal doc ("Alex's big list of
  ideas"); every time he has an idea he fully fleshes it out and links a memo (how it
  works, what it'd make, etc.), then keeps living his life. He only pulls the list back
  out when he sees the team looks rested, smiling, with "a glint in their eye."
- ★ **Distance de-risks ideas.** In the throes of an idea you get emotionally charged and
  "sold" on it; a little space lets you hear it the way other people would — and some
  ideas you were sure would be huge later look terrible.
- ★ **Blind Side shirt test.** Like Sandra Bullock's line about not buying a shirt
  immediately but going back for it if you still think about it a week or two later: if
  you forget an idea, it wasn't a good idea; the ones that keep gnawing at you and keep
  you up at night are the ones worth fleshing out further.
- ★ **Release work at a rate the business can handle.** Even good ideas must be metered to
  the org's throughput; this gives you respect for the business's output capacity.
- **Manpower/talent is almost always the limiter.** This is why billionaires on stage say
  "get a good team and get out of their way" — but the catch is good people don't want to
  work for you (entrepreneurs generally) yet.
- **You must earn the right to A-level talent.** The bootstrap phase exists to prove you
  can attract true A-players. With the best AI person in the world you could build a
  multi-billion-dollar company in six months — but that person won't work for you yet.
  Much of the entrepreneurial journey is creating the proof that lets you attract the
  talent required to build the next thing (e.g., the talent they could attract *after*
  selling Gym Launch vs. during was night and day).
- **You become a collector of talent.** If you treat people right throughout, the stars
  stay and follow you from venture to venture; the bench of stars compounds, and they
  don't want to work elsewhere because of the team and culture. Early on you're the only
  star — "maybe a half star."
- **Why you scale faster the 2nd/3rd time: pattern recognition.** The physics of business
  don't change — your pattern recognition improves. You acquire it by getting "punched in
  the face" repeatedly by bad hires until you can pattern-match a good one instantly.
- **The sales-org "vertical stack."** Alex says he knows the full archetype stack of a
  sales-driven org up to a few hundred reps — SDR, SDR manager, closer, closer manager,
  director of sales, VP of sales — each a different archetype with different skills and
  behaviors. Without that experience you hire and fire VP after VP until one finally works.
- **The "one missing brick."** Business scales like stacking bricks by level; a single
  missing brick can block you for years because you're lacking it and don't know what it
  looks like when it's right.
- **Hard conversations are the real bottleneck.** The skill is being willing to have a
  hard conversation (you may like the person but they're not doing the job) and closing
  that gap fast. Answering "why don't people do what they want to do?" — a lot of them
  simply can't have a hard conversation.
