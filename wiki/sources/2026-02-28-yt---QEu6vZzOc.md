# Does Enterprise Value Matter?

## Summary
Short rebuttal clip. Alex responds to a common objection — that enterprise value is "fictional numbers that can't liquidate" and that "cash flow, hard cash" is what really matters — by arguing why enterprise value matters *once you've reached a certain level*. His core point is that the cash-flow-first objection comes "from the position of already not having enough." Once a business actually *has* enterprise value, it has typically already generated (or is able to generate) more than enough cash flow for the owner — and all that excess cash flow is transferred to the owner in a **tax-inefficient** manner. Above the level where the owner's needs (rent, house, parents' house, cars, kids' schools, vacations) are already met, the leftover cash keeps coming and the smartest move is the **most tax-efficient wealth-building vehicle: enterprise value** (growth in net worth that isn't hit by taxes). He notes a high-value enterprise is also an asset you can borrow against — loans, lines of credit, raising capital. His framing is explicitly **level-based**: "there's levels to this game… if you want to move up levels, you need to play a different game." For someone who doesn't know where next month's rent is coming from, enterprise value is (bluntly) irrelevant; but for the business owners he makes his content for — those trying to scale — enterprise value becomes "increasingly important." The bigger you get and the more your (and your circle's) needs are satisfied, "at some point it becomes about the game," and in the game of business, **net worth is the objective measure**. He caveats that business isn't the only game worth playing in life.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, the "I just want cold hard cash flow" objection comes "from the position of already not having enough" — it's a needs-not-yet-met mindset, not a wealth-maximizing one.
- As of February 2026, once a business actually *has* enterprise value, it has usually already generated (or can generate) more than sufficient cash flow for the owner — so all the *excess* cash flow gets transferred to the owner in a **tax-inefficient** way.
- As of February 2026, above the level where the owner's needs and their circle's needs are covered (rent, paid-off house, parents' house, cars, kids' schooling, vacations), the most tax-efficient vehicle for building wealth is **enterprise value** — net-worth growth that isn't reduced by taxes.
- As of February 2026, a high-value enterprise is itself an asset: you can take loans and lines of credit against it and raise capital on it (Stripe cited — not publicly traded, yet investors will fund it so founders get liquidity).
- As of February 2026, enterprise value is **level-dependent**: "there's levels to this game… if you want to move up levels, you need to play a different game." If you don't know where next month's rent is coming from, enterprise value is irrelevant; for owners trying to scale, it becomes increasingly important.
- As of February 2026, the bigger a business gets, the more the owner's and their circle's needs are satisfied — and "at some point it becomes about the game." In the game of business, **net worth is the objective measure** (with the caveat that business isn't the only game in life worth winning).
