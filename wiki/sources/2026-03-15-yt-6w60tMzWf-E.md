# You Need to Pay More to Get Better Talent

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A trade-show-booth builder (~$10M revenue, ~30% margins, targeting $20–25M in 18–24 months) says his single bottleneck is "key man and manpower" — he can't fill skilled trade roles (welders, carpenters) in a supply-constrained market where everyone is "paying top dollar." Alex walks him through the math to show the constraint is a self-imposed pay ceiling, not a real one. The owner's new welding/fabrication line brought in ~$750k on the owner's own labor; Alex conservatively discounts a hire to ~$500k revenue, nets out ~30% material cost to ~$350k, and frames the gross profit at stake per role at roughly $200k/year. Against that, the owner is offering a non-certified welder ~$50/hr (~$100k/year). Alex's point: if paying $150–180k/year (vs. $100k) reliably fills the role and attracts *more qualified* applicants, you are buying ~$200k of gross profit — refusing to do so is leaving the money on the table. He reinforces with a parallel: a boutique dentist making ~$700k gross profit per dentist per year who spent only $2,500/month on ads and couldn't hire — Alex told him to pay a headhunter $150k/person; profit dips from $4.6M to ~$4M this year, then adds ~$2.8M/year after. He reframes hiring as an *investment trade*: "you give me 600 grand a day [one-time cost], and for the rest of your life you're going to make 2.8 million in profit per year" — nobody would refuse that stock, yet owners refuse it in their own businesses. He introduces the formalism **LTGP:CAT** — lifetime gross profit per employee to cost of acquiring talent — the exact mirror of the customer-side LTGP:CAC (e.g., $20k recruiter + $150k/yr × 6-year tenure = ~$1M all-in to earn ~$200k/yr for 6 years). Macro caveat: for the trades specifically he expects "a compression and then a huge drop off the floor," but for the next few years it's a supply-constrained market where supply holds the upper hand, so talent costs will keep rising and you must pay up. His enduring thesis: "the best way to beat the market is to find A-player talent and pay them well" — you also retain them longer. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's core thesis: "the best way to beat the market is to find A-player talent and pay them well" — paying above market also increases retention.
- As of March 2026, the owner's stated bottleneck (skilled trade hires, "key man and manpower") is really a pay-ceiling problem: he *has* the money and the margins, but is offering a non-certified welder ~$50/hr (~$100k/yr) in a market where certified welders can reach ~$85/hr (~$180k/yr).
- As of March 2026, the hiring decision is settled by unit economics: a hire brings in ~$500k revenue → ~$350k after ~30% material cost → ~$200k gross profit per role per year is "on the line"; paying $150–180k instead of $100k to reliably fill it (and attract more qualified applicants) is clearly worth it.
- As of March 2026, hiring is an investment trade, not a cost: framed as a stock, "you give me 600 grand [one-time], and for the rest of your life you're going to make 2.8 million in profit per year" — an obvious yes, yet owners irrationally refuse the identical trade inside their own businesses.
- As of March 2026, dentist parallel: a boutique practice making ~$700k gross profit per dentist per year but spending only $2,500/month on ads couldn't hire; using a $150k/person headhunter dips profit from $4.6M to ~$4M this year, then adds ~$2.8M/year after.
- As of March 2026, Alex formalizes talent economics as **LTGP:CAT** — lifetime gross profit per employee to cost of acquiring talent — the mirror image of customer-side LTGP:CAC; e.g., $20k recruiter + $150k/yr over a 6-year average tenure ≈ $1M all-in to earn ~$200k/yr for those 6 years.
- As of March 2026, macro/trades caveat: Alex expects "a compression and then a huge drop off the floor" eventually, but for the next few years the trades are a supply-constrained market where supply has the upper hand, so acquiring and paying good tradesmen will get more expensive — you have to accept it and pay up.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes many people in the trades "are not very good at business" and won't think this way, so an owner who *does* run the LTGP:CAT math on talent has an edge.
