# The Hidden Cost of Drinking

## Summary
Short solo monologue in which Alex argues that drinking is an under-priced opportunity cost — "it costs you two days, the day you drink and the day you recover," plus the trailing weeks of cleaning up "the stupid thing you did while you were drunk." He frames abstinence as a competitive advantage stacked during high-output eras of his life, and opens with a Muhammad Ali training-clip he asked his team to pull (Ali on "dodging the nightclubs and the parties," being in bed by 9pm, and not drinking). **Personal practice / biographical detail:** Alex says the longest he ever went without drinking was **2 years** while scaling Gym Launch — the ~20-month run "from zero to 4 and 1/2 million a month, 4.4 to be technical" — during which he didn't drink at all; once things settled he decided he was willing to drink again. Now, with acquisition.com "scaling like crazy," he and Leila have again jointly decided to stop drinking "for the foreseeable," and he expects to continue until things "feel chill again," then maybe partake. That was in his 20s; this is now in his 30s. His decision test: "argue me the opposite" — that drinking more would make him more likely to hit his goals — which he says is unwinnable. Because everything now "compounds and is amplified," he wants to stack every advantage: sleep on time, don't drink, get in shape, do as many repetitions as possible, and humble himself to more-experienced people. The clip then pivots into a related mindset thread: at 25–30 you have "peak processing power," but he's "way smarter now" because he's learned more — someone can beat you on raw processing while still knowing more than you, especially if they're further ahead. He argues for choosing humility over ego ("I would rather humble myself rather than have the world humble me," citing Matthew 23:12), warns that self-exaltation reads as insecurity and that "your feelings can lie to you" about how well a behavior serves your long-term goals, and closes on reference-group theory: your five comparison people are "one of the number one correlations" with the goals you achieve, so "sometimes you have to like your goals more than you like your friends." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
