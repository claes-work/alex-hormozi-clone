# Charge Premium Prices in a Commoditized Market

## Summary

Alex Hormozi explains how commoditized businesses can improve offers and lead magnets. He
says comparable offers need to win on speed, reduced risk, or ease, then charge a premium
for the improvement. He also explains attraction-offer mechanisms from *Money Models*,
lead-magnet types, and why softer asks can scale better than direct workshop ads.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Many businesses have weak offers, weak lead magnets, or both.
- In easily comparable categories such as home services, a business should win on speed,
  reduced risk, ease, or multiple of those.
- Premium pricing is justified when the business truly reduces time, risk, or effort for
  the customer.
- A useful offer question is what it would take to get the outcome in one third the time.
- Risk can be reduced through warranties, guarantees, or insurance against the customer's
  largest fear.
- Ease can be improved through intake, onboarding, pre-work, and customer-experience
  preparation.
- *Money Models* attraction mechanisms can wrap the current offer: giveaways, win your
  money back, decoy, pay less now/pay more later, and buy X get Y free.
- In buy X get Y free, Alex prefers giving away more free units than the buyer pays for,
  while adjusting the paid-unit price.
- Businesses should check advertising laws and attorneys before running promotions.
- Direct workshop ads may have higher immediate ROAS, but the Scaling Roadmap lead magnet
  has higher overall ROAS and scales better across a longer time horizon, according to
  Alex.
- Large scale often requires softening the ask and building the conversion process around
  that softer front end.
- Three lead-magnet types are revealing a problem, giving a trial of the solution, or
  giving one step of a multi-step process.
- A strong lead magnet should give something people actually want, ideally something other
  people charge for.
