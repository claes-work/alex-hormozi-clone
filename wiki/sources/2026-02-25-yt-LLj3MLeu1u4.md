# The Only Way to Win in Business

## Summary

A short solo talk (apparently the close of a live presentation) in which Hormozi argues that business success comes down to resource allocation: putting the most resources (time and money) toward the fewest things that yield the highest returns. He defines strategy at its core as the prioritization of resources and urges the audience to walk away not with a long to-do list but with the one, two, or three right things to pursue. He illustrates with his own habit of setting a single goal per year, and closes with a plug for his free 10-stage "zero to $100M" roadmap at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) The fundamental job of an entrepreneur is to better allocate the resources they have — primarily time and money — to get returns.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) The best-rewarded entrepreneurs allocate the most resources toward the fewest things that get the highest returns (he stresses this is the core principle worth repeating).
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Trying to execute 100 tactics is a trap: you won't be able to, and attempting it gets you into trouble. The goal of working with him is not to leave with a to-do list but with one very clear thing.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) He typically sets one goal per year. Examples given: last year's goal was to launch a book and do $100M; the year before was to launch the advisory practice.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) If you do one thing right, everything else falls away — the alpha/leverage is in opportunity selection, in making sure the fewest things are the ones that get the highest returns.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) If you work every day but the business isn't growing, you're picking the wrong things to work on.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Strategy at its core is prioritization of resources.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Committing to "one thing" doesn't mean doing only one action — it means doing ~20 things to reinforce that one outcome "from every angle" (getting help, calling friends, hiring) so there's no way it fails.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) He promotes a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ that fewer than 1% of companies finish, which he has now done multiple times; it breaks each stage down by eight business functions — what the constraint feels like, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate — across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar businesses. Available at acquisition.com/roadmap.
