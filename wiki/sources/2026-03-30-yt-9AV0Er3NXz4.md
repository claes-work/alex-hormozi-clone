# How To Overcome the Fear of Selling

## Summary
In an interview-style clip, Alex Hormozi is asked why people are uncomfortable selling and promoting themselves. He splits the discomfort into fear of public promotion and fear of asking for money, and argues both dissolve when you "state the facts and tell the truth," outwork your self-doubt through volume, and reframe your apparent weaknesses as damaging admissions that actually increase persuasion. An interviewer poses the questions and scenarios (about manipulation, being a one-man band); only Alex's answers are captured here.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames selling discomfort as two buckets: fear of public promotion/embarrassment, and fear of asking someone for money.
- As of March 2026, Alex says imposter syndrome mostly afflicts people who haven't done the reps; you "outwork your self-doubt" — after doing something ~20 times, conviction rises because you genuinely know the client's problem and that you can solve it.
- As of March 2026, Alex calls "state the facts and tell the truth" one of his core rules of persuasion; most anxiety comes from misrepresentation, so removing it removes the anxiety.
- As of March 2026, Alex treats rejection fear as overstated — the worst answer is "no," and sales, like dating, is a numbers game; he cites his "Rule of 100" (have 100 conversations) as habituation that lowers anxiety, comparing it to exposure therapy for phobias.
- As of March 2026, Alex defines selling as "arranging the variables to increase the likelihood a sale occurs," and says the difference between help and manipulation is intention — the same influence variables are used either way.
- As of March 2026, Alex advocates the "damaging admission" as a persuasion component: openly stating your weaknesses (e.g., a gym's bad parking and old equipment) makes people trust your positive claims more.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues every competitive position has an advantage — a solo operator sells access to the CEO/owner doing the work, while a large incumbent sells proven track record and process; you "play what you got."
- As of March 2026, Alex says setting honest expectations up front creates a clean relationship, and that some of the best deals are the ones you never do because the mismatched customers would have become "calamities" later.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises: if the facts aren't compelling enough, do free work until they are.
- Context: the interviewer (unnamed) supplies the objections and a story about a one-man-band friend; these are not Alex's claims.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "One of my big rules of persuasion is state the facts and tell the truth."

> "If you've done it 20 times and you're like, I know what's going to happen next and I know what your problem is and I also know I can solve it — your conviction goes up because you're not exaggerating anything."

> "The worst thing they can say is no. And then you say, okay, fine, no worries. Not a big deal. I'll just ask somebody else. If anything, sales is like dating. It's a numbers game."

> "If you're afraid of spiders, the fastest way to cure spiders is lock you in a room with spiders. You'll have a couple panic attacks, you'll pass out, you wake back up, the spiders are still there... and eventually you're just like, all right, I'm used to it."

> "I see the process of selling as arranging the variables to increase the likelihood a sale occurs."

> "The difference between help and manipulation is intention. You use the exact same variables to influence someone's purchasing decision."

> "That's actually more compelling because you have a damaging admission, which is one of the components of persuasion. Just tell people all the things that are not good, because when you tell them the good things, they'll actually believe them more."

> "We don't have the newest equipment, our parking is terrible, we have a tiny location, but we have the best workouts on this side of the Mississippi."

> "If the facts aren't compelling enough, do the free work so the facts are compelling."

> "Every position has an advantage. There's no position on the board that doesn't have advantage. You just got to play what you got."

> "Some of the best deals are the ones you never do."
