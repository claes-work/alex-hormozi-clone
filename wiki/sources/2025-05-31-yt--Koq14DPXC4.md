# Building a $2,300,000/yr Business for a Stranger in 57 Mins

## Summary

A live-build/coaching session ("Cash, guys" format) in which Alex Hormozi
diagnoses and re-engineers the business of Annie, a stranger who owns Amy Lash
and Beauty — a chain of seven eyelash-extension salons doing ~$2.3M/yr at ~28%
net margin. Annie's problems: lead generation, inconsistent manager/staff
training, and equity partners who have dropped responsibilities while she does
most of the unpaid work. Alex works through partnership resolution, surge
pricing, rebooking (BAM FAM), a membership offer, an upsell sequence, a staff
career path, ad attribution, and lead follow-up, then re-prioritizes everything
with his ICE framework. The stranger's situation is context; only Alex's advice
is persona data.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **Book the next appointment while the customer is still in front of you** — his
  "BAM FAM" (Book A Meeting From A Meeting, credited to Shiro Savata). Don't rely
  on customers remembering to rebook; get it on the calendar now. Getting a
  four-to-five-week cadence down to three weeks can roughly double annual visits.
- **Sell the rebooking before or during the service, not after** — once someone
  has received the value they want to leave; sell before they've "gotten the
  value." A captive customer in the chair for ~60 minutes has time to book.
- Getting a customer to come back and buy again is a **sales conversation** even
  though it "walks and talks like" an administrative/scheduling one; treat it with
  the respect a sale deserves and train staff on it.
- **Surge/weekend pricing**: raise price ~10-20% on peak days (Thu/Fri/Sat) when
  demand is highest and supply is constrained. Frame it as peak vs. discounted
  off-peak days to smooth the demand curve and improve utilization. "Everything is
  about how you frame things."
- On low ad spend: Annie spends ~$12k/yr (~0.5% of revenue) on Google ads; Alex
  says that's far too little given she's likely "on a gold mine" with near-zero
  customer acquisition cost — she should spend much more.
- **Front-end promo offers (the $99 set) are lead magnets** designed to break even;
  the money is in the upsells and the membership. Focus attention on the step-1 →
  step-2 take rate, not the near-100% close rate on pre-sold walk-ins.
- **Memberships: give away free value rather than discount** — cheaper for a
  low-COGS business and more compelling. Each bonus should be worth roughly the
  whole price on its own ("an offer so good people feel stupid saying no").
- **Assumed/default close via past behavior**: reframe the membership upsell for
  existing repeat customers as "you should already have been getting this, let's
  just formalize it" — modeled on the organ-donor opt-out study. Can lift upsell
  close rates from ~20-40% toward 80-90%.
- **Prepayment (6/12-month) is valuable** because prepayers churn less, it pulls
  cash forward, and it locks the customer in. Sequence: sell membership at the
  chair, then at checkout offer to prepay to "save money" / "have today free."
- **Partnerships**: everything you want is on the other side of a few hard
  conversations; resolve the biggest time-drain first or nothing else gets
  executed. Three paths offered: (A) increased profit share + pay yourself market
  rate for the manager role you're doing; (B) a **shotgun offer** (I'll buy you out
  at X, or accept X to be bought out); (C) an **equity swap** — each partner takes
  full ownership of a location they co-own. Alex recommends the equity swap as the
  cleanest break that preserves the friendship (an in-kind, tax-free swap).
- **Pay yourself market rate for the role you fill** so returns aren't artificially
  inflated; factor the cost of backfilling your skill into the return-on-capital of
  opening new locations. "Nail it then scale it."
- **The fastest way to five locations isn't the fastest way to 50** — going slower
  early to build infrastructure/foundation lets you go aggressively from four to
  10 to 50 later. His own regret: over-aggressive expansion with unvetted partners.
- **Staff career path**: build 6+ title/pay tiers (junior/normal/senior tech →
  junior/normal/senior artist), raising every ~3 months; unlock a new billable
  skill with each tier so both pay and earning capacity rise. Prefer frequent
  small bumps over large infrequent ones.
- **Equity vs. profit share for key staff**: equity = risk + control + profit +
  sale proceeds; they usually only want profit, so give a profit-share bonus, not
  ownership. Offer a future path to run a new location for 10% (yr 1) / 20% (yr 2)
  of that facility's profit, contingent on qualifying/competing — retains control.
- **Ad attribution built into the front desk**: a walk-in checklist (ask for ID
  first as it's least intimidating → confirm card on file → sign-in with a
  "where'd you hear about us" dropdown and referral code). "The longer you stay in
  business, the more you are compensated by the quality of your decisions, not how
  hard or how long you work."
- **Training is repetition/role-play**, not a script handout: daily ~30-min
  role-plays, correct on the spot, repeat. Service businesses are recruiting,
  hiring, and training businesses; the arbitrage is training low-skill people up to
  command a higher market rate.
- **ICE prioritization** (Impact, Confidence, Ease) — maps to his value equation —
  used to reorder all initiatives. Strategy defined as "prioritization of
  resources": allocate to the fewest things with the highest probability of high
  returns.
- **Bonus lever ("2.5")**: hire someone at ~$40k/yr to do one thing — call inbound
  leads within a minute. Called a potential 2-3x for the business; promoted to
  priority zero.
