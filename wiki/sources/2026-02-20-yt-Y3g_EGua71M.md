# Helping a Business Owner Choose Between Two Avatars

## Summary

Short live coaching Q&A. A business owner (audience member; not Alex) runs **two
separate brands under one umbrella** serving two different avatars:

1. **Church motion backgrounds** — recurring digital-license product, ~$348–$708/year
   per church, ~2,000 churches, ~$750K/year, ~60% margin. He is "going all in" on this
   side. *(All figures self-reported.)*
2. **Video-editing assets** (color presets, film transitions, burns, grains) sold to
   video editors — one-time purchase, ~$350K/year, but he only works ~1 hr/week on it
   (~$6,000/hour). He "doesn't really care about that side."

He has ~170,000 leads accumulated over 7 years, and asks Alex about (a) the right
**money model** to scale, and (b) whether to add a **higher-ticket church offer**.

Alex's core steer: don't fragment. Rather than pick "between" avatars in any elaborate
value-vs-cost analysis, he validates concentrating on the recurring church side (the one
the owner cares about and that has better economics) and **just doubling down** — "you
could also just do more of this thing." He rejects the high-ticket church-consulting
idea as a *different business*. The scaling lever is demand, not supply: make more,
better content. And the profit from this kind of digital/service business is not all
yours — it must be reinvested into **talent and brand** (the "cap ex" of these
businesses), with the real bottleneck being recruiting media talent when you don't yet
have a brand.

Note on framing: despite the title, Alex does **not** apply an explicit avatar-selection
formula here. The decision is resolved on money-model grounds (recurring > one-time,
plus owner preference), which only partially overlaps his standard avatar thread
(biggest value-vs-cost gap). See "Overlap" below.

## Key claims (paraphrased unless quoted; Alex-attributed; dated 2026-02-20)

- **Don't invent a second thing to make more money — do more of the working thing.**
  There are many times where the reflex is "to make more money I could come up with
  another thing," when you could just do more of the thing that already works and figure
  out how to get more of it. If "how do I get more?" isn't immediately apparent, *that*
  is the hard problem worth solving — not adding a new offer.
- **Choosing between two avatars → concentrate on the one with recurring revenue and
  that you actually care about.** He affirms going "all in" on the recurring church side
  over the one-time editor side (the owner already leaned this way).
- **Reject the adjacent high-ticket offer if it becomes a different business.** A
  high-touch church offering (helping volunteer teams install better processes/visual
  experience) would make him "a church consultant" — a different business — so he
  wouldn't even explore it here.
- **Constraint diagnosis: demand, not supply.** On the church side the owner has plenty
  of content ideas and is delegating; the growth path is "relatively straightforward" —
  increase demand by making more and better content.
- **Profit in low-capex digital/service businesses is not all yours.** In a business
  with more cash flow, less stickiness than SaaS, and low capex, year-end profit should
  go primarily to two places: **(1) talent** and **(2) brand**. Reinvesting in the
  brand's reputation is what lets you keep growing next year — that reinvestment in
  *people and brand* is effectively the capex of a service business.
- **In the AI era, hire fewer, better people — and use AI instances instead of
  headcount.** For cranking out content he'd "be thinking more in Claude and GPT
  instances and less in head count." Execution is becoming commoditized, so leverage
  shifts to skill, **taste**, and strategic thinking (until even that is automated).
- **Taste is the defensible thing to hire for.** Competitors making "AI-type feeling
  stuff that doesn't feel good" is precisely why customers come for your taste; hiring a
  couple people to increase output of *good* content is fine. ★ (see novelty note)
- **The real bottleneck is recruiting media talent without a brand.** Anyone good at
  media can out-earn what most employers pay, so attracting them before you have a brand
  is the hard part of this growth path.
- **Nothing wrong with a single offer if it isn't capping LTV.** "There's nothing wrong
  with having just one if it's not getting in the way of LTV." Simplicity has elegance;
  a low-ticket product sold at high volume (his example: Skool at $99, "just a lot of
  them") is a legitimate model.
- **Obvious adjacent expansion: international / Spanish-language.** With ~300,000 US
  churches (vs. his ~2,000 customers) there's huge headroom, and expanding into Spanish
  / other countries is low-difficulty growth.

## Overlap with existing avatar-selection thread

Alex's canonical avatar-selection criterion elsewhere is "pick the avatar with the
biggest gap between the value you can deliver and the cost/effort to serve them"
(value-vs-cost gap). This clip does **not** invoke that formula. Instead the two-avatar
choice is resolved on **money-model + owner-preference** grounds: recurring revenue
beats one-time, and concentrate where the founder actually wants to invest. That
"recurring > one-time; don't fragment; double down over adding offers" angle is the
distinct contribution here; the underlying "don't add a new thing, do more of the
working thing" and "single offer is fine if it doesn't cap LTV" points reinforce
existing focus/simplicity material rather than adding new criteria.
