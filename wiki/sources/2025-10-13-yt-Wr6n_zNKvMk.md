# Building a $1,000,000 Business for a Stranger in 69 Minutes

## Summary

In this live-build coaching session, Alex Hormozi works with Philip, an ex-Amazon software engineer who left tech to start Garby Disposal Services, a residential trash/recycling/yard-waste hauler doing $642K revenue at a negative 23% net margin (down $151K last year). Alex diagnoses the business as spread too thin — two avatars (scatter homes vs. HOAs) and five acquisition channels — and prescribes radical focus: pick one avatar (scatter), one channel (door-to-door), and rebuild the front-end offer so upfront cash collected exceeds fully-loaded cost (labor + commission + bin). The bulk of the session is Alex constructing a two-tier A/B offer and a commission-only sales-hiring plan to get the business cash-flow positive without debt.

## Key claims (Alex's advice/frameworks only)

- **One avatar, one channel, one product up to ~$1M** — at Philip's stage, running two avatars and five channels is why he feels spread thin; radical focus is the unlock.
- **The real business you're in is the efficiency business** — for a commoditized service like trash where HOA contracts go to the lowest bid, you can't win the auction; you win on operational efficiency (route density, truck throughput).
- Prefer avoiding **commoditized services that force you into an auction** ("race to the bottom"); scatter customers let you price more aggressively, hold higher gross margins, and out-market competitors.
- **Solve for a single "ram rod" / one need-to-believe**: build the plan on the fewest new assumptions possible, because every additional assumption dramatically lowers the odds it happens. ("If only this one thing were true, what stops me?")
- The core cash constraint: **cash collected in the first 30 days must exceed fully-loaded COGS + CAC** (here: ~$42 first-quarter labor + ~$50 commission + ~$116 bin ≈ $208 to break even upfront).
- **Reframe hard costs as sellable line items**: the bin (real cost ~$116) becomes a "$99 bin fee" and an "initiation fee" becomes the commission — one-sided information that lets you collect enough upfront while the offer still feels reasonable.
- **Build an A/B anchor offer**: a premium annual option (pay $360/12 months upfront, free bin) alongside the core quarterly option ($189: first quarter + paid bin). Even if only ~20% take the anchor, it lifts blended cash-per-door ~$72 and pushes the average above breakeven.
- **Grow debt-free when you can** — restructuring the offer removed the need to finance bins; "there's always a way if you think about it hard enough."
- **Commission-only sales is leverage with no cash-flow risk**: run an Indeed/Craigslist hiring "ad" written to the salesperson (pain of their current job + promise: six figures, work 4 or 8 hours/day, just memorize four questions), screen for hunger, do group calls not 1:1s, hire five to keep one.
- **Door-to-door is a numbers game**: at a 1-in-12 close, the math is knowable and scalable; the best closers sprint between doors and cut across lawns because they know volume drives income.
- **Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate** training loop, drilled via live role-play: obsessively rehearse the door-opener because the first 5 seconds decide the sale; the whole script is ~four questions plus objection handling.
- **Never sell alone** — the owner should always have someone shadowing so effort is double-dipped (selling + training a future lead trainer). Pay a slightly higher commission (e.g. $60 vs $50) to whoever trains with a partner.
- **Married men with kids out-earn single childless men because "they have to"** — screen salespeople for circumstances that create hunger (young hungry go-getters or new fathers).
- Small pricing moves compound: "add three bucks" is a ~10% price increase; charge a 3% processing fee that gets waived if the customer provides a second form of payment.
- **Turn your assets into billboards**: put an ugly QR code on the bins and charge $5/month to remove it (or leave it and let it advertise); make bins a loud color (neon orange) to get noticed.
