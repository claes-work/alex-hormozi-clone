# Why Boring Businesses Make the Most Money

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a plastic-surgery business consultant who offers many services and is wondering whether the model is the constraint. Alex says this broadness is common between $1 million and $3 million, then pushes the caller toward the more boring but valuable HR and hiring work because it is recurring, repeatable, and solves a high-value time problem for surgeons. He also explains that niching down is mainly required when delivery changes; if delivery is the same, different acquisition messaging can serve adjacent avatars.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - plastic-surgery consulting owner. Her revenue, service mix, conference speaking, client types, and medical-staffing opportunity are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Broad service menus are common between $1 million and $3 million because capable founders can solve many problems.
- The business should identify the valuable, repeatable service that takes less time and creates recurring revenue.
- Boring businesses can make a lot of money, and the business does not exist to satisfy the founder's intellectual curiosity.
- The business's job is to serve customers, not the owner's emotional need for novelty.
- Plastic surgeons and med-spa owners may both be viable if the delivery mechanism remains mostly the same.
- Niching down matters for operational efficiency; if operations do not change, multiple front-end messages can feed the same backend.
- The caller should consider medical staffing as the primary business because it makes the most money, solves a common problem, and sells time to buyers who have money but lack time.
- Alex suggests increasing the core price and mapping it to the number of surgeries or time returned rather than an onsite visit.
