# What to Do If You're Constrained by Your Market

## Summary
In a talk/Q&A on @MoreMozi, Alex walks through his mental model for "deconstraining" any business when an operator feels capped by their market. He argues a genuine market-size (TAM) ceiling is rare: the Sahara-desert-lounge case is a real geographic constraint, but most operators who "feel" market-limited have barely penetrated it (100 of 50,000 chiropractors) — the real problem is that no one knows they exist, so the fix is to advertise more, or simply duplicate the working model in another market. Trying to advertise more then surfaces the next constraint, and he chains them into a repeating cycle: Market → Metric (no data to know what "more" is) → Model (is this the right opportunity vehicle?) → Money ("I can't afford to") → Manpower (supply/talent) → back to the top. On Model he deploys "features versus bugs" (via his former Gym Launch CFO Suzanne Shifflet): every business has something hard about it, switching vehicles just trades one hard thing for another, and the hard problem in front of you is precisely what you get paid to solve — reframing it as a $50M enterprise-value payoff makes it worth solving. He stresses time horizon: almost any vehicle can make you exceptionally wealthy over decades (his Panda Express–owning neighbor; Raising Cane's), just not on a 36-month timeline. The Money constraint splinters into leads-too-expensive, sales-too-low, or LTV-too-low (front-end-but-no-back-end, or churn), each diagnosed by pattern recognition. Manpower constraints run the same cycle on the supply side. He notes the correct order is Metric before Market.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-10. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-10) A true market-size constraint is real but rare; his example is a man wanting to make his lounge more money whose lounge is in the middle of the Sahara Desert — advertising more would not fix a fundamentally bad location. [anecdote]
- (2026-02-10) If you genuinely live somewhere remote and 100% of the customers you serve aren't there, your market will limit you — but you could also duplicate what you do in another market; often it just takes "one degree of thinking" to replicate it elsewhere.
- (2026-02-10) Most operators who feel market-constrained are not: a B2B example serves 100 chiropractors out of ~50,000, so the market isn't saturated — no one knows they exist, so the fix is to advertise more.
- (2026-02-10) Trying to advertise more surfaces the next constraint — a metric/data constraint: you can't do more when you don't even know what "more" is because you have no metrics; you must figure out what you're currently doing before you can do more of it.
- (2026-02-10) Once data exists, the next constraint that surfaces is the model constraint: doubt about whether the current model is the best opportunity vehicle for your skills ("what do I do with my life").
- (2026-02-10) "Features versus bugs": every business has something that's hard about it; people want to switch opportunities because another business lacks their current hard thing — but that other business has a different hard thing they haven't hit yet. [attributed to Suzanne Shifflet, his Gym Launch CFO, ~38 years experience]
- (2026-02-10) Example of the trade-off: in home cleaning, finding customers is easy but finding honest, competent, reliable cleaning labor is hard; in fitness, finding people who want to talk about fitness is easy (many do it for free) but getting people to sign up for fitness services is hard because no one wants to wake up early to go to the gym.
- (2026-02-10) All businesses have constraints — some more supply-constrained, some more demand-constrained; if a business truly had no constraints, every business in that industry would be a "gazillion dollar" business, which happens only briefly before competition and pricing pressure emerge.
- (2026-02-10) The hard problem in front of you is the thing you get compensated for solving — "that's the game." The idea that a perfect, one-degree-separated business exists is probably not true.
- (2026-02-10) Reframe the hard problem by its payoff: e.g., building a recruiting + training engine for a cleaning business that upskills low-skill talent more efficiently than competitors could add $10–50M of enterprise value, which makes the problem far more desirable to solve.
- (2026-02-10) Almost all businesses, if you stick with them a long time, can make you exceptionally wealthy.
- (2026-02-10) Time-horizon example: his neighbor owns Panda Express — five locations at ~$3M topline each at 27% margins is unremarkable advice-wise, but doing it for 45 years compounds to ~$3.7B/year in sales, ~$1B/year in cash, tax-advantaged because he owns the real estate. [self-reported / anecdote]
- (2026-02-10) Most of your goals are probably attainable — just not on the timeline you gave yourself; people obsess over getting rich in 36 months when almost all vehicles can make you super rich over 36 years (with many orders of wealth along the way), but people don't think in those time horizons.
- (2026-02-10) A "low opportunity vehicle" market has an advantage: your competitors are typically not that sophisticated, whereas going after something like AI means competing against the best-capitalized, smartest people on earth. (He cites Raising Cane's — a decade-plus building brick-and-mortar, low-skilled-labor fast casual — whose owner is a billionaire.)
- (2026-02-10) The money/affordability constraint ("I can't afford to do more") splinters into three: leads cost too much, sales are too low (close rate), or LTV is too low.
- (2026-02-10) A common LTV pattern: a business has a front end but no back end and knows only that it's not profitable — often it has a break-even acquisition process, and simply selling something more to those customers turns it into a great business.
- (2026-02-10) It may instead be a churn problem: if customers churn out in three months, the cost of acquisition doesn't matter — you need to fix churn first (which itself has messaging, avatar, and onboarding sub-issues).
- (2026-02-10) If leads genuinely cost too much (e.g., insurance, where LTV is largely fixed by the carriers and they close ~30% on the phone), the levers are: increase quality of creative, increase quantity of creative, improve CRO within the funnel, and build better lead-magnet offers to drive lead cost down.
- (2026-02-10) If lead costs and per-customer economics are fine but you "can't close the barn door," the levers are messaging, scripting, price points, follow-up process, training/onboarding, and sales-rep compensation.
- (2026-02-10) If the market, metrics, money, and model are all fine but you lack technicians/coaches to deliver, that's a manpower (supply) constraint — and you run the exact same cycle on the supply side: recruit more → but no recruiting metrics → but maybe not enough talent in the local market → but maybe your offer to talent isn't compelling → is the per-rep economics the problem or is talent acquisition fine because the business works.
- (2026-02-10) The whole thing is a cycle that loops back to the top; it's how you deconstrain anything, supply or demand. He notes he drew the order wrong: metric should come first, then market.
