# We're giving away all our money — 2021-08-30

## Summary

In this solo talk (recorded in a parking lot before the gym), Hormozi explains why he and
Leila signed wills the prior week donating their **entire estate — self-valued at $60–80
million — to charity** after they die. He walks through his reasoning (no children;
inherited money "ruins" people; legacy is a human-condition illusion), argues that the
more valuable gift is transferring skills and lessons while alive via acquisition.com, and
concludes that knowing he'll give it all away eventually removes his desire to trade time
for money.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-08-30)

- The prior week he and Leila signed wills donating their entire estate to charity (after
  death); an Instagram post about it became his most-engaged post to date. (self-reported, 2021)
- Reasons: (1) no kids / no direct descendants; (2) giving people unearned money "ruined
  the lives" of everyone he's seen receive it; (3) "legacy" / permanence is a human-
  condition illusion — like the Sumerians, in 5,000 years no one will know who was rich or
  who founded what. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Genetic-dilution argument: descendants' share of your gene pool halves each generation
  (½ raised to the 100th power ≈ nothing), and they won't carry your traits or remember
  you. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Shifted view on success: he now credits natural predisposition ("we're just naturally
  good at it") more than pure hard work — analogizing business talent to Shaquille O'Neal's
  physical genetics, and speculating Bezos/Gates/Buffett would look mentally "seven-eight."
  (paraphrase, 2021)
- Since he'll donate it all at death anyway, it makes more sense to start giving now — the
  most valuable asset being skills and lessons, not money. He and Leila have donated ~$1M
  to charities over the last few years. (self-reported, 2021)
- Motivation shift: his first businesses were built from scarcity, insecurity and rage;
  beyond a certain bank number the drive to satisfy personal needs diminishes and you must
  change the driver — "money only solves money problems." (paraphrase, 2021)
- **Casino analogy**: entrepreneurship is a casino where you amass chips but can never cash
  out — you leave the table (die) and your chips get pushed back to family, competitors,
  the marketplace. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Powerball anecdote: as a college kid he felt *dread* during a drawing, realizing winning
  would rob him of earning the credit himself. (self-reported, 2021)
- "Uncle Warren" logic: he may hold and compound capital rather than donate now, because as
  a better allocator he can make a larger eventual impact. Notes the constant
  consumption-vs-investment trade-off. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Estate figures: current estate self-valued at **$60–80 million** (depending who values
  the equity slices); at ~10% growth over 40–50 years it would exceed $1 billion even if he
  earned nothing more. He cites $2M given over two years potentially becoming ~$100M in 40
  years. (self-reported, 2021)
- acquisition.com is his portfolio holding company (~7 companies wholly or partly owned,
  ~$85M/year; smallest business ~$8M/year); it doesn't sell time, courses, masterminds or
  coaching. He warns viewers about scam accounts impersonating him ("fake Alex Hormosi").
  (self-reported, 2021)
- Naval Ravikant quotes he leans on: "desire is a contract you make with yourself to be
  unhappy until you get what you want," and "retirement is when you stop sacrificing today
  for a hypothetical tomorrow." (paraphrase/quote, 2021)
