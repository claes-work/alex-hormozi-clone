# Stop Caring What Others Think of You So Much

## Summary

Solo mindset monologue in which Alex Hormozi lays out nine reframes for detaching
from other people's opinions in order to pursue above-average goals. He argues that
criticism almost always reduces to "you don't live the way I would prefer," that
determination (not intelligence) is the true determinant of success, and that pain,
uncertainty, and looking foolish are unavoidable prerequisites to progress. He
illustrates the ideas with personal stories: quitting his consulting job against his
father's wishes, skipping friends' weddings in his 20s, and overriding his own
executive team to build gym launch's outbound sales channel.

## Key claims

- The easiest way to lose in life is trying to avoid looking bad; you must stop
  caring what others think in order to keep operating "because the work just needs
  doing."
- Ignore the opinions of "losers" — people going nowhere have the most to say because
  they have nothing to do; their hate is a leading (positive) indicator that you're
  transitioning toward who you want to be.
- Don't listen to the people closest to you; listen to the people closest to your
  goals. If no one around you is close to your goals, consume information from outside
  ("separate your brain from your body").
- If you don't want someone's life, don't take their advice.
- People want you to fail because your different path implicitly indicts theirs;
  snide remarks are ego protection.
- Almost all criticism reduces to: "You do not live in a way that I would prefer."
- Extreme people get extreme results; obsessing over work-life balance tends to
  produce mediocrity at both. Above-average goals (e.g. $1M/year, roughly a 1-in-200
  to 1-in-300 outcome in the US) require unbalanced, obsessive action for a
  disproportionate period with zero or negative reward.
- "We're the result of our actions, not our aspirations." Many share the goal; few do
  the work.
- Raise your standards, which come from beliefs about what's possible; we live in
  "prisons of our own making" built on beliefs that were mostly given to us by others,
  not our own.
- Determination beats intelligence (cites Paul Graham's intelligence-vs-determination
  tweet and Charlie Munger's "never underestimate the man who overestimates himself").
- Determination is an "extinction curve" — how many times you keep acting without
  seeing results; maniacs keep knocking until the door breaks.
- "Sometimes other people's dreams have to die in order for yours to live." Don't take
  advice from someone with smaller dreams for you than you have. His father opposed his
  risk not from malice but from a different (higher) fear of failure.
- Pain is a prerequisite to progress; to get adaptation you need the extra stress.
  Cites Paul Graham: to earn a million dollars you have to endure a million dollars of
  pain. The harder battle is uncertainty, not the pain itself (mouse-drowning survival
  study). You don't habituate to pain when its intensity/variety keeps changing — which
  is exactly what life and business do.
- Play every card you have (Shaquille O'Neal / "play the cards you have" story from his
  father); rejects "guilt of privilege" as a handcuff. Worst-case downside in the
  developed world is free housing and food; the true downside is living as a slave to
  someone else's dreams.
- Success demands courage; courage is in far shorter supply than genius/intelligence
  (attributed to Peter Thiel). A man with twice the courage and half the advantages
  beats a man with twice the advantages and half the courage.
- Fear exists in the vague, not the specific; playing out the specifics dissolves it.
- Be okay with no one cheering for you — consistency never looks impressive in the
  moment. People root for you only at the start and the finish, never in the middle
  where the winning actually happens ("master the middle").
- Hold yourself to a higher standard: self-love is bridging the gap between who you are
  and who you can become. Lead behavior by modeling it (Leila dressing up; he and Leila
  working 6-7 day weeks). Introduces "one of zero" — doing things you didn't yet know
  you could do, beyond "one of one."
- Accept hard trade-offs; there are no do-overs in your 20s. Skills compound and your
  40-year-old self will value them; "living it up" often turns out to be chasing others'
  dreams.
- Be willing to look like an idiot (Sam Altman's "power of being misunderstood"; trade
  short-term low status for long-term high status). Asymmetric returns come from betting
  against conventional wisdom, and you have an information advantage on yourself.
- 2025 is his hardest year in the last eight (fifth-worst of the last 13).
- You become successful the moment you decide you are; you can be both successful and
  dissatisfied.
