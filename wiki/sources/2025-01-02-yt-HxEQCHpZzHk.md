# How to Grow Your Business SO Fast in 2026 It Feels ILLEGAL

## Summary

A dense solo talk in which Alex Hormozi walks through the strongest growth levers he
used to take Gym Launch, Prestige Labs, and ALAN (Used Allen) past $1M+/month within a
year each. The through-line is advertising volume and message quality: most people are
invisible, the marketplace is far bigger than they think, and the biggest wins come from
100x-ing reach (advertising as a "boom") plus obsessing over hooks, proof, and dead-simple
language. He layers on frameworks for handling hate, reinvestment allocation (70/20/10 and
give-to-ask 3.5:1), buyer awareness levels, targeting, and mastery via better metrics.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed)

- His last three companies each passed ~$1.2M–$2M+/month by end of year one (ALAN/Used
  Allen $1.2M, Prestige Labs $1.5M, Gym Launch $2M+) — framed as illustration, self-reported.
- Under $1M in revenue, "basically everyone on Earth doesn't know you exist"; the first 4
  hours of every day should go from obscurity to awareness via the **Core Four** (warm/cold
  outreach, content, paid ads). Pick one, go all in.
- **Advertising is a "boom"** — his internal term for a "business order of magnitude change."
  Optimizations (close rate, opt-in %) are capped at 100%; advertising can 100x the number
  of people who find you. Small businesses get stuck in an "optimization mousetrap."
- The marketplace is far bigger than owners assume; you only need a tiny slice (a gym owner
  needs ~200 of a city's million people). New competitors don't meaningfully take your share.
- **Shrink your competition** rather than beat it: grow so big no one can hear them, or kill
  them with kindness. Claim your own flaws first (learned from Eminem in 8 Mile) so critics
  have nothing to point out.
- **Repetition is not bad**: people need to be reminded more than they need to be taught. You
  must master variety — saying the same thing many different ways.
- Respond to hate with "you are right" + kindness; never argue point-by-point (it only invites
  more back-and-forth). Hate usually reduces to "I don't prefer the way this person does things."
- **Clear, not clever**: he made this a core marketing rule after learning the
  lowest-grade-language presidential candidate tends to win. Write to a 3rd–5th grade reading
  level; run emails through a reading-level app. Doing this to his email follow-ups gave a 50%
  conversion increase with no change in content. Simpler language helps experts and beginners alike.
- Match the analogy to the audience's history of learning (cars for mechanics, houses for
  realtors); broader audience → broader, more universal analogy (food, sleep, driving).
- **Proof over promise**: proof is the single highest priority. Get beta users, work for free
  in exchange for testimonials/reviews/feedback ("never work for free" advice comes from people
  who don't make much money). Show, don't sell. His channel was listened to only because he'd
  sold a company for just under $50M — proof he could deliver.
- **The hook is greater than everything** — it's the shortest part but the biggest force
  multiplier; his hook formula is **proof, promise, plan**. Both visual and verbal/auditory
  hooks matter. Reframing a speaker's 1–2 minute intro changed their net promoter score even
  though the talk was identical. Cutting 3 weak seconds off a video took it from 40k to 780k
  views (19x).
- **More, better, new**: "more" is the highest risk-adjusted return strategy (deviating from a
  working control usually loses). Shiny-object/new obsession is "a cancer." Owners have far more
  runway than they think. Flyer story: 300 flyers in a day taught nothing; his mentor tested with
  5,000 and ran 5,000/day for 30 days (150,000). **What presents as volatility is a symptom of
  low volume.** Take your yearly activity and compress it into a day to see the volume required.
- **Word of mouth**: negative is far stronger/faster than positive (Disney study: 37 tragic
  moments to offset 1 magic; ~5x vs ~37x spread). Rising CPL at flat CPM signals negative word
  of mouth. This is why he keeps early negative word of mouth "concentrated and quiet" — work
  for free so you don't also hold an upset customer's money. "Half of you are below average."
- **Steal from yourself** — 70/20/10 allocation (attributed to a math proof by Brin/Page): 70%
  carbon-copy what works, 20% adjacent variation, 10% brand-new. Most owners have it flipped.
  Nike didn't drop "Just Do It" in year two; you'll get bored of a winner long before customers
  do. Demolition/building analogy: only one arrangement builds; near-infinite arrangements destroy.
- **Emotional vs. logical buyers is a false binary** — it's a continuum of *information
  requirement*. High-info buyers vastly outnumber low-info ones; direct-response-only businesses
  stay small because they fish the small pool. Uses Eugene Schwartz's **five levels of awareness**
  (unaware → problem → solution → product → most aware).
- **Reinvestment / give-to-ask ratio**: New Balance's Chris Davis (via Gymshark's Ben Francis)
  flipped ad spend from 70% direct-selling to 70% brand/emotional; ROI took 18 months. The
  studied **give:ask ratio is ~3.5:1** (TV ads, Facebook feed 3 posts:1 ad, TikTok grew ad-free).
  Founders dig the well; corporate execs who flip the ratio pull demand forward and drain it.
- **B2B vs B2C** high-info pairing differs: B2C uses emotional stories/endorsements; B2B uses
  aspirational outcomes you/your clients achieved, plus free content and free products — "degrees
  of separation" (his exit = 2°, portfolio companies = 1°, "I used this and made money" = 0°).
- **Marketing laws**: (1) State the facts and tell the truth — do epic stuff, then tell it
  truthfully, rather than exaggerating something mediocre. (2) Show only what you can show. (3)
  Say what only you can say. Get narrow to be unique ("It's toasted," Mad Men; double-secret black
  belt). You don't need to be the best in the world, just the best in a puddle. Rockefeller
  overpaid for the biggest Cincinnati refinery to own "the biggest" story, then did 20 M&A deals
  in 30 days as the gorilla.
- **The list = targeting**: showing winter coats to South Florida fails regardless of offer.
  Make the person in the ad look/talk like the avatar — putting Leila's picture (instead of his)
  on the Gym Launch thank-you page sent response rates "through the roof." His audience is ~89% male.
- **Masters have more ways to win** — more/better metrics and leading (not just lagging)
  indicators. HR mastery example (time-to-fill, two-sided fit, cost-to-acquire-talent). His
  outbound "intervention" story: the team saw one sale in four months (lagging); he saw progress
  moving the buck along each step. "A heart surgery looks like murder to someone who doesn't
  understand the milestones."
