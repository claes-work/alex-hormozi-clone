# What's Working Right Now for Long-Form Content

## Summary

In this clip (part of a group/panel Q&A alongside at least one other creator, "Sam," and an audience of content creators), Hormozi answers "what's working right now" for long-form content. His core argument: the **idea** — the unique insight behind a piece — is still the biggest arbitrage in long-form, and it "trumps the packaging" and the headline. He argues exceptional content is "too good to fail" and that genuine depth of thinking comes only from time spent sitting with a problem. He ties this to a personal creative-process point: real insights require excess capacity (clean space, good sleep, no busyness), and the best content comes from the ~20% of the time you make something you are personally interested in rather than just "feeding the machine." He uses his recent Tony Robbins podcast as a worked example of choosing curiosity over an algorithm-optimized format. The clip ends with a promo for the acquisition.com 10-stage roadmap.

## Key claims

All claims paraphrased unless in blockquotes; all Alex-attributed (solo speaker, with brief interjections from co-panelists noted).

- (2026-02-26) For long-form, the **idea / unique insight is the biggest arbitrage** — the actual substance of the thing still matters more than the hook, headline, or production polish. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-26) There is a content parallel to "too big to fail": **"too good to fail."** If content is genuinely exceptional, it wins regardless of packaging. His example: the original Jordan Peterson clip that went viral was a "shitty iPhone angled" shot of him debating students, with no hook engineering, yet got enormous views. (paraphrase, with verbatim phrase below)
- (2026-02-26) You demonstrate expertise by the **depth of your questions** in a domain — surface-level reading yields service-level insight, but the unique insights live two or three layers underneath, which only come from time on the problem. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-26) ~80% of the content a creator makes is "feeding the machine"; ~20% is stuff you are genuinely, personally interested in — and that 20% is where the magic is. You can't schedule those "inspired moments" to fit a Monday/Wednesday/Friday 10 a.m. cadence. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-26) When an inspired moment happens, **try to recreate the conditions** that produced it (sleep, eating, not worrying about other things) rather than trying to force inspiration on demand. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-26) **You cannot create insights while busy all the time** — insight requires excess/excess capacity and space to think; eliminating things from your life creates that space. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-26) He still lives by his own "maker vs. manager" framework: currently **four or five days a week have zero meetings** so he has time to think, and his biggest business gains have come from insights during that unstructured space. Exploring / wandering / browsing random stuff sparks ideas — but you must have time to follow a spark or it "vanishes" and "finds another person." (paraphrase; references his own maker-vs-manager video)
- (2026-02-26) On the **Tony Robbins podcast**: he consciously chose NOT to make it an algorithm/headline-driven format (i.e., ranking the 10 most-interesting things about Tony and asking them in order). His wife Leila suggested he "just ask Tony whatever you want," and he did. He notes Robbins does ~five podcasts a year, timed around his next event as his promotional schedule. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-26) That curiosity-led Tony interview generated **more messages/texts than any other piece of content he's made besides his launches** — which he attributes to genuine personal interest coming through. (paraphrase, self-reported)
