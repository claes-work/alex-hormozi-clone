# No BS Business Advice to Get Rich in 2026

> Source: YouTube, @AlexHormozi, published 2024-12-19. Solo monologue (year-in-review).
> All content is Alex-attributed unless flagged in Notes. Title says "2026" but the
> video is Alex's 2024 year-end recap looking into 2025; he refers throughout to "this
> year" (2024/2025) and "next year" (2025).

## Summary

A long-form, densely packed year-end review in which Alex distills the lessons that
drove three of his companies past $100M valuations. He argues for fewer goals and less
long-term planning, ruthless focus on a single highest-leverage constraint, and treating
talent/hiring as the real business. He walks through operational frameworks (SCALE for
scaling, question-delete-optimize-accelerate-automate for turnarounds, the WHO-WHAT-WHEN
meeting cadence, pay-for-performance), the "six horsemen of business stagnation," his
branding philosophy (7:30 top/bottom-of-funnel, 3:1 give/ask ratio), and personal
optimizations around work volume, sleep, and training.

## Key claims

- **Plan less.** Set fewer goals; only seriously plan the next 12 weeks. Obsessing over
  the minutiae of a multi-year plan is wasted effort because conditions change (cites
  Jensen Huang doing little long-term planning at Nvidia). Growth is not a smooth line —
  it's flatline → breakthrough → flatline.
- **Concentrate resources on one domino.** Instead of delegating tasks A–E to five
  people, put all five people on the single highest-leverage priority (A); solving it
  makes B–E happen faster. A good strategist asks: given current resources, what one
  thing would make all the other problems disappear? That is leverage.
- **Leverage is the strongest force in business.** The acquisition.com logo encodes only
  two ideas: the supply/demand cross and the triangle as a fulcrum for leverage.
  Diagnose whether your business is **supply-constrained** (cleaning, accounting, legal,
  high-end consulting, home services — hard to find people, demand is easy) or
  **demand-constrained** (e-commerce, B2B software, weight loss — many suppliers). Then
  gain leverage over that specific constraint.
- **Pay far more for talent than you think.** If a plumber/lawyer brings $400K/yr in
  gross profit, a $1,000 referral bonus is absurd — paying $25K–$50K to solve it a month
  sooner is a bargain (treats a hire like a stock yielding that gross profit annually,
  discounted for risk). Incentivize the existing workforce to recruit.
- **Work volume correlates with output.** Alex took zero days off the first 100 days of
  the year (~90-hour weeks, ~7 days at ~12h with 2–3 days of 18h). Total ~10–12 days off
  for the year. Argues the entrepreneurs most obsessed with work outperform; compares
  breaks to the "summer gap" in kids' learning.
- **Three portfolio growth themes:** (1) key-player/leadership recruiting, CEO actively
  courting top talent; (2) top-down focus & prioritization (delete everything that isn't
  the one priority); (3) CRO — constant split-testing on high-traffic revenue surfaces
  (one company got +20%, +50%, +28% lifts from ~100 tests run by one guy and a couple
  devs).
- **"What would it take?"** was his question of the year — it assumes success and forces
  working backward from the desired result. Reject the "soft no" ("we can't"); ask what
  resources it would require, then decide if the trade is worth it (framework from Elon).
- **Detractors of growth:** entrepreneurs taking their foot off the gas after making
  "enough" money; and unsupportive spouses/partners (a big, deliberately controversial
  point — he says the partner has ~0.7 correlation with subjective well-being and huge
  influence on behavior, so vet a key hire's spouse for support).
- **Two process frameworks:** **SCALE** (Start, Compound/do more, Augment/do better,
  Leverage/make consistent & automate, Expand) for building; and for turnarounds:
  **question the requirements → delete → optimize → pull up timelines (do it in 1/10 the
  time) → automate.** End-of-week deadlines become end-of-day to 7x team speed.
- **Six Horsemen of business stagnation:** (1) **underpriced** (raise price via "5 & 20"
  — bump volume 20%, intensity 5%, keep raising until people stop buying); (2)
  **overcompensation** (a legacy comp/rev-share you can't outgrow — shore up
  infrastructure, then renegotiate); (3) **too many avatars** (niche down via 80/20,
  price for the top 20%); (4) **overextension** (opening location 2 before location 1
  runs without you — a "who" problem; run a 6-month test where the business grows without
  your involvement); (5) **more than one business** (pick one; opportunity is in the
  problems you must solve, not the missed ones); (6) **"no data daddy"** (track only data
  whose change would change your behavior).
- **"Rush is imaginary."** Urgency (outside network-effect/winner-take-all markets) is
  purely a chosen competition reference point — who you compare yourself to. His biggest
  personal lesson: **missed opportunities < problems.** Problems are finite; missed
  opportunities are infinite and are "the gateway drug to mediocrity." He keeps a "big
  money ideas" list and lets ideas marinate before acting (Lincoln's unsent-letters
  method).
- **Branding.** Story from New Balance's CMO: flipped ad spend from 70% bottom-of-funnel
  / 30% top to 30/70; ROAS fell for 18 months, then surged from month 19 — brands are
  measured in years, not months. Maintain a **3:1 value-to-ask ratio** (studied in TV and
  radio; drop below and audiences leave; from $100M Leads). B2B branding buckets: help
  them hit their goal for free, help someone like them, or achieve the aspirational
  outcome yourself. His $46M exit was brand-building because it was aspirational; the next
  aspirational milestone is crossing a billion.
- **Skool** was his first-ever brand deal (negotiated 2023, public Jan 2024); millions of
  users; average paid community earns ~$1,360/month. Bets: IRL and AI are opposite ends
  of a continuum and both are worth doubling down on. Bought aq.com from Yahoo for
  $480,000. Stood up **ACQ Ventures** (SaaS/tech-enabled services; family office, no
  outside LPs, active large positions; first three deals closing). "Sawdust" businesses
  (Airbnb/Uber; his Workshops division from empty desks) — recombine existing paid-for
  resources to check multiple boxes (deal flow, cash flow, content, talent flow).
- **Culture = the rules that govern reinforcement.** Keep to three values. His focus:
  **"kind not nice"** — deliver candid feedback. **Criticism vs. insult:** criticism is
  the factual gap between desired and actual + steps to fix; an insult attaches judgment
  to that gap. Reed Hastings frame: "professional sports team, not a family."
- **Operations/comp.** Create a technical *and* a management track so experts don't have
  to manage to earn more. Pay for performance (keep existing salary, add performance on
  top; new hires get performance-based). **Fire when things are good, not bad** — "bloat
  is like cancer." Problems and solutions have delays: you're living with problems you
  created ~6 months ago; give a solution proportional time before judging it. When a
  metric declines, look at where it *stopped growing*, not where it started shrinking —
  often you *added* a bad person.
- **Content.** A 16-week experiment with wide content (meals/workouts/vlogs) reduced
  *absolute* business-owner opt-ins → reverted to business content. Education content
  loads effort into pre-production; entertainment into post. Speak at the lowest grade
  level, use avatar-relevant analogies. **Three Ps** in every intro: Proof, Promise,
  Plan. Introduce yourself (for people who don't know you yet). Maximize "value per
  second, not seconds of value."
- **Platforms (2024):** email was MVP/most-improved (written word is his best format);
  Facebook high-leverage for book sales per follower; IG + YouTube perennial traffic
  champs; Amazon/books drive ~15% of site traffic; TikTok deprioritized earlier but
  reframed as a skill gap (up 300% in a week after an incentive change).
- **Personal environment.** Sleep "big three": pitch black, cold, earplugs (earplugs
  dropped his resting HR ~4 bpm). Blacked-out office windows; two desks (sit/stand);
  kneeling chair fixed shoulder/neck pain. Cut training to two hard weekend workouts;
  transitioned at 35 to longevity, dropped to ~203 lbs (from a heaviest of 250).
