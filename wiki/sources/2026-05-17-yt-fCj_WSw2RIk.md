# Why Marginal Returns are Still Worth a Shitload of Work

## Summary

Alex argues that marginal returns can still be worth heavy effort when the absolute
scale is large enough. Games are often won at the edges, and tiny relative improvements
can produce huge absolute gains when multiplied across large audiences or campaigns. He
also explains why he may withhold goals before a launch: if publicizing them does not
increase the likelihood of success or add a meaningful benefit, it can occupy attention
that should be used for more salient messaging.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Marginal returns are still returns.
- Many competitive games are won at the edges, where the top performer receives
  disproportionate gains.
- Tiny improvements can be worth a large amount of work when the audience or campaign
  scale is large.
- A 1% improvement in front of 500,000 people can affect thousands of people.
- Alex says preparation intensity should depend on whether the expected absolute return
  justifies it.
- He describes himself as more of a maximizer than an optimizer.
- Before sharing goals publicly, Alex asks whether sharing increases the likelihood of
  success and whether it creates another benefit.
- Even neutral goal-sharing can be costly if it displaces more useful audience messaging.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Marginal returns are still returns."

> "Most games are won at the edges."

> "When you have that much scale going through it, the crumbs become bakeries."
