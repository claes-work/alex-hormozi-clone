# $850K Profit on Content That Dies in a Day

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A (recorded at an in-person event — Alex references "yesterday" and
"the list yesterday," so this is one seat of a multi-day workshop). The questioner is
**Joe Hulka**, a sports-betting / fantasy-football content creator since 2016 who spent
a two-year stint as head of content for Cody Sanchez before going back out on his own.
In the ~10 months since returning (since the prior June), he reports **$1M revenue and
$850K profit**, mostly from short-form content (TikTok, Instagram) driving incentivized
affiliate sign-ups to betting apps on a CPA basis — paid once per conversion, owning
neither the customer nor the LTV. He wants to get to $5M. His two questions: (1) his
best-converting format is a picks "listicle" for a specific event, but posting more of
them on the same account seems to cannibalize views — why, and should he run multiple
accounts since his content "dies in a day"? (2) Is it okay to not have his own product
behind the affiliate traffic — is he "just a whale affiliate" without a real business?

Alex's answers: on posting volume, apparent cannibalization is **usually a quality
thing**, not a platform penalty — Instagram (and Mosseri publicly) in no way penalizes
posting more; each post is another "dot on the circle" of what gets shown, so five posts
is just more dots. Evergreen vs. topical doesn't change this — at the end of the day
you're just looking at **impressions per month**. Verdict: one account, just post more
on it; multiple accounts add nothing. On the product question, Alex says it **depends on
the goal**: if the goal is money, just make more money; if the goal is a sellable asset,
it's genuinely difficult in the betting space — you'd more likely have to own a betting
platform. Even the adjacent play he's seen (a ~$4M/year business in the same space
running a Skool-style community teaching its betting methodology and stacking affiliate
commissions on a suite of tools) is "probably not" inherently valuable. The realistic
exit is being bought **as a media asset** by a large betting platform — but that
requires being much bigger. He runs the enterprise-value math: get to $10M/year in
affiliate revenue; if they'll pay you $10M/year you're making them $30–50M/year minimum,
which at ~40% net margins is ~$20M EBITDA, and at a ~12x trade that's ~$240M of
enterprise value to the acquirer — so they'll pay a fat multiple just to own the
channel. The strategy: **become a whale — so big they're afraid of losing you — because
that's when you get leverage.** Net prescription for the questioner: "more." The video
closes with Alex's standard acquisitions.com/roadmap CTA (10 growth stages across
product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-16. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Apparent view cannibalization from posting more is usually a quality problem, not a
  platform penalty.** When more posts on one account seem to eat each other's views, the
  cause is typically content quality, not the algorithm punishing frequency.
- **Instagram does not penalize posting volume.** Alex cites accounts posting 100x/day
  and says Mosseri has stated publicly that Instagram in no way penalizes you for
  posting more. _(Alex's characterization of Instagram's public statements.)_
- **Each post is another "dot on the circle."** Every post is an independent shot at
  being shown; posting five times is simply five dots instead of one — more surface
  area, not diluted reach. (References a framework he taught "yesterday" at the event.)
- **Evergreen vs. topical/perishable content doesn't change the volume math.** Even if
  content is only relevant for a day, the metric to manage is **impressions per month**;
  the decay profile of an individual post doesn't matter.
- **One account beats many.** Don't split the same-event content across multiple
  accounts — just post more on the one account. Alex says he doesn't even know where
  multiple accounts "would even go" (i.e., sees no distribution upside).
- **A topical/news-style content business is *easier*, not harder.** Alex tells the
  questioner his model is easier than Alex's own, because a "news reporter" just talks
  about the news instead of having to engineer a fresh LTV:CAC angle every week.
- **Whether you need your own product depends on your goal.** If the goal is income:
  just make more money as an affiliate. If the goal is a sellable company: hard in the
  betting space — you'd more likely need to own a betting platform.
- **Owning the customer via an education/community layer still doesn't create much
  enterprise value in this space.** A ~$4M/year comparable ran a Skool-style community
  teaching its betting strategy, then monetized commissions across a whole suite of
  tools — they own the customer and stack affiliates, yet Alex judges that business
  "probably not" inherently valuable either. _(Second-hand example, self-reported
  numbers.)_
- **The realistic exit for an affiliate media business is acquisition as a media asset
  by the platform it feeds** — and only at much larger scale. Sufficient revenue is
  "checkbox number one": get to $10M/year first.
- **The acquirer's math is what sets your multiple.** If a platform pays you $10M/year,
  they're making $30–50M/year minimum off your traffic; at ~40% net margins that's
  ~$20M EBITDA, and at a ~12x multiple you represent ~$240M of enterprise value to
  them — which is why they'd pay a fat multiple to swallow the acquisition channel.
- **Become a whale to get leverage.** The strategic goal of an affiliate is to become so
  big that the platform is afraid of losing you — that fear is when you gain pricing
  power and acquisition leverage.
- **The prescription is volume: "more."** For a working affiliate machine with strong
  unit economics, the answer to "how do I grow?" is to do more of what's working, not
  to diversify accounts or bolt on a product prematurely.
