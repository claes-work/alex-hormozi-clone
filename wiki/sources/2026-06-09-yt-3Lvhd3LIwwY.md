# SMB Clients Will Quietly Bankrupt You

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises an Australian digital-marketing agency serving small service businesses that grew quickly after pivoting from fitness. Alex warns that SMB churn has not shown up yet because the company is only four months into the model. He predicts CAC will rise, churn will compress margins, and the owner will be tempted to take over more of clients' businesses. His prescription: either go down-market with a very low-cost automated nuisance offer or go up-market to better operators; the $1,500-$3,000/month SMB middle is a dead zone.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- SMB volatility becomes the agency's volatility.
- Early growth can hide churn because the model is too new for retention problems to surface.
- CAC usually rises over time; it is rarely cheaper later than at the start.
- Churn can make revenue rise while margins compress until the agency feels like a nonprofit.
- Taking on more responsibility for poor SMB operators is the wrong response to churn.
- At roughly $1,500-$3,000/month for SMB agency services, average retention may be only four to six months.
- To make SMB work, go very cheap and automated, such as a nuisance-style $300-$500/month recurring offer.
- The other viable path is to go up-market to businesses with metrics, a sales process, and a proven model.
- The middle pricing zone is the trap: too expensive for SMB stickiness and too cheap for high-quality service.
