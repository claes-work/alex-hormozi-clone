# Scale Any Business With These 3 Levers

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a $1.2M pool service and repair company in Raleigh with strong retention, about $4.5K annual revenue per client, and weak profit after owner salaries. The owner sees the talent constraint as hiring, retention, and training. Alex traces the thread backward: sales motion and pricing fund better technician pay, while the current acquisition economics are already excellent. He recommends spending far more on ads, calling inbound leads within 60 seconds, using first-30-day gross profit to fund growth, and incentivizing faster technician certification with accountability for mistakes.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A talent-retention problem can originate in pricing and sales, because higher prices fund better pay.
- If a business acquires a customer for about $150 and earns much more gross profit in year one and month one, acquisition spend should scale.
- Strong first-30-day cash flow means growth can fund itself if the owner is willing to spend into proven payback.
- Some operators play defense by spending only enough to cover fixed costs instead of going on offense with profitable acquisition.
- Inbound PPC leads who already saw pricing should be contacted immediately; speed-to-lead can materially lift conversion.
- When demand and supply are near equilibrium, get demand and cash first, then use the cash to unlock capacity.
- Training time can be compressed with incentives, but the trainer should remain accountable for the trainee's early quality.
