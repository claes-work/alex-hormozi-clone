# My Approach to Dealing With Life's Problems

> ⚠️ Two-speaker clip. Alex is interviewed by (at least one) host who is a Pokemon-card

## Summary

Asked about the biggest *problem* (not challenge) he has faced recently, Alex names Leila's
serious health issues over the past year — she is both his wife and business partner, so it
hit personally and operationally, and they are still working through it. He uses this to draw
his core distinction about problems: some problems have no solution to engineer — you can only
endure them. He calls these "kick-them-in-the-nuts problems" — life overhead you simply have
to suffer, and suffer well. He extends the same "fixed cost of living" logic to criticism
(you get criticized whether you act or not, so it's a fixed cost) and to ignorance (every
gap in your knowledge is an ongoing opportunity cost — an "ignorance tax" you pay daily).
His response to the ignorance tax is to pay it down as fast as possible: pay to get into any
room, learn from anyone, treat every exposure (including this very interview) as a learning
opportunity.

## Key claims (2026-02-19, paraphrased unless quoted)

- The biggest recent *problem* was Leila's serious health issues throughout the year, still
  being worked through; because she is both his wife and his business partner, the impact
  landed on both the personal and business sides. *(paraphrase)*
- ★ Some problems cannot be solved, only suffered — he mentally files these as
  "kick-them-in-the-nuts problems." There is no "what do I do about this?"; the only move is
  to take the hit and suffer well. These are "just life overhead." *(paraphrase; fresh label)*
- Criticism is unavoidable in both directions: do nothing and you get criticized, do anything
  and you get criticized — so criticism becomes "a fixed cost of living." The only people who
  escape it are dead. *(paraphrase + verbatim phrase)*
- Ignorance is an ongoing opportunity cost he calls the "ignorance tax" — everyone is paying
  it down every day. Not knowing how to do a valuable thing costs you the value you'd
  otherwise capture, every year you don't know it. *(paraphrase)*
- His response to the ignorance tax is to pay it down as fast as he can: he'll pay to get into
  any room and learn anything from anyone he can, treating even a casual visit (like this
  interview / seeing the host's media team) as a valuable learning experience. *(paraphrase)*
- The opportunity-cost logic scales without limit — pushed far enough, "every year you don't
  make a trillion dollars" / don't become Elon Musk is also technically costing you; add more
  zeros and it stays true. He holds this frame deliberately as motivation to keep learning.
  *(paraphrase)*
