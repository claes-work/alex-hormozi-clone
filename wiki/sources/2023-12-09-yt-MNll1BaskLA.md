# How to Win (At ANYTHING)

## Summary

A recorded keynote/stage presentation laying out Alex's unified framework for "winning"
at anything (health, relationships, sales, real estate, business, investing). He
redefines winning as impressing "future you," then walks five questions — what to do,
how to do it, why you're not doing it, who you must become, and when to start — anchored
in inversion, operationalizing traits into observable behaviors, and identity-as-action.

## Key claims

- **Win = impress future you**, not the people around you; comparing yourself to people
  you already beat breeds complacency.
- **Inversion ("invert, always invert"):** list what the *least successful* version of
  you would do to guarantee a loss (don't follow up, don't advertise, be transactional,
  show up unprepared), then flip each into the actions that win. The smaller/more potent
  the loss-list, the clearer your north star.
- **Operationalize traits into observable behaviors.** "Charismatic" isn't teachable;
  "smile when people walk in, use their name, change tonality, keep eye contact, nod and
  respond" is. Break commands down to a level the least-skilled person can execute — the
  more skilled you are, the more general a command you can follow. Two people attend the
  same event and get different results because of prerequisite skills, not the event.
- **Definitions:** **Learning** = "same condition, new behavior." **Intelligence** = the
  *rate* of learning (how fast you change behavior under the same conditions).
  **Confidence** = a percentage likelihood built from *proof* (reps in the exact
  situation); it is domain-specific.
- **Motivation comes from deprivation, and money-deprivation is psychological, not
  physiological** — you feel poor only when you perceive a gap between where you are and
  where you want to be. Rich people feel more deprived because they compare up. To get
  more motivated, change who you compare yourself to.
- **"B-do-have" is BS — just do.** Identity is a description of what you repeatedly do,
  so being is 100% doing and therefore 100% under your control. "The work works on you
  more than you work on it."
- **When to start = now, especially when busy.** If change is contingent on being
  "not busy," you'll quit the moment you get busy again. The "when-then fallacy" (I'll do
  it when I have more time / save when I have more money) inverts the correct sequence:
  you do to get, not get to do. "Smart, busy, broke — pick two."
- **Reframing hard times:** downturns are when champions are made and wealth transfers
  to "the few left standing"; you can't claim a skill until it's tested, so hardship is
  proof-of-self. "What makes you extraordinary isn't what you do, it's how long you're
  willing to do it for."
- **The great games (marriage, health, business) are never won — you outlast.** That
  shifts winning to a decision you fully control: keep doing the actions.
