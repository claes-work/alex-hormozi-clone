# Starting a software company is a TERRIBLE IDEA...

## Summary

Solo, deliberately contrarian monologue aimed at info-product/course/service
entrepreneurs (roughly the $1–3M revenue stage) who bolt a SaaS product onto their
business hoping it will make them "stickier" and earn a high exit multiple. Alex argues
both motives are wrong: software isn't sticky just because it's software, and acquirers
(who are smarter than you) won't hand you a tech multiple for a bad product tacked onto
a service. His core reframe: valuation is a risk-adjusted discount on future sales, not
magic that attaches to "tech." The prescription is to stay in your lane — make your
service stickier, raise retention, expand your TAM, roll up similar companies, or use
affiliate deals instead of building software you're not equipped to build.

## Key claims

Why SaaS is a trap for beginners / info-service operators:

- **Software isn't inherently sticky** — "Really good software is sticky. Your software
  will not be sticky and will just cost you a lot of money."
- **You end up doing both badly** — 99% keep running the info business and try to drive
  it into software, so they get neither a real exit nor sticky software, losing to
  someone whose sole focus is the software.
- **It's one of the hardest businesses to enter**: (1) development costs real money
  (offshore teams carry their own product-quality risk); (2) your competitors are
  software people who are "much smarter in general than the guru world" because there's
  real money there.
- **It's a portfolio/VC game with brutal odds** — VC works because ~2 of 100 bets carry
  the portfolio; being one of the 100, your odds are very low, so you take your eye off
  your main game to play a ~1-in-50 shot and destroy both. Then sunk-cost fallacy and
  ego lock you in.
- **Tech doesn't "get" great multiples** — valuation = a discount applied to future
  sales between now and when the company dies, weighted by how likely those sales are.
  Insurance gets good multiples because renewal/retention is high, not because it's
  "magic."
- **Churn benchmark**: software churn is measured *annually*, not monthly; if your churn
  is above ~10% annual, "you're just a service business" — meaning most info-operators'
  software doesn't hit real SaaS churn metrics.
- **Acquirers see through it** — they look at time on screen, daily active users, real
  usage. Re-labeling a mastermind subscription as "software" doesn't fool them.
- **Competitors copy you instantly** — a real software player will copy your product
  fast, ship your features plus theirs with a better UX, probably cheaper, leaving you
  with a worse value proposition you're emotionally invested in.
- **Product-market-fit test = "buy, use, tell."** Real software is *bought* (proves the
  problem is valuable), *used* regularly on its own, and *told* about to others
  specifically. If people only get in because you sold them the service and told them to
  use it, it's not good software. Most software fails; of those that sell, most sell "for
  parts"; only the rare high-multiple exits have real growth, low churn, and word of mouth.
- **The better path**: make your service stickier, increase retention, expand your TAM,
  get more referrals/affiliates/paid ads/outbound, or roll up similar companies —
  instead of trying to trick an acquirer. When you actually solve the problem you won't
  want to sell, because a well-run business grows month over month "even if you do
  nothing."
