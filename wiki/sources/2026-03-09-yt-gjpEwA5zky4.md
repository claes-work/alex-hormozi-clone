# You Need to Work 100x Harder

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks two prompting questions; all substance is Alex's). Alex's thesis: effort scales non-linearly at the top end — "if you work a hundred times harder you can sometimes get like a million times the payout." He concedes there are diminishing returns in *absolute* terms but argues that *relative* to competitors the returns are exponential, because being the best carries outsized reward: an Olympic gold-vs-silver gap of a tenth of a second is "everything," and the gap between number one and number two can be Uber-vs-Lyft (10x the size). What flipped him into working "a hundred x harder," per Alex, is simply that **his standards went up** — he looks back at his first presentation (≈19 slides, four bullets each, a full ~8-hour day of work) and finds it "truly embarrassing." His practical heuristic is to **count in hundreds**: ask how many *hundreds* of hours you spent on something, because depth of understanding compounds at that scale. He illustrates with a video-editor anecdote — given successively longer deadlines (30 min → another 30 → 2 hours → 2 weeks) the editor keeps making the clip better and would eventually "restructure the whole thing," proving "work does expand to the amount of time that you allow it." His own definition of done: **"I deem work done when there's nothing else that I can do to make it better."** He applies it to his book — 19 drafts, four full rewrites, written at a third-grade reading level, 106 hand-drawn images — concluding that if it fails, "it will not be for lack of effort." A recurring self-directive: "I will not be the reason this is not good." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
