# How to Buy Back Your Time as a Business Owner (2021-05-07)

## Summary

Speaking as the operator of three companies that had done over $110M in sales, Hormozi frames
the entrepreneurial journey as a repeated cycle of **buying back your time and relinquishing
control**. He walks through the order in which owners must give up control as they scale —
delivery, then admin, then operations/money, then selling, then a sales team, then leadership
— and argues the hardest part is that the thing you currently identify with is the next thing
you must hand off. He closes with his top managerial rule of thumb: **"know where the bodies
are buried."**

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- The core reason entrepreneurship is hard: it forces you to change your identity and how you
  operate as you grow; you first leap from *lack of control* to *complete control*, but being
  the sole point of control then caps growth because "there's only one of you."
- The **buyback method**: as you grow, you continually buy back your own time — and every hire
  means relinquishing a piece of control. Hormozi credits hearing the "buyback principle"
  terminology recently from "Denmark" (Dan Martell) and says he liked the term. *(attribution:
  Alex credits the term to another person; the elaboration is Alex's.)*
- The order of buyback / letting go (Alex's sequence): (1) low-skilled support tasks (e.g. an
  assistant), (2) higher-level admin (billing, collections, contracts), (3) selling, (4)
  building/managing a sales team, (5) leadership — leading leaders rather than everyone.
- The trainer example: gym owners insist "no one trains a class like I do," but training is
  both the first delivery task *and* the first thing to outsource, because the market fills it
  at ~$15/hr while the owner's time is worth ~$100/hr.
- Rough revenue milestones (2021 framing): you can sell yourself up to ~$1M/yr; crossing $1M
  usually requires scaling sales beyond yourself; a sales team gets you to maybe $3-10M;
  going from $10M toward $30M requires giving up control of **leadership**.
- A business model that only works if the founder sells at the founder's level is broken —
  closers doing half your rate should still make the model work.
- The paradox of delegation: you relinquish control but keep full accountability and
  responsibility for results.
- **Rule of thumb — "know where the bodies are buried":** if you can't name the flaws/problems
  in a department (marketing, sales, support, finance), you're too far away and need more
  transparency and attention. Hearing only good news from a department is a warning sign.
- Mindset note: trust issues are the real blocker; refusing to trust others hands your success
  to the people who hurt you. Take back control of your outcomes even if it means admitting you
  "suck," because you can control what you do.
- Callback to a prior "zero to $100 million" video: that one covered the business-model steps;
  this one covers the *personal* side, where the issue is control.

> The market will pay someone 15 an hour... if you have work that pays you a hundred dollars

> You have to say I'm going to give this over to you, I'm losing control, and yet I'm still

> The rule of thumb that I use is: know where the bodies are buried... if I don't know where
> the flaws are in a department, I'm too far away.

> If I start hearing only good news from a department and I don't hear anything negative, I'm
> too far away.

> How can you be free if you control everything?
