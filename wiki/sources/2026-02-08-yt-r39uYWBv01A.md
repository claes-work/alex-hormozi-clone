# How I Actually Make YouTube Videos

★ L3-candidate — Alex's "record once, live, harvest multiple assets" production model is a concrete, repeatable content-strategy playbook (one live session → 3 polished videos + shorts + a bonus ~100k-view live asset) that belongs in `wiki/topics/content-strategy/`.

## Summary
Short Q&A / coaching clip in which Alex Hormozi explains the actual mechanics of how he records YouTube content. A questioner asks why he'd spend three hours a week doing lives instead of planning a more polished video in that same time. Alex's core argument: his recording session is the same either way — he simply does it live, so the polished videos still get made, but the live becomes a "fourth asset" that generates extra reach (~100k views for free), deeper parasocial connection, and a better performance from him. A second belief he surfaces is that "the people behind the camera matter a ton": early content skewed hardcore because he was effectively yelling at the young crew in the room, whereas a live audience of varied people (different genders, countries) rounds off that edge and diversifies his examples and energy. He also explains how he integrates live viewer questions into the video (treating them as value-adding gaps to address rather than interruptions) and how being live forces him to actually show up. The clip closes with his standard offer read for the free acquisition.com roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-08. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-08) His recording session is identical whether "polished" or live — he just does the same session live, so nothing is lost. [guest-attributed premise, questioned: choosing between lives and polished videos assumes they trade off — Alex corrects this]
- (2026-02-08) One live session yields multiple assets: the three polished YouTube videos he'd make anyway, plus shorts, plus the live recording itself as a "fourth asset."
- (2026-02-08) The live asset typically gets around 100,000 views on its own "without me doing anything."
- (2026-02-08) Lives create deeper involvement and a parasocial connection with viewers that recorded video does not.
- (2026-02-08) He shows up better when he feels real people are watching, rather than "just the three 20-year-olds behind the camera."
- (2026-02-08) One of his biggest belief shifts about content is that the people physically in the room while recording matter a ton and shape what he says.
- (2026-02-08) His earlier content was "a little bit more hardcore than I am in reality" because he was essentially yelling at the young crew who were the only people in the room; a different room (his example: "all women") would have produced different content even under the same title/packaging.
- (2026-02-08) Seeing a diverse live audience (different genders, people in Algeria, Australia, the UK) reminds him in real time who is consuming the content, which diversifies his examples and energy.
- (2026-02-08) Being live gives him energy and accountability — you can't quit mid-session; he says that unless it's live he doesn't want to do it.
- (2026-02-08) He does not treat live questions as interruptions; he treats them as viewers asking something that, if unanswered, he'd lose from the video — so answering adds value and makes a more complete video.
- (2026-02-08) He doesn't answer every question, but keeps an eye on the comments and addresses select ones; the editing crew can cut out the gaps where he reads comments.
- (2026-02-08) Before going live, his method was: record the video, have the crew write down questions, ask them afterward, and slice those Q&A answers back in to make a more complete video.
- (2026-02-08) He currently goes live only on YouTube but believes he could multi-stream to several platforms — he just hasn't.
- (2026-02-08) He still produces his three YouTube videos a week from within the live session — the live doesn't replace them.
- (2026-02-08) Viewers are fine with watching the raw production process for two reasons: they're genuinely curious how he makes the videos, and if he announces "I'm going to do this intro real quick," a strong/interesting hook makes them want to keep watching anyway.
- (2026-02-08) His live workflow: open with a short (~5 minute) Q&A, announce the day's plan ("first video is on this, second on this, third on this"), then say "I'm going to rip" and deliver each video (e.g. a "seven ways" list).
