# Top Signs You Have The Wrong Business Partner

## Summary

Calling himself "the partnership killer," Hormozi shares hard-earned lessons on when — and
when not — to take on a business partner. He argues most early partnerships form for emotional
rather than business reasons, that the only valid reason to add a partner is money or skills
you lack, and that every business needs three roles filled: acquisition, product, and
operations. He stresses written agreements and clear division of responsibilities, and notes
he has had nine failed partnerships himself.

## Key claims

- (2021) The only legitimate reason to take on a partner is that they have **money or skills
  you do not have** — nothing else.
- (2021) Common failure mode: two similar, promotion-oriented entrepreneurs partner because
  they enjoy each other's company; if both like the same work, "one of you is not required"
  and it almost always fails.
- (2021) Partnership red flags / mistakes to avoid:
  - Partnering for the **emotional need** for company rather than a business reason.
  - **Overlapping skills** — both partners want the same role (e.g. both marketing/sales).
  - **No contract, terms, or agreement** — avoided out of fear of "messing up the vibe."
  - **Unmet, unspoken expectations** — the usual reason partnerships (like relationships) end.
  - **No clear division of responsibility** — "if everyone's responsible for everything, no
    one's responsible for anything."
  - Giving away equity too early — the biggest dilution of your future earnings is the initial
    decision to hand over half.
- (2021) He recommends going "through the lawyers first" before partnering — stress-tests how
  the person behaves under disagreement, a simulation of future conflict. Use two lawyers if
  possible, or a cheap standard template plus one hour of legal review if funds are tight.
- (2021) Every business rests on **three pillars**: (1) **Acquisition** — the rainmaker /
  money-getter (marketing & sales); (2) **Product** — fulfillment, the thing being delivered;
  (3) **Operations** — everything else (payroll, taxes, legal, IT, finance, HR). These roles
  must exist but the people filling them need not be equity partners; operators are more
  plentiful and often cheaper.
- (2021) **Skill stacking**: acquiring multiple of the three skills yourself lets you own more
  legs of the business and avoid diluting equity.
- (2021) Practical tool: put every activity in the business on a board (do a 15-minute
  time-study for a week if you don't know), then divide activities into clear ownership — the
  beginning of an org chart and specialization.
- (2021) Advice for beginners: acquire the skills yourself, get going, and make your **first
  hire a strong operational second.**
- (2021) Bonus observation: **married couples** often make great business partners because
  they already have differentiated skills — usually one hunter/acquisition type and one
  organized/operations type — plus a built-in emotional sounding board.
