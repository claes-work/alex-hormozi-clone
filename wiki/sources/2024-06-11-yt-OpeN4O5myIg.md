# This Email Campaign Generates Sales [Full Breakdown]

## Summary

Alex Hormozi breaks down a single 7-day email campaign that one of his acquisition.com
portfolio companies (a nine-figure, consumer, paid-ads-driven business with a ~3.3M-email
list) ran to a 464,000-person sub-segment, producing $1.6M in front-end sales plus $800K in
back-end ascensions for **$2.4M total (~$6 per email)** — and adding over $100M in enterprise
value. The mechanism: pull a proven piece of the product into a **challenge** used as a lead
magnet, then run it as a quarterly "cleanup" over the whole list. He walks the full funnel
math, ties the challenge to the value equation from *$100M Offers*, and closes with four
takeaways (trust smart people, more offers = more money, sell the same thing in different
mediums, and the quarterly cleanup).

## Key claims (campaign structure)

- **Context:** consumer business on paid ads; ~3.3M-email list collected over years via an
  e-commerce-style opt-in → $2,000 core sale → ~$8,000 upsell. Cost to acquire a customer
  via ads was ~$1,200 (thin margin); **cost to acquire via email is $0** once the lead is
  already on the list, so email sales drop straight to the bottom line.
- **Mental model:** ads/outbound are like a paycheck (work in, paid back); the email list is
  like a 401k/investment account that compounds. Email is a form of *content* — one-to-many
  distribution to people who know you, free, and you control 100% of who receives it.
- **Enterprise-value case:** email sales drop to the bottom line (95% of businesses trade on
  a multiple of bottom line), and a list size doesn't swing overnight the way ad performance
  can — so consistent email revenue smooths volatility and makes the business more attractive
  to investors.
- **The offer = a 7-day challenge as lead magnet.** Idea came from the marketing director,
  not Alex or the founder. The challenge was pulled from the existing product — a mini
  version of the big outcome (his weight-loss analogy: a "7-day cleanse," lose ~7 lbs in 7
  days, mostly water, but builds belief/action/reward before the long-term pitch).
- **Why challenges work — the value equation** (from *$100M Offers*): clear **desired
  outcome** × high **perceived likelihood of achievement**, minimal **time delay** (a
  deadline forces speed), and low **effort & sacrifice** (perceived as easy). Challenges hit
  all four.
- **Challenges "approximate brand":** direct-response marketers use them because they have no
  brand — a challenge crams a ~6-month relationship into 7 days ("microwaving the prospect"
  vs. slow-cooking via branding). Alex's own version is the free **Skool Games** (14-day
  challenge, leaderboards/prizes; ~1 in 3 get their first dollar within the first month).
- **Funnel math (test send):** sent to ~464,000 (~15% of list, many non-buyers) as a test to
  avoid burning the whole list → ~18% open rate (~83,000 opens) → ~5% click → landing-page
  opt-in → **10,000 registered** → **800 bought (~8%)** at $2,000 = **$1.6M**; then **20%
  ascended** to the ~$8K product = **+$800K** → **$2.4M total**, ~$6/email.
- **Proof by repetition** ("do something that works, do it again"): they immediately re-ran
  the exact same play to a different sub-segment the next month and hit **$2.2M**; next
  quarter they'll run it to the whole list using the learnings.

## Key claims (the four takeaways)

1. **Smart people (and trust them):** the winning idea came from the marketing director;
   killing every team idea trains the team to stop bringing ideas. Front-line people often
   have data the CEO/founder never sees. Pick which hills to die on — if it won't damage the
   brand and they're passionate, let them run it.
2. **Offers → money:** the number of offers you make to a prospect is directly correlated
   with money made, with the caveat that **every offer spends goodwill** — so deposit enough
   value/goodwill to "earn the ask." He frames email as content and references the **give-to-
   ask ratio**. It's OK to ask for the sale again — people get busy and forget (abandoned
   cart, kid screaming); don't be afraid of the second ask if you've earned it.
3. **Sell the same thing in a different medium:** the same product can be sold via webinar,
   phone, live workshop, or email — and people judge goodwill per medium, so switching
   mediums reduces offer fatigue and buys more "at-bats." Same information priced differently
   by delivery medium (free content vs. course vs. book vs. virtual-live vs. in-person; his
   college analogy).
4. **The quarterly cleanup (most tactical):** once a quarter, run all leads through a
   conversion mechanism (challenge / defined-outcome lead magnet) with a deadline and a big
   deliverable promise. Test on ~10% or less first (subject lines, opening, CTAs/links,
   landing pages, offer, challenge name), then roll to the list. **Re-wrap the same core
   offer** seasonally or by pain point so non-buyers see it as new — his gym examples:
   "42-day fix," "six-week booty blast," "slim for Santa," "lean by Halloween," "slim for
   summer" (the deliverable is still "eat less, move more"). Local businesses need more
   variety because small audiences fatigue faster. Between promos, provide value to deposit
   goodwill; once you have one annual calendar of four tested promos, they run on autopilot.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "$2.4 million in additional profit in 5 days — that's what one email campaign that we did
  in one of our portfolio companies generated."
- "If I can have free customers, that means whatever my gross profit is of selling my main
  thing drops straight to my bottom line."
- "I like to think about email kind of like a long-term investment account… it's kind of like
  your 401k… that you put money into and then over time it starts to compound and compound
  and compound."
- "Fundamentally a lead magnet is a complete solution to a very narrow problem, and ideally
  once you solve that problem you create the next problem which you solve with your offer."
- "You're just rapidly warming up — you're microwaving the prospect rather than slow cooking
  it, which is kind of the normal branding."
- "We didn't want to blow our load on the whole list… if this thing bombed, we didn't want to
  piss off the entire list."
- "The amount of offers that you make to any prospect is directly correlated with the amount
  of money that you'll make — with the single caveat that every time you make an offer you
  lose goodwill."
- "One of my favorite business strategies: do something that works, do it again, do more of
  it, and ideally do it better."
- "Once you have your four big promos that you're going to run throughout the year that you
  know are tested, those just become autopilot and they just become cash printers."
