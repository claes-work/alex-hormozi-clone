# Increasing Cash Flow in a Consulting Firm

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a financial-planning firm serving people 55+ ($12M revenue last year, targeting $100M in four years) says the one thing throttling growth is cash flow. Model: charge ~1% of assets under management (tiering down from there), ~1,000 clients, ~$2.5M average AUM per client; advisors cap around ~125 clients (≈$250M AUM ≈ ~$2.5M revenue per advisor); a new advisor takes six to eight months to break even, so heavy front-loaded hiring (grew from 30 to 64 team members last year, reinvesting all profit) consumes the cash. The unit economics work; the tension is turning away organic leads (YouTube + podcast) while not over-extending. Alex confirms the model is fully "excel-sheetable" and that they're likely already throttling at the fastest safe hiring/onboarding rate. He gives **two levers to grow faster**: (1) build a sales motion that gets customers to **pay upfront** — e.g. a ~$10,000 plan credited toward their first year of services — to **pull cash forward** and accelerate the cash-flow cycle; or (2) **raise equity** (the classic venture play: big opportunity, fast growth, strong returns on capital). Otherwise growth stays pinned to the rate of new customers onboarded/activated and assets coming in. On how fast is too fast, Alex reframes it as risk tolerance (Elon "go negative a billion" vs. Dave Ramsey "never lose money"), notes a bootstrapped firm simply can't go below zero, and stresses that with recurring revenue the fiscal risk is small — the **bigger risk from growing too fast is reputational**: quality of service drops, reputation falls, lead flow dries up, and you're left with half-utilized advisors making no money, missing on both fronts. He cites Keith Cunningham's frame (what's the upside, what's the downside, can I live with the downside — if not, don't do it, even for a small chance) and Leila's line that a real opportunity "isn't closed — it'll stay open and only get bigger" as you accumulate proof. His verdict: nothing's wrong with the business; it's expectation-setting — every point of margin given up is recaptured in growth but adds risk — and "this is yours to not [mess] up." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
