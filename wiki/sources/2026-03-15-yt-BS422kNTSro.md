# Helping a Wellness Clinic Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a regional dietitian-consulting and wellness-services company in Pennsylvania: 10 locations, 30 dietitians, $3.5M revenue in 2025, 35–50% year-over-year growth for three years, driven mostly by a provider-referral network (~400 referrals/month from ~18 ancillary practitioners), strong local SEO, and a local brand (podcast, content, free giveaways). ~60% of clients are already virtual. She wants to reach $10M (and eventually a $100M revenue vision) but keeps getting seduced by adjacent models — franchising, licensing, opening a gym, launching a "university" — and struggles to say no. Her framing: she has built a great "escalator" while VC-funded, virtual-only competitors are "elevators," and she can't decide whether to keep improving the escalator or hard-pivot.

Alex's counsel is a decisiveness-under-risk framework and a strong case against the shiny pivots. He offers **Keith Cunningham's** decision test — what's the upside, what's the downside, and can you live with the downside (optionally: how likely is it)? If the downside isn't livable, don't do it. He reframes the real question as risk tolerance: her existing vehicle *can* reach $100M but likely in 10–15 years; is she okay with that? He plays out the hard-pivot downside — competitors with unlimited money and smart teams haven't cracked profitable virtual-only, so betting the good, decent business she already has against that is a bad wager ("I like to assume my competitors are not morons"). He validates her "when they go quantity, we go quality" mantra. He then argues the **franchise is the wrong tool** because franchising exists to solve a *capital* need — it's the most expensive form of capital (you give almost the whole business away for a royalty), and hers is not a capital-intensive business. Working her unit economics live (~$500k top line/location, ~15% margin ≈ $75k profit), he shows why licensing at 6–8% of top line is defensible but secondary. His primary recommendation: since she can't control price in insurance, **win from the bottom up on efficiency/COGS** — a ~10% margin improvement is a ~67% bottom-line lift, equivalent to opening six locations, doable in ~6 months. Order of operations: fix margins → generate cash flow → hire out the roles you hate → the business grows without you → *then* license (a quarter-cadence location run by a team that isn't you). He closes with the **Panda Express** neighbor as proof that patient, one-location-at-a-time compounding beats the pivot ($3.7B top line, 27% net margin, 45 years selling chicken), and the "grass is greener because it's fertilized with [BS]" reminder that there's nothing on the other side of the rainbow.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex uses Keith Cunningham's decision framework for yes/no calls: assess the upside, the downside, and whether you can live with the downside (an optional fourth question is how likely it is); if the downside isn't livable, you simply don't do it.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes "escalator vs. elevator" pivot decisions as fundamentally a risk-tolerance question — the real question isn't which model is theoretically bigger, but "can your existing vehicle get you there, and are you okay with how long that takes?"
- As of March 2026, Alex assumes competitors are "not morons," especially well-funded ones with smart teams; if they have not made a model (e.g. virtual-only) profitable, that is strong evidence the model is hard, and betting a good existing business against it is a poor wager.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes the flip side too: sometimes a flashy, well-funded competitor simply "has a shitty business" propped up by funding that will bust — so quality operators can win by doubling down while rivals chase quantity.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues franchising is the wrong structure when a business is not capital-intensive: the point of franchising is capital, and it is "the most expensive form of capital you can get" because you give almost the entire business away and keep only a royalty.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that a licensing model taking ~6–8% of a licensee's top line can be sound even though licensees perform worse than the owner, because the fee comes off top line and it lets the owner cross state lines / partner with passionate local operators — but only as a later step.
- As of March 2026, Alex's core prescription for insurance-reimbursed businesses (where you cannot control price) is to win from the bottom up on efficiency and COGS: improving margin ~10% on a ~$75k/location profit is roughly a 67% bottom-line improvement, equivalent to opening about six more locations, achievable in ~6 months.
- As of March 2026, Alex's order of operations: fix core unit economics first, use the freed cash flow to hire smart people into the functions you hate, let the asset grow without you, and only *then* start a licensing arm — with his "stamp of approval" conditional on being able to open one location per quarter run by a team you are not personally involved in.
- As of March 2026, Alex maintains that scaling via M&A/acquisition of existing practices can be faster than greenfield if the owner can convert an acquired practice to their metrics quickly (client estimate: ~6 months for a stood-up practice vs. ~1 year otherwise).
- As of March 2026, Alex claims that at the pinnacle of every business your life converges to the same thing regardless of industry — you manage 7–10 department heads (sales, marketing, product/CS, IT, legal, finance) and "really changing your life is managing those 7 to 10 people and making sure they're ones you like."
- As of March 2026, Alex uses the Panda Express founder as a patience/compounding proof point: ~$3.7B top line at ~27% net margin selling chicken, built one location at a time over ~45 years.
- As of March 2026, Alex reiterates the "grass is greener because it's fertilized with [BS]" principle (crediting former CFO Suzanne Shiflett) — there are hidden "buried bodies" in the model you're envying, so the biggest gift can be realizing there's nothing on the other side of the rainbow.
