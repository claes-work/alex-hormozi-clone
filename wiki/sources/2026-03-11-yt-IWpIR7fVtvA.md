# What I Did When I Was Broke

## Summary
Podcast clip in which Alex lays out the money philosophy and mindset he ran during his broke period, framed as advice for someone starting from zero. His core financial move: he never made his **bank balance** the goal — he made his **monthly income** the goal, treating income as "potential energy" and savings as "stored energy," and he reinvested the stored energy back into raising income (spend $2k to turn $5k/month into $6k/month, because over a year that's $12k more income). He states three "foundations" in order: **(1) it's your fault** you're where you are (and therefore you're the one who can get out); **(2) spend less than you make**; **(3) take all the excess and spend it on making more.** He argues you cannot save your way out of minimum wage — the famous "one girl at McDonald's" made ~$1M over 40 years, but nobody counts the 100M+ who didn't make it, so aim higher. The hardest part, he says, is not the business difficulty but **swallowing the pride** to look like you're "amounting to nothing" in the short term while everyone tells you so. He reframes failed bets as **experience points**: a failed experiment doesn't put you back at zero, it puts you at "where you were plus one experience," and enough accumulated experience "levels you up." Experiments cost money (ads that don't work, a $30–50/month Shopify store), but the bar to start has never been lower than now — versus the early 20th century when you had to mortgage your house. On education spend: courses are fine and he's had an "amazing return on education" because he actually applied everything (he wanted to be the best student and treated business as another kind of school), but the real ROI is doing the work. Finally, his skills thesis: **the wealthiest people he knows only think about skills** — what can I learn and what can this skill get me — and they spend any money they can to buy more skills. Early on you buy skills for yourself; once you generate excess income you "buy other people's lives," i.e. buy their time and their years of accumulated skill, which is why you first get good at *stuff* and eventually generalize to being good at *people*. He notes YouTube didn't exist and Instagram was brand new when he was doing this; he was already a business owner when podcasting emerged. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
