# What I look for to invest in companies [no bs]

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video laying out the three-part checklist he and Leila use to
assess whether a business is worth investing in (or "an opportunity vehicle worth
pursuing"). He frames the criteria around maximizing the "opportunity" a business
represents, then explains how any owner can reconfigure their own business against
the same three lenses. He closes with an argument for why he thinks TAM (total
addressable market) is an overrated indicator of a company's potential size. At the
time of recording he describes Acquisition.com as a portfolio of businesses doing
$10 million a year.

## Key claims

- **The three-part investment / opportunity checklist:**
  1. **Number of potential units sold** — how many people could potentially buy the
     thing. This tells him "how long we can run this game" and whether demand is
     durable (he uses weight loss as an example of durable demand: food will keep
     tasting good and people won't change their genetics).
  2. **Potential profit per unit (gross margin)** — a function of the value the
     customer gets from the outcome minus the cost to deliver it. High-value but
     high-cost-to-deliver businesses are less interesting; low-value but near-zero-cost
     can be enticing depending on volume.
  3. **Competitive / supply-demand dynamics of the landscape** — how hard it is to
     break in, how new the space is, whether established players could price you out.
- A business can check two of the three boxes and still be a pass. Example: wireless
  cell service checks huge demand and near-zero incremental cost, but fails on
  competitive dynamics (you'd have to beat Verizon and AT&T).
- His hypothetical "crazy good" example: cheap hardware (~$500) that does crypto mining
  earning ~$50k/year — huge demand, huge quantifiable value, low cost, and (at the time)
  no competition in a brand-new fast-growing space — would be an incredibly interesting
  opportunity.
- **Gross margin rule of thumb for service businesses: 80% or higher.** Selling
  something for $1,000/month should cost less than $200/month to deliver. Gross profit
  (before cost of doing business) is what you chip marketing, admin, payroll, HR,
  finance and legal out of.
- The biggest companies sell something very valuable that costs them almost nothing at
  the margin: Google and Facebook (ad space/eyeballs), phone companies (one-time build,
  near-zero incremental user cost), and software (one-time build, incremental user is
  almost all profit).
- **Information / education businesses have software-like ~100% gross margins without
  the development cost** — which is why he and Leila like course/e-learning/coaching
  businesses. The downside is higher churn, because a skill's value declines once
  learned.
- **Reduce churn by separating one-time-consumable from ongoing-consumable components.**
  Information (the skill) is consumed once; community, accountability, and newly-created
  problems are consumable on an ongoing basis. Peeling these apart builds high LTV and
  enterprise value.
- "All solutions create more problems" — solving old problems creates new ones, which
  are ongoing opportunities to sell into.
- **Why he distrusts TAM as a size indicator:** founders and businesses expand their
  vision over time (Facebook started as a college yearbook network). On a long-enough
  horizon, adjacent markets/silos open up and mergers/acquisitions expand the total
  market, so current TAM ≠ future TAM. He does use TAM on a short 3–5 year horizon
  (assumes no avatar change), but not long-term.
- Caveat: expanding into adjacent markets prematurely is a mistake — you can scale a
  single avatar to ~$100M/year before needing to branch.
- To grow the "opportunity size" of any business, ask: can I make it applicable to more
  people (units)? Can I increase value or lower delivery cost (gross profit per unit)?
  Can I enter a more unique/newer space (by going somewhere clearly new or redefining
  what I do)?
