# The Formula for Creating a $100M Business

## Summary

Alex Hormozi opens by recounting how he scaled a business from $0 to $4.4M/month in 20 months, and how miserable and fragile that growth felt while he was personally doing everything. The turning point came when an investment-banking friend told him the business could sell for millions — but only after fixing what would scare off an institutional investor. From that, acquisition.com developed the "value acceleration method": the idea that a company's Enterprise Value is driven by three variables — number of customers, lifetime gross profit, and risk — and that ten specific levers each unlock another layer of that value.

## Key claims

- He grew a business from $0 to $4.4M/month in 20 months, but the experience "also sucked" — a constant emotional roller coaster where he personally ran every part of the operation.
- Growth felt fragile: whenever customers dropped, he had to personally go make ads, get on camera, and drum up business, so the company depended entirely on him.
- Attending masterminds and coaching groups stopped helping once he was "by far the biggest person in the room."
- An investment-banking friend told him the business could sell for "millions of dollars," but not in its current state — an institutional investor would first need "a ton of things" fixed.
- A business that merely "makes money" is different from an "incredibly valuable asset"; the goal of the value acceleration method is to convert the former into the latter.
- Within a business, the party you provide value to (for valuation purposes) is the investor who will buy a share of it.
- Three variables drive how much a company is worth: (1) number of customers, (2) lifetime gross profit, and (3) risk — the value formula is customers × lifetime gross profit ÷ risk.
- Lifetime gross profit = the profit generated from a customer over the lifetime of their stay, not lifetime revenue. (Example: 10 meals at $10 = $100 lifetime revenue, but at $1 profit/meal = $10 lifetime gross profit.)
- To make more money you either get more customers or make each customer worth more.
- Risk represents how likely the cash flows are to continue in the future; he invokes Warren Buffett's definition of value as all future cash flows discounted into the present.
- Together these three variables equal "Enterprise Value" (EV), and there are ten things that each unlock another level of Enterprise Value.
