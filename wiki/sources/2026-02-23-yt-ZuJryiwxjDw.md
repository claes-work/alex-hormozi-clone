# Why you aren't making as much money as you want

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues the main reason people don't make the money they want is that they sell to people who don't have it. He walks through US wealth distribution (scaled to $100 across 100 percentiles) to show how concentrated wealth is, maps that onto a business power law (Pareto within Pareto), and prescribes a tiered pricing ladder: for every new tier, 5–10x the price and expect ~20% to take it. He backs it with his own origin story of accidentally selling $6K–$10K gym launches after being used to $500 packages, plus tactical advice on pricing, anchoring, close rates as a pricing signal, and lead qualification.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed)

- The core reason you're not making as much as you want: **you're selling to people who don't have the money to give you** — go where the money is.
- US wealth is extremely concentrated (his scaled-to-$100 illustration): bottom 50% ≈ $2.50, next 40% ≈ $28, next 9% ≈ $38, top 1% ≈ $32 — so the top 1% holds more than the bottom 90% combined, and the top 10% hold ~69% of the wealth.
- "Sell to the rich, they pay better" is not a pithy line — it's a mathematical reality that takes people years to internalize, often blocked by belief issues because everyone they know is poor.
- Pareto (80/20) is fractal within a business: ~20% of customers drive ~80% of profit; within that, ~4% drive ~64%; within that, the top 1% drives ~51%. This is "how you do less and make more."
- Serving one high-paying customer often costs little more than serving one of the other 99 — more work, but significantly more profit — **only if your business model allows them to pay more**.
- Rule of thumb: **for every new pricing tier, 5–10x the price and expect ~20% of people to take it**; each tier should roughly double total revenue, or it isn't worth the added operational constraint.
- Because overhead is often already covered by the base tier, a new higher tier can contribute disproportionately to the bottom line (his example: profit jumps from 10 to 90).
- Build **top-down** (the Tesla ladder: Roadster → Model S → Model 3/Y). Anchoring high is a stronger brand narrative and easier to operationalize than scaling mass volume from the start.
- The only way to profitably serve the poor masses is to already have tons of money and serve them automated at low price via volume — expensive and hard (Tesla nearly went bankrupt; Netflix/Spotify were propped up by outside capital). ~78–80% of US businesses are service-based and have no automated way to serve masses, so they should go the opposite direction: best customer, highest price.
- Mispricing is the common error: people go $1,000 → $1,500. The buyer's next rung from a $1K thing is $5K–$10K.
- "The only thing worse than offering a $1,000 thing to someone with a $100 budget is offering a $100 thing to someone with a $1,000 budget" — you lose $900 instead of $100, and that $900 is disproportionately profit.
- Top 10% of Americans have a $1M+ net worth (1 in 10 people) — the money exists; you're just not selling them something they want, and being too cheap can make them doubt you're any good.
- His health-space example: a 50% price raise **increased** close rates because the offer had been too cheap to be believed.
- Think **absolute profit, not relative**: one person paying $10K (cost $2K) equals 400 people paying $50 (cost $25).
- Close rate is a pricing signal: closing 80% ≈ 3–4x price sitting on the table; 60–80% ≈ 2–3x; 50–60% ≈ 1.5–2x; 40–50% ≈ 1.25–1.5x; 30–40% ≈ appropriately priced; below 30% ≈ get better at selling (better offer or better customers).
- Rich buyers think in ratio (cost vs. value: "For what?"), poor buyers think in cost ("That's expensive"). Rich buyers want it **fast, easy, and guaranteed**, and shop high-to-low.
- Pricing is two-way communication: raise price until demand/supply equilibrium; higher price → higher margins → better talent → better service → better reputation → more demand → higher price (the virtuous cycle of price in services).
- You can tell how advanced a service business owner is by how expensive their product is; people take price as an indication of value.
- Rough price anchors: consumer impulse ≈ $500–600; consumer high-ticket service ≈ $3K–10K; small-business cheap ≈ ~$500/month, mid-tier ≈ $2–3K/month; large business can be sold billion-dollar things.
