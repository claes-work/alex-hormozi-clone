# How I Scaled My Sales Team [Compensation, Recruiting & Training]

## Summary
Recorded as a keynote/interview for Taki Moore's 8-figure-and-up mastermind, Hormozi walks through two things his portfolio companies (then ~$85M/year) had learned: how to build a high-performance outbound sales team, and higher-level lessons about scaling and exiting a business. He explains why he added cold outbound as a second acquisition channel after private-equity diligence flagged that his gym-licensing business was 100% channel-dependent on Instagram/Facebook, that his name was the brand, and that churn was high. He details the full outbound org chart (BDR → SDS → BC), the funnel math, the "baseball farm system" of ranked roles with milestone-based promotions, on-target-earnings by tier, recruiting and try-or-die closer rules, and his CLOSER sales script. The second half covers the reality of a "$100M sale" (earnouts, notes, taxes netting far less), the buy-build-refinance alternative to selling, the "beta process" for de-guru-ing product innovation, and the emotional multi-year transition from CEO to owner. Taki Moore and Leila are present as interlocutors; only Alex-attributed material is treated as voice data below.

## Key claims
- Portfolio does ~$85M/year; the largest of the portfolio sales teams is a 26-person team, run across both inbound (application funnels) and outbound (cold calls).
- Roughly two years prior, doing ~$1M/week, Leila wanted to shut the business down; an investment-banker friend told him they could get ~$250M if they finished 12 months at that rate (he cites $17M profit in year two). This began a ~2.5-year journey that ended in a sale.
- Bankers/PE named three weaknesses: churn (how long customers stayed), his name being the brand, and channel dependency on Instagram + Facebook (100% of sales volume). He chose **outbound** as the second channel because he could never get YouTube to work in his niche.
- Outbound ramps **linearly, not exponentially**: month 1 = 1 sale, m2 = 2, m3 = 4, then ~6–8, 12, 16… By month 14 it did ~45 sales/month, roughly 60% of total sales volume.
- Economics rule: to justify manual outbound the ticket should be **at least $15k** (he prefers $20k+ for margin). Their LTV was $40k (started during Covid for brick-and-mortar gyms).
- **Three roles (org chart looks like a Christmas tree):** BDRs (first-touch outbound, mentally "replace the advertisement") → SDS = Sales Development Specialists (qualify the lead and deliver the promised value) → BC = Business Consultants (the closers). A separate fourth "ascensions" team sells the $40k product.
- **BDR funnel math:** 100 dials/day → ~20 pickups (20%) → ~2 appointments that show. BDRs are compensated **only** on shows ($50/show). Lists scraped off Google; VAs in the Philippines enrich/verify the data; calls run through **Phone Burner** wrapped with a **local area code** (he says without local-area-code wrapping there's no point). BDRs work **4-hour blocks**, not 8 — his sales manager's view was that 8 paid hours yields ~5.5 hours of calling, so pay 4 and get ~3.5.
- BDR ramp is fast (~2 days to train); a good BDR researches the gym before calling; tonality matters. Pitch frame: "we've been calling gyms in the area… if gyms don't exist, our company doesn't exist… we just want to help you out," setting an appointment with the SDS.
- **SDS call** (~45 min = ~30 min delivering genuinely valuable, unscalable-if-needed help + ~15 min "frame shift"): SDS are almost exclusively ex-clients for domain credibility. They deliver a real "top 1%" tactic, then transition ("can I switch hats for a second?") and hand to the closer via a **live scripted speaker-phone call** — this solved a two-month problem of SDS-set appointments not showing for the close.
- **BC/closer** closes ~10% of scheduled+showed SDS appointments. CAC ≈ $500 in BDR pay per close against a $40k LTV. Closing framing is set explicitly as a sales call.
- **Baseball farm system:** AA = BDRs, AAA = SDS, "the bigs" = BC. Each role has four tiers — junior, associate, [role], senior. Milestones (objective qualifiers, e.g. ~50 then ~100 showed appointments) pace ~8 weeks of production apart, because "a salesperson has six to eight weeks of steam before they get disheartened." Each promotion is a title bump + a tiny/symbolic pay bump (e.g. $50 → $55/show).
- "You don't get promoted, you get the ability to be promoted" — at senior tier you become eligible; scaling more BDRs trickles demand up the tree and a qualified senior is picked for the bigs. **Relegation** exists: drop below qualifiers and you drop a level.
- **On-target earnings (OTE):** BDR ~$40k; SDS ~$55–65k (tight ~$10k range); BC ~$120–220k (top performers ~$225k). BDRs are attracted despite $40k because the clear path shows they can hit ~$200k in 9–12 months, "no degree… pure meritocracy and the numbers don't lie." The career-path ladder also kills HR pay conversations and reduces churn.
- **Try-or-die for new closers:** first 10 deals / ~2 weeks; must close at least 1 of 10. "I have yet to find a killer who doesn't close in the first two weeks."
- Inbound close rate rose from ~20–25% to ~60% because closers "cut their teeth at the bottom" on cold outbound (got hung up on and cussed at).
- **Recruiting fix:** stop over-screening BDRs. Because ramp is ~2 days and there's effectively no cost-per-lead, "let them all in and see how they do."
- **CLOSER script** (question-based, not heavily scripted; he prefers questions except for ~$200-and-under high-transaction sales): **C**larify why they're there, **L**abel it, **O**verview past pain ("pain cycle"), **S**ell the vacation, **E**xplain away concerns, **R**einforce the decision. Sell the vacation = sell the outcome/status ("the trees and the wind"), not the "plane flight" (features); limit to **three** points. Concerns handled via **past agreements** (decision-maker), **conviction/value** (price — drill the sales team's belief, not the prospect), and a best-case/worst-case + decision framework (delay/"think about it").
- Objection handling uses concession → reciprocity → "fair enough" (people don't like to feel they're being unfair).
- "R" (reinforce) reframes the close as the start of the customer journey: a **handshake, not a handoff**, to customer success — an immediate personalized welcome video to kill buyer's remorse.
- **Sales is a transference of conviction/belief; the bridge is trust.** The job is making sure the salesperson actually believes — and it's not binary but a matter of *how much* they believe.
- **The "beta process"** (learned from management consulting; PE firms loved it) to de-guru product innovation: survey clients for problems → have them upvote the most salient → round-table the top ~1% (10–20 people) → "do the things that **all** of them do, not all of the things they do" → productize → test on bottom/middle clients → measure → **report even failed tests** (failures still provide value). A *process*, not the founder, drives innovation.
- **Taking the name off the door** is a ~3-year process. Edify customers into **siloed subject-matter experts**, not generalists (a generalist either becomes a replaceable face or a face that can walk/threaten the business).
- **"$100M sale" reality:** headline number is inflated by seller notes, earnouts, and rolled equity; e.g. ~$60M upfront, minus 3–4% broker, ~20% capital gains, escrow → nets closer to ~$40M. Point: the sale changes far less than the story you tell yourself.
- **Buy, build, refinance** (attributed to "Chiron"): the wealthiest don't sell; you can borrow ~70% tax-free against a fully-owned asset instead of selling, and the growing company pays down the debt. Only sell if you think "the party's over."
- Cites the man who lives above him: **$3.8B revenue, 100% owned, 26% net margin ≈ $935M take-home**, whose secret was "I've been doing the same thing for 40 years."
- **Levels of letting go:** 0→1 give up doing/fulfillment; 1→10 give up selling (build a selling team); 10→30 give up marketing; 30→50+ give up leading → decision-making. The CEO-to-owner transition took ~2.5 years and was harder emotionally than expected (identity).
- Passive income is overrated: the goal should be "freedom **to** do things I want," not "freedom **from** work." The team became more creative and self-reliant once he and Leila stepped out.
