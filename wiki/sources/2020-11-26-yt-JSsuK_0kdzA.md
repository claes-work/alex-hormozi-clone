# The Oh Sh*t Moment (2020-11-26)

## Summary

Addressing gym owners during the COVID-19 shutdown, Hormozi warns that those waiting in
"denial phase" for things to return to normal are in for an "oh sh*t moment": when gyms
reopen, far fewer members will come back than owners expect, because the pandemic force-
adopted remote fitness and pulled ~10 years of mass adoption forward. He argues the smarter
move is to learn to sell remote/online fitness now, presenting statistical model numbers
comparing remote vs. in-person ROI and framing the choice as "do you want to be a business
owner or a gym owner."

## Key claims

- (2020-11-26) Owners are currently in a "denial phase," hoping to hold out a few more weeks
  until it's over; Hormozi believes sitting and waiting won't work. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) When gyms reopen (and he says gyms will be allowed back "last"), far fewer
  members will return than owners anticipate, and recovery will be slower than expected
  because people remain wary and convenience wins. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Analogy: people said online fitness would die once gyms reopen, just as
  Blockbuster executives believed customers would always value the in-person experience over
  streaming — humans rationalize away bad-case scenarios and pick convenience. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) COVID dragged remote services roughly 10 years into the future in terms of
  mass adoption — "forced adoption" — so a larger-than-expected share of people will stay
  remote. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) A second "oh sh*t" layer: companies that did layoffs may not rehire everyone,
  because demand won't match the pre-COVID "single greatest moment in economic history."
  (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) He says the reopening will actually be great for him because he'll aggressively
  market to gyms that need differentiation. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Remote/online economics (self-reported statistical model averages): marketing
  a 25-mile radius with zero decrease in sales percentages; remote gyms getting ~40% of leads
  into a sales conversation vs. ~20% in person (twice as many "at bats"). (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Average phone close rate ~29% vs. ~42% in person, but with double the at-bats
  the net is more sales. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Bigger radius = cheaper leads due to "quadratic expansion" (area of a circle);
  a 25-mile radius reaches roughly 10x+ the people of a 5-mile radius. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Front-end math: guys are essentially breaking even on the first transaction
  (vs. usual 2:1–4:1); spend ~$1,500 → ~$6,600 upfront revenue in the first 30 days on
  average, with ~80% back-end gross margin. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Worked example: spend ~$2,200 in ad spend over 30 days → ~50 people, after
  accounting for only 60% converting into continuity at $200/month = a $10,000/month remote
  business built in 30 days for an average operator. (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) He disputes that a single gym can make $1M/year unless it has a massive
  facility (e.g. 6,000 members at $100/month) — "show me the bank statement." (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) Post-corona, winning businesses will have lower overhead, higher margins, and
  greater ability to reach customers where they are — all of which remote fitness satisfies.
  (paraphrase)
- (2020-11-26) The one skill to acquire is selling over the phone; doing so also improves
  in-person conversion once gyms reopen. (paraphrase)
