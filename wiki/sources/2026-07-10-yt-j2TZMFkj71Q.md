# Building a $3,000,000 Business for a Stranger in 31 Minutes - Episode 2

## Summary

Episode 2 of Alex's **"Scale or Fail"** show format: he coaches a real business owner
on camera, builds a growth plan live, then delivers a verdict on whether he will keep
working with them ("scale") or not ("fail"). The guest is **Mim Jenkinson**, an
Australian ex-corporate marketer who teaches crafters to make stickers. She started the
business ten years ago as a creative project during breast-cancer chemotherapy and has
since taught 20,000+ crafters. Her model: low-ticket digital products around a core
membership, **Secret Sticker Society** ($27/month or $270/year), acquired through a
**$10 paid live boot camp** run three times per year, with upsells in the funnel to
offset CAC. Self-reported numbers: ~$1.1M AUD (~$800K USD) trailing-12-month revenue,
30–35% profit margin, ~$37 CAC per boot-camp ticket, ~$36 AOV on the front end
(near break-even), 22% conversion of ticket buyers into the membership, ~$300 LTV,
~5% churn, ~$16K ad spend on the last launch, and a goal of $3M USD/year within three
years.

Alex runs his standard diagnostic — "if you double the amount of customers, does it
break anything?" — and, since nothing breaks, declares it **a pure demand issue** and
splits the plan into **offer** and **traffic**:

**Traffic.** (1) A launch **ad-spend cadence**: ~5–10% of budget in week one purely to
test creative, roughly flat spend through the middle weeks while scaling winners, then
the majority of the budget compressed into the end — on the order of 60–70% in the
final week, with as much as ~50% in the last three days. (Alex mentions he has spent
$500K/day on ads at the end of his own launches — self-reported.) (2) **Creative
volume: "think in hundreds," not fours.** She ran ~4 creatives last launch; he maps a
path to 80+ founder ads alone (her 20 best-performing ads of all time, each re-shot in
4 different settings) plus community-sourced creative: screenshots of member posts as
static ads, member sticker photos as ads, clipped moments from her daily launch lives.
(3) A **customer-content activation loop**: incentivize members to film themselves
making their first sticker within 7 days (unlock a "super secret sticker set") — it
doubles as onboarding activation AND "every single customer makes you an ad."
(4) **Hooks up the awareness ladder** (Eugene Schwartz's five levels of awareness, from
his hooks/ads playbook): to scale spend she must target pain-/problem-aware audiences,
not just product-/offer-aware ones — that is what stops CPA from tripling when spend
scales. (5) **"100 ponds and no oceans"**: scale comes from draining a hundred small
sub-audiences with avatar-specific callouts, not from running two generic ads at the
whole ocean. (6) The **decision**: he challenges her to be willing to spend up to $100
CPA — her ~$50 cap was self-imposed, not economic. Side notes: optimize campaigns for
purchases, and track EPCs (earnings per click).

**Offer.** (1) Continue **annual-only at launch** (a change he gave her previously that
already lifted annual take-rate from ~10% to ~30% and doubled average cart value);
don't mention monthly during the launch at all, then run a post-cart-close "mop-up"
campaign selling monthly without the bonuses. (2) Add a **physical-product premium for
annual**: bundle the physical sticker kit (cost ~$10–20 incl. shipping) as a ~$149
"decoy"/false offer that is free with the annual — pushing annual take toward 50%.
(3) The "wild idea" (test B, run later as a derisked split test at the same $10 price
point): reposition the paid boot camp as a **7-day trial of the membership itself** —
100% of ticket buyers enter the continuity instead of 22%, then push everyone to
annual; he walks the unit-economics math showing the trial variant wins even with a
lower ~55% post-trial retention and no upsells. Verdict: **scale** — Alex commits to a
year of working with her, closing with "I just want you to not be afraid to spend
more." In his to-camera debrief he frames her business as **media arbitrage** ("she
buys clicks for less than she can make on them") and says the only things missing were
the decision and the strategy.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-10. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **"What breaks when you double?" as the opening diagnostic.** If doubling customers
  breaks nothing on delivery, the business has a pure demand constraint and the work
  is offer + traffic only. (Same test as in earlier coaching sources.)
- **Launch ad-spend cadence:** ~5–10% of total spend in week one solely to test
  creative; hold roughly flat through the middle while scaling winners; back-load the
  majority (call it 60–70%) into the final week, with around half of total spend in
  the last three days.
- **Alex spends up to $500K/day on ads at the end of his own launches.**
  _(Self-reported.)_
- **Think in hundreds of creatives, not handfuls.** Going from 4 ads to hundreds is
  the prerequisite for scaling spend. Founder shortcut: take the 20 highest-converting
  ads of all time and re-shoot each in 4 different settings/backgrounds → 80 ads.
- **Community content is an ad factory.** Screenshots of member posts (with
  highlights), member product photos, and clips from live Q&As are all ads — "stuff
  that's happening that we just need to capture."
- **Activation incentive doubles as UGC engine.** Reward customers (an unlockable
  bonus) for filming themselves getting their first win in the first 7 days: it is
  onboarding activation and "every single customer makes you an ad" — a compounding
  loop (200 customers per launch = 200 ads).
- **Scale requires moving up the awareness ladder.** Ads targeted only at product-/
  offer-aware audiences stop performing when spend scales (CPA can triple); to open up
  targeting, hooks must speak to pain-/problem-aware audiences. (References the five
  levels of awareness from his hooks/ads playbook; framework credited to Eugene
  Schwartz.)
- **"100 ponds and no oceans."** Scale is achieved by going *down* into a hundred
  sub-specific avatars with tailored callouts (per the lead-magnet/leads book
  "callouts" concept), not by broadcasting the same two ads to everyone — "which is
  counter" to intuition.
- **Spending caps are usually self-imposed.** With ~$300 LTV and profitable unit
  economics, capping CPA at ~$50 has no economic basis; he challenges her to be
  willing to pay $100 CPA. (Echoes the self-imposed-ceiling diagnosis from the
  2026-02-19 HRT-clinic Q&A.)
- **Annual-only at launch.** Present ONLY the annual membership (with bonuses) during
  the launch; after cart close, run non-buyers to a monthly "mop-up" campaign without
  the bonuses. Her partial implementation previously moved annual take-rate from ~10%
  to ~30% and doubled average cart value. _(Result self-reported by guest.)_
- **Physical-product premium for annual.** Package the physical starter kit customers
  need anyway (~$10–20 cost) as a ~$149 offer, positioned as a "false offer"/decoy,
  then given free with annual — "I want a physical product premium for annual" —
  projected to push annual take toward 50%. Higher AOV (~$300) also buys spending
  confidence: "It's a different level of confidence."
- **Order bumps should be need-based.** Replace the "similar course" bump (30% take)
  with the sticker paper/tools customers need to do the thing — if they must buy it
  anyway, they might as well buy it from you at high margin.
- **Boot-camp-as-trial (test B).** Reposition the paid challenge as a 7-day trial of
  the membership: 100% of buyers enter continuity instead of 22%, everyone is pushed
  to annual during the event, and tools are upsold for cash flow. His math: 100
  buyers × 22% × $300 LTGP ≈ $6.6K vs. ~55% trial retention × $300 ≈ $16.5K — a
  ~50–60% lift even with zero upsells and no annual takers.
- **Derisk bold tests as split tests.** Run trial vs. non-trial at the identical $10
  price with identical ads so only one variable changes — "we've derisked this test."
- **Scarcity justification for the annual bonus:** the physical kit is only available
  during the event because it costs real money and logistics — a truthful reason-why.
- **Her business model is media arbitrage** — buying clicks for less than she makes on
  them — which is why it is fundamentally scalable once the math is right; 5% churn is
  "really good." _(Debrief; churn figure originates from guest data.)_
- **The verdict logic:** the question is whether anything would prevent scaling over a
  year of working together; here nothing does — she has a working model, technical
  chops, and only lacked "the decision and the strategy."
- **Fear of spending is the final boss.** Closing prescription: "not be afraid to
  spend more" — she can always revert to what worked before, but the plan "has the
  potential to 5x or 10x the business."
- Sequencing note: do the annual + physical-premium offer first (manageable), run the
  trial test as V2 — "The last thing I want to do is break everything" when a model
  already works.
