# Behind the Scenes of The $100M Leads Launch [Ep 006]

## Summary
A behind-the-scenes / produced-vlog episode of *The Game* documenting Alex Hormozi's
launch of his second book, *$100M Leads*, via a live online event attended by roughly
half a million registrants. Hormozi narrates in voiceover his two weeks of all-day
presentation prep, the deliberate design of the launch itself as the "example" for a
book about generating leads (using warm outreach, affiliates, and paid ads rather than a
single post to his own audience), and the sales-craft behind a 17-minute "stack" that
culminated in giving everything away for free. Fly-on-the-wall footage captures the day
before (his birthday), the launch morning, and three simultaneous tech failures that hit
as he stepped on stage under 562,000 link clicks in ~60 seconds. He frames the whole
move around long-term brand associations and legacy — turning down an estimated
$50M payday to "make history" rather than "make bank" — and closes on the emotional
motivation of passing on skills that might outlast him. Leila Hormozi, co-author Trevor,
and team members appear and speak; only Alex-attributed lines are voice training data.

## Key claims
- The book/launch was designed as a "meta example": just as *$100M Offers* (the book) was
  itself an example of a good offer, *$100M Leads* was to be exemplified not by the offer
  but by "how we promote the book... how we got leads," which is why the event used every
  advertising method (warm outreach, affiliates, paid ads) rather than a single post.
- Self-reported launch scale: ~half a million people registered; over 100,000 in check-in
  with ~66 on the feed early; 562,000 link clicks "in a matter of like 60 seconds."
- Tech failures: ~140,000 people could not get in; the team pivoted "on the fly" to a
  YouTube link and recovered another ~40,000-50,000; the YouTube live stream was ~3
  minutes delayed during three important minutes; some Zoom rooms were misconfigured so
  attendees could unmute. (all self-reported)
- Publishing/logistics: sold on Shopify rather than Amazon because Amazon limits any new
  book to ~5,000-10,000 hardbacks and they expected to sell far more; self-published,
  fronted all the money, did all the prints, and handle housing and shipping. (self-reported)
- On rights: "whenever you publish a book you actually sell the rights to your content,"
  like musicians selling to a label — which is why a normal publisher wouldn't let him
  price *Offers* at $1.99 or give away a free chapter, "because that's where they would
  make their money."
- Presentation craft: one idea per slide (credits a ~15-minute TED talk, "Death by
  PowerPoint," as forever changing how he made presentations), pairing each idea with a
  visual (from the marketing/sales world), and compressing "zillions of hours" like a
  stand-up comedian perfecting each 30-second bit.
- The "stack" is described as a proven direct-sales tactic; he stacked the offer for ~17
  minutes to create ~15 minutes of suspense and an emotional "villain to hero" reversal
  when the price was revealed as free.
- Sales results (self-reported): ~100,000 books sold the first day; *$100M Offers* had
  sold "just under 500,000 copies over two years"; he believes *Leads* will ultimately
  outsell *Offers* because more people perceive they have a leads problem than an offer
  problem.
- Aftermath (self-reported, mid-first-week): his name trended on all of Twitter; the
  *Leads* audiobook hit #7 on all of Amazon; his podcast moved from 50 to 26 on all
  podcasts, #1 in entrepreneurship and #2 in business.
- He declined an estimated "$50 million dollar payday" (charging for the book/course) to
  keep it free, arguing it builds long-term trust and brand associations; "if you measure
  on a one-year time Horizon then it will never make sense."
- "The most expensive cost in almost all companies is meetings"; he screens meetings by
  two questions — what problem are we solving, and who's required to solve it — and cut a
  scheduled hour-long meeting to 28 minutes.
- The team's content standard evolved from "one-of-one" (say/show only what only you can)
  to "one-of-zero" — doing what no one else would do (giving away material others sell).
