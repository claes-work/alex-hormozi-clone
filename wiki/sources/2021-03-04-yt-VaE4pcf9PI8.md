# "What Business Should I Start in 2021?" [Perfect Business]

## Summary

In this 2021 solo talk, Hormozi lays out his personal criteria for the "perfect business,"
building on three attributes he had long used plus two he added after studying Warren
Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway shareholder writings. He frames the ideal business as
**unique, expensive, sticky, "air"** — run by a person who is **risk-averse, long-term
thinking, and of unimpeachable character**. He credits Buffett's line about a business that
"costs a penny, sells for a buck, and is habit-forming" as the seed of the framework.

> ⚠️ PROTO-FRAMEWORK: These "perfect business" attributes are an early (2021) precursor to
> the business-model attributes Hormozi codified in later work. Treat this as the origin of
> that thinking, not the mature version. Confirm continuity against later sources before
> presenting as his current model.

## Key claims

- (2021) Hormozi's criteria for a perfect business: **unique, expensive, sticky, "air"** —
  i.e. something that costs almost nothing to make ("air"/a penny), can be sold for far more
  (expensive), is habit-forming so customers keep buying (sticky, long LTV), and that no one
  else can sell (unique — a brand moat / share of mind competitors can't copy).
- (2021) He adds two operator/management criteria drawn from Buffett: the person running the
  business should be **(1) risk-averse, (2) a long-term thinker, and (3) of unimpeachable
  character.**
- (2021) He argues you should not feel bad about high margins — "you charge what the market
  will give you"; markets run on information advantages/inefficiencies between seller and buyer.
- (2021) Margin math: gross margin improvements compound profitability far more than they
  appear. Selling a $1 item that costs a penny is ~10x more profitable than one costing a
  dime, because per unit of cost you can sell ten times as much. Every increment (e.g. 90%→95%
  gross margin) can roughly double a business's profitability.
- (2021) He notes turnaround work on gyms typically found owners near 66% margins ("horrible")
  and pushed them toward 80%.
- (2021) Risk-aversion principle: "anything times zero is zero" — one bet to zero can wipe out
  decades of track record; the wealthiest people give up upside to protect downside, while poor
  people chase lottery-style guaranteed-downside bets.
- (2021) Long-term thinking: the longer someone's stated time horizon, the wealthier/more
  successful they tend to be; people fixated on this week/this month rarely earn much. He
  favors family-owned businesses because families think in generations, not quarters.
- (2021) Character compounds: reputation becomes a sustainable, compounding competitive
  advantage — a person true to their word for 30 years has a more valuable word than one at
  10 years — and reliability (checks clear, do what you say) wins more and faster deals.
