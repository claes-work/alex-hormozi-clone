# 'I'm Facing A Billion Dollar Decision' [Ep 012]

## Summary
A day-in-the-life / personal-reflection episode of *The Game* in which Alex Hormozi
narrates his approach to a major, multi-year business decision while traveling to San
Diego to keynote an event for Real (a publicly traded real estate brokerage run by his
friend Sharran Srivatsaa). Hormozi says he currently sees two roughly billion-dollar,
relatively irreversible "paths" and applies a personal litmus test — an opportunity must
be ~10x his current scale to earn his attention. He interweaves frameworks on
irreversible risk, opportunity cost of time, and committing fully ("no half measures,"
"burn the boats") with fly-on-the-wall footage of him charming staff into bending rules
(a steakhouse dress code, a closing ice cream shop) to illustrate practical sales
micro-skills. Candid domestic moments with his wife Leila (a running Chili's bit) and
banter with a co-host round out the episode. He closes by advising first-time
decision-makers to take the lower-risk, higher-probability path, while reserving the big
irreversible swing for those with enough "singles, doubles, triples" already banked.

## Key claims
- His current opportunity litmus test is roughly 10x: "we're at 100," so an opportunity
  "has to be a billion or more" for him to dedicate time to it.
- He says he presently has two paths he considers billion-dollar opportunities: one with
  more upside but more risk, the other more certain but lower upside — both "relatively
  irreversible."
- Sources of irreversibility he names: betting a large percentage of net worth/capital;
  reputational damage ("you only get one reputation," can't bankrupt your way out); and
  time (opportunity cost — every moment traded has the cost of a higher-return use).
- Distinguishes a *risk* (only downside) from a *trade-off* (e.g. backing a cause that
  alienates one group but attracts another) — the latter is acceptable, the former is not.
- Attributes to Sean/Sharran Srivatsaa the maxim: "Never bet the empire for a pot of
  gold. You bet what you have and need for something you don't have and don't need — it's
  never a good bet." (attribution: to friend, quoted approvingly by Alex)
- Cites a Tony Robbins story ("rich people make bets where they can't lose either way,"
  illustrated by a billionaire stockpiling nickels whose metal content exceeds face
  value) to argue you want high-return, low-risk bets. (attribution: story credited to
  Tony Robbins, retold by Alex)
- Says his single most-viewed YouTube presentation is on the idea that "the only base
  thing we have is time and we make trades for stuff" — better traders get more stuff.
- "No half measures" framework: it's either sell or don't sell — give value OR sell,
  never mix them. Illustrated by his own worst-ever presentation (a personal-trainer
  conference during the Gym Launch era) where he soft-tossed a 90-minute pitch in ~15
  minutes after 3+ hours of value and "made two sales."
- On full commitment: to go all in, eliminate the possibility of going back; being
  "half in, half out" is "just not in at all" and only feels like progress. Ties this to
  "going pro / burning the boats" books.
- Success metric he offers on stage: "You measure your success by whether you completed
  the actions that would make it unreasonable that you will not fail. And then you
  succeed by default."
- Self-positioning as a speaker: he refuses to teach real estate agents how to sell
  houses (they've sold more than he has); instead he frames it as "if I were to start,
  here's how I would win," so the lesson transfers.
- Sales micro-skills: get people into a good mood first, then ask; make them *want* to do
  the thing rather than feel they're doing you a favor; tone, laugh, and jokes are
  deliberate leverage tools.
- Names-as-leverage: cites *How to Win Friends and Influence People* — a person's own
  name is their favorite word; remembering someone's name when they've forgotten yours
  gives you leverage.
- Presentation-prep process: outlines a talk far in advance, then changes ~half of it the
  day before; adrenaline is "directly proportional to the prep." This keynote (300+
  slides, finished ~20 minutes before) was the first time he'd delivered that material.
- Closing advice: first decision / first business → take the lower-risk, higher-
  probability path (the "tens of thousands" who make the massive bet and lose 5–8 years
  go unmentioned in success stories); only take the big irreversible swing once you have
  enough wins banked, and then commit fully.

  environment... My favorite place to think is actually on planes... I always make really

- "Right now, 10x is kind of my litmus test. It's like we're at 100. It's got to be a

  they really aren't."

  no bet, you're guaranteed to be wrong."

  here's how I would win."

  then ask for the nice thing."

  unreasonable that you will not fail. And then you succeed by default."

  take a bigger swing. I'll either win or I'll learn."

  tens of thousands of entrepreneurs who make that massive bet and then lose everything
