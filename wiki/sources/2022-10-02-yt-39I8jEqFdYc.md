# I Was Wrong About Mentorship...

## Summary

A solo, direct-to-camera essay in which Alex Hormozi recants an earlier attitude toward
his mentors. Early in his career he surpassed several mentors financially and took that
to mean he was "better than them" and that they hadn't known much. With more years and
"more scars," he now frames mentorship generationally: you only surpass a mentor because
they gave you a head start, and the whole point of teaching is that the student passes
the teacher. He closes by urging viewers to text an old teacher or mentor a note of
respect.

## Key claims

- **BEFORE (early career):** When he passed his mentors financially, he thought that made
  him better than them; he looked at earlier teachers as if they "didn't know anything"
  ("this guy didn't know anything, this guy is the guy"). He treated it competitively —
  as vanquishing people rather than being helped.
- **AFTER (now, ~2022):** He realized he was *only* able to get better than them because
  they "gave [him] the head start" — the shoulder to stand on to reach the next branch.
  Surpassing a mentor is evidence the mentor did their job, not that they were an idiot.
- **Reframe via the arithmetic analogy:** Learning basics from a first teacher and
  advanced material from a later teacher is a *sequence*; you don't resent your arithmetic
  teacher once you reach algebra. Early teachers get you "ready to receive the next thing."
- **New definition of a good teacher:** "The students surpass the teacher." If your
  mentees/students don't move above you, it means you're a *bad* teacher, not a good one.
- **Generational/legacy framing:** Two things to accept — you are going to die, and no one
  will remember in 10,000 years; and at the end of life your "pallbearers" are the people
  you helped, not the people you vanquished. Each generation clears the path and then dies
  on the battlefield so the next generation can move through it faster.
- **Behavioral prescription:** If both sides see it this way, mentees stop dumping on the
  mentors who helped them, and mentors stop holding back secrets. Call to action: text an
  old mentor and say "respect, I appreciate you" — it means a lot to them and helps you
  "spit out the poison" you invented in your head.
- **Personal data points (self-reported):** He was "stuck at mid-$30 million for three
  years." He cites gratitude to GaryVee (12 years older) for forging a blueprint he could
  execute on. He has portfolio founders in their 20s "making twice what [he] was making
  at 30," and expects many mentees will pass him — "I don't think Gary minds."
