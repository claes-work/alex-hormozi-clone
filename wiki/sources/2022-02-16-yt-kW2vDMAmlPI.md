# $100M CEO: How I know who to trust...

## Summary
In this solo video Alex Hormozi argues that the "heart side" of business — culture, values, and how people behave — is undervalued precisely because it is not easily quantified, yet it is the more important side of building a company. He frames values as the mechanism for scaling decision-making: they are non-negotiables you hire, fire, and set culture by, and they must be stringent enough to repel people rather than serve as feel-good platitudes. He shares the three core values of acquisition.com — unimpeachable character, sincere candor, and competitive greatness — and explains the reasoning behind each, including the trust heuristics he uses to read someone's character. He teaches that three values is the sweet spot the human brain can use as decision lenses, and that candidates or entrepreneurs must meet all three, not two of three. Underlying it all is his view that success in business is often about making fewer dumb mistakes and avoiding "cancers" on the team rather than making brilliant moves.

## Key claims
- The "heart side" of business (culture, belief, values) is less quantifiable than the "brain side" but is actually the more important side; both he and Leila hold this view.
- When people believe in what they're doing, you can get 2x, 4x, or 8x more output from the same people — not just 20% more (analogy: a new coach in *Remember the Titans* who changes execution, not the rules or strategy).
- Values must be true and innate to you and the team — not aspirational. They are the basis for hiring, firing, and culture.
- A real value must "draw a line in the sand" and repel people; if your values do not repel anyone, they are platitudes, not values.
- Values should be phrased in words you would actually say (e.g. gym launch: "speed is king," "do the boring work" instead of "work ethic" or "fast turnaround times").
- Three values is the sweet spot — the number the human brain can hold as decision lenses; ten values makes decisions and candidate evaluation impossible. Their value set has distilled down over time.
- The three acquisition.com values (applied both internally to employees and externally to portfolio-company entrepreneurs, and to themselves): unimpeachable character, sincere candor, competitive greatness.
- Trust/judgment heuristics for "unimpeachable character": Can I trust this person with my life? Can I trust this person with my wife (leave her a month, expect no flirts, moves, or stray looks)? Can I trust this person with my money (leave a large sum, come back a month/year/decade later, expect it untouched)? — Alex attributes the wife/money framing to a story relayed via a friend about someone on SEAL Team Six; he adopts it as his own filter.
- An egotistical high performer is the hardest and most dangerous case — such people become "cancers" on the team, damaging culture and overall company performance.
- In interviews he watches not the answer itself but *how* people answer, to get insight into character ("what hot water could I put this teabag in and see what comes out").
- "Sincere candor" = the self-awareness to give useful feedback AND the humility to receive it without taking it personally; you cannot make progress without feedback, and running a company is fundamentally about clarity of communication.
- "Competitive greatness" is borrowed from John Wooden (both he and Leila are fans): "Are you at your best when your best is needed? The enjoyment of a hard challenge."
- Candidates must be three-out-of-three on non-negotiables; two-of-three is a rejection because "things will get hard" in business is a virtual guarantee, and weak character reverts under pressure.
- "If you would not do business with someone for a decade, do not do business with them for a day" — apply business core values to all aspects of life.
- Success in business is often just making fewer mistakes — avoiding dumb decisions and cancerous people — more than making very smart moves; activity decreased as they scaled but execution skill and deliberateness improved.
- If most people don't fit your non-negotiables, that's good — most people shouldn't be in your company. Removing misfits is "the pruning of the tree that will ultimately grow the tree."
