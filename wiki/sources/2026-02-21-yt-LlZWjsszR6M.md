# Competition Is Good for You

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi (@MoreMozi), short clip. He addresses founders who feel "plagued" by competition and upset about being copied — admitting he was once one of them — and offers a reframe to get over it. Two arguments: (1) the alternative to being copied is that *no one* copies you because no one cares what you're doing, so copying is actually a requisite/byproduct of success and just a normal part of business; (2) by definition, whoever copies you is second — if you want to lead, you can't look at anyone else, you have to keep deriving the next unseen step ("step six" from your first five) rather than parroting. The moment you start copying, you've "admitted defeat" and handed over the baton, accepting second/third place — which matters especially in a winner-take-all, attention-driven world where the first place captures all the fruits. Closes with a promo for his free 10-stage zero-to-$100M+ roadmap (acquisition.com/roadmap).

## Key claims (paraphrased unless quoted; all dated 2026-02-21)

- **Being copied is a symptom of success, not a threat.** The alternative — nobody copying you — means nobody cares what you're doing. So as long as you're successful, being copied is just part of business. (He notes he personally struggled with this too — self-reported.)
- **Whoever copies you is, by definition, second.** Copying is inherently a following position.
- **To lead, don't look at competitors — innovate ahead of them.** Consistently derive the next unseen step (the "step six" that follows from your own first five steps) instead of parroting whatever comes next.
- **Copying = admitting defeat.** The moment you catch yourself copying, you've conceded you're no longer the leader; you're following in someone else's footsteps and handing them the baton — accepting second, third, or fifth place.
- **Attention is winner-take-all.** In a winner-take-all world (attention specifically), all the fruits go to first place — which raises the stakes on leading rather than following.
- **Promo:** free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ that "less than 1% of companies finish," broken down across eight business functions (what each constraint feels like, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate), drawn from doing it multiple times across software, physical products, services, and brick-and-mortar. Link: acquisition.com/roadmap.
