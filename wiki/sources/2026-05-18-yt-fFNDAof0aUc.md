# The Hardest Business Model to Get Right

## Summary

Alex Hormozi advises Sebastian, who has a free relocation-listings tool in the
Netherlands but almost no paid conversion. Alex frames freemium as one of the toughest
business models: if it works, acquisition is effectively free, but the effort must move
to ascension. He tells Sebastian to stop tinkering with the software, get on the phone
with users, and discover whether the next monetizable problem is paid help, automation,
or selling warm leads to related service providers.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Freemium is difficult because the free tier must be useful enough to attract users but
  constrained enough to make paid ascension attractive.
- When freemium works, free-user acquisition should cost essentially zero; capital and
  effort should go toward ascension.
- A free tool with 300 users is still low-volume for freemium, but it is enough to start
  learning through conversations.
- Alex says Sebastian should manually get on calls with users because those calls will
  both make money and reveal what the users actually want.
- A founder should channel existing demand rather than try to create demand from scratch.
- Early traction from students should not necessarily dictate the whole product roadmap,
  but the business still has to make money.
- Serving a cheaper demographic first can force automation, which may later create a
  low-cost, high-margin position.
- Solving one customer problem creates the next customer problem; after apartment search,
  the next monetizable problem may be moving services or warm handoffs.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Freemium is one of the toughest models out there."

> "We want to channel demand. We do not want to create demand."

> "Whenever you solve a problem for a customer, if you do it well, it always creates another problem."
