# How to End a Partnership the Right Way

## Summary
In this short coaching exchange, a caller running a 50/50 heat pump installation company in Germany tells Alex Hormozi he no longer wants to work long-term with his business partner but feels stuck: he can't simply quit because the business would collapse and customers would lose money, he lacks the capital to start over, and he doesn't want to screw his partner over. The friction, he explains, is that his partner has a big ego and communicates in an unnecessarily complicated way. Alex walks him through a script and a plan for exiting cleanly rather than either staying trapped or blowing the business up.

Alex's advice is to open the conversation directly — ask the partner whether he feels the partnership is working — and to have a wind-down plan ready before that conversation happens: current run rate, profit, and costs, with a proposal to wind the business down over roughly 12 months. He notes that shutting a business down deliberately can throw off a surprising amount of cash, since growth investment drops to zero and costs get cut hard, so revenue increasingly converts to profit during the wind-down period; the plan is to run it until customers/staff attrit away and then take the resulting cash into the next venture. He's candid that shutting down is emotionally just as hard as selling a business, minus the payday, but frames the upside as gained caution about future partnerships and the skills to run the business solo going forward.

The exchange closes on a personal, mindset note: asked whether he felt confident he could rebuild after losing everything multiple times, Alex says he didn't really think about it in those terms — there was no alternative but to go figure it out, so he treated himself as the asset that would carry him through. The clip ends with a scripted ad for Acquisition.com's free "$100 million scaling road map" tool.

## Key claims

- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The caller is a 50/50 partner in a heat pump installation company in Germany; he wants to exit the partnership but can't simply quit because the business would go down, customers would lose money, he lacks capital to start fresh, and he doesn't want to screw his partner over. His stated reason for wanting out: the partner has a big ego and communicates in an overly complicated way.
- (2026-07-20) When a partnership isn't working, open the conversation directly by asking the partner whether he feels it's working; if the partner claims it's great, say plainly that you don't think it is — if the partner agrees it isn't working, pivot immediately to jointly planning an exit rather than staying in limbo.
- (2026-07-20) Come to that conversation with a wind-down plan already prepared: current run rate, profit, and costs, with a proposal to wind the business down over about 12 months.
- (2026-07-20) Deliberately shutting a business down can spin off a lot of cash: growth investment drops to zero and costs get cut aggressively, so revenue increasingly converts to profit during the wind-down; the approach is to run the business until people (customers/staff) leave on their own and it slowly winds down, then take the accumulated cash into the next move.
- (2026-07-20) Shutting a business down is just as emotionally hard as selling it, but without the payday.
- (2026-07-20) The upside of having gone through a bad partnership and wind-down: more hesitancy about entering future business partnerships, and having learned to run the business's functions solo — which itself will still produce a return going forward.
- (2026-07-20) Having lost everything multiple times, thinking of himself as the asset (rather than the business or its assets) is what got him through those periods.
- (2026-07-20) Asked whether he was always confident he could rebuild after losing everything, Alex says he didn't really think about it as a confidence question — there was no framed alternative ("what else am I going to do?"), so the response was simply to go figure it out.
