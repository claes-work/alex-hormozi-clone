# Helping a Real Estate Consulting Firm Get to $100M

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a real-estate buyers'-advisory firm ($15M last year, tracking $22M, flat $22K fee per purchase) who wants to reach ~$54M revenue / $125M valuation but feels blocked by two things: CAC reduction hampered by muddy multi-touch attribution, and duplicating his top two performers across divisions. Alex reframes both problems: rather than chase forensic attribution, just double everything on the acquisition side (it will work; data is for later efficiency), and use qualitative "where did you first hear about me?" attribution to power-rank reinvestment. For people duplication, he says study the top two reps, break what they do into teachable behaviors, and make it the team standard because everything is teachable.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- When you feel blocked on acquisition, ask what would actually stop you from **doubling everything you already do** — usually nothing physical stops you, so do it; Alex will bet that doubling all current acquisition activity works.
- **Attribution data is for efficiency, not for permission to grow** — it "comes into play if you want to just get more efficient," not before you scale spend.
- One layer below forensic (exact) attribution is **directional/qualitative attribution**: ask customers "where did you hear about me first?" — it's closer to the sale and gives usable direction on where people enter.
- The deeper you go into attribution the more muddied it gets, because a buyer may opt in a year ago, then see a short, then a long, then buy — Alex argues the more **recent (last-30-day)** touch converted them, not the year-old first touch.
- **Scientific attribution** blends first- and last-touch: if the touch is **within 30 days** treat it as **last touch**; if **more than 30 days**, treat it as **first touch**. Pure last-touch skews everything toward direct response.
- (Quoting George Mack) "If you split test enough, everything just becomes porn" — a caution that relentless direct-response optimization degrades the brand.
- Model reinvestment on first-touch data: **power-rank channels and put disproportionate resources into the single greatest point of leverage** for bringing people in (in his live hand-raise demo, YouTube), while still maintaining the other core brand touches because they perform a **nudging/conversion function** — especially for higher-touch, complex, higher-price services where buyers take longer to convert.
- Owners can **mimic the hand-raise exercise with their own existing customers** to find where the majority of first touches come from, then reinvest there for a disproportionate return.
- For duplicating top performers: **study your top two people, be a better student of exactly what they do, and break it into behaviors** so everyone can copy it, then make that the team standard and watch if the rest of the team rises.
- **Everything is teachable — there is no magic**; it's only a question of how teachable, and **most sales skills are exceptionally teachable within a couple of weeks**.
- If reps won't adhere to the new standard, the gap may be other skills like **adherence and work ethic** — those are real skills, "but probably not worth training."
