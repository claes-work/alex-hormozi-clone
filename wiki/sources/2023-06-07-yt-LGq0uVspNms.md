# Alex Hormozi's Advice on Branding in 2023

## Summary

A keynote by Alex Hormozi to a room of brick-and-mortar gym owners. Asked to speak
about social-media branding, he deliberately spends only ~5 minutes on brand — arguing
it won't make this audience more money — and hijacks the rest to teach strategy,
constraint-finding, and building sellable assets. His core branding claim: a brand is
not a thing but a set of deliberately cultivated *associations* between intangible ideas
and an otherwise-unknown company (his "bouquet of flowers" analogy). He then reframes
strategy as prioritization, walks through theory-of-constraints, the more/better/new
sequence, impact-vs-ease ordering, the "make them worth more" LTV lens, and closes with
the "only four ways to get customers" model (warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads,
content) plus the Rule of 100. Recurring theme: fix the "sandwich" (the product) before
spending on marketing.

## Key claims

- **Brand is an association, not a thing.** "A brand isn't actually a thing — it's an
  association we make between things that we know and things that people don't know,
  which is usually our company." Branding = deliberately associating an unknown company
  with intangible ideas the ideal audience already likes.
- **Reputation, not social media, is the lever for local businesses.** For a local
  brand, reputation is built on the quality of product/service and on consistently
  meeting expectations — "make promises, keep promises." Most businesses fail to even
  *meet* expectations because they oversell.
- **One "off" flower ruins the bouquet.** A single wilted flower (bad experience /
  employee) massively shifts perception of the whole brand.
- **Content branding at scale is brutal repetition.** His team was putting out ~250
  pieces of content a week, targeting ~1,000/week within six months — "an absolutely
  brutal repetitive violent process to get as many people as possible to remember one or
  two things about you."
- **Strategy = prioritization.** "Strategy is just prioritizing." Fancy version: "how you
  choose to allocate resources that are limited against unlimited options." The most
  costly things are the *unknown unknowns* — the best moves are often "off the board."
- **Emotion-to-diagnosis mapping.** Anxiety = too many options, don't know which to pick
  (lack of strategy). Sadness/hopelessness = don't know what to do at all (ignorance /
  lack of options → solvable by getting informed).
- **Volume × Leverage = Output.** You can be lazy and rich if you have enough leverage;
  otherwise you cap out on available hours, so you need other people's hours.
- **Theory of constraints.** Every business has one thing limiting growth. "If your
  business is not growing as fast as you want it to, you are working on the wrong stuff."
  Diagnostic question: "Why can't we 10x this business?"
- **Three strategic objectives.** Every activity must map to: (1) get more customers,
  (2) increase LTV / make them worth more, or (3) decrease risk that 1 & 2 stop
  (increase enterprise value). If it maps to none, cut it.
- **You get wealthy from what you own, not what you do.** Two owners with identical
  $5M top / $2M bottom lines: the one whose business runs without him is far richer
  because the asset is sellable (10–20x). Going from $2M to $3M income can add ~$10M
  to net worth.
- **A-players are the difference between small and big businesses.** Big owners keep a
  lower tolerance for mediocrity and keep hiring until they have several stars. The
  biggest growth lever is "on the other side of a few hard conversations." Use a 14-day
  performance-improvement plan; say to someone's face what you say behind their back.
- **More → Better → New.** Exhaust doing more of what works and doing it better before
  chasing anything new. Small owners jump straight to "new" chasing a silver bullet.
  Growth is "golden BBs" — many small improvements — not one thing.
- **Impact/Ease ordering.** Number tasks by high-impact + easy first; do those before
  consuming more information or starting new things.
- **Fix the product ("sandwich") first.** Truly exceptional products compound via
  referrals (exponential: two bring four). Example: *$100M Offers* sold ~25,000 copies
  the prior month with no marketing spend. Don't spend to market a mediocre product —
  "you're just going to let everyone know you're mediocre."
- **LTV / lifetime gross profit over everything.** "The guys who can spend the most money
  on marketing are the ones who make the most money from ads… if you print cash on the
  back end you can get silly on the front end."
- **Intelligence = rate of learning; learning = same condition, new behavior.** If you
  attend an event and behave identically afterward, you've learned nothing.
- **Only four ways to get customers:** warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, content.
  Quantify with the **Rule of 100**: every day either spend $100 on ads, do 100 cold
  outreaches, 100 warm outreaches, or 100 minutes of content.
- **Leverage via "other people" (OPM/other people's work):** customers (referrals),
  affiliates, employees, agencies — each does the four activities on your behalf, but
  acquiring them still starts with you doing the four activities.
- **Scaling brick-and-mortar:** watch cost-to-open vs. profit-per-facility (cash-on-cash
  return). To scale from 5 to 50 locations, massively *simplify* the model (minimize
  square footage and operational drag), then build "the machine that builds the
  locations." Never depend on a single acquisition channel — all ad platforms
  eventually die; fix the back end so you'd survive months with no new customers.
