# She Turned 5800 Followers Into $1 Million Per Year

## Summary

Alex recounts a dinner with a registered dietitian who nets just under $1M/year selling
insurance-billing help to other dietitians off an Instagram account with only ~5,800
followers, ~2,800 monthly reach, and posts averaging ~19 likes. He uses her story to argue
that vanity metrics (followers, views, likes) do not equal business metrics (sales, opt-ins,
revenue): a tiny, hyper-niche, die-hard audience can out-earn a huge general one. He ties it
to his own two experiences of losing organic sales when he broadened his content away from
its niche, and reaffirms his commitment to "only business" content.

## Key claims (Alex's small-audience monetization lessons)

- A hyper-niche business (dietitian teaching other dietitians to bill insurance correctly)
  nets ~$1M/year from ~5,800 followers, ~2,800 monthly reach, ~19 likes per post — proof
  that a tiny audience can produce huge income (profit, not revenue).
- The subject does ~10+ low-ticket sales/day plus ~3 higher-ticket sales/week (~$4–5K price
  point); low-ticket product is billing-code cheat sheets. 100% of acquisition is organic
  Instagram.
- "The followers will make you famous; understanding business will make you rich." Chasing
  followers as a path to wealth is the wrong mental model.
- Business metrics do not follow vanity metrics — more views/subs don't yield more revenue,
  opt-ins, or sales. Subscribers don't mean sales.
- Niche content makes more money if the niche is what you sell. Broad content brings views
  and vanity metrics but fewer buyers.
- Alex says he learned this twice the hard way: (1) when he shifted his podcast from gym
  content to general business ~18 months before selling Gym Launch, organic sales dropped to
  ~zero and took ~6 months to diagnose; (2) 3–6 months before this video, "wider content"
  experiments raised views but lowered YouTube CPMs, acquisition.com opt-ins, and book sales.
- Relevancy beats reach: a niche podcast (e.g., dentists-only) gets fewer listens but far
  higher per-listener relevancy and buying intent.
- The "empty room" isn't bad — low views mean a more specific audience for a more specific
  problem, which is how you make big money. He cites an insurance brokerage he invested in
  whose founder had ~5,000 YouTube followers, videos getting 100–500 views, doing many
  millions/year in a highly specialized niche.
- Modern algorithms reliably serve niche content to interested people (he only sees
  philosophy, comedy, and gym-equipment content, including tiny 5K-follower accounts); the
  Instagram head has said the platform now favors smaller creators.
- Endless content is easy in a niche: the subject simply answers her audience's questions,
  posts one post + one story daily, and adds a "link in bio" CTA — that was the whole strategy.
- "1,000 true fans" is enough: at even $10/month you can make $100,000/year; die-hard fans
  who like you a lot buy, versus many people who like you a little.
- People who like you a lot buy from you; make content that helps one specific person deeply
  rather than content aimed at performing.
- You can be rich or famous (or both), but getting rich is often faster; separate the two goals.
