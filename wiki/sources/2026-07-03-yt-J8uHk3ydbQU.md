# You're Growing Too Fast Because Cash Flow Can't Keep Up

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises James, whose financial-planning business serves people 55+ and wants to scale from $12M to $100M in four years. The business is constrained by cash flow because it hires advisors ahead of revenue. Alex frames the issue as a risk-tolerance and cash-conversion problem: either pull cash forward with an upfront plan fee, raise equity, or grow at the current bootstrap-limited rate. He warns that the biggest risk of growing too fast is reputational damage from service quality dropping.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **James** - the coachee. His AUM model, podcasts, client count, team size, advisor ramp, and growth goal are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- This kind of growth constraint is spreadsheetable: the business can model advisor hiring, onboarding, client load, cash burn, and payback.
- To grow faster than current cash flow allows, the business has two main options: charge an upfront plan fee credited against future service, or raise equity.
- If neither option is used, growth will be pinned to the rate at which new customers and assets can be onboarded and activated from internally generated cash.
- Margin targets are not universal; they depend on the founder's risk tolerance.
- For bootstrapped companies, the practical lower bound is zero cash, but the safety padding depends on projection confidence and how much owners want to distribute.
- Alex says he is relatively risk-averse and uses Keith Cunningham's framework: upside, downside, and whether he can live with the downside.
- Every point of margin sacrificed can be recaptured in growth, but it also increases risk.
- The biggest risk of overgrowth is not only fiscal; it is reputational. If service quality drops, organic lead flow can dry up while the business is stuck with underutilized staff.
