# How I Make Content Without Getting Burned Out

## Summary

Roundtable/Q&A clip in which Alex Hormozi is asked how he handles information overload
and creative burnout given the endless platforms (X, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube) and
daily AI announcements. He reframes the problem: he isn't burned out on *making* content,
and he's "not a huge consumer" to begin with. His system is a low-friction idea-capture →
batched-production pipeline. He does roughly one content day a week (realistically closer
to once a month around travel), captures interesting thoughts by texting his creative
director the moment they occur so they don't "flutter away," then dedicates a "hero
session" recording day where he gives himself complete permission to accomplish nothing
else. He works from bullets, not scripts (teleprompter with bullets, never fully scripted
copy), and his team supplies him a few headline and intro options to pick from. He also
touches on capacity as a recurring content theme and how ads are more formulaic (hook +
three points). Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap CTA.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed, 2026-02-23, paraphrased)

- He is **not burned out on making content** — and he is "not a huge consumer" of
  information/media, which he implies insulates him from the overwhelm others describe.
- His cadence: **one content day per week**, and realistically closer to **once a month**
  because he travels; whenever he has a free Saturday he films.
- Per session he picks **two interesting things he actually wanted to think/talk about
  that week** and those become his YouTube videos (his examples that week: avoiding
  "people who have a problem for every solution," and the idea of building excess
  capacity).
- **Idea capture must be the lowest-friction possible**: the instant he has an
  interesting thought he texts his creative director so the idea "doesn't just flutter
  away" — then a dedicated block later to "beef it out" and make it full.
- **Batched "hero session" production**: he records everything at once because he gets
  into a flow, and he gives himself **complete permission to accomplish nothing else that
  day** besides recording.
- Batching is a personal-fit choice, not a universal rule — he contrasts himself with
  Leila, who hates sitting down for ~10 hours of recording and would rather spread it out
  (~2 hours twice a week). (Leila = context, not persona.)
- He **paid vendors to edit even before he had a team** — the batching approach predates
  having in-house production help.
- He **works from bullets, not scripts.** His team hands him ~three headline options and
  two-to-three intro options; he picks one of each, and the rest is just bullets.
- ★ "If I had to script things, I would have quit a long time ago" — full scripting /
  teleprompter reading of prose is a dealbreaker for him; he uses a teleprompter only for
  bullets.
- **Ads are more scripted/formulaic** than his organic content: a short ad (e.g. 15
  seconds) is essentially **hook + three points**, so there's little room and it's easier
  to lean on the teleprompter for those.
- Recurring content theme he name-checks: **you expand at the rate of your free
  capacity** — you can't seize opportunities with no capacity left over, so deliberately
  build excess capacity into your work life.
