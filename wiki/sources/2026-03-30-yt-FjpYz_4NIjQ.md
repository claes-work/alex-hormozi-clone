# How to Build a Marketing System From Scratch When You've Never Done It

## Summary

A garage-door business owner (a former auto-body-shop operator, now doing ~$7M revenue with $1.5M cash flow and $4M acquisition debt) explains he has almost no marketing — one lead per month — and relies heavily on home-warranty companies for leads, a single-channel-risk he wants to diversify. He asks Alex how to organize building a marketing function from the ground up: hire a manager, use agencies, or build in-house. Alex advises starting new channels with vendors/agencies for speed (especially local PPC and SEO), building Meta/social ads in-house because local creative burns out fast, and eventually hiring a young local-ads person; he points the owner to the agency chapter of his Leads book as his standard playbook.

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, Alex frames every business as a three-legged stool: customer acquisition, delivery/fulfillment, and operations ("someone to make sure that no one goes to jail" — HR, finance, legal). All three must keep running.
- As of March 2026, Alex's default approach to cracking any new channel is to start with vendors/agencies rather than building in-house first.
- Alex recommends going vendor-first for local PPC, saying good agencies run a repeatable playbook (same links, same build) and are usually well-priced for local.
- Alex says SEO requires more skill than PPC in his opinion, but local SEO is easy to win compared to national — so he'd still start with an agency.
- Alex advises building Meta/social ads in-house because local creative "gets tired" in roughly 21 days (two to three weeks), requiring constant new creative — a burden agencies serving small companies handle poorly.
- Alex suggests hiring a younger, tech-savvy person who understands local social ads, offering an earn-in mini-comp; such a hire could become "a weapon" for acquisition.
- Alex encourages continuing to expand the existing warranty-lead sources (from eight toward ~20) since those leads are already working.
- Alex's stated agency playbook: buy the agency's services, tell them he'll take over in six months, have them train his in-house person, then no longer need them.
- Unverified caller claims: owns four auto body shops, pivoted to garage doors after a hail storm; ~$7M revenue, $1.5M cash flow, $4M debt from purchasing the garage-door business; currently ~one marketing lead per month; is a top vendor for a warranty company "along with Home Depot."
- Alex references that both his books can be read quickly — one about three hours, the other about four and a half.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Every business needs all three. Someone to get customers, someone to deliver customers, and someone to make sure that no one goes to jail."

> "Creative gets tired in local marketing like two to three weeks. So you have to make creative every 21 days."

> "Whenever I do anything in a new thing, I start with vendors."

> "It just gives you my playbook for how I crack every new channel. It's really what I do. I buy the agency and then I tell them I'm going to take over in six months. You help me train the person. And then eventually I won't need you."

> "PPC will be harder to crack initially but then once you have it it's very stable."

> "There's more skill in my opinion for SEO than there is for PPC. But the nice thing is that local is easy to win compared to like national."
