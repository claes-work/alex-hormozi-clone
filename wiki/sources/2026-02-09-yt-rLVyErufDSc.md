# I'm Stuck at $3M/Year With My Coaching Business

★ L3-candidate — substantive canonical live-coaching teaching on scaling a coaching/mastermind business (market vs. metrics vs. advertising diagnostic; lead-magnet + time-horizon fix).

## Summary
Live coaching Q&A in which Alex Hormozi diagnoses a Brazilian coaching-business owner (masterminds/group coaching for professionals) who is stuck around $3M/year (peaked at $4M two years ago when "everyone went online") and is tempted to expand internationally into English/USD to escape the weakening real. Alex systematically rules out the member's own hypotheses — currency and "market complexity" — and shows the member has not remotely saturated Brazil at ~20M reais. He walks a three-part diagnostic (market → metrics → advertising), crossing out market, exposing that the member's problems trace to poor attribution ("everything is working a bit" = nothing tracked), and lands on advertising as the real constraint. His central prescription: the member is only advertising to bottom-of-funnel, product-aware buyers, running effective retargeting that feels like cold traffic; he must "graduate" to a higher level of marketing by building a superior lead magnet matched to the avatar as a top-of-funnel asset, and by extending the time horizon — "accordioning" out the 5-day launch content across all channels (paid + organic) during the between-launch periods, with every lead magnet carrying a ticket to the next launch. He also teaches that the intuitive marketing move is usually wrong (kill the "expensive" lead, keep the "cheap" one) using an 11x-ROAS vs 4x-ROAS lead-cost example, and confirms the international expansion is a distraction.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-09. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-09) The member sells mastermind and group-coaching programs for professionals who want to run their own masterminds — doctors teaching doctors, lawyers teaching lawyers, restaurant owners teaching restaurant owners. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) The member is at ~$3M/year now, peaked at ~$4M two years ago "because everyone went online," and wants to reach $5M; unit sales are declining, not growing. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) The member's total is ~20M reais/year, making him a top-5-to-10 player in his space in Brazil. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) Program structure: 100 people in the mastermind at $20K and 200 people in the mentorship at $10K (USD), plus a lot of lower-ticket product feeding into them; ~600K Instagram followers and ~200K YouTube followers; content is about packaging knowledge into digital products, courses, and mentorships. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) The member's growth theory is that the weakening real (from ~1 to ~5+ reais per dollar over time) makes it easier to add "an extra million in dollars" by expanding into English/USD than to keep scaling complexity in Brazil. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) Alex rejects that framing: "I'm not sure I buy it." A 20-25% revenue lift from an already-proven acquisition channel is not evidence of a capped market — the member is nowhere near saturation in Brazil at $4M/year.
- (2026-02-09) One of the biggest downsides of being an international entrepreneur chasing the US is forgetting you're also competing against Americans; you want the pros of the US market without the cons.
- (2026-02-09) "If you take everything we do and go to anywhere else in the world, you're five years ahead" — US-market tactics are more advanced, so the tactics the member teaches likely work better in Brazil than they would in America.
- (2026-02-09) Diagnostic frame — separate the problem into market, metrics, and advertising. "We crossed out market. We're going to ignore market." Then move to metrics.
- (2026-02-09) The member admits his metrics/attribution are "a bit messed up" and "not as clear as I wish." [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) Trying many things at once is a symptom of not knowing what works: "you're trying a lot of stuff because you don't know what's working and you don't know if it worked."
- (2026-02-09) "Everything is working a bit" is just poor attribution — in reality most of it isn't working at all and one thing is working a lot; the job is to figure out which one and put all resources toward that thing.
- (2026-02-09) Attribution will never be perfect, but affordable software gets you directional data good enough to inform the decision — get the tracking.
- (2026-02-09) Doing all the ideas (4 launches → 5 launches → adding evergreen) simultaneously is where the member is stuck; each idea is individually fine.
- (2026-02-09) Evergreen cannibalizes launches — the people who would have bought in a launch get pulled into the evergreen flow instead.
- (2026-02-09) The member's core numbers are healthy: price points ($10K/$20K) are fine, the sales process (DM funnel → application → audio message → close by team if needed) works, and there are zero supply constraints — he can handle more customers. So Alex refuses to touch the offer or sales process.
- (2026-02-09) The member's DM funnel: boost reels, DM new followers (especially verified accounts), send to an application page that pitches $20K (7-figure+ applicants) or $10K (sub-7-figure), then a personalized audio message to close or route to the team. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) Diagnosis: it's an advertising issue. When the member increases ad spend, ROAS drops to unprofitable — Alex's theory is he is predominantly advertising to his existing audience, so it's effectively a retargeting campaign that feels like cold traffic but isn't.
- (2026-02-09) The launches do convert cold traffic (last one: 50K reais spent → 500K reais, a 10x), because a launch takes someone from no awareness to a purchasing decision in ~5 days — but outside of launch windows the member has no strategy to capture those people. [10x figure guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-09) The lead magnet you put out dramatically changes lead quality: 20% of people who go through Acquisition.com's scaling road map are marketing-qualified leads, versus 5% of people who buy a book — and a book purchase is in some ways harder to get than a lead-magnet opt-in.
- (2026-02-09) Counterintuitive-cost lesson (Acquisition.com launch data): lead-gen-optimized campaigns averaged $5 leads worth $20 each (~4:1); ROAS-optimized campaigns averaged $17 leads worth $189 each (~11:1 on the paid side). Most owners would shut off the $17-lead campaign and keep the $5 one — which is exactly wrong.
- (2026-02-09) "Most business problems are counterintuitive because the intuitive answer is usually wrong because you've already tried it."
- (2026-02-09) Prescription 1 — build a superior lead magnet made specifically for the avatar you're talking to, and run that as your top of funnel; this is "graduating you to another level of marketing."
- (2026-02-09) The member is currently only advertising to the small bottom-of-funnel dot: people who are product-aware and ready to buy, just shifting them over — with no products or advertising sitting higher up the funnel besides organic content.
- (2026-02-09) Prescription 2 — extend the time horizon. Buyers already take their second or third launch to convert, so build assets and advertising for that longer journey.
- (2026-02-09) Prescription 3 (the "accordion") — a 5-day launch contains enough information for someone to make a purchasing decision; take that content and stretch/distribute it across the between-launch time, in as many formats as possible, across all channels, paid and organic.
- (2026-02-09) Make the between-launch lead magnets match that content, and have every lead magnet include a ticket to the next launch.
- (2026-02-09) "More better content" applies to Alex too — he grows by making more and better content, writing another book, building a scaling road map; the member should make content better and go cross-platform.
- (2026-02-09) The international/English expansion is a distraction — Alex confirms this directly when the member asks.
