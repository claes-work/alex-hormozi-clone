# NO NEW FRIENDS (My "Extreme Views" on Friendship)

## Summary

A solo talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out his openly transactional philosophy of
friendship and relationships. He frames friendship as a spectrum (not binary) measured
by whether a person roots for you and helps you toward your long-term goal, argues that
relationships have "seasons" that can legitimately end, and reframes cutting people off
as "pruning a tree" rather than "burning a bridge." He ties the extremity of his views
to having many demands on his time and to prioritizing his goal above most
relationships.

## Key claims

- Friendship is **not binary** ("are we friends or not") but a spectrum of *how good*
  friends you are; the opposite of a friend is an enemy/rival who doesn't root for you.
- The **one-strike disqualifier**: a friend only has to do one thing to be out — not
  root for you, i.e. resent (explicitly or implicitly) when you win.
- People who resent your winning stay around only because your success *might* benefit
  them, but will still try to bring you down "in case they can."
- Real friends **root for you in front of you and, most importantly, behind your back** —
  hardest among status-competitive male groups, where your rising status makes others
  look worse by comparison, giving them incentive to diminish you.
- He rejects "good people / bad people" framing; the only question is whether someone
  helps or hurts his long-term goal. Relationships "pay rent" for his time and attention.
- He deliberately made **his goal his most important thing**, above most relationships,
  believing achieving it would improve his identity and unlock a new tier of friends.
- These views intensify when many people compete for your time; he concedes the advice
  may be irrelevant to people who don't yet have that demand on their day. Cites LeBron's
  "No New Friends" — rare life, rare rules.
- He is **transactional**: the pros of a relationship should outweigh the cons; think
  like an **investor** who invests time/energy into relationships expecting a positive
  return, and builds the best network by investing where returns are highest.
- **Abundant exchange**: if both people feel they're getting the better end of the deal,
  the relationship continues indefinitely and neither has reason to end it.
- **Family and spouse** should ideally be *most* aligned with you long-term; the spouse
  is the most extreme friend — the lifelong best friend/ally who should root for you
  hardest and never resent your success. Friends are "non-sexualized versions" of that
  core ally.
- People don't want the best for you — they want **the version of you that best serves
  them now**; the closer the long-term alignment, the more those two versions overlap.
- **Seasons of friends** are okay: few 85-year-olds have kept the same friends for life.
  Diverging "train tracks" is natural and fine; losing a friend doesn't make them an
  enemy (Tupac: "I still want to see you eat, just not at my table").
- Decision heuristic: when torn between the **hard thing and the easy thing, do the hard
  thing** — if the easy thing were right you'd have already done it. Applies to keeping
  vs. ending a relationship ("you probably already know you shouldn't").
- **True help makes your life easier**; "I was just trying to help" that makes life 10x
  harder is destruction, not help.
- Reframe "burning bridges" as **pruning a tree**: a tree has limited sunlight, water,
  and soil minerals; the healthiest thing is to cut branches so it can grow.
- On the "you've changed" accusation: agree with it ("you're right, and you haven't");
  growth requires change, and entrepreneurs should expect it.
- Two ways these friendships end: (1) decrease communication frequency → fewer invites →
  it fizzles (how most loose acquaintances end); (2) for very close people, a hard
  conversation.
- Hard-conversation framework (credited to Leila): keep the **other person as a human
  being at the forefront** rather than being right; accept responsibility ("I've changed,
  that's on me").
- You can have friends who aren't pursuing greatness as long as they help you pursue
  yours; he wants to **admire something in every friend** and be around people committed
  to being excellent at their craft (example: a close friend who is an FBI agent and
  gains nothing from his status/followers).
