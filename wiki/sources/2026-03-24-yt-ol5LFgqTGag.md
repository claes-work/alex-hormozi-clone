# Helping a $4M ARR SaaS Scale and Exit

## Summary
Alex Hormozi advises the founder of an enterprise customer-experience SaaS platform (selling to banks, retailers, and government facilities) who is at ~$4M ARR and wants to reach $12M ARR by end of 2026. The founder's stated constraints are long sales cycles, high CAC, and the capital-intensity of delivering large enterprise projects. Hormozi reframes the long sales cycle as a "feature not a bug" of enterprise, identifies capital/cash flow as the real constraint, and prescribes front-loading cash via up-front fees and phased/tranche payment terms plus moving upmarket in the marketing immediately.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues the long sales cycle is not a problem but a fact — a trade-off of selling enterprise, which is offset by longer contract retention, higher gross margins, and far higher value per customer.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that the real constraint in the founder's business is capital (cash flow), not the length of the cycle; he frames the cash flow squeeze as an existential threat while the timeline is merely a feature of the market.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends two paths: (1) keep selling the smaller, shorter-cycle deals as a cash bridge while bigger enterprise accounts onboard and begin generating gross profit, or (2) change terms on big accounts to pull cash forward.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises adding initiation, enrollment, onboarding, and setup fees up front to offset CAC, and crediting them back later (e.g. "pay first two months up front, get two months free on the back," or "pay first and last month now, then roll into monthly").
- As of March 2026, Alex suggests structuring the offer into phases paid in tranches, and landing a smaller-scope initial engagement (e.g. an initial 90-day) that is easier to get approved, then upselling from the small scope to the big scope once the customer is in the ecosystem.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises changing the marketing to upmarket messaging sooner rather than later, on the principle that advertising upmarket still captures downmarket buyers but not vice versa: "big guys don't want to hang out with the minnows, but the minnows want to hang out with the big guys."
- The founder claims (unverified) ~$4M ARR run rate, a goal of $12M ARR by end of 2026, that the product started three years ago as a simpler/cheaper offering, and that pushing higher-ROI/higher-value has lengthened the sales cycle; the founder also confirms the model is properly priced (margins good once accounts mature).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "So the problem is — cuz the fact that it takes a long time isn't a problem, it's just a fact. The constraint in the business is capital. That's the constraint."

> "Oftentimes we describe features not bugs. It's like we think there's something wrong with our business when it's really just like that's just an element of the business that you're in."

> "One of the tradeoffs is it will take them longer to buy. One of the things that you get for that is that they tend to keep their contracts, they tend to stick for multiple years, they tend to have higher gross margins, they tend to be worth way more per customer. It's a trade, it's not like you have a problem."

> "That's where adding in kind of initiation, enrollment, onboarding, setup fees up front can make a huge difference to the business, and then you can always credit it off the back."

> "It's easier to get the approval on a much smaller scope, and then upsell from the small scope to the big scope."

> "Typically when you advertise upmarket, you will still scoop up downmarket, but it doesn't work the other way. So big guys don't want to hang out with the minnows, but the minnows want to hang out with the big guys."
