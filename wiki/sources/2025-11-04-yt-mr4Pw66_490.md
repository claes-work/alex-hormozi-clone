# If I Wanted to Create a Business That Runs Itself, Here's What I'd Do

## Summary

Solo talk in which Hormozi lays out why an owner-dependent business isn't really a
business and walks through a process for making a company create value without the
founder being required. He frames the payoff in wealth terms — a self-running business
earns a sale multiple (his example: 6x profit), so removing yourself is worth far more
than the after-tax income you'd otherwise pocket. The core method is: inventory
everything you do (via a time study), convert each task into a project/process/person,
build decision trees and KPIs, replace yourself in operations, marketing, and management,
then stress-test by taking 90 days off.

## Key claims (how to make a business run without you)

- **A business that requires you isn't a business** (in the sense most founders want —
  freedom). Owner-dependence turns a company into "a job you cannot quit."
- **Owner-run vs. owner-free is a ~20x wealth gap.** Two firms with identical top line
  ($10M) and bottom line ($2M): the owner-run one only adds ~$500K/yr to net worth after
  taxes and living expenses; the owner-free one is a sellable asset worth ~6x profit
  ($12M), and each extra $500K of profit adds ~$3M to enterprise value vs. ~$250K after
  tax to the operator.
- **Step 1 — Self-inventory / time study.** List literally everything you do; if you
  don't know, run a time study (Excel sheet, 15-minute intervals, a timer, one word per
  slot). Get as granular as possible so you can hand tasks off without breaking the machine.
- **Assign project / process / person to each task**, then red/yellow/green triage:
  green = can hand to existing team now; yellow = a one-time project/process you know how
  to install; red = you don't know how or need a person you don't have. Solve green → red.
- **Build decision trees ("if this, then that").** Document rules of behavior for common
  scenarios (change card, change flavor, change billing cadence). The more duplicatable
  the job and the more people in a function, the more standardized the questions become.
- **Set decision-authority boundaries** — dollar thresholds under which staff can act
  independently without owner approval (e.g. under $500 / $1,000 / $10,000 depending on
  company size), backed by financial review after the fact.
- **Give every role a scorecard/KPIs tied to how it makes the company money.** Ask each
  person "How does your role make the company money?" Reverse-engineer potential mistakes
  (e.g. a camera person: footage saved, named, correct drive, timestamped, lighting/camera
  set up, on time) into positive metrics.
- **Step 2 — Trade doing for managing and leading.** Swap ~200 hrs/month of doing for
  ~20 hrs of managing (10x leverage), then hire a manager and trade down again. Leverage
  through labor: turn effort into an organization that produces without you.
- **Graduate people with an 80% test.** Accept 60–80% as good as you initially if there's
  a clear path to improvement. Three-stage handoff: shadow (they watch you) → supervise
  (they do it in front of you) → support independence (available but not involved).
- **Measure employees by rate of improvement, not starting point.** "Intelligence I
  measure as rate of learning" — prefer a faster learner over a more experienced slow one.
- **Recruit A-players; hire people who can teach you.** At leadership level and above, if
  you're not learning from the candidate it's a waste. Interview far more people than feels
  comfortable; use group interviews for frontline-to-manager roles. Pattern recognition
  ("your picker") is a top asset built by volume (Sam interviewed 600 developers to find
  CTO Daniel at Skool).
- **Step 3 — Remove yourself from marketing/acquisition.** Founder-as-breadwinner is one
  of the biggest risk factors. Install ~7 systems to generate marketing without
  direct-to-camera founder ads: (1) weekly screenshots of praise in your community;
  (2) incentivized testimonials (credits, unlockable service tiers, trials — a "double
  whammy" that also ascends customers); (3) "life cycle ads" compiled from existing sales/
  onboarding/delivery recordings showing the before→after journey; (4) documenting emotive
  client-deliverable moments (ribbon cutting, kitchen reveal, weigh-in); (5) paying support
  reps ~$5 per chat testimonial captured; (6) turning existing Google/Yelp/Amazon reviews
  into ads (including funny 1-stars, Liquid Death style, to repel the wrong customer and
  attract the right one); plus affiliate programs / TikTok Shop / whitelisted ads.
- **The litmus test.** Baseline: does it burn down without you? Better: leave for a month
  and it's the same. Best: leave and it comes back *stronger* — that's what buyers pay a
  big multiple for (a fast-growing annuity). Ultimate test: take 90 days off and have the
  business grow anyway. Overexpansion (2nd/3rd locations before the 1st runs without you)
  is a top reason businesses fail.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "A business that requires you isn't a business."
- "You lose your optionality. You lose your freedom. And you have a job you cannot quit."
- "It's very very inefficient to become wealthy off of regular income because it's taxed
  to oblivion."
- "I can do a mountain of work but I can't do Mount Everest. That requires more hands and
  more shovels."
- "If I can do it right with part of my time, someone else can do it better with all of
  their time."
- "You can outwork anyone in your business. You will not be able to outwork everyone."
- "Intelligence I measure as rate of learning."
- "When you start really building businesses, you're not actually building the business.
  It feels much more like assembling the people who then build the business."
- "In order to make an important business, you need to become less important."
