# It's Never Been Easier to Be Successful

## Summary

A short interview clip (off-camera interviewer prompts; Hormozi answers) built
around his tweet that "it has never been easier to be successful." Hormozi's
argument: the tools (software, AI) have lowered the bar to start a business,
make content, or advertise to near-zero — but comfort ("Netflix and chilling,"
Uber, DoorDash) has lowered the bar to do *nothing* even further, so the real
scarcity is willingness to act, not opportunity. He reframes the modern safety
net (you won't go homeless or starve) as the *most exciting* part: the downside
of a huge risk is now trivially survivable, so people should swing big. Calls
the era a "blessing," not a curse, on the view that technology always moves
things forward. On the person who just scrolls at home: he refuses to call it a
problem — it's only a problem if you make "a demand of the universe" that your
life mean something while acting otherwise. Introduces his "conditional shoulds
vs. absolute shoulds" distinction. Closes with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap
plug. ★ for the "comfort-maxing / distraction bar is lower than the action bar"
framing and the "conditional vs. absolute shoulds" distinction.

## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased)

- ★ It has never been easier to be successful, but it has also never been easier
  to be distracted — the bar to act has never been lower, yet the bar to *not*
  act is lower still.
- People have never been more comfortable; society is "comfort-maxing," driven by
  on-demand entertainment and delivery (Netflix, Uber, DoorDash).
- Software platforms and AI have lowered the bar for people to make content, start
  businesses, or advertise — whatever they want to do.
- The hard part is taking a risk when you could "literally just do nothing."
- ★ The modern safety net (you won't go homeless or starve; you'll have shelter)
  is the *most exciting* part of the era, because the worst-case downside of a
  huge swing is now survivable — so take the big shot.
- The era is a blessing, not a curse: technology "is always going to move things
  forward," and it has been getting progressively easier to start a business at
  every point in history (easier today than 20 years ago, and so on backward).
- Someone at home scrolling all the time is "a them thing," not a problem — unless
  they are making a demand of the universe that their life be a certain way (that
  it have meaning, impact, etc.) while acting inconsistently with that.
- ★ Distinguishes **conditional shoulds** from **absolute shoulds**: he rejects
  absolute "my life should have meaning" claims but endorses conditional ones —
  *if* you want X, *then* you probably shouldn't do Y (e.g., if you want to get
  in shape, don't eat more than you burn).
- His core objection is only to the gap between what people say they want and how
  they actually act — otherwise "do whatever you want, it's your life."
- Plugs the free 10-stage "zero to $100M+" roadmap (broken down by 8 business
  functions), finished by <1% of companies, at acquisition.com/roadmap.
