# I Own a Marketing Agency and Teach People How to Start One. Which Should I Shut Down?

## Summary
In this live Q&A, an entrepreneur explains he runs two businesses: a marketing agency serving doctors, and a coaching business that teaches people how to start a marketing agency (monetized as an affiliate for a software company, GoHighLevel). The coaching side is his passion — more profitable, easier, but with limited enterprise value — while the agency is operationally hard and where his ego is attached. He wants to delegate the agency and focus on coaching but fears becoming "that guy" who teaches something he no longer runs. Alex reframes the credibility worry using his own Gym Launch experience: customers don't care how many gyms (businesses) you personally own — they only care whether your advice will make them money. He then gives tactical advice on an R&D/test-kitchen process for staying ahead, personal brand vs. separate brand, self-liquidating offers via selling implementation/onboarding, favorite upsells, and a content strategy of building long videos as many proven shorts stitched together.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-13. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-13) The guest's premise: he owns a marketing agency serving doctors and a coaching business teaching people how to start a marketing agency; the coaching business is his passion, is more profitable and easier, but "doesn't operationally scale" and has little enterprise value, while the agency is hard to run and staff (media buyers, back-end delivery). [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-13) The guest's fear: he doesn't want to teach something he doesn't run — "that guy who teaches you how to start a marketing agency, but he closed his marketing agency." [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-13) Alex's central reframe: customers do not actually care how many businesses you personally own; the only thing a customer cares about is how likely it is that your advice will get them to make more money. The fact that you run the thing is just used in their mind as an approximation of risk/proof that you know how to do it.
- (2026-02-13) Alex ran Gym Launch while owning a small number of gyms and says essentially no customer cared about that count — as long as the advice on how he scaled his gyms worked, it was fine.
- (2026-02-13) A frame Alex deliberately took at Gym Launch: he hoped every student would make more money than his own gyms, so success wasn't framed as competing against him ("I made more money at my gym than Alex did" → "I hope so"); students got to start on day one with all of his findings.
- (2026-02-13) Alex's best gym made $650,000 (of six locations he had); the average Gym Lord (member) gym made $600,000 a year. [self-reported figures]
- (2026-02-13) Alex claims he learned the gym business far better after he stopped running gyms day-to-day, because a bigger distribution base gave him a larger "test kitchen" to experiment across many markets and models — running gyms had actually kept him too busy to experiment.
- (2026-02-13) On being "in the trenches": Alex says he was 100% in the trenches because he worked directly with people to figure out how to help them win — reframing coaching itself as trench work, not a step removed from it.
- (2026-02-13) The R&D process that kept them ahead: take 10–20 representative markets, allocate a monthly R&D budget (his was $50,000/month, scalable up or down with revenue), identify the single biggest problem the whole distribution base is suffering from, and spend that budget/time solving it with a separate group of 10–20 members who get direct access to him.
- (2026-02-13) Release the R&D results every month. About half the time the experiment doesn't work — but members still get value from learning a given idea doesn't work ("I always wondered if that would work and now I know it doesn't"), letting them scratch the entrepreneurial itch without taking the risk; winners get rolled out across the whole distribution base, improving the model piece by piece.
- (2026-02-13) The guest currently has only one coaching product: being an affiliate for a software company (GoHighLevel) — he teaches people to start a marketing agency and tells them to sign up for the software. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-13) Personal brand vs. separate brand: Alex advises that if you have a personal brand, it will be about you, and whatever you point that traffic to, people will buy — if they trust you. (Implication: you don't need a separate brand like Gym Launch/Acquisition.com to promote your things.)
- (2026-02-13) On creating a self-liquidating offer to upsell into the software: Alex says just sell implementation — offer paid calls (e.g., 6 or 12 calls) to help the customer implement the steps into their business, which requires some service but accomplishes the self-liquidating component.
- (2026-02-13) Alex's two favorite upsells of all time, which "only always work": more of the thing you just bought, and more help with the thing you just bought.
- (2026-02-13) Selling help is especially easy when you sell a tool — everyone wants help — and you can charge much more for it; Alex suggests you could sell onboarding for ~$5,000 even for a tool that costs only $300–400/month.
- (2026-02-13) Content sequencing advice: before moving to short form or ads, the guest has a lot of room to make his long videos better; getting a short-form agency or one editor to clip moments from long videos is relatively easy.
- (2026-02-13) The harder skill is scheduling in advance where the shorts will be when you make a long video. Alex's model: the perfect long video is 20 shorts put together on one single theme.
- (2026-02-13) Alex says his "brutally honest advice" videos have over a million views each — he can crack a million on almost every one like clockwork — because he takes his top tweets from the last 30–45 days and says them in a row, each already tested for resonance, each acting as a little loop that keeps retention high and reach wide.
- (2026-02-13) Advice: think of your long videos in terms of shorts / slicing many shorts together (like "60-minute sound bites") rather than "I'm just going to talk about this topic today" — this yields easier shorts to clip and makes the longs better too.
