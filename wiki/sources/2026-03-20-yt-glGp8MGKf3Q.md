# How to Market a High-Ticket Service

## Summary
In a live coaching exchange, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a psychiatric clinic (~$3.5M revenue, offering telehealth and an in-person TMS procedure for depression) on how to grow. Hormozi diagnoses the business as demand-constrained: filling patient rosters would keep hired nurse practitioners busy, happy, and retained. His core marketing prescription is to strip all medical/technical language out of the advertising and speak in dead-simple, outcome-focused terms to the depressed customer, and he favors direct-to-consumer ads (especially local, high-ticket services) over referral chasing.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the clinic's problem as a demand constraint: more patients would fill the nurse practitioners' rosters, making them happier and more likely to stay, which in turn eases the staffing/retention problem.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues a smart, credentialed operator's biggest advertising weakness is complexity — he says you must "take how you advertise down 100 levels" and speak to the customer where they are.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he runs every piece of copy through Hemingway to hit below a fifth-grade (ideally third-grade) reading level, because a reader who has to pause to decode a word stops absorbing the message.
- As of March 2026, Alex advocates "clear over clever" in marketing.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the depressed consumer only cares about two things — the outcome ("sad face go happy face") and that insurance covers it — not technical/medical explanation.
- As of March 2026, Alex prefers direct-to-consumer ads for this business because "most people in that space suck at advertising," making it comparatively easy to win.
- As of March 2026, Alex says local high-ticket services are the "easiest button" because local markets and competitors have far less marketing sophistication, so applying global-level tactics in a local market crushes competitors.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes hateful comments as "you live your life in a way that I would not prefer," which he says is how he has gotten past many negative comments.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You have to take how you advertise down 100 levels. I'm being super real with you."

> "There's a reason that I run every single piece of copy through Hemingway and I get it, you know, below fifth grade, ideally below third grade reading level, because if someone has to pause when you say a word, you've lost them. They're trying to decode rather than just immediately absorb."

> "The more I've been doing this, the more I'm an advocate of clear over clever."

> "If I'm the consumer, I care about sad face go happy face. And I care about insurance covers."

> "I like direct to consumer personally just 'cause most people in that space suck at advertising, so it's like not that hard."

> "Local services that are high ticket is like it's hard to lose money. Because in person you have so much less sophistication in a local market and local competitors."

> "The thing that makes you great — you're very smart, did the medical stuff — is actually going to be everyone else's weak point."

> "If you ever get like hateful comments, the easiest translation in the world is: you live your life in a way that I would not prefer."
