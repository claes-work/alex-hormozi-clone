# Simple Operational Framework

## Summary

In this 2020 Gym-Launch-era podcast-style video (which he jokes is titled "operations for
dummies"), Hormozi shares his mental framework for structuring and scaling operations.
He argues operations — not marketing — is the unspoken reason some entrepreneurs make far
more money, and offers a repeatable method: list desired outcomes, derive the activities
that produce them, time each activity per customer, and multiply by inflow to build out
each role's daily calendar.

## Key claims

*All paraphrased from Alex Hormozi, 2020-10-15, unless quoted.*

- (2020) Operations is an underrated, "unspoken secret" of entrepreneurship; the internet
  space over-emphasizes marketing/sales/lead-gen, but the people who are better
  **operators** are the ones who make more money. Aimed at owners roughly from multiple
  six figures to several million per year.
- (2020) **The operational framework** — build roles from three variables:
  1. **List the desired outcomes** that need to happen.
  2. **List the activities** required to produce each outcome.
  3. **Time each activity** (units of time per customer / per unit).
  4. **Multiply by inflow** (expected volume) to get total time needed for each activity.
  5. **Place those time blocks on each role's calendar**, hour by hour, to design an ideal
     day; "that's honestly all there is to it."
- (2020) He models business as a **pipeline: click → close → resell** (click → opt-in →
  schedule → show → buy → buy again); resell = retention or ascension.
- (2020) Worked example (online fitness, with lead-gen and lead-nurture assumed handled):
  the recurring activities are **first sales calls → onboarding (nutrition orientation +
  app/tech walkthrough) → ascension conversation into continuity**, plus ongoing
  fulfillment and recruiting coaches. Each new customer needs roughly 30 min front-end +
  30 min nutrition + 30 min ascension ≈ 1.5 hrs/week; fulfillment ≈ ~2 min/client/day
  (coach capped at ~100 clients).
- (2020) There are ratios per 10 customers (e.g. "for every 10 customers we need 4 of
  these, 2 of these, 2 of these, 1 of these"), so activities scale predictably with
  volume. **Breaking points** occur when one person can no longer do all activities — that
  is when you split (block) whole activity types to a new role.
- (2020) This exercise reveals **utilization** — you can tell a coach is at 40–50%
  utilization and challenge claims of being "too busy" or needing another hire; the bigger
  you get, the more partial roles you find (full-time pay for half-time work).
- (2020) If you don't know where to start, run a **time study**: an Excel sheet, every 15
  minutes of the day, employee writes what they did. "You are never more efficient than
  when you do a time study." He and Leila use a one-week time study whenever an apartment's
  numbers feel off, to spot inefficiency or dishonesty.
- (2020) Operations wins are boring, repetitive "roll your sleeves up" work — meetings and
  training the team — and are why people who copied Gym Launch stayed one-tenth to
  one-twentieth its size. Culture and the leader's tolerance drive it: "you get what you
  tolerate."

> ⚠️ Proto-framework note: The **click → close → resell** pipeline is an early, informal
> version of the customer-lifecycle / value-ladder thinking in his later work, and the
> activities-per-outcome staffing method prefigures his later operations and org-design
> teaching. Treat as a 2020 precursor, not the mature framework.
