# How to Fix Creator Burnout

## Summary

A short Q&A clip (@MoreMozi). A creator (2nd speaker, unnamed) tells Alex he took the
"just do more" advice literally — found one video style, boiled it to a repeatable script,
and cranked volume until he hit his personal physical cap and is now hiring a team to run
it. His question: can you just do more forever, and at what point does relentless volume of
the one thing that works trade off against burnout and against other parts of yourself you
want to express?

Alex's answer reframes anti-burnout as a **portfolio-allocation problem**, borrowing
Google's 70/20/10 innovation rule and applying it to *content longevity*: keep 70% on
repeating the proven, successful format, spend 20% on one-degree-adjacent experiments, and
reserve 10% for wild moonshots unrelated to the core. The 20% keeps creative juice alive and
the 10% gives breathing room — and the counterintuitive payoff is that adding these buckets
*increases* total lifetime volume (not weekly output) because you won't want to stop. He
grounds the "don't abandon what works" half with a bricks-into-a-building analogy for
"bottling lightning," warns that the market decides what it rewards (some 20/10 returns will
be non-monetary, which he rates as good or better), and contrasts all of this with the
"Mr. Beast version" — care nothing about yourself, pure pain, fastest biggest results, but
gated by how long you can take it. Closes with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap plug.

Note: this sits inside his existing "content without burnout / production OS" thread but
the *specific 70/20/10-for-longevity framing as a burnout remedy* is the fresh angle here
(see overlap note below).

## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased unless quoted)

- ★ **70/20/10 applied to content longevity (anti-burnout).** Allocate ~70% of content effort
  to repeating the proven successful format, 20% to adjacent "one-degree" experiments, and
  10% to moonshots that have "nothing to do with the core." The 20% keeps creative energy
  alive; the 10% gives breathing room. He explicitly ties this to Google's 70/20/10
  innovation rule. *(Distinct from the existing 70-20-10 **hook**-allocation in
  [[../topics/content-strategy/content-engine]] — same numbers, different problem: here it is
  a burnout/longevity mechanism, not a hook-testing mix.)*
- ★ **Adding the 20/10 buckets raises total lifetime volume, not weekly volume.** "If you add
  those 20 and 10 you will do more because you'll do it longer" — output goes up on a 5-year
  horizon, not week-to-week, precisely because you never wanted to stop. Longevity beats
  weekly max-out.
- ★ **"Bottling lightning" / bricks-into-a-building analogy.** Treat every variable of content
  creation as bricks on the floor; a random "refresh" of the arrangement rarely forms a
  building — you have to hit it enough times before the bricks assemble into a working format.
  Once you have that building (a format that works), hitting refresh to try something new is
  unlikely to produce an even bigger building — so don't abandon the proven format; branch
  from it via the 20/10.
- **The market decides what it rewards; some returns are non-monetary.** You can pursue the
  20/10, but the market "will care about what it cares about and it will tell you that." You
  may not get a monetary return on those experiments — and a non-monetary return (it improves
  your life) he rates as "just as good if not better."
- **Cash flow is the constraint on everything.** "If you stop making money, you won't be able
  to do this at all" — the 70% proven core funds the freedom to run the 20/10.
- **The "Mr. Beast version" is the opposite pole.** Care nothing about yourself, pure pain —
  the fastest, biggest results, but capped by how long you can physically take it. Tempting,
  but a longevity gamble.
- **Creative outlets are the release valve.** Giving yourself outlets (the 20/10) is what
  makes relentless output sustainable rather than a straight line to burnout.
