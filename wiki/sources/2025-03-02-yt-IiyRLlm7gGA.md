# Building a Brand: This Strategy will help you Make more Money!

## Summary

Despite the "branding" title, this clip is Alex Hormozi on **focus, work ethic, and
constraint theory** — how money actually gets made and why most people who think they
are working are not. He argues that the entrepreneurs who succeed differ mainly in their
**expectation of the effort required** (which can be "100x off"), that learning happens
through *procedural* action (doing) not *declarative* research (reading), and that
progress comes from cutting away everything that is not "the work that moves the business
forward" for a dedicated season. He closes on **optimization vs. maximization**: you can
only optimize to 100%, but you can 100x an input like lead flow, so the highest-leverage
move is often an order-of-magnitude change, not a tweak.

## Key claims

- Alex describes creating "environments of focus" by default in each season of his life:
  studying 4 hrs/day for the GMAT after his consulting workday; then driving across the
  country and cutting off friends/family to start his gym; then flying around the country
  doing gym launches seeing "no one" besides Leila; and currently declining travel and
  speaking, only taking podcasts if hosts fly to him. (self-reported, biography-relevant)
- On "The 100" (a side onboarding-experiment group he runs with Kirby): reportedly ~86%
  of members made a dollar; among those who had **already** made money elsewhere before
  joining Skool, 100% made money on Skool; among those who had **never** made a dollar
  anywhere, 62% did. (stats stated in-video; co-host confirms figures — see Notes)
- He infers from the "made money elsewhere → 100% success" cohort that Skool is a great
  platform to immediately build on, and that transferable skills exist which beginners
  lack — the goal is to identify and eliminate the extra skills a beginner must arrive with.
- The main separator between people who make (more) money and those who don't is the
  **expectation of effort required**, not a secret. Expectations can be off by 100x
  (e.g., expecting 1 customer from 5 outreaches when it may take ~20,000 people).
- These are **expectation problems**, framed as **procedural** (know-how) rather than
  **declarative** (know-about) knowledge deficiencies — you don't learn to sell by reading,
  you learn by getting on the phone; you don't learn ads by reading, you learn by running ads.
- Definition of how money is made: at the most basic level, **converting attention into
  goods and services exchanged for money** — get someone's attention, then transact.
- Audit test: look at your weekly calendar; if a task does not get more leads/traffic/opt-ins,
  increase conversion, or increase retention/resell/referral/review, it is **not work**.
  These map to three buckets: **track / convert / deliver**.
- Two failure modes by stage: at the **beginner** level you do "a lot of stuff that's not
  working" and call it work; at the **advanced** level you fill time with real work tasks
  that are not the **constraint** of the business (doing what you like vs. what's required).
- **Constraint / throughput example:** if you love marketing and generate more leads but your
  bottleneck is not enough sales reps to take calls, you add *potential* to the system but not
  *throughput*; adding reps without adding delivery/customer-success/inventory just moves the
  constraint. This holds at $1M/year, $1M/month, $1M/week.
- Dedicated seasons of saying "no" let you "pull four years of the future into your life in a
  matter of months" — but you must confront the discomfort/unknown of figuring it out rather
  than avoiding it via more research.
- **Optimization vs. maximization:** you can only optimize to 100% (never 100% close rate or
  opt-in), but you can 100x an input like lead flow. The biggest gains come from asking "what
  would need to occur for me to 10x this in a year, and what would have to be true?" — usually
  only one thing must be true; if it's achievable with current time/money, maximization beats
  optimization.
- Reduce the time between knowing what you should do and actually starting it; the most focused
  people, watched closely, are "astounding" in how simple their approach is.
