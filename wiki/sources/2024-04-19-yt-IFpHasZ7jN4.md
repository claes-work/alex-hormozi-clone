# 54.1% of People Who Try This Make their 1st Dollar Online

## Summary

Alex Hormozi walks through the full decision-making framework behind his single
largest investment to date — becoming co-owner of Skool alongside founder Sam
Ovens — and then uses it to pitch the Skool Games, a 30-day competition designed
to help beginners make their first dollar online. He lays out five reasons for
the bet (a "lollapalooza" of aligned forces): the crisscross shift from formal to
alternative education, his personal track record monetizing that trend, mission
alignment, Skool's passive "golden ratio" growth, and the higher enterprise-value
multiple of a software/platform business. He closes with a long, unscripted team
Q&A that includes his favorite zero-to-one playbook: cold-outreach to mid-size
influencers to partner on a community.

## Key claims

- **"Hundred golden BBs, no silver bullets."** Explosive growth comes not from one
  thing but from many incremental advantages multiplied together — Charlie Munger's
  "lollapalooza effect." Hormozi says he sees that stack of aligned forces in Skool.
- **Education crisscross (self-reported market figures):** global education ≈ $10
  trillion, US ≈ $1.3 trillion. Formal education (2- and 4-year degrees) ≈ $850B and
  **shrinking ~6%/yr (~$50B lost)**; alternative/e-learning ≈ $200–250B and **growing
  ~20%/yr (~$40B gained)**. The amount formal loses roughly equals what alternative
  gains — a shift in buying behavior as people learn monetizable skills directly from
  creators rather than paying for degrees with poor ROI. He'd "rather be in this boat."
- **Personal fit (self-reported):** his first two big exits (Gym Launch, Prestige
  Labs) served creators; there was "no university for gyms," so he built one. He says
  that at ~27 he "took home $16 million in income" capitalizing on this trend — so he
  has both the macro trend data and the personal anecdote of the wealth moving.
- **The golden ratio of passive growth:** a business's passive growth rate = % of
  customers who refer ÷ % who churn/exit per interval. 10% refer vs 10% leave = flat;
  20% refer vs 10% leave = doubles every interval regardless of size. Most businesses
  refer ~2% and churn ~10%, so they must buy ads to refill the bucket. Only once
  referrals exceed churn should you "pour gas on the flame." He says Skool's passive
  growth metrics were the best he'd ever seen (acquisition.com reviews "several
  thousand businesses a month").
- **Enterprise value / multiples (why a platform):** same $10M topline / $3M profit
  business is worth ~$15M as a service (5x earnings), ~$20M as e-commerce (2x
  topline), and ~$100–200M as SaaS (10–20x topline). Doubling each adds ~$15M / ~$20M
  / ~$200M respectively — "same amount of work, you get more for what you put in."
  This is the acquisition.com lever logo: get more for what you put in.
- **The Skool Games:** a 30-day competition co-designed with Sam Ovens; **14 days
  free** inside the 30 days so people can dip out; **top 10 fly to Vegas**. Free
  give-away of what the industry charges for — the "C's": coaching/calls, courses,
  community, code/tools — which also builds **conviction**.
- **First-dollar stat (self-reported; see Notes for discrepancy):** of people who
  finished the first month of the first Games, a share made their first dollar online.
  The title says **54.1%**; the transcript variously reads **3.44%**, **30.4%**, and
  his spoken framing **"just under one out of three"** / "one of the 30%."
- **Transparency thesis:** because Skool owns the platform, all member revenue is
  public — "no one's fronting" — bringing honesty to a deceptive industry.
- **Skool business model:** flat **$99/month** per group (revenue = groups × $99),
  deliberately un-tiered to maximize students/teachers in **growth mode** not profit;
  platform is **losing money** by reinvesting in (gaming) engineers. He "wrote a big
  check into it." Aimed at the $10T global education nut.
- **Borrow-the-brand (Amazon vs Shopify):** his book's Shopify page converts ~2–3%;
  the **same book on Amazon converts ~30% (~10x)** because of platform trust. As Skool
  trust compounds, Skool pages will outconvert anything a beginner builds. Skool page
  layout copies the convergent Zillow/Airbnb/Amazon template (video, carousel, reviews,
  buy button, bullets) because those platforms have the most conversion data on Earth.
- **The beginner model:** get eyeballs (post, ads, outreach, or partner with an
  audience) → drive to a Skool page → recurring paid community ($1–$9,999/mo). No
  inventory, no integrations, no tech knowledge; works internationally as long as the
  audience shares a language (avoids shipping/tariffs). "Get the first dollar across
  the bridge."
- **Gamification & network effect:** points/status unlock abilities like a game (core
  dev team are ex-gaming developers); members see each other's posts and help each
  other, unlike Facebook groups that throttle paid reach (Evelyn testimonial: 300 of
  6,500 reached; 234 net new members organically from Skool's Discovery/directory,
  which ranks communities like Google ranks pages).
- **Highest-willingness-to-pay community features** (from a ~14,000-person survey by
  a friend who sold "Profit[Well]" for ~$250M, mapped on care × willingness-to-pay):
  the big ones are **access** (Q&As/proximity to the expert), **exclusive content**
  (courses/trainings/private feed articles), and **events** (in-person meetups);
  newsletters and merch/discounts rate lower.
- **Zero-to-one outreach playbook (his favorite):** if starting over at zero, cold-
  reach ~50k–150k-follower influencers (people you already follow) to partner on a
  community; you only need one yes. Interview 5 of their commenters, build a deck about
  their business, and the work itself proves you'll work hard. Rejections become
  portfolio pieces for the next pitch. The underdog's advantage is undivided
  attention. Don't be greedy on the first split (take ~20%), bank reps and proof.
- **Sam Ovens / partnership philosophy:** Sam is "obsessed but not compulsive" — the
  ideal entrepreneur holds attention on one goal for years. Good partners are equally
  obsessed with *different* things (product / promotion / people), not the same thing.
- **Never lower risk to start a business:** in 1924 you'd mortgage a house to open a
  bakery; today cost of delivery and distribution are both ~zero, so it's the lowest-
  risk era ever — same work required, far less risk. Formal education resists
  disruption only because the government backs student loans (cost feels like zero now,
  pain deferred years out).
