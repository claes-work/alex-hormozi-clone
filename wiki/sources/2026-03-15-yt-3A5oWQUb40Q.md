# Helping a Fitness Coach Agency Get to $1M per Month

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client sells GoHighLevel/CRM setups (four pre-built funnels) to online fitness coaches, does ~$2M/year, and wants $1M/month. Their metrics are healthy — 7% monthly churn, >$9,000 LTV, ~$1,500 CAC (LTV:CAC 4.6:1) — with customers coming ~40% from strategic partnerships (other mentors sending clients), plus organic and Meta/IG ads. Alex diagnoses the bottleneck as under-spending on paid ads: they run only $2,000/month "just on." His first rule of thumb — you can't validate a paid campaign unless you're willing to spend **~2x your target CAC** as a pure test, because feedback cadence on a tiny budget is too slow to learn anything. On the offer, Alex builds a lead magnet around the tight avatar: "You having trouble turning your Instagram followers into customers? All of this can be automated — we'll show you in 7 minutes how to do it," anchored by **five diverse case-study coaches** (different psychographics/avatars, all under 5,000 followers, all getting ~five clients a week) to preempt the universal "will this work for me?" objection, then VSL → sales call → close with as much cash up front as possible. The client's worry: they're a software/automation company, not a mentorship/guru business. Alex's key strategic move — drawn from his own near-identical business (with Alan, brick-and-mortar) — is to **go up one level**: instead of marketing to SMBs (the coaches), market to the **agencies/business-mentors who serve them**. Marketing directly to SMBs "sucked"; selling agencies his platform (do the back-end work they don't want) gave far stickier revenue. He recounts the Alan economics: acquire an agency for ~$5,000, they pay ~$25,000 to onboard, ~$1,000 per customer (50 customers = ~$50k), plus recurring ~30% of top line — high margin because the client's team did the back-end the agencies didn't want. Key caveat: going up a level means serving **two customers** (needing a CSM for the gurus AND success for the end customers). He also warns against selling to would-be coaches who lack demand: "you don't want to generate demand, you want to channel it" — meet customers who already have the problem (coaches already doing $10k+). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's paid-ads testing rule: you can't validate a campaign unless you're willing to spend ~2x your target CAC as a test — on too small a budget (e.g. $2,000/month against a ~$2k CAC) the feedback cadence is so slow you learn nothing; you must overspend first to then make it efficient.
- As of March 2026, spending "just enough to have ads on" ($2,000/month here) is effectively no real test; expecting to nail a campaign on the first shot is unlikely.
- As of March 2026, for a niche software/CRM offer to a tight avatar (online fitness coaches on Instagram), the lead magnet should promise automation of their pain (living in the DMs): "turn your Instagram followers into customers without DMing anyone… we'll show you in 7 minutes."
- As of March 2026, the strongest proof asset is **multiple diverse case studies** — e.g. five coaches spanning different psychographics/avatars, all with <5,000 followers, all getting ~5 clients/week — because every prospect's real objection is "will this work for me?" and varied avatars answer "yes, it will work for you."
- As of March 2026, the accelerator move for this business is to **go up one level**: market to the agencies/business-mentors who serve the coaches, not the coaches (SMBs) directly — marketing directly to SMBs "sucked" in his own identical business (Alan); agency revenue is far stickier.
- As of March 2026 (Alan economics, recounted): acquire an agency for ~$5,000, they pay ~$25,000 to onboard, ~$1,000 per end-customer onboarded (≈$50k for 50 customers), plus recurring ~30% of top line — highly profitable because his team did the back-end work the agencies didn't want.
- As of March 2026, the messaging hook to agencies is stickiness of recurring revenue: "aren't you tired of giving people the keys to the kingdom and then having them leave 3 months later? Wouldn't it be nice to have recurring revenue that sticks year over year?" — several such angles worth testing.
- As of March 2026, a caveat of going up a level: you now serve **two customers** (the gurus/agencies and the end customers), requiring a customer-success manager for each side.
- As of March 2026, don't sell to prospects who then ask "how do I get leads?" — "you don't want to generate demand, you want to channel it"; target customers who already have the problem (coaches already doing ~$10k/month).
