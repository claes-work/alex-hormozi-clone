# High Ticket Sales Are Easier Than Cheap Ones

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a clinic offering telehealth and in-person TMS for depression. The founder is balancing patient demand, clinician retention, and referral/direct-to-consumer channels. Alex diagnoses the clinic as demand constrained and recommends direct-to-consumer local advertising, but the deeper advice is copy simplicity: strip the technical medical explanation down to the patient's desired transformation and insurance coverage.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - physician/clinic owner. His clinic type, revenue, TMS procedure, clinician retention, insurance, and credentials are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The clinic is demand constrained if it can handle more patients and needs to fill clinician schedules.
- More patients can also improve clinician retention because clinicians with full rosters are happier and earn what they expected.
- Referral partnerships are possible, but Alex personally likes direct-to-consumer ads because most local healthcare competitors are weak at advertising.
- Highly educated founders must simplify their advertising dramatically; if a prospect has to decode a word, attention is lost.
- Alex says he runs copy through Hemingway and wants very low reading levels because clear beats clever.
- For depressed patients, the message should be the simple transformation: sad face to happy face, insurance covers it, and it works better than talk therapy.
- Local high-ticket services are easier to advertise because competition within a 25-mile radius is often unsophisticated.
- Competitors may morally judge simple advertising because it works and violates their professional self-image.
