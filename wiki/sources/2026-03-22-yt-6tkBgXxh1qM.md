# How Do I Go From Word of Mouth to a Real Marketing System?

## Summary
In a live conference Q&A, Alex Hormozi coaches a caller who runs a barbecue catering and restaurant business doing $1.2M/year (~80% catering) almost entirely on word-of-mouth, who wants to reach $10M in three years. Hormozi advises focusing on the more scalable catering arm, reframes the caller's perceived seasonal "volatility" as merely a lead-generation gap, and prescribes paid advertising (starting with PPC for high-intent leads, then Meta ads) plus the sales infrastructure needed to work paid leads.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues a business trying to scale should usually pick one focus; here catering is more scalable than the restaurant because the restaurant is limited by its "four walls" while catering is not.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that seasonal volatility is not inherently a problem — some businesses (he cites Harry & David and See's Candy earning most profit in Q4) make nearly all their money in one season and that is fine, as long as you "print money" in-season to cover the off-season.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes the caller's slow months as an artificial, self-fulfilling constraint rather than real volatility: if the business captured all the catering demand in its market each month, even the worst month would become the best, so the fix is simply to out-market competitors and smooth the demand curve with better marketing.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends paid ads for a local catering business — PPC first (warmest, highest-intent leads searching for a caterer), then Meta ads.
- As of March 2026, Alex warns that paid leads require sales infrastructure the caller lacks (having only worked warm referrals): a landing page, a lead-nurture process, scheduling, and a VSL to frame the prospect before the closing call — and that paid leads cost more and must be "worked," unlike free referrals.
- Caller claims (unverified): the business did $1.2M last year (~$1M in catering), feeds over 1,000 people out of one commercial kitchen with up to ~10 teams pushed out, is underpriced with a "broken model" (per prior advice from someone named Ed), and peaks in spring, fall, and Q4.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I think you'll probably need to pick one or the other. Realistically, it sounds like the catering business is more scalable... the restaurant's going to be limited by your four walls relatively quickly, whereas the catering not so much."

> "Volatility doesn't in and of itself mean that there's anything wrong with the business. So, like Harry and David's makes like 80% of their revenue in December. And they lose money the other 11 months of the year. See's Candy, same thing."

> "Some of you guys have this artificial constraint and being like, you know, March is always a bad month for us. It's like, why? There's no actual reason. It's just historically then we start having a self-fulfilling prophecy."

> "All we have to do is just outmarket the competition so that that'll basically smooth out the demand curve. So you don't have volatility, you just need better marketing."

> "If I were to start with one, I'd probably start with PPC. And then I would move on to meta, but I think PPC is probably the highest likely cuz it'll be the warmest leads. They're high intent. You'll pay more than you're used to. Well, you're not used to paying anything, so."

> "You'll have to have a landing page. You'll have to have a lead nurture process. You'll have to get them scheduled. You'll have to have them watch a little VSL beforehand so you kind of frame what they're getting into... Yeah, that's what you have to do. You have to advertise."

## Notes
Synthesis candidate: reframe that perceived seasonal "volatility" is often an artificial self-fulfilling constraint solvable by out-marketing competitors to smooth the demand curve — a lead-gen argument worth a marketing topic note; PPC-before-Meta channel ordering for local high-intent services is a concrete tactical nuance.
