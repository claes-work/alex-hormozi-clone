# 'How Do I Get My Team to Use AI?' | Hormozi Hotline

## Summary
Hormozi Hotline call-in clip. A caller who has personally embraced AI (to cut grunt work) but is wary of its societal effects — as is their team — asks how to get the team to actually use AI "without being like an AI dictator," while keeping information safe and staying authentic. Alex's core adoption mechanic is **raise the output expectations, don't mandate the tool**: if you increase the standards of output, a team member who pushes back ("this is taking so long") creates the opening to say "let me show you a faster way to do it." Without raised standards there's no incentive to adopt AI, because employees are already hitting the minimum bar for keeping their job without it. He reframes the pitch to the team as relief, not threat: "I don't want to take the most challenging parts of your job away. I want to take all the terrible parts of your job away" — the repeated, boring work. He includes himself: content, emails, tweets are repetitive, and AI as a prompting/ideation/"thinking partner" speeds him up far more than starting from scratch every time. Tactically he recommends **boiling each role down to its tasks/behaviors as SOPs and turning those into SOP prompts** ("Sarah, you do nine things here"); even taking 80% or half of a task while giving 2x–8x leverage is worth it. On the "dictator" worry, he says adoption is partly a **hiring/culture problem**: hire people who embrace change and growth; you won't convert everyone, but the switchers move over and the refusers get replaced by people open to technology — like refusing to use the internet, they're just early. On **data security**, two layers: (1) get a professional/corporate instance because personal accounts aren't protected — OpenAI can be subpoenaed (e.g. in a divorce a court could compel your messages), whereas corporate accounts are protected; (2) don't put anything sensitive/non-public in there at all. On business numbers he's candidly "not as concerned as I probably should be" and not alarmist — he expects the data is used mostly to curate advertising/offers rather than to expose users, and notes how much personal-information risk to accept is a personal decision (he openly states his YouTube revenue; other companies stay fully private). On AI-art backlash, he's dismissive: anyone whose job is threatened by technology understandably gets upset (phone-connection operators, professional typists disappeared too), but "you're not going to get in the way of the future" — technology wins "100 to zero" against people trying to stop it, so you'll "get steamrolled by progress." His TLDR: he ignores the hate; acquisition.com's goal of making real business education accessible requires using the most cutting-edge tools to maximize ROI. Ends with the 10-stage roadmap promo (ignored for claims).
