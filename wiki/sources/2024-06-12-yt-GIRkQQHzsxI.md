# You're Wasting 80% of Your Time (here's how to fix it)

## Summary

Solo talk in which Hormozi frames productivity as return on time invested ("how much
money you get out for the time you put in") and teaches the **maker vs. manager
schedule** distinction — which he credits to Paul Graham's essay from ~14 years prior.
Managers thrive on many small slots and a fully booked calendar; makers (creators,
entrepreneurs, coders, editors, writers) need a few large uninterrupted blocks and are
maximally productive with an *empty* calendar. Most damage happens when managers
interrupt makers, imposing a disproportionate trade. He gives a three-pronged solution
(for managers, for makers, for the organization), walks through his own calendar, and
argues that ruthless time protection has been the single highest predictor of his
success.

## Key claims

His time / prioritization system (all Alex-attributed; paraphrase unless quoted):

- **Productivity = money out / time in.** The best investors of time make the most
  money; time is what you invest, money is the return.
- **Two schedule types, and you switch hats within a day/week:**
  - *Manager schedule* — divides the day into 20+ small chunks (5 min to 90 min); an
    empty slot is a lost opportunity; treats time like currency; clear start (prepping
    for meetings) and end (last meeting); goal is to fill every slot. Output = decisions,
    direction, coordination. Works *on* meetings.
  - *Maker schedule* — only a few large chunks (typically 2/day sustainably, up to 3 if
    working 18h: morning, post-lunch, post-dinner blocks of ~4–6h); fixed start time but
    *variable/open-to-goal* end time; an empty block is the opportunity to get the
    return. Output = the things they build. Works *off* meetings.
- **The interruption math is asymmetric.** One meeting can blow one of a maker's ~10
  weekly work blocks (≈10% of output), while costing a manager only 1 of 100+ slots —
  a 10x-more-expensive trade for the maker.
- **The Zeigarnik effect:** open loops (a scheduled meeting) occupy the mind; makers
  can't reach flow knowing they'll have to exit it, so even a late-day meeting "kills"
  the block.
- **Maker work is important-but-not-urgent** — the deep work that actually moves the
  business forward over a month/quarter/year; 100% of what he accomplishes comes from
  maker time, not manager time.
- **The maker's "no"** is not a real-world no — it means the maker is protecting a
  larger commitment; managers should not penalize it.
- **Three-pronged solution:**
  - *Managers:* understand the double disruption (coordinating + the meeting itself) and
    the 10x cost; honor the maker's no; ask the team what an ideal/most-productive day
    looks like and follow it; don't assume all work must happen simultaneously.
  - *Makers:* let good managers help by telling them how you work; when forced to take a
    meeting, "fight fire with fire" and batch all ad-hoc meetings into that ruined block
    (switch to manager mode); set standard meeting windows (e.g. Mon/Thu afternoons);
    communicate about how you'll communicate (announce when you'll be slow to respond);
    actually work when you say you will.
- **Personal evolution (V1→V2→V3):** V1 — do maker work in early mornings, nights and
  weekends on top of the day job (his gym-owner era: ad copy at dawn, sales calls at 9am;
  ~9 maker blocks/week); V2 — 50/50 split, maker first half, manager second half, and
  schedule meetings *back-to-front* (from 5pm backwards) to keep the morning open and
  avoid dead gaps; V3 (current) — entire maker days (2–4/week) plus one stacked manager
  day for all meetings/decisions/portfolio coordination.
- **Plan the calendar back-to-front** so the front of the day stays maximally open and
  you avoid useless 45-min gaps between meetings.
- **Organization level:** institute mandated quiet time (his editors, then org-wide,
  have a Wednesday "acquisition.com quiet day" — Wednesday chosen so the longest wait for
  a guaranteed quiet day is two days); remote work makes this more important because it
  removes the visible transparency of someone working, so extend trust and measure on
  output/deadlines; announce which "hat" you're wearing ("I'm putting my maker hat on
  today") to reset expectations in one move.
- **Meeting-cost framing:** a 1-hour call with 10 people at ~$50/hr each is a ~$400–500
  expense; a team doing that repeatedly is "taking the whole team out to Chili's 10 times
  a day" — this is why people "stay poor," by mis-allocating time.
- **Audit meetings:** after each meeting ask (1) does this need to happen again, and (2)
  is there a person or system that can replace me. Leila (context — his wife, an
  operator, not the persona) starts her week by *deleting* meetings first and reviews all
  recurring meetings quarterly across the whole company.
- **Freedom vs. control:** entrepreneurship is a progressive relinquishing of control;
  you cannot have absolute control and absolute freedom at once, so you extend trust
  (with guardrails and output measures).
- **His schedules vs. Leila's:** she runs ~1 maker day + 4 manager days (inverted); he
  runs ~4 maker days + 1 manager day. Both need the minority type for leverage.
- He deliberately puts recurring *customer* calls (e.g. weekly Skool Games Q&A) on his
  manager day because they feel like interruptions.
