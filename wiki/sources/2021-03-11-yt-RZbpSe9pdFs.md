# 3 Steps To Starting (Or Fixing) Any Business

## Summary
In this early-2021 solo video, Hormozi recounts advising a high-school friend who was
starting a business and worried about being "overwhelmed" by fulfillment — despite not
having made a single sale. Hormozi's diagnosis is that the friend was solving a problem
that did not yet exist, and his prescription is a sequencing rule: create demand first
(via an offer so good people feel stupid saying no), monetize it, and only then add
friction and optimize. He argues that in the beginning you should over-deliver and even
"scale the unscalable," because you cannot know which parts of your offer to trim until
sales reveal what buyers actually value versus what merely eats your time. He closes with
the origin story of the "offer so good they'd feel stupid saying no" principle, which he
credits to business coach Travis "TJ" Jones at a $3,000 conference he attended at age 23.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)
- (2021) The most common early-stage mistake is trying to solve problems that don't yet
  exist — e.g. worrying about fulfillment overwhelm before making any sales.
- (2021) His core sequencing rule: **create flow, monetize flow, then add friction** — in
  that order.
- (2021) Before you have demand, sell "the world" / "give away the farm": stack as much
  value as possible so the prospect feels stupid saying no, even if the offer is
  unreasonable to deliver (selling your time, one-on-one work, flying/driving to the
  client).
- (2021) You don't have to be good at sales in the beginning — a good enough offer
  compensates; if someone says no, keep adding value (rather than cutting price) until
  they say yes.
- (2021) Being overwhelmed when starting out is normal and expected; the money from sales
  gives you the resources to then handle fulfillment problems.
- (2021) Only after sales exist can you learn which promised items were valuable in the
  sale but cheap to fulfill, and which the buyer didn't care about but consumed your time
  — then peel those back into an offer that is both profitable and attractive.
- (2021) Mindset framing: "money is made in your mind first" — poor people hold poor
  beliefs and expect poor outcomes; rich people expect rich outcomes and won't make a
  decision unless they believe the outcome is possible. Self-limiting belief, not the
  offer stack, is often the real blocker.
- (2021) Buyers won't remember the details of the offer stack; they remember how they felt
  and how well you understood their problem, and whether the solution felt unique, easy to
  understand, and easy to believe would work for them.
- (2021, biographical) At age 23 he paid $3,000 he didn't have for a weekend conference run
  by business coach Travis "TJ" Jones (who mentioned making $1M/year); Hormozi was the only
  non-business-owner there and felt over his head amid talk of CTRs, conversion rates, and
  cost per lead. On a break, TJ gave him the line that became foundational: make an offer so
  good people feel stupid saying no.
