# The Power of Focus: How to Get Ahead in Your Business Career

## Summary

In a talk to his Skool community, Alex Hormozi reframes focus not as willpower or trying harder, but as the active removal of distractions and the resolving of unmade decisions. He argues his biggest financial growth periods have coincided with "seasons of no" — extended stretches of committing wholeheartedly to one thing and doing nothing else. He layers in ideas about deciding fast on imperfect information, the trainability of work capacity, and the necessary trade-offs and unglamorous process behind any worthwhile outcome.

## Key claims

- Focus is best defined as the quality and quantity of things you turn down or say no to; the most focused person would do literally one thing and nothing else.
- Most of focus is the active removal of things that drain attention, not trying harder to stare at a screen. "Value is created by removing all friction; focus is created by removing all distraction."
- The most focused people don't have willpower you lack — they've removed distractions you still have.
- His biggest growth periods in life and finances have followed or coincided with extended "seasons of no."
- Decisions are made in an instant based on information, not slowly; "it takes time to make decisions" is a fallacy.
- To resolve indecision, define the variables that would make something a yes (or a no) and reverse-engineer from there.
- The "perfect pick" is a fallacy at all levels of business; most decisions must be made on imperfect information, and if you have perfect information about an opportunity, the opportunity has already expired — the risk of incomplete information is part of what makes an opportunity.
- Analogy: walking up a mountain through mist — the next few feet clarify as you move; you may reach a subtop and have to step down, but you'll always have better perspective after taking action than those plotting a perfect path from the bottom.
- Multitasking makes people significantly less effective; switching back and forth kills efficiency by ~75%.
- Skill is leverage on work: skill = leverage, work = output; more skill yields more output for the same time, so choosing to do fewer things is itself a skill.
- Focus is not a trait but an action you can repeatedly exercise; few business decisions are truly irreversible, especially early when stakes are low.
- People have a "preference problem" — they want the outcome but want to change the variables that create it (want the six-pack but not the workout and nutrition plan).
- Work capacity is trainable: like muscle recovery, your ability to withstand work and recover from it improves over time — "you get a bigger shovel that can move more dirt faster."
- Elimination hierarchy for building focus/capacity: (1) unmade decisions, (2) things that are not the work (e.g. obsessing over morning routines), (3) relationships and hobbies.
- You should work as much as you possibly can and then rest as efficiently as possible so you can resume at max output; hobbies/games are fine as a reward after work, not as a substitute for it.
- Most of the daily process isn't fun; people conflate the thing they want with the process of getting it. "No one does what they love all the time to do the things to get the things they want."
