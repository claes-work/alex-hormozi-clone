# What People Get Wrong About Content

## Summary
Short solo clip. Alex recounts an experiment he ran with his editing team, who he says sometimes "get cute" and "want to get fancy" in post-production. To test whether production polish actually drives results, he had one video shot entirely on an iPhone — bad audio, shaky, handheld, no tripod — and it still pulled roughly 1.7-2 million views because the underlying **concept** was strong. His takeaway: teams over-invest in post-production on videos where it "doesn't matter," or matters maybe ~10%, instead of allocating resources toward the thing with the highest return. The core thesis: **"the words are the hard part."** The words require thinking, and thinking is what most people avoid — so they hide in production polish instead. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (the $100M scaling roadmap quiz).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex ran a deliberate experiment: shoot a video entirely on an iPhone (poor audio, shaky, handheld, no tripod) to isolate the effect of production quality; it still got roughly 1.7-2 million views.
- As of March 2026, the video succeeded because of the **concept** he knew would perform well, not the production values.
- As of March 2026, teams (his own editing team included) over-allocate time to post-production; polish "might matter 10%" but rarely earns the highest return on the resources spent.
- As of March 2026, his correction to what people get wrong about content: **"the words are the hard part"** — the message/idea, which requires thinking, matters far more than production.
- As of March 2026, most people avoid the words/thinking precisely because it is hard, and default to fancy editing as the easier substitute.
