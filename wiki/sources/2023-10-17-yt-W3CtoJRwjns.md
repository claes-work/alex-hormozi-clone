# What If You Were Alex Hormozi's Friend?

## Summary
In this short clip Alex Hormozi recounts a candid conversation with his friend Trevor, who told him he is "hard to be friends with" and broke down why. Alex uses the exchange to reflect on how, earlier in his career, he was a "dealer of punishment" whose team avoided him, and how — even as that has softened — he now reads as "threatening" because of the power, physical presence, and mental edge he holds in an organization. He argues this distortion makes him a poor judge of people's intent (people don't act like themselves around him), praises Leila for reading people better, and lays out a reward-based ("Skittle") model of positive reinforcement over punishment for giving feedback and building durable behavior.

## Key claims (attributed to Alex unless noted)
- Alex's friend Trevor told him he is hard to be friends with: Alex habitually asks "what's new / what's going on," and when others have nothing new, he shares his own new things, which can be perceived as self-absorbed when he just wants to talk about anything new or interesting. (Trevor's diagnosis, relayed by Alex.)
- The pattern is self-reinforcing: Alex doesn't want to talk to people who have nothing new going on, and if someone taking no action gets offended by him, he "doesn't lose much" — so he has continued the behavior because it lets him keep doing what he wants.
- Trevor told Alex he "learned business so that we can be friends" — learning the topics Alex wanted to talk about so they could talk — which Alex found "incredibly endearing."
- Early in his career, people (including his own team) avoided him because he was a "dealer of punishment": he would just tell people what they were doing wrong, and all they heard was "you suck," even though he didn't intend it that way and simply didn't know as much back then.
- Trevor clarified that Alex is less "punishing" now but is "threatening" — because of the space he occupies in the organization (he holds people's livelihood in his hand), his physical presence, and the mental element (he can do serious damage if he wants). Even the risk of punishment changes people's behavior around him. (Trevor's framing, relayed by Alex.)
- Because people don't act like themselves around him, Alex says he is actually not good at judging people's intent — many of his decisions about people have been wrong, which has been humbling.
- Leila has "never been fooled" because people keep their guard up around him but let it down around her, so she sees more of how people really behave.
- People often associate bad news with the employee who delivers it and punish the messenger for the bad feeling, when they should instead thank the person for being accurate — Alex frames delivering accurate bad news as something to reward.
- Reward model ("Skittle"): reinforce coming to you and being accurate by rewarding it — good news, accurate facts, and improvement each earn a metaphorical "Skittle"; this keeps people coming to you and reinforces the desired behavior.
- To give negative feedback, the easiest method (which he's seen Leila use) is to ask "how do you think that went?" or "watch that call and tell me what you think happened" — most people are hard enough on themselves to name the issue, then you affirm the observation ("awesome observation, great job seeing that"), and next time they fix it.
- Reward-based development requires more patience and skill and is harder and slower; punishment is much easier and faster.
- Reading analogy: someone who reads 600 words/min but burns out after two books reads less over a year than someone who reads 100 words/min consistently for a year. With enough reward the slow reader can eventually exceed 600 wpm (even starting at 50) — it just takes longer.
