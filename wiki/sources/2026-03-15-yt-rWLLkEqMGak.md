# The Perfect Offer for a Home Service Business

## Summary
Live coaching clip in which Alex builds an offer for a home-service business (the example is an artificial-turf installer, with landscaping/edging references). His premise: home services are commoditized — high demand, lots of supply, low skill barrier — so bad players underprice and it becomes a race to the bottom. His fix is a two-part offer that lets you *raise* price and still win bids. **Part 1 — the "on-time, on-budget guarantee."** Bump price by ~20%, then guarantee the job finishes by a stated date and under a stated number; if not, you refund your margin (he assumes ~20% margins → give the 20% back). Competitors quoting lower never guarantee time and budget ("with me… it will be done by this time and under this number, period"), so the guarantee wins every bid. Two protections make it safe: a **"no-change contingency"** (the guarantee is void the moment the customer changes scope — which they almost always do — so you still win the bid but rarely have to honor it), and the fact that even if you do go over on an unchanged job, you simply return the 20% price raise and keep your original planned profit — covering you on both sides. **Part 2 — the maintenance plan monetized via reviews.** Price a cheap annual maintenance/repair plan (e.g., $200–$500/year covering up to ~2x that in repairs, "we make up the prices on repairs anyway") pitched low so the customer buys a "sales consult" for next year and to double the product's lifespan / protect home value. His favorite twist: offer the plan free in exchange for a **five-star review** ("ownership is running a special… if you leave us a review, it'll be free"). Best delivered by the salesperson/product-manager (not the owner) framing it around the crew — "the guys get a little spiff, it means a lot to them and their families" — which he says "crushes" and "everybody does it." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, home services are commoditized (high demand, high supply, low skill), so underpricing bad players trigger a race to the bottom — the reason to differentiate on guarantee rather than price.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends raising price ~20% and attaching an "on-time, on-budget guarantee": the job is promised done by a set date and under a set price, or you refund your margin (he models ~20% margins → refund 20%).
- As of March 2026, the guarantee is a bidding weapon because competitors never guarantee time and budget ("I can do it for a dollar as long as I don't have to guarantee time and budget"), so it lets you win every bid while charging more.
- As of March 2026, a "no-change contingency" protects the seller: the guarantee is contingent on the customer not changing scope, and customers almost always change scope, so the seller wins the bid but rarely has to pay out.
- As of March 2026, the guarantee covers the seller on both sides: even on an unchanged job that runs over, you only return the 20% price raise and keep your originally planned profit.
- As of March 2026, Alex adds a low-priced annual maintenance/repair plan (example: $200–$500/year covering up to ~2x that in repairs) priced cheap so the customer effectively re-buys a sales consult next year — he applies this to "every service business I can."
- As of March 2026, the maintenance pitch leans on doubling the product's lifespan and protecting/adding home value ("if we do this, it'll add to your home value rather than being something you have to replace later").
- As of March 2026, his favorite tactic is comping the maintenance plan in exchange for a five-star review — framed as a limited-time special from "ownership."
- As of March 2026, the review ask converts best when delivered by the salesperson (not the owner) and framed around the crew getting "a little spiff" that "means a lot to them and their families" — Alex says this "crushes" and "everybody does it."
