## Key claims (2026-02-25)

_Paraphrased. Attribution flagged: **[Alex]** = confidently Alex; **[uncertain]** = could be Alex or the guest._

- **[uncertain]** The "quality of your hater" is a useful signal — trash/low-status haters mean you're doing well; high-quality haters are rare and only appear once you're actually in the game taking action.
- **[uncertain]** Restrict feedback to a trusted "council" / trusted "generals," not the crowd — an anonymous "kid on TikTok" getting 27,000 likes on a comment is just "27,000 more of him," worthless next to the opinion of a proven mentor (a figure said to have made "$1.2 billion in actual liquid cash" last year). Contrasts with a younger, different-generation figure who "was big on crowdsourcing feedback."
- **[uncertain]** "There's no such thing as a successful hater" — as soon as someone gets in the game and takes action, they stop hating; most hate is people projecting their own insecurities.
- **[uncertain]** Once you have credibility, money, and your own work to focus on, you naturally stop caring about hate.
- **[uncertain]** Empathy reframe: it's rational that people who hold an opposing worldview would find you offensive — "I would find me offensive if I shared your worldview" — which makes the hate a self-cancelling, expected thing.
- **[likely Alex]** Humility reframe via religious figures: Jesus and most revered religious figures met gruesome ends, and "they're all better people than I am," so it's arrogant to think oneself above getting hate — offered as a practice for the next time you get "a bunch of hate poured on you."
- **[Alex]** Chronic haters / hostile comment sections "don't see reality accurately" — they live in "a distorted view of the world"; Alex says he has had to cut ties with "fully delusional" people in real life because they simply don't perceive shared events the same way.
- **[Alex]** Proof philosophy: credibility comes from demonstrable results, not claims — "how do you know my stuff works? Well, the book about monetization made 100 million in a weekend." He notes he used ~12 of the ~15 strategies in the book to market the book itself.
- **[Alex]** Stated personal mission: reach **verified / liquid** billionaire status ("verified blue check billion dollar status"), because current wealth is "paper money"; he wants it unambiguously "very, very clear" before moving on to other projects (e.g., a suggested self-help / confidence book after the money-model books).
- **[uncertain]** Non-reciprocation: even toward people he disagrees with, the healthy end state is "we good" — "that's where I don't become a hater. I just go, we good."
