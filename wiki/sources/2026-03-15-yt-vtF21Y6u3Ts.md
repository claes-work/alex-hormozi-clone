# I Sell Live on TikTok Shop. How Do I Get to $38M per Year?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a live-selling business on Whatnot (also framed as TikTok Shop) selling designer handbags and sunglasses, doing ~$6M/year with a following of ~110–150k. She wants to reach $38M/year (chosen as roughly "$100,000 a day"), and names **recruiting** as her only bottleneck: she and three salespeople only go live ~5 hours a day "because we get tired," and she also self-runs her own logistics/3PL and packing. Alex diagnoses the business as fundamentally a **distribution-and-sales / talent-media business**, not a logistics one, and works several levers. (1) **Outsource the commoditized parts**: shipping is largely commoditized unless it's a differentiator (his Amazon-shipping counter-example), so a warehouse behind you "is not special" — hire people with a pulse and a good leader and stop letting it consume founder attention. (2) The real constraint to $100k/day is simply **more on-camera sellers** streaming more hours to the same follower base. (3) **Buy vs. build talent**: buying (e.g. recruiting Amazon-affiliate micro-influencers who already live-stream and sell, paid a percentage of profit, under a contract to sell only for her) is faster and lower-risk since it's already proven; building means "kissing a lot more toads" to find soft skills, then training the small skill deficiency. (4) His signature **sales-training-as-concrete-behavior** teaching: never use "amorphous words" like "more energy" or "charisma" — those are labels observers apply *after* the fact; instead instruct in visible, copyable behaviors ("raise your voice," "talk faster," "shoulders back"). Vague language is why most companies can't train presenters — it's a "telephone game where no one agreed on what the behaviors are." Concrete behavior compresses training time and lets you build a "stable" of trained sellers running for you. (5) **Finance**: she needs a real finance/forecasting hire (not just an accounter buying more inventory whenever cash is in the bank) to avoid the cash crunches she's feeling despite being profitable (~$5–7k profit/show). (6) On her star seller (a former house cleaner who "became me"): keep her **selling, not managing** — put her in front of the camera and out of the building. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a live-selling handbag/sunglasses business as a distribution-and-sales / talent-media business — "the value's in the distribution and the sales," not in running a warehouse.
- As of March 2026, shipping/logistics is "pretty commoditized" and should be outsourced unless it's a genuine differentiator (his counter-example: Amazon shipping became a competitive advantage); running a warehouse alongside a marketing/sales/talent business is "not where the value is."
- As of March 2026, a warehouse behind you "is not special" and "not a core differentiator" — staff it with people who "have a pulse" and a good leader, and let them handle it.
- As of March 2026, the direct path to more revenue for this client (toward ~$100k/day) is simply more on-camera sellers streaming more hours to the same existing follower base (~110–150k).
- As of March 2026, the buy-vs-build talent choice: buying is faster and more expensive, building is cheaper, longer, and harder — and for on-camera sellers he'd lean buy, recruiting proven Amazon-affiliate micro-influencers who already live-stream and sell.
- As of March 2026, pay on-camera sellers a percentage of profit (like a salesperson), and lock buy-side talent into a contract to sell only for you for a set period (lower risk because the skill is already proven).
- As of March 2026, sales/presentation training must be given as concrete, visible behavior — "raise your voice," "talk faster," "shoulders back" — never amorphous words like "high energy" or "charisma," which are labels observers apply after seeing the behavior.
- As of March 2026, most companies can't teach people to sell/present because they "do not use words that other people understand" — vague instruction becomes a "telephone game" where no one agreed on the behaviors; concrete behavior compresses training time.
- As of March 2026, a live-selling business needs a strong finance/forecasting person (not just an accountant) so inventory buying is governed by growth-rate-based forecasting rather than "how much cash is in the bank," which causes cash crunches even when profitable.
- As of March 2026, a star seller who can sell should be kept selling on camera and out of management — "put her in front of the camera, have her sell, and get her out of the building… Don't have her manage people."
