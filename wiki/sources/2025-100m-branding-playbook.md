# $100M Branding Playbook (2025)

**What it is.** A short Acquisition.com playbook by Alex Hormozi, subtitled "How to Make Anyone or Anything Famous on Purpose," defining branding as the deliberate pairing of your business with things your ideal audience likes, and laying out a four-step method to start, grow, and pivot a brand for profit (`PDF p. 1`, `PDF p. 7`). Roughly 33 numbered content pages plus front matter and a one-page checklist; raw extraction at `raw/books/2025-100m-branding-playbook.md` (`PDF p. 3`, `PDF p. 36`). This is a primary source for `wiki/topics/content-strategy/branding.md`.

## Thesis

Branding is not a "gut feeling" or logos-and-colors exercise but a concrete, teachable, money-making practice: it is pairing two or more things together so that a majority of your ideal audience associates your business with things they like. Good branding lowers customer acquisition cost, raises lifetime value, and lowers risk (repeat purchase) — "the only three things that matter in growing a business." It has higher returns than direct response over a long horizon, which is why few people do it (`PDF p. 6`, `PDF p. 9`, `PDF p. 33`).

## Structure and content

- **Opening story / "Big Brand"** (`PDF p. 4`–`p. 5`): supplement-company anecdote — his superior, higher-commission product lost to a competitor's "cool factor"; he had built product but no brand.
- **Branding (why it makes money)** (`PDF p. 6`–`p. 7`): the three value levers (more customers, worth more, keep happening); CAC/LTV/risk examples (The Rock, Starbucks, Mac).
- **What Branding Is NOT** (`PDF p. 8`–`p. 9`): rejects marketers' "gut feeling" definitions; "Learning = same condition, new behavior"; "Branding As I See It" = pairing things.
- **Branding vs. advertising, measuring brand** (`PDF p. 10`–`p. 13`): cattle-brand origin; reach vs. influence vs. direction; eight brand positions; polarization notes.
- **Case Study: Kendall Jenner x Pepsi** (`PDF p. 14`–`p. 16`): good advertising, bad branding; "any publicity" debate.
- **Translating Brand Into Sales** (`PDF p. 17`–`p. 21`): transfer reach/influence into product; nine-step logo-to-money process; branded-vs-unbranded profit table.
- **How to Start, Grow, then Pivot** (`PDF p. 22`–`p. 32`): the four steps; ideal-customer criteria; Levels of Authority cycle; the Bouquet metaphor; brand pivots and mistakes; "Final Step: Patience."
- **One Page Branding Checklist** (`PDF p. 36`).

## Key claims

- Branding is defined as "pairing two or more things together"; good branding pairs your business with something a majority of your ideal audience likes, bad branding with something they dislike, and no branding leaves you "practically invisible" (`PDF p. 9`).
- The three (and only three) ways to grow a business are getting more customers, making them worth more, and making it keep happening; branding is his single lowest-cost, longest-horizon way to do all three (`PDF p. 6`).
- Advertising and branding are distinct: advertising is letting people know about your stuff (measured by reach); branding is what you let them know — the pairing (`PDF p. 10`, `PDF p. 11`).
- Brand is measured with two variables, influence (chance it changes behavior) and direction (toward or away), plus reach from advertising — yielding eight brand positions one person can occupy depending on the audience (`PDF p. 12`, `PDF p. 13`).
- He reframes "there's no such thing as bad publicity" as "Good beats bad. Bad beats none. Lots of good beats everything" — any publicity beats none, but good beats bad (`PDF p. 15`).
- Strong brands need not be polarizing; deliberately trying to polarize interferes with business — just focus on getting people to buy (`PDF p. 16`).
- A strong brand turns a commoditized product (a $10 hat) into a premium product buyers pay to associate themselves with; you transfer the reach/influence of what you pair into the product (`PDF p. 18`).
- Illustrative economics (`PDF p. 20`): a branded thing at $5,500 vs. unbranded at $2,000 (same $500 cost of goods) yields ~4.5x the sales and ~11x the return on the same $2,000 ad spend (45-to-1 vs. 4-to-1).
- The four-step method: (1) figure out who your ideal customer is, (2) figure out what they like, (3) associate your stuff with those things, (4) optimize associations for max profit (`PDF p. 22`).
- Ideal-audience criteria: growing, has enough money, easy to target, and in pain (`PDF p. 23`).
- Levels of Authority (weakest to strongest): what we say (content/ads/outreach), what other people say (affiliates/customers), and what they experience themselves — the last has the most authority (`PDF p. 26`).
- Advertising drives brand more in the short term; the product drives brand more in the long term, because people have more impactful experiences with the product than with ads (`PDF p. 27`).
- Market expansion has five directions — up market, down market, adjacent, broader, narrower — each requiring you to associate with what the new avatar likes (`PDF p. 28`).
- Brand claims for his own results: a 10.1-million-person audience across platforms in ~4 years, over a million copies sold of his last two books, and deals worth hundreds of millions in his holding company Acquisition.com (`PDF p. 7`).
