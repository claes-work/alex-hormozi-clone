# Helping an Auto Repair Shop Hire Better Talent

## Summary
In a live Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises an auto-hail-repair-shop owner (doing $5.5M across four
locations, targeting $15M) whose main constraint is leadership/people. Hormozi reframes the
hire from "C-suite/COO" to a VP/director-level operator, walks through sourcing (LinkedIn
outreach and Indeed ads, targeting people who held the same role at similarly sized auto
shops), and lays out how to design compensation via reverse-engineered on-target earnings,
profit-share pools, and phantom-equity for exit upside. The caller's business figures
(revenue, per-store profit) are self-reported and unverified.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that at ~$5M revenue an owner is usually mistitling the hire: they need a VP or director of operations (or GM), not a true C-suite executive; correct titling helps set comp expectations.
- As of March 2026, Alex identifies the owner's core need as an operator / "right hand" — the owner is the visionary and everything currently routes back to them operationally.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the two most fruitful talent sources at this level are LinkedIn targeted outreach and simple Indeed ads (ads yield lower-quality candidates); this hiring level is the transition point between running ads and doing targeted outreach.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends targeting people who held the same operator role at similarly sized auto shops, reaching out to ~500 candidates, and offering a relocation package (like a signing bonus) if the local talent pool is thin.
- As of March 2026, Alex says to hire a leader people admire and look up to — not a "processes and systems" person who dislikes dealing with people — because a strong operator works "two levels above" (fixing people, not cars) and can run almost any business.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames compensation as a "reverse sale": the offer is proportional to the leverage/experience the candidate has; someone who has scaled 5-to-50 twice can justify more.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends a profit-share pool of roughly 10-20% (he favors ~10-15%) distributed among leaders; the percentage stays fixed while the pie grows, so leaders think like owners and accept diluting their share to bring in more leaders.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes equity as bundling four things — cash flow, sale (exit), risk, and control — and notes most people who say they want equity actually want only cash flow and exit upside, not risk or control.
- As of March 2026, Alex's preferred structure gives cash flow via a profit-share pool plus phantom profits interest (e.g., 4% vesting 1%/year) that is forfeited if they leave and can be accelerated on a sale, conditioned on being present at sale and tenured 12-24 months; the sale bonus is taxed as ordinary income like an option.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he has never seen exceptional private multi-location expansion without an on-the-ground operator in each store who has no tie to the bottom line, and that giving such operators profit share enables true decentralization and expansion.
- As of March 2026, Alex distinguishes two comp structures: for fast-growth, non-geographically-constrained businesses, pay a high percentage (e.g., 30%) of year-over-year growth above a rising watermark; for geographically constrained local businesses, use a profit-share percentage instead, because growth is hard and growth-based pay discourages people.
- As of March 2026, Alex's method: reverse-engineer on-target earnings (OTE) — decide what the right caliber of person should earn (bad = fired, mediocre = on-target, crushing it = a set high number), then back into the percentage; the specific percentage doesn't matter.
- The caller reports (unverified) ~40% margins, four locations with one store generating ~80% of revenue due to storm activity, and roughly $200,000/month in total profit (~$50,000/month per store).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "A really good operator can just come into just about any business and run it. Because they're usually two levels above — like they're not fixing cars, they're fixing people, and that's the same."

> "It's kind of like a reverse sale. So the offer that you're going to be giving is going to be proportional to the leverage you or they have over the role."

> "As the business grows, that 10 or 20% — that percentage stays the same, but the pie increases. And so they think like owners like you do."

> "Most people say they want equity, but you're like, you want risk? And they're like, no, no, I don't want risk. And you're like, okay, do you want control? Because I'm not going to give it to you."

> "I have yet to see someone do an exceptional job expanding without having the equivalent of an operator boots on the ground in each store that has no tie to the bottom line."

> "I don't like — the percentage doesn't matter. I just want to reverse engineer how much money do I think this person should make. If they're bad, they'll get fired. If they're mediocre, they make on target. And if they crush it, I want them to make this much money."
