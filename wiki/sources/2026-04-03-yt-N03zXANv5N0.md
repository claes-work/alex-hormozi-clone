# Helping an $850K Solar Business Scale

## Summary

Alex tells a California solar marketing company that it cannot meaningfully
differentiate the homeowner offer because it does not control the underlying product.
Its leverage is recruiting, onboarding, training, analytics, and lead generation for
salespeople. If the long-term goal is enterprise value, he suggests moving the team's
lead-generation and sales skills into a more controllable vehicle such as HVAC.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says a reseller without product control can mainly change terms,
  which creates a zero-sum transfer between margin and customer value.
- Reported 50% margins suggest commoditization is not the immediate growth constraint;
  all company economics are owner-reported and unverified.
- He defines the actual solar business here as recruiting, training, and managing sales
  capacity, with overrides on representatives' commissions.
- Competitive advantage should target salespeople through better training, leads, data,
  analytics, and ease of selling.
- Alex self-reports divesting from a solar company because of the same lack-of-control
  issue; no deal detail is verified here.
- If the goal is durable enterprise value, he recommends recombining the team's acquisition
  skill with a business model that controls delivery and retention.
- HVAC is his illustrative alternative because demand is inbound and repeat service can
  improve retention, though technician supply becomes the constraint.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The business that you're really in is the recruiting, hiring, training of salespeople business."

> "Your competitive advantage is not on the customer side but on the salesman side."

> "Recombine it into a business model that actually has enterprise value."
