# The Framework I Use to Never Regret a Decision

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a sports-facility owner who is considering reviving a Las Vegas professional soccer team after a prior failed attempt. Alex separates the business question from the life question: expanding the current facility model is the obvious business move, while reviving the team is a high-risk personal swing. Alex recommends regret-minimization from the perspective of the founder's 85-year-old self, with special attention to the real worst case and the opportunity cost of splitting focus.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - sports-facility owner and former soccer-team operator. His immigration, soccer, business, revenue, and prior-team history are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The coachee is asking a life question, not just a business question.
- From a business perspective, Alex says the clearer move is to expand the facility model that already works.
- Bringing back the sports team is a very high-risk, high-reward play with no universally right answer.
- Some entrepreneurial questions, such as the right amount of risk or how long to push before pivoting, are personal and cannot be answered by formula.
- Alex uses regret minimization: imagine the 85-year-old self asking whether failing and damaging the existing business would still be worth taking the shot.
- The relevant downside is not only financial capital; using other people's money does not remove the opportunity cost, time cost, and focus cost.
- If the founder chooses the team, Alex would rather see him go all in than split focus and hurt both ventures.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You don't have a business question, you have a life question."

> "How much risk is the right amount of risk is a you question."

> "I like the regret minimization framework."

> "That's not the risk I'm talking about. The risk is that you already have something that's good and you're going to probably lose it."

> "If you were to do it, I would say go all in if you really want to make it work."
