# Serial CEO Sells 4 Companies Using This Ancient Business Secret

## Summary
In this early solo video, Hormozi unpacks the maxim "don't be cute," which he says circulates constantly in his community. He credits it to a "mercenary"/serial CEO he knows — a private-equity fixer dropped into acquired companies to grow and exit them, who had done that exit process four times — who explained it with a backyard-football analogy: fancy trick plays fumble, while putting "the two fat guys in the middle" and running right yields consistent yardage. Hormozi generalizes this to a rule that fundamentals and repeating successful actions beat trying to be clever, and that entrepreneurs "need to be reminded more than we need to be taught." He argues you should replicate a proven system exactly until you match or beat the person who taught it before iterating, and closes with landing-page optimization examples about the outsized leverage of "above the fold" headlines.

## Key claims
- The "ancient business secret" is the maxim **"don't be cute"** — do the boring fundamentals and repeat successful actions rather than trying to get fancy/clever. (2020-12-15)
- Source of the saying: a serial "mercenary" CEO (a private-equity fixer) Hormozi knows personally, who had grown and exited companies four times; the "4 companies" in the title are that CEO's, not Alex's own exits. (2020-12-15)
- The analogy: fancy backyard-football trick plays lose yardage and fumble; running two big linemen up the middle to the right produces consistent yardage — i.e., fundamentals win. (2020-12-15)
- A telltale sign of a newer entrepreneur is failing to repeat successful actions — both one's own that worked, and the prescribed actions of a paid system/advisor. (2020-12-15)
- Rule of thumb: until you are doing as well as or better than the person who taught you a system, run the system exactly as outlined; only iterate after you've duplicated their results. (2020-12-15)
- Not taking advice you paid for is "akin to burning your money," because the teacher has already absorbed the trial-and-error cost. (2020-12-15)
- Optimization leverage sits "above the fold": the first one or two things a visitor reads swing the needle most; he has seen 2x-4x throughput changes from just the top headline/image. (2020-12-15)
- Concrete results cited: 3x throughput on an agency partner landing page by changing the headline; 5x on a scheduling (step-3) page by *removing* a "you finished this process" headline; often deleting everything below the fold outperformed adding content. (2020-12-15)
- Once a conversion process is optimized he stops tweaking it (treats it as a successful action to repeat) and redirects attention to filling the top of funnel with traffic. (2020-12-15)
