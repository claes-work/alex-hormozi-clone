# How to Win with AI in 2026

## Summary
Live room Q&A. Alex says AI "probably occupies half of my time right now in terms of what I'm thinking about within the business." His central thesis: the big belief shift everyone must make is **from roles-based growth to workflow-based automation**. Instead of "I need to hire an editor," you decompose what that editor actually does into its constituent actions and assign each to a trained agent. Every business, he argues, can be connected at the most granular level — action to action to action, "120 actions from inbound lead to turf" — and drawing your entire business as one linear workflow is the exercise most people won't do, "which is why they will lose." He walks his own content machine as the example: idea (search the Skool community / trend databases for outperformers) → match against competency → scripting (templated by AI) → packaging (headlines, thumbnails — an AI workflow) → production, all engineered to produce one thing: a click. He details the **Hormozi Hotline** pipeline his dev team (working with Julian at Nomadic) is building: transcript breakdown → speaker segmentation (AB→AC→AD as new callers join) → clip individual conversations → find the highest-tension moment and open on it → strip ums/ahs → keep only the data needed for the insight, collapse the rest → export. The recurring lesson: you must train a machine like you train a human — you can't hire someone, tell them nothing, and expect good work, yet people expect exactly that of AI. His edge is being "black and white with observable behavior": vague language ("be more charismatic," "that's off-brand") is useless to machines *and* humans; you must translate it into describable actions ("raise your voice here, talk faster, nod when they talk"). He predicts agencies — historically "notoriously shitty" businesses — become highly lucrative for AI-operators because demand for customers is infinite and only the ops are hard. On hiring, he calls an AI-automation person a high-leverage hire (~$150k/yr for a "hacker" who duct-tapes tools together) who "will not lack for work." On practical priorities for the room: automate back-office now (invoicing, receivables, lead nurture — "that's now"); text-based AI "is there," voice "very close" (supposed to be the year of voice). Longer horizon: in 12–24 months **UX disappears** — no more clicking; you just talk/text an agent ("what did we do in cash flow last month?") and it answers, and "the world of CRMs" changes. On physical work he expects generalized humanoid robots (Optimus) to win over special-purpose machines because "the world is already built for us" — door handles, the human body as "the skeleton key for the world." He's "not an AI alarmist": adoption takes a while (law firms still use fax machines), but "humans plus technology will beat humans with inferior technology."

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says AI occupies roughly half of his thinking about the business right now.
- As of March 2026, the core belief shift is from **roles-based growth to workflow-based automation**: instead of hiring an editor, decompose the editor's 4–5 (or ~16) actual activities and assign each to a trained agent.
- As of March 2026, every business can be mapped at the most granular level as a linear chain of actions (e.g. "120 actions from inbound lead to turf"); if you can't draw your business as a workflow, you don't really have one.
- As of March 2026, the granular decomposition is the thinking "most people won't do, which is why they will lose" — the mistake is saying "AI can't do what a human can" instead of training it the way you'd train a human.
- As of March 2026, his content machine runs as a workflow: idea (search Skool community / trend databases for outperformers) → match to competency → AI scripting (templated) → AI packaging (headlines, thumbnails) → production — all to produce one click.
- As of March 2026, the Hormozi Hotline pipeline (built by his dev team with Julian at Nomadic) automates: transcript breakdown → speaker segmentation → clip each conversation → open on the highest-tension moment → remove ums/ahs → keep only the data needed for the insight → export; you improve it by continually updating the prompt, "just like updating the training for a human."
- As of March 2026, a machine can only act on describable, black-and-white, observable behavior; vague instructions ("be more charismatic," "that's off-brand") mean nothing to machines — and, he adds, to humans, who just nod. A key future skill is translating vague judgments into describable actions.
- As of March 2026, agencies — historically "notoriously shitty" businesses — will become highly lucrative for people great with AI, because demand for customers is infinite (no product-market-fit question) and only the ops of acquiring customers are hard; operationalizing each flow fixes that.
- As of March 2026, an AI-automation hire is high-leverage: they sit next to teams, watch, and automate task after task, and "will not lack for work." Worth doing now "if you have the cash flow." A capable "hacker" who duct-tapes tools together runs ~$150k/year.
- As of March 2026, for home services / brick-and-mortar, all administrative back-office work (invoicing, matching billables, receivables, lead nurture) should already be automated — "that's now"; text AI "is there," voice "very close."
- As of March 2026, within 12–24 months UX largely disappears: instead of clicking, you talk or text an agent and it answers directly (e.g. "what did we do in cash flow last month?"), reshaping CRMs.
- As of March 2026, generalized humanoid robots (e.g. Optimus) will proliferate more than special-purpose machines because the world is already physically built for the human body — "the skeleton key for the world is just the way the human body works" (door handles, buildings); special-purpose robots (e.g. robotic lawn mowers) haven't taken off because cost/uniqueness don't yet make sense.
- As of March 2026, Alex is "not an AI alarmist": adoption is slow (law firms still use fax machines) but "humans plus technology will beat humans with inferior technology" — non-adopters can still make money; adopters make more.
