# Niche Content: The Secret to Big Earnings!

## Summary

In this solo monologue Alex Hormozi tells the story of a registered dietitian he recently met over dinner who takes home just under $1M/year in profit from an unverified Instagram account with fewer than 6,000 followers, ~19 likes and one comment per post, and a 30-day reach of only ~2,800 accounts. Her niche is teaching other registered dietitians how to bill insurance codes correctly. He uses her case to argue that hyper-niche content, tightly matched to what you sell, monetizes far better than broad "vanity-metric" content, and confesses he learned this lesson twice himself when he broadened his own content and watched sales fall while views rose.

## Key claims (Alex's monologue only)

- A tiny, die-hard audience can produce enormous income: the dietitian nets just under **$1M/year in profit** (not revenue) from **<6,000 Instagram followers**, ~19 likes/post, and ~2,800 monthly reach.
- Her funnel is extreme-niche and single-channel: **100% of customer acquisition comes from organic Instagram**, and **100% of her posts** are about billing insurance as a registered dietitian.
- Her offer mix: ~**10+ low-ticket sales/day** (cheat sheets of billing codes) plus ~**3 higher-ticket sales/week** at roughly **$4,000–5,000**.
- Consistency matters more than polish: she posts **one post and one story every day**, and got her **first sale with only 8 people on her email list**; her list is now ~2,900 and still converts.
- Behind low view/like counts are real people: a clip with 5 views, an email list of 300, "19 likes" — each represents a real person watching or listening.
- The platform algorithm serves niche audiences their niche: Alex's own Instagram feed is only philosophy, comedy, and gym equipment, and he's shown tiny 500–2,000-follower niche accounts because that's what he engages with.
- **Niche content makes far more money if it matches what you're selling**; broad content grows reach but dilutes buyer relevance.
- Alex learned this the hard way twice. First: when he switched his podcast from gyms to general business (~18 months before selling Gym Launch), **organic sales fell to basically zero** and it took ~6 months to diagnose that gym owners cared less about generic business content.
- Relevance beats reach for monetization: a dentist-only podcast gets fewer listens but sky-high relevancy per listener; general business content gets more views but weaker buyer intent.
- Second time (3–6 months prior to recording): experimenting with **wider content** raised views and vanity metrics but **lowered the metrics that mattered** — YouTube CPMs, opt-ins at acquisition.com, and book sales all went **down** despite views going up.
- Core takeaway: you can make a huge income from a small die-hard audience; audience size is not the constraint, relevance is.
