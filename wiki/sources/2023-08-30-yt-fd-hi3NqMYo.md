# I Gained 5,000,000+ Followers with this Secret Method

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi explains the "secret method" behind growing his
audience by 5M+ followers in under 24 months (starting from under 10,000 followers
with, by his account, no prior knowledge of YouTube, TikTok, Instagram or Twitter):
hiring **advertising/content agencies** in a deliberate two-phase sequence to buy
skill fast, then transferring that expertise in-house. He frames the choice as time
vs. money, walks through how to structure the engagement so the agency knowingly
trains your team toward a hard deadline, and warns against the "agency trap" of
open-ended dependency. He says he ran the same playbook across YouTube, TikTok,
LinkedIn, podcasts and paid ads.

## Key claims (the content/follower method)

- **Time vs. money framing.** Entering any new skill/platform means overcoming a
  "massive skill deficiency" fast. You can deploy time (learn it yourself) or deploy
  money (pay to shortcut). If you have no money, you learn it and it takes time; once
  one platform yields cash flow, use that to "pay down your ignorance debt" faster on
  the next.
- **Why agencies over celebrities or gurus.** He tried celebrity creators (strategies
  too personalized/tailored to them) and dismissed coaches/gurus (often teaching
  tactics that worked five years ago, e.g. "do hashtags on YouTube videos"). He chose
  agencies — a business that sells advertising/content services on your behalf.
- **Agency tiers / pricing (his figures).** A basic agency for one brand on one
  platform ≈ $3,000–$5,000/month; advanced agencies working with top creators ≈
  $15,000–$30,000/month.
- **Phase 1 — basic agency to install fundamentals.** For YouTube he first hired a
  $3–5k/month agency whose main job was to force a regular cadence: commit to three
  videos a week, "trimmed" (cut the ums/ahs, add an intro and a thumbnail). Objective:
  get the basics down and build the habit over ~3–6 months while learning what the
  audience likes.
- **Phase 2 — premium agency to learn the nuance, on your terms.** Rather than accept
  open-ended terms, he proposes a fixed ~6-month engagement, pays extra for the agency
  to explain *why* they make each decision, documents it into internal processes,
  trains his team, then downgrades to a lower-cost consulting agreement as insurance.
- **The exact script (paraphrase).** "I want to do what you do in my business but I
  don't know how. I'd like to work with you for six months so I can learn how you do
  it, plus I'll pay extra for you to break down why you make the decisions you do…
  then I'll train my team… and move to a lower-cost consulting agreement. Are you
  opposed to this?" — agencies accept because it starts with clear intentions and a
  more profitable back-end consulting engagement.
- **Why in-house beats the agency long term.** Once the expertise gap is closed, your
  team wins because they have (1) more time (fully allocated to you, not one client of
  many) and (2) more context on your brand. "All we have to fill is the gap of
  expertise."
- **Hard deadline discipline.** Set a clear transition date; frame it in LTV terms
  (e.g. $6k/month = $36k committed). The countdown ("we're one month down, we've got
  to understand this in five") drives the team to absorb the skill faster. If the team
  isn't as good as the agency by the deadline, either change the people or the agency
  isn't teaching everything.
- **Co-creation → decouple.** Both make content in parallel until your in-house output
  matches then beats the agency's; then downgrade to consulting; if the consulting
  calls stop teaching you anything, cut it amicably (expectation was set up front).
- **TikTok variation.** An agency reached out offering to repurpose his existing
  content into shorts at near-zero effort from him; it gained traction. Because his
  director of brand already had deep short-form history (had worked at a premium
  agency), he skipped the usual phase-2 premium agency, "bought the SOPs" and scaled
  short-form output massively within ~6 months.
- **The agency trap (from $100M Leads).** The repeating cycle: excited about new
  leads → valuable-feeling onboarding → assigned their best senior rep → some results
  → senior rep moved to the newest customer → junior rep takes over → results suffer →
  you complain → senior rep returns briefly → results never recover → cancel → find
  another agency → repeat. He says his first agency was great, the next ~10 were
  terrible.
- **Margin asymmetry.** "You have to make a margin, I don't. I'm going to cover cost
  and get five times more output." In-house can run at cost for 5x the output.
- **Same playbook everywhere.** He applied it to YouTube, TikTok, LinkedIn, podcasts
  and paid ads.
