# 3 Ways To Increase Wealth Through Business Now

> Source: [yt-4XWlHPyAico](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4XWlHPyAico) — @AlexHormozi, 2021-01-07. Single speaker (Alex Hormozi).

## Summary

Alex Hormozi lays out what he calls the three biggest levers for business growth — **market, offer, and persuasion** — and argues they must be evaluated top-down: a bad market cannot be out-powered by a great offer or great persuasion. He recommends diagnosing a business by checking each lever in order and fixing the offer first (the fastest lever to change), then persuasion skills, and only later choosing a market where those skills can be applied.

## Key claims

- (2021) The three biggest levers for business growth, in priority order, are: **1) Market, 2) Offer, 3) Persuasion.** Each is more important than the ones below it. [[../topics/business/scaling]]
- (2021) Evaluate the levers top-down: rate each as a plus, neutral, or negative. As long as a lever isn't a negative, move to the next one down.
- (2021) The market dominates — investors know that a bad entrepreneur in a great market beats a great entrepreneur in a bad market. A shrinking market can't be overcome no matter how good the offer or persuasion (illustrated by his friend Lloyd's ad-software business selling to newspapers, a market shrinking ~25% per year for eight years).
- (2021) An **offer** is what you give a customer in exchange for money: the products/services, the terms, the price, and what they must do to redeem it.
- (2021) The offer is the **easiest lever to change** — you can repackage the value proposition, pricing, and terms to go from a normal offer to an amazing one in roughly half a day to a day, without changing the whole business.
- (2021) He calls exceptional offers **"grand slam offers"** — rare and very good; unlike baseball (capped at four runs), a business grand slam can let you "win for a thousand years" / for life.
- (2021) **Persuasion** (marketing and selling, acquiring customers across multiple channels) is the hardest lever to develop — it takes time, effort, volume, practice, and failure.
- (2021) You can tell you're in a good market without analysis (e.g., selling toilet paper during COVID, or hot dogs outside a stadium as the game lets out); most businesses are in normal markets that neither grow nor shrink much.
- (2021) Recommended sequence for most entrepreneurs: fix the **offer** first, then build **persuasion** skills, and finally — once you can make great offers and persuade at scale — go find a strong **market** to apply those skills to.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "These are the three biggest levers for growth in a business."

> "If you put a great entrepreneur in a bad market or a bad entrepreneur in a great market, the market will usually win."

> "If you're in a horrible market, it doesn't matter how good your offer is. It doesn't matter how good your persuasive abilities are, you will not be able to outpower a bad market."

> "With an offer, you simply can repackage the value proposition, how you charge, and the terms under which you offer your services... overnight you can take a normal business to an extraordinary business by doing this one thing."

> "In baseball, you can only win four runs, no matter how good your offer is. In business, you can win for life."
