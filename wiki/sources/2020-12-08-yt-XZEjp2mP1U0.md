# How to Scale Your Business Fast (2020-12-08)

## Summary

An early (2020) solo talk in which Hormozi walks through what he calls the "pi equation" — a single division equation, `inflow ÷ outflow`, that he presents as the most-used equation in his business life. He shows three applications: projecting a recurring-revenue business's hypothetical maximum customer count (new customers ÷ churn %), computing per-customer lifetime value (avg revenue per month ÷ monthly churn %), and projecting a business's steady-state monthly revenue (new revenue per month ÷ churn %). He closes with a "golden ratio" — referral rate over churn rate — as the condition for net-negative churn and unending growth. This predates *$100M Leads* and his later, more formalized scaling frameworks; several ideas here are proto-versions of concepts he later codifies.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2020)

- **(2020)** The single most-used equation in his business is `inflow ÷ outflow`, which he nicknames the "pi equation" (he says the name has no real meaning — it just looked like a pie when drawn). It yields a "hypothetical max."
- **(2020)** He traces the equation's origin to his gym years: he wanted to project where a recurring-revenue business was heading but lacked good data tracking, and worked out the division formula himself while trying to get better at math.
- **(2020)** Use 1 — max customer count: new customers per month ÷ monthly churn % = the ceiling on customer count. Example: 5 new/month ÷ 0.10 churn = 50 clients steady state. The number can only rise by increasing signup rate or decreasing churn rate.
- **(2020)** Whether a business is growing or shrinking is read off the same equation: if the projected max is above current count you grow toward it, if below (e.g. 2 new/month ÷ 0.10 = 20 vs. 30 now) you shrink toward it.
- **(2020)** Use 2 — lifetime value: average revenue per customer per month ÷ monthly churn % = LTV. Example: $1,000/month ÷ 0.20 churn (a 5-month average lifespan) = $5,000 LTV.
- **(2020)** LTV sets the acquisition-spend ceiling: knowing a customer is worth $5,000, he can justify spending to acquire them; he references wanting roughly a **3-to-1** ratio (spend under ~$1,000–$2,000 to acquire a $5,000 customer) but declines to elaborate.
- **(2020)** Use 3 — revenue steady state: new business revenue signed per month ÷ monthly revenue churn %. Example: $10,000/month new ÷ 0.10 churn = $100,000/month ceiling.
- **(2020)** Growth is front-loaded and decelerating: absent negative churn, each period's net gain shrinks because churn eats a larger absolute amount as the base grows; most businesses realistically cap out around 80–90% of the theoretical max due to volatility.
- **(2020)** The "golden ratio" beats the equation: referral % over churn %. If 10% of customers refer a new customer monthly and 5% leave monthly, the business compounds ("net negative churn") and grows without paid ads because the product is good enough. He calls this "the promised land."
- **(2020)** No one grows forever in practice — at scale you eventually "mess something up," referral % drops or churn % rises, and growth stalls.
- **(2020)** His diagnostic first questions for any struggling business owner: what's your inflow, your churn, and your average price per user — plus current revenue. Those four data points plus this equation tell him whether they're growing/shrinking, how fast, and where they'll level out.
- **(2020)** The three levers to make a business "however big you want": price, churn, and inflow.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "What I want to talk to you about is how to answer the hardest questions in business using one math equation that only has three variables."

> "It is a division equation... inflow divided by outflow."

> "50 is going to be my hypothetical max. So I know that if I'm signing up five people every month and I'm losing 10% of my clients every month, that I will reach a business that has 50 clients on average."

> "If I know that my average person pays me $1,000 a month, and I know that on average 20% of my customers leave every month, then I know that my customers are on average worth $5,000, which is 1,000 divided by 0.2."

> "This is called net negative churn. This is where you have a business that grows faster than it churns on its own without paid advertising because your product is so good. That is where we all want to go. This is the promised land. This is the golden ratio."

> "Unless you have negative churn, which most people do not have in service-based businesses, what's going to happen is that your rate of growth is going to slow."

> "What's your inflow? What's your churn? What's your average price per user? It's the first thing I'm asking. Literally, it's the first three questions I'll ask."

> "Either I have to increase the lifetime value... by either increasing the price or decreasing the churn... or I have to increase my inflow. And simply by understanding those three pieces — piece number one, which is your price, piece number two, which is your churn, piece number three, which is your inflow — then you can understand exactly how to make your business however big you want."

## Topic notes

This is an **early (2020) proto-version** of ideas Hormozi later codifies. Worth flagging for synthesis:

- **LTV formula** (avg monthly revenue ÷ monthly churn) and the **3-to-1 spend guardrail** here foreshadow the LTGP:CAC ratio he formalizes later in *$100M Leads*. In 2020 he frames it as raw revenue LTV, not gross-profit-based LTGP, and only gestures at the ratio ("you would want to be a 3 to 1... I'm not going to get into that"). See [[../topics/business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]].
- **The "hypothetical max" / growth-ceiling** concept — a business stalls where inflow rate equals outflow (churn) rate — is an early, math-first articulation of the **constraint / growth-cap** thinking that later shows up in his scaling and enterprise-value material.
- **The "golden ratio" (referral % ÷ churn %)** as the route to net-negative churn is an early statement of referral-driven organic growth; relates to word-of-mouth/referral channels in [[../topics/business/acquisition-channels]] and to [[../topics/business/retention]]. Note the terminology is idiosyncratic to this video ("pi equation," "golden ratio," "promised land") and does not appear to carry into his later branded frameworks.
