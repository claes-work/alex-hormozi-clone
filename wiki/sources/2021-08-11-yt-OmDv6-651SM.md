# My Simple Voice Tweak That Exploded Our Revenue (2021-08-11)

## Summary

A solo talk on sales tonality: Hormozi argues that reps who fail to close are often saying
the *right words the wrong way*. He isolates tone and cadence/emphasis as the levers,
illustrates how emphasis changes meaning, and gives two fixes — becoming a master salesman
through repetition and studying "game tape" of yourself when hot, and (the faster route)
genuinely believing in what you sell, because "a sale is a transfer of belief." Connects
directly to the five voice levers in [[../topics/business/sales-communication]].

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-08-11)

- Five prerequisites must align before a sale (the "five Ps"): the right **promotion** for the
  right **prospect** at the right **price** for the right **product** following the right
  **process**. This video is about a separate layer — *how* the words are delivered.
- Reps can have a proven script and still not close because they say the right things the
  wrong way. The two delivery components are **tone** and **cadence/emphasis** (where you pause
  and stress words).
- Analogy: handing someone Jerry Seinfeld's comedy script won't make them funny — same words,
  different intonation and pauses, different result. A script that works for the owner may fail
  for a rep for the same reason.
- Emphasis alone changes meaning — he demonstrates by stressing a different word each time in
  "I didn't say he hit his wife" (a device he credits to Jason Fladlien), and by saying his own
  name three different ways.
- Deliberate tonal control communicates importance: slowing down and lowering the voice signals
  "pay attention, this matters"; fast and excited communicates something different even with
  identical words.
- Fix #1 (slower): become a master salesman through repetition and studying **game-tape footage
  of yourself when you're "hot"** (on a closing streak) — the micro-nuances of pauses and
  tonality in those calls are what separate great from average reps. Great salesmen "never get
  cold" because they can recreate success.
- Fix #2 (faster): **believe in what you sell.** This is hard for owners who see every flaw.
  He hacks it with two reframes: (a) you'll never have a perfect *product*, but you can have
  perfect *intentions*; (b) you can't guarantee outcomes, but you can be the prospect's *best
  shot* at success — and honestly believing that lets you speak with conviction.
- Conviction is felt, not stated: prospects "choose to believe you not based on what you say
  but on how you say it." He ties higher close rates on his own team to days when they watched
  clients' success stories and got on the phones convicted about what they sold.

> "I didn't say he hit his wife. I didn't say he hit his wife..." (demonstrating that shifting
> emphasis across the same sentence changes its meaning)

> "When you are hot, those are the videos you should watch. That's the game tape footage that
> you should go over and over and over again."

> "Great salesmen never get cold. They are always on a hot streak because they know how to
> recreate success."

> "I will never have a perfect product... but I can have perfect intentions."

> "You can't say 'I guarantee that you're going to lose weight'... but you can guarantee that
> you will be the best shot that this person has at being successful."

> "A sale is simply a transfer of belief. And if you do not believe, then you cannot transfer
> that belief. And the way that you communicate belief is not in what you say, but how you
> say it."
