# How To Build Large Sales Teams (Starting from 0)

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi walkthrough of the full lifecycle of building and scaling a sales
team: interviewing/hiring, a 14-day onboarding, the sales script structure, sales
management as a training-and-motivation discipline, culture maintenance, and the
communication cadence with reps as they graduate from trainee to full-time. He frames
the whole system as ongoing coaching (review calls, prioritize the 1–2 highest-impact
fixes, drill via role play) rather than passive check-ins, and grounds retention in
competition, standards, and conviction from client results.

## Key claims

- **What to test in the interview**: (1) speed of response — a proxy for work ethic,
  because "you are a lead to them"; (2) intelligence — a rep who "sounds like an idiot"
  gives that impression of your brand; (3) listening / ambiversion — not a motormouth,
  socially aware; (4) coachability — measured via a role play where you give feedback
  and ask them to try again; take feedback without ego + improve on the second try =
  coachable.
- **Prospect knowledge over product knowledge**: product tech is overemphasized,
  prospect knowledge underemphasized. Focus on *who* you're selling to more than *what*
  — "the person" matters more than "the product." Understand the prospect's problems so
  deeply they believe you intrinsically understand them, and they'll buy.
- **14-day onboarding**: new hires spend the onboarding "bathing in good sales" —
  listening to good sales calls all day (record your own if you have none); by ~40 calls
  they have a good idea. Between watchings they study the script.
- **Script = question-based framework** (the CLOSER structure): **C**larify why they're
  there → **L**abel them with the problem → **O**verview past experiences and pains →
  (once enough info: "I think you'd be a good fit, can I tell you about it?" = get
  invited to sell) → **S**ell via short analogies solving 2–3 of their stated problems,
  then ask for the sale → **E**xplain concerns → **R**einforce the decision.
- **Boil the decision to its most basic unit** so the prospect doesn't have to figure
  out what they're deciding. For B2B service: "can we help you make more money than you
  could on your own — yes or no?" A salesperson repeatedly asking "do you have any
  questions for me?" signals they don't know how to close (inviting reasons not to buy
  instead of asking for a decision).
- **Ramp on a half schedule**: after passing role play, put reps on a half schedule so
  they burn through lots of leads; the second half of the day they review their own
  calls, with manager meetings twice a day in week two.
- **Sales management = motivate + train**, not weekly pipeline check-ins. The coaching
  loop per rep: 30 min before the call, listen to 1–2 of their calls at 2x and take
  written notes; get on the call immediately after (fresh); prioritize/bold the 1–2
  highest-impact fixes (~5 min to communicate); spend the remaining ~25 min role playing
  the changes; next day check if they did it, then drill specific objections ("I need to
  think about it," "I have to talk to my spouse," "that's a lot of money").
- **Culture is maintained by three things**: (1) a visual, always-up-to-date
  leaderboard (reps are competitive); (2) clear KPIs + a bonus, with a recurring
  competition every ~4–6 weeks (30–42 days) — the objective that worked best across
  their portfolio companies is optimizing for **cash up front**; (3) cutting the bottom
  10% quarterly — not cutting communicates that it's "okay to suck."
- **Management is a skill of tolerance**: "the person who tolerates the most is the one
  who has the weakest team." A new sales manager sets new standards (e.g. mandatory 5am
  training, on time to every call, clean pipelines daily, call notes in the CRM) and
  enforces them even against the best closer.
- **Best salesperson ≠ best sales manager**: managers tend to be more giving, more
  emotionally stable ("the rock"), and must lead by example (first to show up, finishes
  the checklist).
- **Cadence**: first 14 days = 1–2 touches/day; after graduating (meeting the minimum
  close-rate quota) → full schedule + weekly meetings, moving to every-other-week only
  once a rep is clearly killing it after ~a year. Drop below KPI for two weeks → PIP
  (performance improvement plan) = regress to trainee-level attention; still not
  improved after two more weeks → out. Set this "dangerous place to work / we expect
  excellence" expectation at hire.
- **Meeting stack**: daily morning huddle (training; optional with good culture,
  mandatory for trainees/PIP) + end-of-day huddle (short check-in, clean pipelines) +
  weekly 1-on-1s (~1 hr per rep/week for management) + monthlies. After each 1-on-1,
  message/email the salient points; keep a per-rep tab dated week to week to track the
  1–2 things assigned and whether they improved.
- **Conviction is the failure mode** — sales goes cold when reps lose belief. Keep it
  alive with competitions and with client testimonials/results, ideally by having former
  clients hop on sales calls, watching testimonial videos as a team in the morning
  huddle, or having the rep who sold the client read that client's testimonial aloud
  (doubles down on conviction). Sell from genuine empathy, pushing through the "no"
  because you believe in the transformation — not from ego tied to closing.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "You can test work ethic by how quickly they get back to you because you are a lead to them."
- "If you can explain to somebody exactly the problem that they're going through they will buy whatever you have because they will believe that you so intrinsically understand what they're going through."
- "You want them to be bathing in good sales."
- "You don't want them to try and think about what the decision is, you want to give them the decision that they have to make by the end of the call."
- "It is a lot of work to build a great sales team."
- "If you're not cutting you're communicating with the team that's okay to suck and it will drag down the culture of the team."
- "Sales management is a skill of tolerance — the person who tolerates the most is the one who has the weakest team."
- "No one respects a sales manager who says do all these things and doesn't do it themselves."
- "If it's worth doing it's worth doing well."
- "I have rarely found that the best salesperson is the best sales manager."
- "You want to sell from a place of empathy — genuine empathy, not nice guy, but like I really want to help you, which means you need to make a decision."
