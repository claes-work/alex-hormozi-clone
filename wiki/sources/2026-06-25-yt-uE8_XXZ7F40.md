# Should I Quit My $2.6M Hair Business to Build SaaS?

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises an Australian hair-extension founder who wants to leave a profitable salon/wholesale business to build salon software. Alex pushes back hard. He says the current business is more sellable and recurring than the founder assumes, the software idea is harder and less cash-flow-positive, and already spending money on the build is sunk cost. He recommends confronting the current business: improve margins, get more acquisition, and use the existing stylist base rather than starting a harder game.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - Australian salon and hair-extension wholesale/ecommerce founder. Her revenue, margins, SaaS idea, build cost, and audience context are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Moving from a hard current business into SaaS is not an escape; it is moving into something harder.
- Having a distribution base does not mean the founder should sell every product that base could buy.
- Money already spent on the SaaS build is sunk cost; stopping bad spend can be some of the best money spent.
- Audience-led founders often create multiple mini-businesses for the same customer pool instead of solving the real constraint: audience growth or acquisition.
- The hair-extension business is more sellable than the founder assumes because extensions are reoccurring and can show revenue retention.
- Recurring and reoccurring revenue can have similar value if retention is demonstrable.
- Software is a poor path for someone who wants passive online income; Alex says software may require years of no profit.
- The current business should focus on margins, acquisition, stylist activation, and helping salons make more money with extensions.
