# This Advice is What No One Ever Tells You - Alex Hormozi

## Summary
In this Skool coaching-call segment, Alex Hormozi argues that anyone who needs external accountability will never win — you must be your own witness and take responsibility for your own distractions, which usually means having a few hard conversations and cutting the things (spending, subscriptions, habits) you claim to want less than your goals. He then answers audience questions: he distinguishes education from entertainment by output (education changes behavior; the "finish line" is solving the problem you started with), and he explains how he lost his fear of missing out over ~14 years, reframing FOMO as an illusion that arises because you see other people's wins but never their unseen years of work. His throughline is enterprise/aerous focus: every business "has hair," and no outside opportunity is better than the one you have.

## Key claims
- If you need accountability, you will not win; you must be your own witness and hold yourself accountable, "because no one's going to save you."
- He reframes an Epictetus line — "if you are ever tempted to look for outside approval, realize that you have compromised your integrity; if you need a witness, be your own" — into "if you are ever tempted to look for outside accountability, realize that you have compromised your integrity."
- A coach can get you a result (lose the weight) but is not a substitute for taking responsibility; "at some point you have to take responsibility."
- People are distracted but don't take accountability for the distraction; taking accountability means being willing to suffer the pain of removing what has been distracting you. Most people are "a few hard conversations away from having five times the amount of time."
- Success does not happen fast; he says his Skool ads deliberately avoid promising speed and even tell viewers it "probably won't happen for you" — you won't be the richest in your neighborhood or on the Forbes list, but it can help you get started.
- Skool's cohort had 62% of members make money; they aim to find the fewest skills that let someone win in the shortest time, and to raise that toward 100% over time.
- If you can't afford to wait beyond 14-30 days, you'll never make it; you must be willing to trade off something else in your life that costs you more than this and that you don't need.
- Honesty test for commitment: if you won't cut the nails/shoes/car payment/streaming services, then state plainly "I'd prefer to have shoes than the business of my dreams" — if you're good with that statement, fine; if it isn't true, change reality to match your ideals.
- General/mindless consumption of information is actually entertainment; you should consume content relevant to your constraint and begin any consumption by asking "what problem am I solving," which gives the consumption a finish line (solving the problem).
- The difference between education and entertainment is the output: entertainment's point is to be consumed, education's point is to change behavior — so the same piece of content can educate one person and merely entertain another. A piece isn't inherently one or the other; it's educational if it raises the likelihood that a larger share of the audience changes behavior.
- Learning is a loop: define a problem (e.g. a traffic problem), consume information, execute, observe whether conditions changed; if not, consume again and repeat until conditions change — then start the next micro-loop.
- Litmus test: if you get off the call and change what you do, you learned (net positive); if you change nothing, it was entertainment.
- He no longer has FOMO after ~14 years; it wasn't something he actively worked on — he noticed that when he sees someone crushing it he now thinks "good for them, I'm doing my thing."
- The more you learn about something, the more you realize how much depth it has, and you come to know that the things you feared missing out on have depth you don't understand — so you're not actually missing out.
- His CFO (seasoned across 15-20 businesses, multiple nine-figure exits and a ~$5B exit) told him the lesson across all industries is that "it's all [expletive]" — not negative, just that every business has hair, every business has problems.
- FOMO is the distortion that someone else is getting a better return for the same work; really they're getting an adequate return for work you have no visibility into.
- You only see people once they win, so you get 100x discrepancies between what people think it takes and what it actually takes: "you don't see 10 years of podcast before it pops off; you don't see the 5,000 pieces of content before we had our first viral clip."
- FOMO rests on the "uninformed optimism of the new opportunity" — the belief it has no cost and only upside, which is untrue. "Everything has hair"; he attributes to Bezos "everything has overhead."
- The hardest lesson he's had to learn is enterprise/"aerous" focus — there is no opportunity out there better than the one you have, "because you just need to get better anyways regardless."
