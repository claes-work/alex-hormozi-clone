# The 4 Tiny Habits That Made Me The Most Money (2021-11-24)

## Summary

In this short talk (re-published from a private community video he recorded ~2016–2021),
Alex Hormozi walks through what he calls his four highest-ROI habits — learned behaviors
he credits with accelerating his success. The content is early mindset/productivity
material: three of the four habits concern discipline and self-management, one concerns
relationship-building. He repeatedly stresses that all four were *learned*, not innate,
and that mere consistency — regardless of the specific behavior — is itself a rare human
capability. Only Alex speaks.

## The 4 habits

1. **Waking up at 4:00 a.m.** — to secure 4–9 a.m. uninterrupted time for working *on*
   the business (the "big dominoes") before the day's reactive demands begin.
2. **Consistent sleep/wake times, including weekends** — going to bed and waking up at
   the same time every day, weekday and weekend alike (he presents habits 1 and 2 as a
   paired unit).
3. **Giving to others in your network without expectation of return** — genuinely
   serving people, connecting them, over-delivering; the relationships and future
   reciprocity follow naturally.
4. **Absolute responsibility / extreme ownership for all situations** — never assigning
   the cause of any situation to anyone but yourself, because blame gives your power
   away.

> Note: Alex's own numbering is loose in the video. He first labels waking early +
> consistent bedtimes as the "first two," then treats sales as a candidate fourth before
> replacing it with ownership, and at one point loses track of the third (giving first).
> The four habits as finally stated are captured above.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-11-24)

- He describes his portfolio company Acquisition.com as doing about $85 million a year
  at the time of the video's public release.
- He says entrepreneurs are, by nature, more reactive and scattered / emotionally
  prone to distraction than most people, which is why controlling one's space and
  attention matters — and also why entrepreneurs gravitate toward problem-solving
  (they're more uncomfortable than everyone else).
- He claims the reason he gets "so much done" is not superhuman output but that most
  people accomplish very little in their day because they work *in* the business, not
  *on* it. He frames time *in* the business as an expense and time *on* the business as
  an investment.
- He argues you cannot wake at 9 a.m. two days a week and then five hours earlier the
  rest and still be productive — the body needs consistency; irregular
  weekend/weekday schedules undermined him for a long time.
- He frames the early-riser lifestyle as a deliberate trade-off/sacrifice: e.g., he and
  Leila go to dinner at 5:45 p.m. to preserve their routine, and treats a friend's
  unwillingness to accommodate that as a signal of how much they value the relationship.
- On giving first: he cites a lifelong pattern of being voted into leadership positions
  (high school newspaper/literary magazine president, college powerlifting VP,
  fraternity president) and reframes leadership as servant-style giving, not being "the
  big boss man." He notes Leila observed this habit early and prompted him to mention it.
- He self-attributes the giving habit partly to insecurity — a desire to be liked and to
  lower his anxiety in new networking groups — and says it later produced large monetary
  value via "IOUs" / favors he could call on.
- On skill acquisition: he says the habit behind sales is jumping into a skill you're bad
  at while expecting *zero* return on your first 20 hours; the emotional fear of "sucking"
  is what stops most people from starting.
- He claims, ironically, that the first 20 hours of a new skill yield ~70% of your gains,
  with massive diminishing returns after hour 20; spending 20 hours learning (e.g.)
  Facebook ads would make you better than 99% of people "because no one does anything."
- He asserts most people are bad at most things — including things they think they're
  good at — because ego/pride makes them stop learning.
- On ownership: he tells clients he will take complete ownership of their situations
  because that's his job (referencing Gym Launch), but that they should take extreme
  ownership and blame his business for nothing if they want to grow.
- He invokes his recurring line that everyone is self-made, but only millionaires and
  billionaires own it — the willingness to claim self-made status correlates with the
  habits and outlook that produce results.
- He relays a story from his PhD friend "Dr. Cashy" (Dr. Trevor Kashey, associated with
  Prestige Labs): people pay him to tell them to do obvious things — sleep, eat
  consistently — and merely being consistent (with anything) transforms their health.
- Closing line he had posted to Instagram that day: "may your desire be greater than
  your obstacles."
