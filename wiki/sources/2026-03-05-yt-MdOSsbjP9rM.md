# Solve Rich People's Problems (They Pay Better)

## Summary
Solo monologue. Despite the title's framing ("solve rich people's problems"), the extracted argument is really about **choosing bigger problems**: the way to make more money in your 20s and 30s is to solve bigger problems, and — crucially — big goals and small goals cost roughly the same effort, so you should pick the big one. Alex builds this on a **pain-is-a-constant** thesis: to make $1M you must endure "$1M worth of pain," to make $10M, "$10M worth of pain," and the *nature* of the pain evolves (early: not knowing what you're doing; later: being judged; later still: lawsuits). Because "not knowing" never goes away, trying to solve *for* certainty is futile — you must "make uncertainty your friend." He cites **Stephen Schwarzman** (Blackstone founder): "Big goals and small goals are usually just about as hard," reinforced with the VC observation that a genuinely successful local restaurant can demand as much labor (80-hour weeks, managing people) as building a billion-dollar unicorn — so if it's hard either way, go big. He adds a counterintuitive competition point: competition for big goals is *rarer* ("thinner air") because most people deem big goals unrealistic and never try, which thins the field. Most problems are solvable (most things are knowable); the reason people haven't arrived is they "haven't actually started trying yet," and success demands being far more **obsessive** than they expect. He closes the motivational arc by reframing social deviance as a green flag: it's normal *not* to be like your normal friends if you want extraordinary outcomes — doing the same things as everyone else and expecting different results makes no sense (illustrated by his EAs telling him a "normal person" would drive a certain car). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo (100M scaling roadmap quiz, 10 stages, book-a-call → Vegas).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, to make more money in your 20s/30s you should solve **bigger** problems; earnings scale with the size of problem solved.
- As of March 2026, making money requires enduring a proportional amount of pain: $1M of income ≈ "$1M worth of pain," $10M ≈ "$10M worth of pain"; most people have never endured that.
- As of March 2026, the *nature* of the pain changes by stage — early it's "I don't know what I'm doing," later "people judging me," later "lawsuits" — but not-knowing is a **constant** at every stage, so solving for certainty is futile; instead "make uncertainty your friend."
- As of March 2026 (crediting **Stephen Schwarzman**, founder of Blackstone): "big goals and small goals are usually just about as hard" — so if it will be hard regardless, go big.
- As of March 2026, a common VC point: a genuinely successful local restaurant can require as much work (80-hour weeks, managing many people) as building a billion-dollar unicorn.
- As of March 2026, one of Alex's operating beliefs: **suffering is a constant** — "if we are growing, I am in pain… plateaued… in pain… declining… in pain" — so pain shouldn't be a reason to do or not do something; it's always there.
- As of March 2026, **competition for big goals is actually rarer** ("thinner air") — most people believe big goals are unrealistic and never shoot for them, thinning the field; in some ways a really successful local restaurant is harder than a big business.
- As of March 2026, most problems are solvable (most things are knowable); the reason you haven't reached your goal is you "haven't actually started trying yet," and you'll have to be far more **obsessive** than you expected.
- As of March 2026, being unlike your "normal friends" is a **green flag** — doing the same things as normal people and expecting a different outcome is illogical; the deviance is a positive indicator you're pursuing extraordinary outcomes.
