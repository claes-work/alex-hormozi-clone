# Do You Have to Go Through Hell to Become Successful?

## Summary
Short clip answering a single interviewer question: "Do you have to go through hell to become successful?" Alex's answer is a flat **No** — with an important reframe. You don't need suffering *per se*; you need to **learn the skills** that produce success, and learning those skills is "oftentimes painful" precisely because you don't have them yet and mistakes hurt. So the hell is incidental, not the mechanism: it's "very hard to learn them scar-free," but the pain is a byproduct of skill acquisition, not the price you pay for character. He notes suffering comes in **degrees of hell** — some suffer far more than others — and that inherited advantage can compress it: if your father were a Rockefeller and you had the ambition, he could teach you much you'd otherwise learn the hard way, "significantly less painful." Alex then attacks the "hard childhood → success" narrative as a **survivorship-bias / bad-variable** error: plenty of people had hard childhoods and are not successful, so the hard childhood isn't the causal variable. When you see the trait on both sides of the outcome, "maybe this thing doesn't matter — it's not a variable worth studying." The things successful people actually have in common are mechanical business behaviors: products people love and keep buying, priced to generate significant gross profit, reinvested for disproportionate growth. His broader claim: **suffering is a fact of life** — poor people suffer, rich people suffer, everyone suffers and looks to the other side hoping they have the answer; "life is painful, some people make money at the end and some people don't." He closes by noting people **retrace the data** and invent tidy causal stories only in retrospect. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
