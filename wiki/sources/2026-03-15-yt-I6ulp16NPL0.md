# I Build Sales Teams. How Do I Find My Ideal Avatar?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a door-to-door sales-team training/recruiting business (senior recruiting firm + mastermind + online education + events) capped around $7.5M for a few years against an "invisible ceiling," wanting $15M. His problem: **three avatars** — sales rep, sales manager/leader, and owner — each with its own track, product ladder, and fulfillment, spreading a small team too thin. Alex diagnoses it as a **model issue**, not a marketing one. His core teaching: an organic-only creator with a fixed audience ("pond") keeps inventing new products to sell to the same people ("what else can I sell these people?"), which "makes sense until it doesn't"; the better question is *what one thing has the highest operating leverage and highest revenue retention, and how do I expand the pond?* He prefers businesses that are **straight lines** — a single front door, then door two, door three, then stop — and then asks how to put more people through the front door. Because a small business has such limited resources, it should pick one thing rather than compete on three fronts against specialists. The selection test: **which avatar is most viable?** The client says owners (≈80% of revenue), so Alex's move is to cut the 80% of work serving reps/managers, accept ~20% less revenue, and watch margins rise — "prune the tree," since "unconstrained growth is also called cancer." He layers on **stickiness** as the deciding metric (the $50/user/month owner product is very sticky; rep and manager products are not) and his **front-door / ascension** model: a front-end team sells the front-end offer, the resulting customer base becomes the lead base for the ascension, and just one or two of your best people can run ascensions all the way to $100M given these ticket sizes. On niching, he's comfortable with either "door-to-door" or the slightly broader "home services," framed by the **same-sale test**: "Do you knock on doors and get people in their houses to give you money? Same sale." Sequence: fix the model first, then drive 10x more volume through the front end. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses the client's stalled growth as a **model problem, not a marketing problem** — serving three avatars (rep, manager, owner) with three tracks and three fulfillment systems over-taxes a small team's limited resources.
- As of March 2026, organic-only creators fall into a predictable trap: they have a fixed audience ("pond"), so they keep adding products ("what else can I sell these people?") to extract more from it — logical, but it "makes sense until it doesn't."
- As of March 2026, the better strategic question is *what one thing has the highest operating leverage and highest revenue retention*, and *how do I expand the pond* — rather than adding more offers to the same people.
- As of March 2026, Alex prefers businesses that are **straight lines** (one front door, then door two, door three, then stop); once the line is set, the only question is how to put more people through the front door, which keeps the business simple.
- As of March 2026, a small business has so few resources that its ability to do anything well is highly constrained, so it should pick one thing — it competes against rivals who do each of those elements as their sole, main business.
- As of March 2026, avatar selection starts with "**which avatar is most viable to the business?**"; here that is owners (client estimates ≈80% of revenue).
- As of March 2026, cutting to the single best avatar means cutting ~80% of the work to lose only ~20% of revenue, and margins go up as a result.
- As of March 2026, **stickiness / retention** is a deciding factor: the owners' $50-per-user-per-month product is "very sticky" (the best business), while the sales-rep and manager products are not sticky — so the sticky line is the one to keep.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames pruning offers as horticulture: prune the bad branches so water/sunlight/nutrition concentrate on the high-margin, sticky central stalk; "unconstrained growth is also called cancer, which kills you."
- As of March 2026 (front-door / ascension model): a front-end team sells the front-end offer; because there are far fewer customers than leads, the first-sale customer base becomes the lead base for the ascension; you need significantly fewer people on ascension and can promote your one or two best front-end people to run it — reaching "$100 million a year with like two or three guys on ascension," especially at high ticket sizes.
- As of March 2026 (niching), Alex is comfortable niching to "door-to-door" or the slightly broader "home services" — both feel fine because the buyers share the same problems; going all-in on a single vertical like roofing is also acceptable.
- As of March 2026, his **same-sale test** for how wide a niche can stretch: if solar and pest-control owners both "knock on doors and get people in their houses to give you money," it's the "same sale" — vertical-specific wrinkles (e.g. financing) are just a "separate sub-module," not a different business.
- As of March 2026, the sequence is: **clean up the model first** (cut to one avatar), then "drive 10 times more on the top end" (front-end volume).
