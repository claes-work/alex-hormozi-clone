# Brutally Honest Advice to Build Your Confidence

## Summary

Solo monologue on confidence: Hormozi argues confidence is a **prediction built from evidence**, not something you psych yourself into — so you should build evidence (do the reps, review the script, get the proof) rather than "fake it till you make it." He connects this to tracking leading (input) metrics over lagging (output) ones, playing the infinite game (the point is to keep playing, so not quitting = winning), rejecting other people's opinions and self-labels, and choosing whichever action makes "the more epic story."

## Key claims

- "Don't build confidence, build evidence — confidence comes as a result of evidence, not the other way around." Confidence is literally a prediction (like a statistical confidence metric): the way to raise it is to have the thing already happen via practice. Replace "fake it till you make it" with "walk it before you talk it." Be **certain** (based on what you've done), not merely confident.
- Context beats content: "the message is 10% of how people consume it, 90% is the context around the message." His reach grew from proof (selling a company for $46M) and a rename (Gym Secrets → The Game), not better content; solve for evidence/proof and the content takes care of itself.
- Track **leading/input** metrics you control (calls made, calories, reps), not lagging/output ones (sales, weight, downloads). "The more ways you measure, the more ways you can win." Redefine winning as leaving nothing in the tank — the Delta between how hard you tried and how hard you could try being zero.
- **Finite vs. infinite frame**: marriage, health, business have no win condition — "the point is to stay married / stay in shape / stay in business," so if you don't give up you win by default. Cites Kobe (loved "playing to the best of my ability") and Naval ("what looks like work to other people should feel like play for you").
- Drop "because" statements and self-labels ("I'm the type of person who… because my mom…") — they hand power to an unchangeable past. Say "I do these things, period," then change the present behavior. Heroes vs. villains (Donald Miller): same backstory of pain — "heroes use pain, villains are used by it." The single strongest predictor of his behavior: "what will make the more epic story."
