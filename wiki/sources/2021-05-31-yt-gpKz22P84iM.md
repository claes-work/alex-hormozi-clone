# The Talent Grid — How I Recognize Skill in Self & Team (2021-05-31)

## Summary

Hormozi lays out an early ("nine grid") framework for grading skill level — in
yourself, in a team member, or in a whole department. It has three ascending levels
defined by two lenses: the **question** the person is answering (What → How → Who) and
the **role/activity** that maps to it (Individual Contributor / doing → Manager /
teaching → Leader / systematizing). He frames the endgame as "building the machine
that builds the machine" and defines a truly delegated business as one where he is no
longer on the company's Slack channel.

> ⚠️ Note: This is a **proto-version** of Hormozi's later talent/hiring frameworks.
> The "What / How / Who" ladder and the doing→teaching→systematizing progression
> anticipate his subsequent public teaching on levels of leverage, delegation, and
> "the machine that builds the machine." Treat as a 2021 formulation.

## The Talent Grid (as described, 2021-05-31)

A three-level ladder, read across three parallel lenses (his "nine grid" = 3 levels ×
3 lenses):

| Level | Question | Role / Title | Activity | Rough scale marker |
|-------|----------|--------------|----------|--------------------|
| 1 | **What** to do (learn the skills) | Individual Contributor | **Doing** | You / startup, getting things done yourself |
| 2 | **How** to do it (the sequence) | Manager | **Teaching** others to replicate the skill | — |
| 3 | **Who** does it | Leader | **Systematizing** — "building the machine that builds the machine" | ~$3M/yr level; leaders required to crack $10M+ / $1M+/mo |

- Axis 1 (question): **What → How → Who.**
- Axis 2 (role): **Individual Contributor → Manager → Leader.**
- Axis 3 (activity): **Doing → Teaching → Systematizing** ("build the machine that
  builds the machine").

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-05-31)

- (2021) Frameworks are what make you faster over time: his first 8-figure company
  took 6 years, first multiple-8-figure ~6.5 years, the next multi-8-figure company
  1 year, and the one after that 6 months — with 120M+ in cumulative sales across
  industries by this point.
- (2021) Level 1 = **Individual Contributor**: you must personally get things done;
  time is spent **doing** and learning **what** to do.
- (2021) Level 2 = **Manager**: ensures others can consistently execute the thing;
  time is spent **teaching how**. A manager doesn't need to do the task better than
  the IC (niche expertise exists) but must understand how it works.
- (2021) Level 3 = **Leader**: distinct from managers; answers **who**, and
  **systematizes** rather than merely delegates — he deliberately chose the word
  "systematizing" over "delegating/handing off." Leaders (not just tenured managers)
  are required to break past ~$10M/yr.
- (2021) The same grid grades **departments** (marketing, sales, product, shared
  services = IT/legal/HR): ask "what level am I involved at here?" and aim to move the
  CEO **above** the grid so the business runs on vision/strategy only.
- (2021) Your **strengths** are often the bottleneck: you readily give away what you
  don't know, but cling to what you're good at, so the CEO must consciously push those
  strong areas up the ladder (do → teach → systematize).
- (2021) Identity is fluid — he and Leila hold that you must change what you believe
  about yourself to de-bottleneck the business; people often stick at one level due to
  self-belief.
- (2021) His personal test for a truly delegated, portfolio-style business: he is
  **no longer on that company's Slack channel** — reachable only via one weekly
  meeting with the operator, and the company grows between meetings without his direct
  involvement. Not having to respond in the moment is not the same as being systematized.
- (2021) Citing Reid Hoffman (LinkedIn founder): most people struggle to jump these
  levels, which is why fast-growing companies often swap out "leadership"; early-stage
  managers frequently never become true leaders. Great ICs are often terrible leaders
  and vice versa — mastering all levels is why the operator role is so hard.
- (2021) Entrepreneurship forces self-growth: to attract high-level people you must
  improve your own character; ~95% of entrepreneurs claim their team is amazing while
  carrying teammate "horror stories," so their judgment on team quality is unreliable.
