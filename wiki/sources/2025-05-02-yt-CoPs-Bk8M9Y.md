# Business is hard until you do this

## Summary

Alex Hormozi argues that stalled, low-margin businesses usually have the wrong
customer avatar, not a scaling-tactics problem — selling to volatile small
businesses (e.g. agencies selling $1,500/mo to SMBs) builds "a castle on a
foundation of sand." He walks through the *what* (six red flags of bad-avatar
selling), the *why* (bad customers wreck LTV, CAC, and payback period), and the
*how* (a customer profitability analysis to redefine the ICP, realign the whole
business to it, and transition off "brokie" customers even if it means going
backwards in revenue). Framed as one of his "seven deadly sins of business" and
tied to the acquisition.com $100M scaling roadmap (stage four: "Prioritize").

## Key claims

- Not making enough money is often a *who you sell to* problem, not a *how to
  scale* problem. Agencies selling ~$1,500/mo to small businesses plateau around
  $1M–$3M/yr.
- A recurring cost of $1,500–$2,500/mo is usually too high for small business
  owners; a bad month and they immediately cancel marketing — making revenue
  volatility a function of *customer* volatility.
- Selling to bad customers forces misalignment between your maximum potential
  service value and the customer's ability to realize and pay for it, creating a
  vicious cycle of lowering price and under-delivering.
- **Structural churn**: customers who leave for reasons core to their own
  business, not because they dislike you. Example: a gym-space CRM had ~3%/month
  churn (~30%/yr) mostly because gyms go out of business — nothing the vendor
  can do. Selling to small businesses caps retention at best ~70% year-over-year.
- Business is a **barbell**: sell *custom* only to high-end customers who can
  afford it and honor commitments; sell *templated / DIY* solutions to small and
  very small businesses (VSMBs). The middle — mid-price to small customers — is
  "where people go to die."
- Six red flags of the wrong avatar: (1) short customer lifespans (~3–4 months),
  (2) excessive support demands, (3) price resistance / discount requests,
  (4) dissatisfaction despite you going above and beyond reasonable expectations,
  (5) overpromising just to close, (6) selling people you know will suck just to
  pay the bills.
- The three acquisition forces — **LTV, CAC, and payback period** — are all
  negatively hit by the wrong customer: operational strain and price compression
  cut LTV; demanding customers raise CAC; both extend the cash conversion cycle
  and limit growth.
- Bad customers also erode team morale (revolving-door onboarding/offboarding),
  reputation (negative word of mouth is ~5–10x more potent than positive), and
  raise CAC disproportionately to CPM increases (an "invisible hand").
- The fix: (1) conduct a **customer profitability analysis** across three lenses —
  who they are (demographics/psychographics), what they're doing when they enter
  (behaviors/revenue/headcount), and what they *did* to succeed with your product;
  (2) redefine the ideal customer profile (ICP / avatar); (3) realign the entire
  business — offer, headlines, testimonials, onboarding, sales qualification — to
  that ICP; (4) transition off bad customers, possibly capping the % of low-end
  sales, until the top 20% becomes the 80%.
- You set your own prices — "guess who gets to pick what your prices are? You do."
  You can often make more money by letting cost rise (e.g. $500 → $1,000) while
  charging 3–4x more to a customer who can actually realize the value.
- Saying yes to everyone is a telltale sign of a small business that sucks; a big
  agency (Ogilvy & Mather) won't take a local dry cleaner. Qualification and
  saying no are required.
- Transition mindset: be willing to go backwards (e.g. $200K→$100K/mo) — "local
  maximum," walk down the mountain to walk back up. Ego attachment to current
  revenue keeps founders stuck; the business will compress to that lower number
  anyway if nothing changes.
- Laying people off after over-hiring is sometimes unavoidable: take absolute
  responsibility, give severance, and leave the door open — former employees
  often return later with new skills.
- The end goal: sell something people don't want to cancel out of; if churn goes
  to near zero, you grow almost no matter what — and sometimes the way to do that
  is to change *who* you sell to.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "The thing is, if you try to build a big business off of small businesses, it's
  basically having a terrible foundation. It's building a castle on a foundation
  of sand. As soon as the tide comes out and pulls the sand away, so too goes the
  castle."
- "There's a reason they are small business owners. It's because they make small
  business owner mistakes."
- "The middle is certainly where people go to die."
- "I promise you, you never want to sell to somebody who wants you to be their
  savior. Only you can save yourself. And that goes for them, too."
- "Guess who gets to pick what your prices are? You do. If there's a price where
  it's worth it to you, charge that. And if someone doesn't want to pay it, fine.
  It's capitalism. It's volunteer exchange."
- "Would you rather get to your goal in five years or would you rather get to your
  goal never? Sometimes the fastest way to build a $10 million business is not the
  fastest way to build a $1 million business."
- "One of my big kind of rules in life is that if I know something has to happen
  eventually, I might as well do it today."
- "If anybody's going to destroy my business, it's going to be me. I want to put
  me out of business. I don't want the market to put me out of business. I want to
  skate to where the puck's going, not where the puck is."
- "There's not a single chess master that I know of who has ever won a game
  without losing any at the highest levels. Sometimes when you play chess, you
  have to sacrifice pawns."
- "The fundamentally the whole point of business is to think about what customer
  can I provide the absolute most value to. That's the person you sell to."
