# 200 Cold Calls, 2 Customers: That's Not Luck, That's Math

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises the founder of an environmental-testing software platform selling to enterprise labs. The founder has three customers and frames two cold-call wins from about 200 calls as luck. Alex reframes it as a process: if 200 calls produced two high-ticket customers, the next question is how to get enough lead volume, repeat the activity, and structure the offer so enterprise buyers perceive a valuable setup fee waiver.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Dacian Floria** - environmental-testing software founder. His platform, pricing, margins, and customer count are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If 100 cold calls produce one customer, that is not luck; it is evidence of a process.
- At sub-$1M scale, what feels like volatility is often insufficient volume.
- A 1% cold-call conversion rate can be acceptable for a $50K-$100K upfront enterprise product with annual recurring revenue.
- Alex reframes the economics as dollars per dial: if 100 calls produces a $50K deal, each dial has high expected value.
- The immediate constraint is lead volume and list building, not necessarily a new offer.
- Possible lead sources include list brokers, scraping, and paying community or group owners to advertise to the target niche.
- For enterprise software, a waived-fee structure can make the offer more compelling: quote a large integration/setup fee, then waive it in exchange for a longer commitment.
- Alex says choice architecture can present an "illusion of choice" that makes the preferred option obvious.
- For enterprise sales, Alex even recommends flying to a city after a prospect agrees to meet, then truthfully saying he is in town that week.
