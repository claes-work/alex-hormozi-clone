# The REAL Reason You're Not Making as Much Money As You Want

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi argues that entrepreneurs underperform financially because they chase exciting "missed opportunities" instead of solving the unglamorous "problems" that threaten the business. He defines strategy as prioritizing limited resources against unlimited opportunities, and reframes problem-solving as the highest-probability, "sure money" swing versus the hypothetical payoff of new opportunities. He layers in a data-hygiene rule (only track data that changes behavior), a four-step problem-solving framework, and a critique of Jeff Bezos's baseball/business analogy, closing with a personal admission that he over-indexes on the future and neglected content creation until Leila pulled him back.

## Key claims

- The reason you're not making as much money as you want is that you're working on the wrong stuff — chasing opportunities rather than solving problems.
- A **problem** is something that threatens the future livelihood of the business (customer complaints, a processor red flag, sales team dropping conversions); a **missed opportunity** is something you're not doing but should be doing that could expand or grow the business.
- Entrepreneurs color problems and missed opportunities the same in their heads — both become "things to do" — which is why an undifferentiated to-do list becomes overwhelming.
- Missed opportunities are fun, exciting, shiny, and cluster around whatever you personally love doing (product people have product should-dos, marketers have marketing should-dos), which is exactly why entrepreneurs over-pursue them.
- Problems suck and aren't fun; they're usually something you set up once and now have to fix because new information came to light.
- Prioritizing missed opportunities grows the business but also grows your problems — if you haven't solved a problem before growing, that problem just gets bigger.
- **Strategy = prioritizing limited resources against unlimited opportunities.** You have a fixed pool of resources and an unlimited list of should-dos.
- Solving problems grows you "through getting better"; capturing opportunities grows you "through doing more" — and more can create more problems. A business with no problems grows by default.
- It's easier to solve small problems than big problems; solve them while they're small.
- **Data hygiene ("2011-level hack"):** if a change in a piece of data does not result in a change in behavior, it's a waste of time — it's noise, not signal. The end result of every meeting should be a change in behavior; both you and your team should know what behavior each metric influences.
- Anecdote: a platform vendor advised shorter content because a higher percentage of people consumed it; Alex refused until the vendor could tie view/listen duration to a business metric (e.g., shares → more customers or higher customer value). If a metric can't be tied to getting more customers or making customers worth more, he doesn't care.
- **Four-step problem-solving framework:** (1) the condition/goal — current state vs. desired state; (2) the action — a hypothesis (an action tied to an expected result) that will cost resources/money; (3) before/after — did we actually do it or not; (4) did what we expected to happen actually happen (you can execute and still have the metric not move).
- Your second business grew faster than your first because you already knew the solutions to the problems you'd create along the way.
- Alex keeps a list called "Alex's Big Money-Making Idea List"; he has more ideas than capacity, and learned from Leila that he only gets to do about 1 in 10 or 20 of them — you don't have unlimited shots on goal.
- **Critique of Bezos:** Bezos (quoted from the opening of a book) says a 10% chance of a 100x payoff should be taken every time because big winners pay for many experiments. Alex's flaw with the analogy: that logic only holds if you can bet unlimited times. With a $10 minimum bet, a 100x payoff, and only $20, you get two swings — an 80% chance of losing entirely. Business owners have limited swings and must be measured.
- Highest-likelihood swings are the problems that need solving today — "sure money"; missed opportunities are "certainly not certain" and purely hypothetical.
- "Sleep on it": if you have an amazing idea, don't tell the team immediately — you'll oversell yourself and them. Revisit two months later and you'll find you no longer want ~90% of the ideas, because solving problems gave you more information.
- Personal: he'd been making less content because he poured huge hours into a big new project for 2025; Leila told him his head wasn't in the game and he needed to come back to the present.
- He frames this as "a dichotomy to be managed," not "a problem to be solved" — as the visionary he naturally lives 3–6+ months out, and has to overemphasize the present; Leila is grounded in today (their yin and yang).
- Going forward he settled on a **6-to-1 ratio** — five of every six videos will be deep business content.
- Cites a recent case study: fixing the onboarding process in one portfolio company made ~$3 million more profit, increased customer satisfaction, decreased refunds and chargebacks, and increased ascension rates by over 40%.
- One main takeaway: confront your problems today, forget opportunities for tomorrow, park them on the big list, and once problems are solved go pick the one with the highest likelihood of the biggest return and go all in.
