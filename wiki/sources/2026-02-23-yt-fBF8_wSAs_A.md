# I Sell on Amazon. How Do I Scale?

## Summary

Short Q&A clip. An audience member asks how to scale an Amazon dental-products business:
~$34K/month revenue, ~$3,500/month ad spend, 20% net, ranked #3 in category; scaling ad
spend "destroys margins with little gain," and the #1 competitor is at $250K/month. Alex
Hormozi answers with two moves. First, treat Amazon as just one channel — max out the
existing channel, but if you can't make the product itself more valuable, open new channels
(micro-influencers, other dentists, B2B/wholesale). Second, he reframes the competitor gap
as a product problem: on Amazon (a "winner-take-all" market for the best product), the #1
listing makes disproportionately more than #3 or #10, so out-scaling the leader realistically
means having a better product — better reviews that convert, a backend that sells add-ons —
not just more ad spend. He also flags that Amazon ads eat profit because Amazon effectively
knows your margins and prices its ad inventory to capture them.

Speaker note: the questioner is the audience member (business figures are self-reported by
them, unverified); only Hormozi's answer is persona-eligible.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed, paraphrased, 2026-02-23)

- Amazon ads tend to destroy margin because Amazon effectively knows how much you make and
  prices ad inventory to capture roughly what your profit would be — so scaling spend just
  pays them more (paraphrase; he hedges "that's my guess").
- Amazon is "just a channel." To scale, first max out the existing channel; if you can't make
  the product more valuable, start looking at new channels rather than pouring in more spend.
- Alternative channels/angles for a product: micro-influencers, recruiting other dentists
  (peer sellers) to promote it, and B2B / wholesale.
- Diagnose the leading competitor before assuming they simply out-spend: check whether they
  have better reviews (which convert the listing better) and a backend where first-purchase
  buyers go on to buy other things.
- The $250K/month competitor is probably *making* $250K, not *spending* it — and the likeliest
  explanation is simply a better product.
- Amazon is a "winner-take-all" market for the best products: the #1 listing makes far more
  than #3 or #10, analogous to the #1 business book vs. the #10 book.
- Consequently, for a lower-ranked seller the real lever for getting bigger on Amazon comes
  down to the product — you can market in more ways, but to out-grow the category leader you
  generally need a better product, built to maximize quality relative to price.
