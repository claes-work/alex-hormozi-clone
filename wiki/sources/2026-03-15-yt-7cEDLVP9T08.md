# I Build Sales Teams. How Do I Scale?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs a "we build sales teams" agency for online/service/coaching businesses — they staff the sales team, manage the sales process, and build the systems. They did just under $14M this year (~$1.3M/month), targeting $100M in 3 years, but growth has stalled month-to-month (flat all year at a higher plateau, not compounding). Their block: the math to $100M requires more clients than they believe they can fulfill at the quality they want, and **margins are compressing** — they profited ~$3M this year, about the same as last year on *half* the revenue. Alex diagnoses the cause: last year's fat margins came from unpaid/underpaid founders doing the work of many; now they've hired a real leadership team (sales directors, account managers, engineering), so the true cost of the asset is showing up. The margin leak is that fulfillment pods manage fewer and fewer clients (to fight high churn / low success rate), so profit-per-pod keeps falling. Alex reframes the fix away from a blunt price hike (they charge 25% of client top-line): with sound unit economics, the problem is **bottom-up, not top-down**. The lever is not "25% → 30%" but "get bigger clients so each rep earns more" — chase customers with more zeros on their price tag (you make nothing on $100 gym memberships, a fortune on $100M buildings). Concretely: characterize the customers that came in, run **profitability-per-customer**, derive the truly profitable **ICP/avatar** (probably a "left-of-center" vein, not the everyone-list of "coaches, consultants, blah blah" they rattled off), then re-target messaging to it. Serving one specific avatar unlocks efficiencies — templatized service, automation of parts of the work — so margins can expand or at least hold as they scale. He also flags an activity audit: some fulfillment activities correlate with retention, some don't, and some may *hurt* it — find and prune those. Notes this is "not just a him problem," ~20% of viewers share it, and gently ribs the client for not using his own sales company (Alto). Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's core diagnosis for a service business whose margins are compressing as it scales: the earlier "fat" margins were an illusion created by founders doing the work of multiple people for little/no pay; margins compress once you actually pay a team to build the asset — the question is whether they compress-then-stabilize or keep compressing.
- As of March 2026, for this agency the margin was still compressing because fulfillment staff manage fewer and fewer clients each (to combat high churn / low success rate), so profit-per-pod keeps falling — the real problem is operational (people ops), not pricing.
- As of March 2026, if unit economics are sound (they charge 25% of client top-line), the fix is bottom-up not top-down: not raising the take-rate (25→30) but landing bigger/more valuable clients so each rep generates more revenue — "more zeros on their price tag."
- As of March 2026, the scaling method: characterize the customers who came in → compute profitability per customer → define the ICP/avatar that is actually profitable → change messaging to attract that specific avatar; the business model itself may not be wrong at all.
- As of March 2026, narrowing to one specific avatar unlocks efficiencies (templatized service levels, automation of portions of the work), which lets margins expand or at least hold as the company scales.
- As of March 2026, Alex's retention lever: audit all fulfillment activities — some correlate with retention, some don't, some may actively decrease it — then prune the ones that don't help.
- As of March 2026, Alex's "virtuous cycle of services" (esp. B2B): good reputation → demand exceeds supply (services are always supply-constrained if you're good) → raise prices → afford more A-players → higher reputation → more demand, climbing from $100k hires to $250k to $1M+ to multi-million-dollar-per-year people.
- As of March 2026, you should be able to make plenty of money on 25% of someone else's top-line — but only if the client is big enough: nothing on $100 gym memberships, a fortune on $100M buildings.
