# What is Confidence?

## Summary
In this short clip Alex Hormozi reframes confidence as a predictive metric — the
percentage likelihood that what you expect to happen actually happens — and argues it is
literally the same word as the statistical confidence interval. Because it is built on
accumulated proof, confidence is buildable through repetition: do the thing enough times,
gather enough evidence, and your predictive power (and felt confidence) rises. He
illustrates with a dating example where a hit rate climbs from 1-in-10 to 8-in-10 through
sheer volume of attempts.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex defines confidence as the percentage likelihood that what you
  think will happen will actually happen — a predictive metric grounded in proof.
- As of March 2026, Alex observes that psychological "confidence" and the numerical
  "confidence interval" are the same word, and treats that overlap as literal rather than
  coincidental.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that the way to become confident is to do the thing enough
  times that it becomes unlikely your expected outcome won't occur — building the proof
  stack through repetition.
- As of March 2026, Alex reduces confidence-building to inputs: "you just have to do
  more," using a dating example (hit rate rising 1/10 → 2/10 → 4/10 → 8/10 across
  repeated batches of attempts) to show predictive power accumulating with volume.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If we think of confidence as the percentage likelihood of what we think is going to happen will happen as a predictive metric, then in order to be more confident, we want to have more proof that what we think will happen will happen. And so the easiest way to do that is to do it a lot of times."

> "How do you become confident? Do it enough times that you feel like it is unlikely that what you think should happen won't happen."

> "You start going to girls and you start tanking and then one out of 10 times it goes well. And then you do it another 10 times and two out of 10 times goes well. And then four out of 10 times and then eight out of 10 times and all of a sudden you're confident because you have a high degree of predictive power when you say it will go this way."

> "You can boil it down to inputs. You just have to do more."
