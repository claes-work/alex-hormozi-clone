# I Work 2 Hours a Week and Do $6M. I Want $100M.

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A in which a roofing / exterior-remodeling business owner (doing ~$6M/year, working two-to-three hours a week after replacing himself in the business) tells **Alex Hormozi** he wants to reach $100M but is held back by comfort, distraction (multiple side businesses, real estate, ADHD), and fear of losing family time. Alex reframes the situation around trade-offs — you either "want less or trade more" — and argues the path from $6M to $100M runs through hiring higher-level talent (accepting a short-term profit hit), keeping passive investments passive, and accepting that a big goal means giving up the exciting new things you'd otherwise chase. **Alex is the primary speaker / advisor here; the roofing owner is the coachee and his situation is not persona data.**

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **Regret is usually not real**: it comes from "imagining the upside that we didn't get without also considering the downside that we didn't suffer to get it" — the girl or business opportunity that got away, romanticized without its trade-off.
- **Work-life balance has no right answer** — it's a preference, not a moral question. Dissatisfaction comes from wanting both sides of a trade at once. Analogy: if you like cookies and want a six-pack, you just prefer cookies; the trade is the trade.
- **The core lever: "either want less or trade more."** If you change nothing, nothing changes.
- **You probably can go from $6M to $100M without working more** — but it depends on how much you're willing to pay other people. You may take a **short-term hit to profitability** to bring in the level of talent that will expand the business on your behalf.
- **To attract that talent you must be the kind of character people want to follow** — with a vision big enough that their aspirations fit inside it.
- **Scaling is "the who game," and there are levels of who's.** Alex recounts his own ladder: minimum-wage labor → first $50k employee → first six-figure → first $250k, $500k, $1M, multi-million-dollar-per-year employee — each level a revelation.
- **"The best talent is always in the future"** (attributed to Sheron/Sharan, Acquisition.com's president) — the best people are ahead of you, not behind you.
- **Choose which thing you value least.** Short-term profit can be recovered later; family time cannot. If you're willing to give up short-term profit, you can bring in high-level talent to lead the growth.
- **On the fear of losing family time: "just hold the line" / "just don't."** If you're afraid of losing it, simply don't give it up.
- **Keep passive investments passive; keep active active.** Real estate and similar are fine as long as you're not actively running them — Alex favors REITs and funds with good partners. Buying another building or putting money in the S&P "changes nothing" about his day, so it's not a distraction.
- **A distraction only becomes a problem when it changes what you do day-to-day** — e.g. if you start checking it all the time and it eats your days. And "it's only a problem if you decide it's a problem" — you may simply prefer the ADD, i.e. sacrifice the big goal because you enjoy the variety.
- **The cost of the big thing is everything new you have to give up** — all the exciting opportunities you'll no longer pursue because you chose to do one thing big.
- **Finite shots on goal**: Alex figures he has ~30-40 productive years left, i.e. only "four, five big seasons/runs." Invoking Warren Buffett's 20-punch-card idea — if entrepreneurs treated opportunities like a limited punch card they'd make far better choices. Building something really big takes a long time, so you can't do many things.
- **Don't turn passive money active for marginal gains**: making passive money cost you more time to earn ~5% better returns is a bad trade — your active income yields far better returns than squeezing your passive holdings.
