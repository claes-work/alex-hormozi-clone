# How to Change Your Life

## Summary

Solo mindset talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that people fail to get what they want because they refuse to make conscious tradeoffs and instead stay in "decision purgatory." He reframes every life choice (housing, marriage, kids, sacrificing your 20s, buying $500 shoes) as a trade whose worth depends entirely on the person and their context, and argues the perceived downside of any bet is a fixed, exaggerated fear while the upside is uncertain and undervalued. He closes with an 8th-grade germaphobe story (Expedition 8) and a Ray Dalio anecdote to make the case that other people's opinions, like feared germs, amount to nothing.

## Key claims

- People don't get what they want because they want too many mutually exclusive things at once and won't make tradeoffs. Everything in life has tradeoffs; the task is to weigh price versus value.
- Whether a trade is "good" or "bad" is not inherent — it depends on the person and their context (the $500 shoes example: sensible for a billionaire, not for someone on food stamps).
- The worst trades are the ones you make unconsciously — e.g. giving up the fun of your 20s but also failing to build the nest egg, reps, or experience for your 30s.
- You can't expect a "1% outcome" without paying a "1% tradeoff." Your 30s are largely a reflection of how you lived your 20s.
- The cost of a bet is guaranteed and fixed; the upside is uncertain — which is why people struggle to bet on themselves. They know what they stand to lose but not what they stand to gain.
- The real worst-case scenario for a functioning, social person is crashing on someone's couch for a while — not going homeless and dying. Alex claims the vast majority of homelessness is due to addiction and mental-health issues, and that even the unhoused in the developed world have food and medical access.
- Subjective well-being is largely inherited/genetic and doesn't change dramatically across life; solving the next problem won't make you lastingly happier. Brains evolved to find threats and stay alive, which keeps us stressed but drives civilization forward.
- Frame life as happening *for* you, not *to* you — this enables a life without regret, because you had to go through each stage to reach the present.
- Only take criticism from people ahead of you in the relevant domain. Alex says he has never received criticism on fitness, wealth, work, or marriage from anyone further along than him in those areas.
- "All failure besides death is psychological" (attributed to Jocko Willink) — if you didn't die, try again.
- All the upside you want is on the other side of uncertainty and delay; that is where the arbitrage lies, because those are the two prices people won't pay.
- If someone else figured something out, you can too — problems are solvable, and at minimum someone who can solve it exists and can be paid, favored, or otherwise enlisted.
