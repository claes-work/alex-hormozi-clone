# Why Most Info Business Owners Fail at Software

## Summary

Asked whether an information-product owner should build software for the same audience,
Alex says he has not seen anyone successfully operate both models at once. He rejects
Skool as a counterexample because he does not sell courses and bought Skool after it had
a technical founder, engineering leadership, and product-market fit; he contributed the
marketing capability it lacked. His broader rule is to match exceptional distribution
with an exceptional product rather than build every capability from scratch.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says he has yet to see an operator successfully run both an
  information-product business and a software company at the same time.
- He says Acquisition.com's Skool involvement is not a counterexample because he does
  not sell courses and Skool was already a fully formed software business when acquired.
- Alex self-reports that Skool already had a technical founder, a head of engineering,
  and product-market fit; the missing capability was marketing.
- He says a strong marketer with distribution should find the most efficient existing
  product rather than necessarily build software.
- He frames the market symmetrically: great products seek distribution and great
  distribution seeks products.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I have yet to see anyone succeed to do both."

> "This isn't an info business."

> "Great products find distribution and great distribution finds great products."
