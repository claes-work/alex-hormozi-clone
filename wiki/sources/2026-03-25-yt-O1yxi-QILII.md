# Why Can't I Scale My PT Business?

## Summary
In this coaching clip, Alex Hormozi advises a caller who runs a ~$3M/year physical therapy and personal training business (targeting 35–45-year-old athletes) that is growing but stuck because it cannot hire enough practitioners. The caller reports strong demand ("sold out") but a severe supply shortage of therapists, and asks whether to keep raising prices toward a concierge model or go all-in on recruiting. Hormozi reframes the constraint: the business is not actually in physical therapy but in "recruiting physical therapists," and the fix is to treat therapists as the customer and apply the same acquisition playbook plus large referral incentives.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that most businesses are either supply-constrained or demand-constrained, and the first job is to correctly identify which — solving the real constraint rather than changing a working model.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that when a business already works with good margins and revenue, the lowest-risk move is to solve its single binding constraint (here: recruiting), not to break the model by pivoting to a luxury/concierge price model.
- As of March 2026, Alex applies his "core four" acquisition channels (warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, organic content) to hiring, plus headhunters/recruiters and word-of-mouth referrals from existing staff — treating candidate acquisition like customer acquisition.
- As of March 2026, Alex contends entrepreneurs under-invest in talent acquisition relative to its return: he frames the willingness-to-pay for a referral against the gross profit a hire generates.
- Caller claim (unverified): the business does a little over $3M/year from two locations with good margins, roughly $350,000 gross profit per physical therapist per year, and faces an industry shortage of ~16,000 open practitioner positions.
- Illustrative anecdote (unverified): a former gym owner who scaled via Gym Launch built a cleaning company with a ~$25 customer acquisition cost but was constrained by the supply of reliable English-speaking cleaners willing to work for $15/hour.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You're not actually in the physical therapy business. You're in the recruiting physical therapists business."

> "We have to approach this the same way we approach getting customers. They are just now the customer."

> "If I told you that I could add $350,000 in gross profit per year to your business, how much would you be willing to pay for that in the first year?"

> "Giving someone $500 for a referral, not that motivating. If you said I'll give you 20 grand for a referral, I'll bet you'll get them to move."

> "The nice thing that you have is you have a supply constrained business... it's not hard to get people to say they want somebody else to clean their house."

> "For me, the lowest risk thing to do is not change the business model, but just figure out the problem of the business and solve it."

> "People are happy to put $100,000 in the stock market and get 10% back and make 10,000. But in their businesses, they're hesitant to give a commission of $10,000 for a $300,000 thing."

> "With enough incentive, you can move the world."
