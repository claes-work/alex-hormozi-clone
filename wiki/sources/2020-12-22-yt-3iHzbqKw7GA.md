# No Opportunity is Forever (And this is why that's good)

## Summary

Hormozi addresses people who refuse to start something because they can see it won't
last forever. He argues that all opportunities are by nature arbitrage — exploiting
temporary inefficiencies that inevitably disappear as markets grow efficient — so
finiteness is not a reason to decline; a large but fleeting return is still worth
capturing. The real blocker, he says, is usually fear of failure dressed up as strategic
caution.

## Key claims

- (2020) The only things that don't have an end are things you don't understand; if you
  see an option as "forever," you probably don't understand it well enough to begin.
- (2020) All opportunities are arbitrage — taking advantage of inefficiencies between two
  systems or markets — and over time all arbitrage disappears as markets become more
  efficient.
- (2020) You should not discount an opportunity merely because it is finite, since all
  opportunities are finite; if the return is high enough, a two-to-three-year window can
  be well worth it.
- (2020) Of the Fortune 500, he claims only one company (GE) has been on the list for
  over 100 years — evidence that even dominant firms are not permanent because markets
  change faster than large infrastructures can re-shift.
- (2020) Businesses built on a fleeting arbitrage are fine: they generate cash flow and
  human resources that can be redeployed and pivoted when the opportunity closes — like
  an investment that triples in three years, after which you reinvest elsewhere.
- (2020) The manufactured "it won't last" objection usually masks fear of failing (or
  laziness); for some people the fix is simply to get started.
- (2020) Caveat for existing operators: if you're already in a working vehicle you've
  already found your arbitrage — don't chase another one unless it feeds your original
  model.

## Notable verbatim quotes
