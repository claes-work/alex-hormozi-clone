# How to Win in Saturated Markets

## Summary

Alex explains how to think about saturated markets and niches. A red ocean can signal
that a lot of demand exists, but beginners should artificially constrain the pond so
they can become the biggest player in a smaller arena before expanding. He maps five
market moves: up-market, down-market, adjacent, broader, and narrower.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Heavy competition can indicate a large market with large rewards.
- Beginners should constrain their market so they can compete away from the biggest
  incumbents.
- Alex describes the progression as puddle to pond to lake to ocean, tied to skill and
  experience.
- Niching down creates pricing power because fewer alternatives are directly comparable.
- A niche's upside is capped to some degree, though usually higher than founders expect.
- A business can move up-market, down-market, adjacent, broader, or narrower.
- In Alex's Gym Launch example, up-market would mean multi-location owners or
  franchisors, down-market would mean trainers, adjacent would mean chiropractors, and
  broader would mean health and wellness.
- Many small business owners never define their market and instead accept anyone with a
  pulse and a credit card.
- Lack of market clarity confuses customers because they do not know what the business
  is really about.
