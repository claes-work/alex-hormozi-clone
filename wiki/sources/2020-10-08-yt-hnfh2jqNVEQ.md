# Pricing Lessons From a Private Equity God (2020-10-08)

## Summary

In this 2020 solo talk, Hormozi recounts sitting next to a Vista Equity Partners
pricing/packaging/product executive at Dan Martell's SaaS Academy event and relays the
private-equity firm's core value-creation approach: score prospects, opportunities, and
customers by their value, then find and amplify the "vein" of high-lifetime-value
customers. He describes applying this at Gym Launch by heavily qualifying the front end,
and adds two related lessons the PE executive shared on retention: customers decide to
stay early (between sale and onboarding), and expectation management is everything.

## Pricing lessons

The talk is less about setting price points and more about **customer selection and value
extraction** (proto-pricing-psychology): who you sell to, how you qualify, and how
expectations shape perceived value and retention.

1. **Score prospects, opportunities, and customers by value.** Most companies never score
   leads/customers by their value based on characteristics and behaviors. The PE approach
   parses the data to find "buckets" or "veins" of the most valuable (high-LTV) customers,
   then expands and focuses on that vein. Scoring is applied at all three pipeline stages
   (prospects, opportunities, customers), not just customers.
2. **The best data comes from the customers who left.** To find your good-vs-bad customer
   profile, look at the last ~3 months of exits/escalations and reverse-engineer the
   traits of who churned or got the least results vs. who succeeded (age, job, goals, what
   they signed up for, money down, continuity, etc.).
3. **Qualify the front end even if it cuts volume.** At Gym Launch they required prospects
   to have ≥25 clients, a signed lease, be a full-time gym owner (ideally ≥1 employee).
   This roughly halved intake but eliminated escalations and raised customer quality — the
   new, more-qualified vein pays more, makes more, and requires less work.
4. **Retention is decided early — at two points.** Customers in a recurring business decide
   whether to stay (a) between the sale and first service/onboarding, and (b) during the
   onboarding/orientation itself. Treating onboarding as an afterthought loses money;
   choreograph it and transfer the skill (do it with them, have them duplicate it).
5. **Expectations are everything; sell below results.** Setting expectations too high
   ("selling hot") makes even great outcomes feel like failures. Sell on averages — or
   ideally the bottom 25% of results — so customers over-achieve against a low baseline and
   stay. Shift beliefs toward long-term behavior change rather than short-term hype.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2020-10-08)

- (paraphrase) Most companies don't score their leads, prospects, or customers by the
  value of that customer.
- (paraphrase) Vista, when acquiring a company, learns more about that company's customers
  than the founder and team know themselves, then finds and amplifies the high-LTV vein.
- (paraphrase) Vista's mantra is "three and three" — tripling their money in three years —
  and their playbook (internally "VSOPs") is treated as top-secret.
- (paraphrase) The most advanced operators still do the fundamentals; Vista sticks to
  simple systems and simple processes.
- (paraphrase) Applying customer scoring at Gym Launch: analyzing 90 days (roughly
  Jul–Sep 2020) of exits and successes revealed the winning profile — full-time gym owner,
  signed lease/own building, ≥25 clients, ideally ≥1 employee.
- (paraphrase) After qualifying the front end for October 2020, monthly gym sign-ups fell
  from 100+ to about 40–50, but escalations dropped from 22 to zero, and the more-qualified
  customers paid more and required less servicing.
- (paraphrase) Entrepreneurs (non-gym-owners) were found to be a poor demographic for Gym
  Launch's intensive program; better served, if at all, by a self-paced info product.
- (paraphrase) A brief experiment selling a full-year up-front package worked worse than a
  "defined end" program (a 90-day sprint framing); defined-end is easier for customers to
  grasp.
- (paraphrase) Customers decide whether to stay at two early points: between sale and first
  service, and during onboarding/orientation.
- (paraphrase) Expectation management is everything — setting expectations too high
  ("selling hot") turns even a $20k-first-month or 5-lb-in-14-days result into a
  disappointment; sell on averages or the bottom 25% of results and then over-deliver.
- (paraphrase) Gym Launch changed its marketing/testimonials to show average results
  (e.g., ~$16k in the first six weeks) rather than only outlier results (e.g., $100k),
  explaining that half of customers get less than the average.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The people who are the most advanced never don't do the fundamentals."

> "They know how to extract and amplify value. And the biggest thing is not change... they
> tweak the pricing, they find the right buckets, etc., but they do that by analyzing the
> customers better than the business that they're purchasing even knows their own customers."

> "The best data is from all the people who leave."

> "Expectations are everything... And this is what we call selling hot. It's where you set
> expectations so high it's impossible for you to ever fulfill."

> "In an ideal world you would probably try and sell on the bottom 25% of results so that
> the expectations are so low that everyone is excited about the fact that they're
> overachieving from that baseline that you have set for them."

> "Customers in a recurring based business, or really any business, decide whether they're
> going to stay at two points at the beginning of your life cycle."

> "This new vein of customer who's higher qualified is paying more, is making more,
> requires less work. Now that sounds like a recipe for a higher profit business."
