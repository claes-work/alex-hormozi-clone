# Building a $2,500,000 Business for a Stranger in 36 Minutes

## Summary

Live-build coaching session in which Alex Hormozi works through the growth constraints of Corey, owner of an HVAC/duct-cleaning company (ProShine) doing ~$1.25M/yr. Alex diagnoses the business as being at the "productize" stage of his scaling roadmap and prescribes a prioritized set of incremental improvements: a 10% price raise, paying off debt, a dedicated ads landing page, ad-creative and ad-spend changes, reactivation email campaigns, brand-term ownership, and a productized affiliate offer. A one-year follow-up shows the business on track for $2.3–2.5M. Only Alex's advice and frameworks are persona-relevant.

## Key claims (Alex's advice / frameworks)

- **Supply- vs. demand-constrained is the first diagnosis.** Right off the bat in any business, determine whether it is supply-constrained or demand-constrained: if you can double lead flow and they can handle it, it's demand-constrained; if not, you must first build the resources/infrastructure. A common mistake is fixing a problem that, once fixed, makes an existing problem worse.
- **Growth is usually incremental, not a Hail Mary** — "consistent yardage," not big swings, especially when margins are healthy and nothing is majorly broken. Don't break what's working.
- **Raising prices can raise close rate for a normal business.** For a normal (non-Veblen) product, raising price increases the customer's conviction that you can deliver, which raises perceived likelihood of achievement on the value equation, and therefore perceived value. Underpricing makes you look like a "seedy duct-tape operation."
- **Pricing is often the biggest lever for sub-$3–5M businesses.** An 80%+ close rate signals room to raise price. He references the "virtuous cycle of pricing" (Offers book, ~p.48): raise price → increase emotional investment → increase perceived value → increase results → decrease customer demandingness.
- **Price-raise math:** a 10% price raise only needs the close rate to stay above ~65% to make more money; for this business a 10% raise translated to roughly a 25% increase in net profit (~$100K+/yr at prior volume).
- **Debt increases risk, and risk over a long horizon comes due.** Alex skews toward the Dave Ramsey (debt-free) end vs. the Wall Street end; enduring companies like Chick-fil-A operate debt-free. Businesses have seasons and volatility. Paying off debt is partly an emotional / peace-of-mind lever.
- **Build a dedicated ads landing page, not the homepage** — it serves one function: conversion. Every extra step/click typically loses ~50% of traffic. Shrink the top nav bar (prime above-the-fold real estate), remove site navigation, lead with a clear offer, add FAQs and locations.
- **Redirect all site buttons to the optimized conversion page** to convert more of existing traffic.
- **If ROI holds, spend more on ads.** "If for every dollar you gave me I gave you $5 back, your budget should be as many dollars as you possibly could." Businesses getting 10:1–30:1 on tiny spend should scale it. If unsure of returns, you have a data/attribution problem — fix that first. Leveling up in business = taking greater risk for greater reward.
- **Judge ad creative at phone size.** Your CMO should text you the ad image because that's how big it will be on someone's phone. If text/phone number/CTA can't be read at that size, it fails. Use high contrast; don't worry about logos in direct response.
- **Lead with the offer; run many creative variations.** Put the offer ("free 18-point inspection — get yours") as the dominant element, run ~40 variations (color/background combos), and let the algorithm test. Show pictures of the actual work and happy customers near the unit.
- **Repurpose best organic posts into ads** by tacking a ~5-second CTA on the end; cheapest way to test new ads is to post them organically first.
- **Same message, said the same way repeatedly, looks cheap and hurts the brand.** Say the same thing in different ways, not identically.
- **Reactivation emails are high-leverage, near-pure profit.** Just telling past customers you exist generates business. Strong angles: "I owe you $X / a free checkup" (high open rate), a "we messed up, let us make it right" frame, savings stats (ask customers for before/after bills), and standout case studies. Estimated 20–30% revenue lift, and it compounds as the business grows.
- **Emphasize "at no cost to you"** in bold/underline because people scan emails.
- **Use seasonal pain points to get year-round coverage.** With one or two seasonal peaks (pollen/allergies in spring, mildew smell in fall/winter — analogous to summer and New Year's in the gym world), you run pre/during/post campaigns twice a year across email, ads, and PPC. Communicate a single theme to the CMO for a ~2-month window.
- **Own your brand terms in search.** Advertise on your own name and branded permutations even if you have to pay per click — otherwise a competitor can outbid you on your own terms and scoop your customers. You will always be able to outbid them profitably on your own name. Also set up cross-platform retargeting.
- **A real affiliate program needs a compelling offer for the partner, not just "I'll send you business, you send me business"** — reciprocal referral is unscalable because you can't generate enough volume to feed 20 partners. Give the partner the highest-gross-margin, lower-ticket item (his $175 dryer-vent cleaning) as a lead magnet: they sell it, keep 100% of the money, no work; Alex/you eat the delivery cost and upsell the core offer once inside. (This is offer #2 of three affiliate structures in the $100M Leads book, ~pp.223, 237–239.)
- **CAC math for the affiliate lead magnet:** cost per customer = (cost to deliver the lead magnet) × (conversion rate from lead-magnet buyer to core offer). At ~$25–100 delivery cost and ~1-in-3 conversion, CAC is trivial relative to a $3–4K core sale — "who cares?"
- **Force a choice between plays.** He tells Corey to pick one of the two affiliate/outbound avatars rather than both, because each needs a dedicated full-time owner.
- **Sequence improvements so upstream fixes may remove the need for downstream ones.** Fix the landing page and ads first; that alone may double/triple lead flow, at which point you may hit supply constraints before ever needing to increase spend or do outbound.
