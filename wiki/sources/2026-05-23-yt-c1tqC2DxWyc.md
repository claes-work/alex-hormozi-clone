# Why Your Brain Hates Finishing Tasks

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says beginning work is the highest-leverage productivity move because output
per unit of time rises once work actually starts. He describes compressing the delay
between thinking about work and beginning it, and he uses open loops, unfinished
sentences, and a no-shortcuts refrain to get back to the work faster.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Beginning work is the highest-leverage productivity hack because output rises after
  work has started.
- Alex says large mental tasks often require only a few minutes of actual engagement
  before the shape of the work becomes clearer.
- He has trained the gap between "I should start" and "I started" to compress over time.
- Stopping mid-sentence can reduce the activation energy required to restart the next
  day.
- Alex uses David Goggins's no-shortcuts refrain as a way to shorten the path back to the
  work.
- He wrote his "the work just needs doing" thought during an 11th run-through of a recent
  presentation.
- His normal workday is self-reported as about 10 to 12 concentrated hours, six and a
  half days per week.
- In "triple shift" periods, he says he works after dinner until late and reaches 16 to
  17 hours of productive work.
- One of his internal refrains is that he will do what is required.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The moment you begin working is when your output per unit of time goes up."

> "Stop halfway through the sentence because it'll drive you mad."

> "I will do what is required."
