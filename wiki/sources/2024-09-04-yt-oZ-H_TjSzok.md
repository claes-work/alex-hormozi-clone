# No BS Advice to Get Rich in the Next 10 Years

## Summary

A solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi lays out ~17 numbered rules for making and
keeping money, framed as the lessons that separate the two times he lost all his money
from the run that took him to a $100M net worth at age 31. The through-line is that
money is "bottled time": there is no truly passive income, only a spectrum of
return-on-time, so the highest-leverage move is to invest money and effort into
multiplying your own active income and skills rather than chasing passive returns or
status. He covers saving as optionality, the 10-year mindset, partnerships, buying
time over stuff, paying down "ignorance," and automating investing while adding friction
to spending.

## Key claims

- Passive income is "a lie" — active vs. passive is a spectrum; what matters is return
  on time (money made per hour). Everyone trades hours for money; it's just not always
  denominated in hours.
- To find your true hourly rate: divide last year's earnings by ~2,000 (40-hr work
  week) or by ~8,000 for your per-hour-alive rate. If an activity pays more than that,
  it's worth your time.
- He realized his best activity paid ~15x his average hourly, so he should do more of
  it, then find something higher — instead of trying to convert active income into
  passive income, invest money to multiply the active income that already works.
- "If you can't buy it twice, don't buy it once." Price purchases in post-tax hours of
  your own labor; feeling that time-cost changes buying behavior.
- Saving money doesn't make you money, but it gives you money, which lets you look
  further out, take bigger risks, and thereby make more money.
- 10-year framing: people overestimate a year and underestimate a decade. He pitches
  new hires a "10-year, add-a-zero (or two) to your net worth" contract; if they'd sign
  it, they should act like it now and make reasonable compounding bets so winning
  becomes inevitable rather than a gamble.
- Invest in your potential earning (time, experience, skills) over grabbing the biggest
  immediate paycheck — e.g., take the internship that 10x's your potential over the
  $100K job. "Put in the you 500" instead of the S&P 500; the asset that is you beats
  the stock market.
- "You should only have to get rich once" — don't bet what you have and need on
  something you don't have and don't need.
- Partnerships: a partner must clearly contribute one of three things you lack — money,
  experience, or time/energy — and must actually contribute it. He had 9 failed
  partnerships before Leila; equity is the most expensive thing you own, and one of his
  two big losses came from a partner he took on out of insecurity.
- "If you never learn to manage your time you're never going to learn to manage your
  money." Money is an encapsulated version of time; the denomination represents less
  time as you get wealthier.
- Check your bank account before social media every morning; "what gets measured gets
  managed" (Peter Drucker). Measurement alone drives improvement (weight-loss analogy).
  He checked daily from the hundreds up to ~$20M net worth, then shifted to
  monthly/quarterly.
- "Learn how to make money before you try to learn how to make money make money" —
  small capital ($1,000) is better invested into your income-earning skill than into
  seeking compounding returns; adding 50% to your skillset beats a 10→20% return.
- Everyone has 5–9 AM and 5–9 PM free (8 hrs/day) even with a full-time job or studies.
  Work makes money twice: you earn, and while working you aren't spending.
- Patience = "figuring out activities to do in the meantime" — you work while money
  compounds.
- He skipped business school: it would cost ~$180K plus 2 years' forgone income for an
  avg ~$10K/month outcome; he saved $50K, invested it in learning to make money, and
  hit ~$10K/month himself.
- "If you don't know how you can lose money in something, you're going to lose it."
  Map your downside, assign a likelihood, then multiply by three because entrepreneurs
  are optimists.
- "The biggest cost you have is ignorance." The cost of ignorance = the gap between
  your goals and your current reality. He prioritizes paying down ignorance with
  whatever currency (relational capital, favors, then money as the least efficient).
- He paid $10K (first info purchase), then $25K and $35K to get into rooms (a gym-owner
  room before he had a gym; an internet-marketing room before he had that business),
  funded by his $50K savings.
- "Don't half-ass a free gift" — if you do a favor, do it in full, treat them like a
  paying customer, and reciprocity follows. He gave sales help in those rooms and built
  a network he could barter with (bartering avoids tax; "Uncle Sam don't take a piece").
- Two kinds of knowledge: declarative (that something exists — unknown unknowns, the
  biggest breakthroughs) and procedural (how to do it). Know which you're buying.
- Document, demonstrate, duplicate — his method for learning a skill from an expert.
  He paid an ads expert $750/hr for 8 sessions to master national-scale ads.
- "A watch isn't going to replace work; a Rolex isn't going to replace reality." You
  only impress people below you with assets; impress poor people by outspending them,
  rich people by outworking them. Overspending on status scales up (watch → car → house
  → yacht → jet) but never impresses those above you.
- "Buy time like a rich person, buy stuff like a poor person." Buy back low-income time
  (cleaning, groceries, food prep, laundry). Avg American spends ~$1,500/mo to replace
  ~96 hours of draining chores; if you earn >$15/hr it's logical to buy that time back
  and go earn more — and you get better at your job with the reclaimed high-energy hours.
- "Who you compare yourself to, not who you spend time with," is the highest predictor
  of how much you make. We're motivated by what we lack; to want money you must see
  people making far more. The richest man compares himself to his future self.
- "There will always be time to make money later" is what a poor person says. Spend-now-
  work-later is "the fundamental crux of poverty." Only listen to opinions from people
  whose dreams for you are bigger than your own.
- "Invest, then spend the rest." Switzerland has the highest millionaires per capita
  without the highest GDP — different money rules produce different outcomes.
- Operationalize: automate investing, add friction to spending. Take money off before
  you see it; as you earn more, keep the spend amount fixed (not a percentage) and
  invest the rest. Automate deposits, manualize withdrawals (pay bills by hand to feel
  them). He owns zero Lamborghinis, Rolexes, or jets while able to buy several monthly.
- "It's easier to make a million dollars than $100,000." $100K assumes trading time via
  wages; bigger targets force you to change your "solution set" and use different tools
  (block-running/car analogy).
- "You don't want to love money; you want to learn to love making money." Money isn't
  good or bad — it's potential energy / bottled time that can build bombs or hospitals.
  ("Love of money," not money, is the root of all kinds of evil — 1 Timothy 6:10.)
- Career earnings = (total value you create) × (your ability to negotiate your share) ×
  (scarcity — how few others can create that value). Uniqueness comes from your stack of
  skills, which is your moat because no one else has lived your life.
- Closing: "Money loves speed, wealth loves time, poverty loves indecision."
