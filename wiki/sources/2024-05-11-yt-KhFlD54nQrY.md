# 13 Years Of Brutally Honest Business Advice in 90 Mins

## Summary
In this solo, ~90-minute compilation, Alex Hormozi distills 13 years of business into eleven "brutal business truths," each stated up front and then unpacked with stories and examples. The through-lines are talent (the best people are the single biggest arbitrage in business), focus (strategy is prioritization; the big obvious problem is usually the problem), and product/brand quality (better leads to bigger; you make the money in the edit, not the creation). He anchors the lessons in his own arc — gyms, Gym Launch, acquisition.com, and his co-ownership of Skool — plus outside examples (Tesla, Amazon/Walmart, Netflix, Chick-fil-A vs. Boston Market, Google AdSense, Elon Musk's mission-framing).

## Key claims
- Sell to the rich until you have the money to serve the poor; "the middle is where you get killed." Either build for volume day one and win on efficiency (Amazon, Walmart), or build premium and niche down. You can't half-do both.
- Rich buyers pay for time above all, and for the perception of guaranteed outcomes (reputation substitutes for a literal guarantee); you sell the wealthy on time saved, not money saved. Easiest hack: do what an existing premium provider does, in half the time, at twice the price.
- "You lack priorities, not information." Strategy is the prioritization of limited resources against unlimited options. Most stuck entrepreneurs haven't defined current state, desired state, and obstacle.
- People solve the problems they enjoy solving (a sales guy asks about closing when sales isn't his constraint), not the actual constraint. The individual contributor doubles down on strengths, but the business as a whole must be balanced — it's only as strong as its weakest link.
- Hire to raise the average bar of the team (Amazon rule). Uncontrolled, companies dilute talent — sixes hire fives, fives hire fours — because people hire who won't threaten them.
- Lots of stupid rules ("don't watch Netflix on a service call") is a tell that your standards are too low and you've hired people who need them. Great teams get problems, not 100-step processes (Google AdSense story: "These ads suck. Go fix it.").
- Bring in someone with 10 years of experience so the business "starts on year 10" and you don't pay for the scars. A-level talent is a marketplace with a rate; if you won't pay it, a smarter company will.
- Paying A-talent A-wages for 10x output is a fundamental arbitrage — and the multiple grows as you spend more. His examples: first $50K employee → 5x; first $300K salesperson ($5M in sales) → ~14x; first $1M executive (saved $3M in 90 days, then enabled the sale) → 50x.
- "If you want to get bigger, get better." Better leads to growth; bigger-for-its-own-sake leads to bloat (Chick-fil-A vs. Boston Market). Missionaries beat mercenaries. If money is the goal, too many paths make you distracted and unfocused.
- Growth is a lagging indicator; you can't make an outcome into an input. Get better via "100 golden BBs, not one silver bullet" — a compounding checklist per function, one improvement stacked each quarter.
- Let some fires burn. Almost nothing kills a business in the short term (payment processing going down is a rare exception). Protect the first 4–6 uninterrupted hours of the day for the highest-leverage work; take meetings only in the afternoon, and book urgent meetings from the back of the day forward.
- Ask "what's the one thing that, if it were true, would make all my other problems go away?" and put all effort there.
- Brand is the most valuable thing you can own: higher ROAS, pricing far above market, and customers who only buy from you. It's built by (1) what you say/show, (2) what others say, and (3) what customers experience — the experience weighs the most. It compounds like a 401k; measure the same metrics as direct response but over a longer horizon.
- Direct-response tactics (lead magnets, nurture sequences, 5-day challenges) are attempts to approximate brand/trust in a compressed time window; big brand is doing that across millions of people at once.
- Know your money-making system's inputs and outputs down to the smallest action so you can crank it. Diagnose constraints by asking "why can't we 10x this?" ("why aren't we spending $50k/day on ads?"). The vaguer the direction you can give, the more skilled the person must be — which is why smart people are also more communication-efficient.
- Stop looking for hacks and algorithm tricks; optimize for what the platform ultimately wants (something so good people share it). Quantity matters because it teaches how much work quality takes; you "make the money in the edit," in "coats of paint," not in creation.
- The big obvious thing is the problem, not the 100 other things. "Have you been a customer of your own product?" The hard truth is usually the one that hurts your ego — your food/service may just be mediocre, and SEO won't fix that.
- He's made his wealth in distinct arbitrage windows (gym: 30:1 ROAS on underpriced Facebook; Gym Launch: 100:1, $100k → $10M), then "backs the truck up." He invested in Skool only after watching it grow with zero marketing on word of mouth since 2018 — buy because of the association, stay because of the product.
