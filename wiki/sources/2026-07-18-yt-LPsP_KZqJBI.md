# What Actually Changes About Your Tuesday?

## Summary
This is a live coaching Q&A. The questioner, David Haas (audience), owns a glamping resort with 40 units doing just under $2M revenue and just under $500k profit last year. He wants to reach $20M. Glamping was always intended as the vehicle to eventually sell higher-ticket men's retreats — the model he's building his personal brand on. He asks Hormozi whether to keep doing more of the current model to build a bigger profit cushion, or to shift now into the retreat model and drive the higher ticket.

Hormozi's default answer is "do more" of what's working, but he adapts it to what David actually wants: David's passion is hosting the retreats, and he does not want to sell the business eventually. Given that, Hormozi endorses the shift. His core diagnostic is a decision frame for organizational change: ask "what does it actually change about my day-to-day?" For David that means leading retreats he isn't currently running, hiring and training a sales team, building funnels, and running ads to hit volume. Hormozi reverse-engineers the goal: selling a ~$5,000 / 2-day retreat for the men he described, ramped to two retreats a week, mathematically produces $36M/year using only the existing 40 glamping sites.

Hormozi also weighs sellability: going from 40 to 400 glamping sites is a "high-likelihood bet" and a sellable asset, whereas the retreat business has "more hair on the deal" and is harder to sell. Because David doesn't intend to sell and his passion is the retreats, that concern matters less. Hormozi frames the existing asset as the thing that earned David the right to deliver a differentiated experience — "what got you here isn't going to get you to what you want next," but there's nothing wrong with the path that got him the 40 campsites. He recommends a staircase ramp (one per quarter → one per month → one per week → two per week) driven by paid ads, plus a back-end move: get guests to book their next visit immediately to create recurring revenue.
