# Watch What They Do, Ignore What They Say

## Summary

Alex describes learning as observation and replication. He studies the top 1%-10% of
performers, copies observable behavior rather than their explanations, and then uses his
own first-party repetitions to identify the differences between top and average output.
He separates performing a skill from teaching it: excellence does not guarantee accurate
self-explanation.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says untracked output signals that the learner does not care
  enough to measure improvement.
- He recommends observing what top performers do rather than relying on what they say
  they do.
- Teaching and performing are different skills; he uses Michael Jordan as an
  illustrative example of elite performance not automatically producing elite coaching.
- Alex self-reports that early social difficulty forced him to study observable
  differences in how socially successful people behaved.
- Initial imitation will be incomplete because novices cannot yet see every micro-step
  that distinguishes good output.
- After enough repetitions, the learner should compare their own top 10% with the rest
  and build first-party insight.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you don't track, you don't care."

> "What do they do, not what they say about what they do?"

> "Teaching is a different skill than doing."

## Notes

Reinforces observable learning, modeling, and first-party feedback loops.
