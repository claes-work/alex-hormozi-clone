# How to Make Offers for Equity

## Summary

A short coaching clip in which Alex answers a founder's question about structuring offers in
exchange for equity — what he wishes he'd known on his first deal. Two speakers: an
interviewer/coachee asks the questions and pushes back briefly; all substantive teaching is
Alex. He splits the topic in two. First, why he personally no longer does **consulting-for-equity**
deals: the equity someone will part with never justifies the open-ended work expected, and being
"owned" for X hours a week is the antithesis of the freedom he built his life around. He'd rather
just write a check. The only version he tolerates is a bounded **advisor** relationship (e.g. meet
once a quarter, defined role). Second, he reframes the coachee's specific business — a **roll-up**
in a vertical with heavy M&A activity — as a clean **arbitrage offer**: because the coachee already
does the integrator's work (unifying price points, messaging, CRM on the front end), the pitch to
targets is a valuation uplift they can't get alone, split so the seller keeps the large majority.
He closes on the deal mechanics that make a roll-up hold together: **decision-making authority** and
**tag-along rights** negotiated up front, because any one participant can blow up the collective exit.

This complements Alex's recent **four-elements-of-equity** framework (control / risk / profits /
sale-upside, each synthetically contractable — see [[2026-02-24-yt-0U6nqErQcRM]]): that page is about
*what equity is made of*; this clip is about *how to structure an offer to earn or acquire it*.

## Key claims (2026-02-20, paraphrased unless quoted)

- ★ **He no longer does consulting-for-equity deals** — the equity a counterparty is willing to give
  never justifies the amount of open-ended work expected; the trade isn't worth it to him.
- **He'd rather just write a check** and wish the founder good luck, then negotiate any specific help
  (e.g. promotion, brand assistance) *separately* as its own clean transaction.
- **His objection is structural, not stingy:** he dislikes the "handshake of unclear expectations
  around generic help," and he especially dislikes "I now belong to you for X hours per week,"
  which he calls the antithesis of the freedom he got into business for.
- **The one acceptable form is a bounded advisor relationship** — a clear, defined role (e.g. meet
  once a quarter, alongside other advisors). It's not his preferred way of doing business, but the
  role exists and some people run it well.
- ★ **For a roll-up, the offer is a pure arbitrage pitch:** frame it as a valuation uplift the seller
  can't reach alone. His worked example — "on your own you sell at 4x; with me you sell at 9x; I give
  you 7 of those 9 and take 2." Most sellers prefer getting 7 to getting 4.
- **Disqualify spite:** if a seller would rather take 3 (instead of 7) just to deny you your 2, they're
  not the right fit for a roll-up — you need team players.
- ★ **A roll-up requires complete decision-making authority rolled into the deal**, because any single
  participant can mess up the collective transaction; that authority must be negotiated up front.
- ★ **Tag-along rights are negotiated up front** and sold with the line: "you don't want anyone else
  here to screw up *your* exit" — the shared-exit risk is what justifies giving the integrator
  control.
- **The trust hinge:** as long as all participants trust the integrator is incentivized to make the
  deal happen, they can grant decision-making authority — conditional on clearing an agreed valuation
  / multiple for the aggregate.
- **Why the coachee earns the spread:** the roll-up operator is already doing the private-equity
  integrator's work up front (unifying price points, messaging, and back-end CRM across the acquired
  businesses), so capturing part of the multiple expansion is justified.
