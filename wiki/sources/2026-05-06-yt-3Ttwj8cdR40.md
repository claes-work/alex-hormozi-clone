# The #1 Thing That Separates You From 99% of Your Competition

## Summary

Alex argues that the bar for excellence is low because most competitors quit at the first
sign of difficulty and have never seen what tremendous effort actually looks like. The
core distinction is not knowing a tactic but experiencing enough volume and exposure to
understand consistency. He uses Leila's mentor's question about a house they were pursuing
-- "Are you settling or did you move mountains?" -- to explain that people should work
backward from assumed success and then negotiate the price with themselves.

## Key claims

- Most people do not know what hard work looks like because consistency cannot be seen in
  a snapshot, sound bite, or reel.
- Some lessons require long exposure before they become learnable; "trying" is one of
  them.
- If the trade were clear enough, many people would trade far more for the thing they
  say they want; the constraint is often that they have never deconstrained their
  thinking about effort.
- His decision frame for effort is to start from guaranteed success, list increasingly
  expensive ways to get it, and then work backward until the price is one he is willing
  to pay.
- Self-reported: high-volume sayings such as "volume negates luck" and "violence is the
  answer" are posted around his office because they have been true in his experience.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you cannot count the amount of repetitions that you're having in the hundreds, that is not trying."

> "You negotiate the cost with yourself backwards from the assumed success."
