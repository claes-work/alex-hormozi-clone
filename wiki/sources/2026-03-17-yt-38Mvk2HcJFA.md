# Brutally Honest Advice for Relationships

## Summary
Podcast clip (Alex in conversation with a host — apparently Chris Williamson: references to being "unmarried at 37," Louise Perry, Zach's wedding, and the host reading Darwin's and Feynman's letters). Alex lays out his **rationalist theory of partnership and marriage**, built around choosing a partner with your head while the "love drug" is active. When deciding whether to marry Leila, he was genuinely unsure and asked a mentor, whose "most Hormozian" advice was to *look at your stats*: business up, eating better, working out, less stressed — she had created the space for all of it. That reframe ("what has changed about my life" rather than the "chemical compound" of feeling) made it an easy yes; his "proposal" was a statement ("I think it would make sense if we got married"), not a question. He argues most people chase the "fireworks" (passionate attachment) rather than the slow-heating "coal furnace" (companionate attachment) that endures good times and bad — and that **falling in love is choosing to deal yourself drugs for 6–24 months**, during which you can't trust your own judgment, so you must make the most rational decision up front. He warns women especially against the "put it off / no rush" cultural message because biology hasn't advanced with culture (he does the arithmetic on the kids-timeline back from "geriatric pregnancy" at 35). He rejects the soulmate/"perfect pick" fallacy: don't seek a partner who completes you, seek one who helps you become the highest-potential version of yourself; "the person you marry marries two people — the person you are and the person you want to become." He describes his relationship with Leila as **incredibly traditional** despite its public "modern power-couple" framing — she chose to support her husband in every possible way, including becoming his business partner — and pushes back on the idea that strong women can't have this: they need an even stronger man. Practical closers for younger men: hardship early ("she saw me lose everything twice") proves she likes you for you; find a *competent* wife because the incentives take care of themselves once married; and, contra Darwin, **getting married makes you more money** because you reclaim the third of your mental effort otherwise spent "chasing tail." Ends with the acquisition.com roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends deciding whether to marry by **looking at objective life outcomes ("stats")** — is your business, health, stress, and behavior better with this person — rather than trusting the "chemical compound" of feeling; a mentor's version of this made his decision to marry Leila easy.
- As of March 2026, he frames attachment as **passionate vs. companionate**: most people chase the "fireworks" but the durable relationship is the slow "coal furnace" — "if it makes sense without emotion, it will make sense when the emotions are gone."
- As of March 2026, "falling in love" is a **6–24 month endogenous drug state** (dopamine/norepinephrine, serotonin drop) during which you cannot trust your own decision-making — so you must make the most rational decision **up front**, betting that's where you'll land after the window closes.
- As of March 2026, he warns (especially women) against the cultural "delay / no rush" message because **biology has not advanced with culture** — working back from a ~35 "geriatric pregnancy" cutoff and wanting multiple kids leaves a "very small window"; "it's easier to work with reality the way it is, not the way you hope it's going to land." (He notes this is null if you don't want kids.)
- As of March 2026, he **rejects the soulmate / "perfect pick" fallacy** — no one partner completes you; the best thing a partner does is "help you be the best version of yourself," and other friends can fulfill other needs (increased cohabitation/"surface area" is a modern change from ~50 years ago).
- As of March 2026, the filter isn't "perfect partner" but **"will this person make me better or hold me back from my goals?"** plus checking non-negotiables (kids, travel); part of the excitement of life is "learning how to be married."
- As of March 2026, he describes his and Leila's relationship as **"incredibly traditional"** despite the public "modern power-couple" image — she chose to "support my husband in every possible way to help him achieve what he wants," including becoming his business partner; he says strong women can have this but need "an even stronger man."
- As of March 2026 (advice to younger men): going through **hardship together early** builds deep bonding and proof of love — Leila (23) and Alex (26) went through him "losing everything twice" before marriage, so he felt confident she liked him for him, not the "institution."
- As of March 2026, "find a **competent** wife — the incentives take care of themselves once you get married," framed via competence vs. incentives.
- As of March 2026, contra Darwin's "less money for books," **getting married makes you more money** — you reclaim the ~third of mental effort spent "chasing tail" and gauging intentions in modern dating.
- As of March 2026, "the person you marry **marries two people** — the person you are and the person you want to become; make sure they love both," because a partner is either the thing preventing your growth or "a rocket pack" for it.
