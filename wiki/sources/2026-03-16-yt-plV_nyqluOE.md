# Helping a Coffee Company Doing $4M/Year Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A coffee-company owner does $3-4M/year across three brick-and-mortar cafes (the third opened two months prior) plus an $800k vertically-integrated roasting operation that also sells wholesale B2B to ~15 reliable recurring accounts. He wants to reach $10M retail / 20 locations (targeting $30-50M in ten years) and frames his constraint as the roasting side, which he must keep to vertically integrate as he scales — but which needs the wholesale volume to stay operational. Alex's core reframe: **the problem is not over-expansion, it's under-talent and broken unit economics — you're "under talented," not over expanded.** He homes in on the retail margins, which the owner reports at 15% (vs. 30% wholesale), and declares them the thing to fix before adding any locations: "You have to make those stores so profitable that you don't know what to do with all the money you're making," and *then* opening more becomes inevitable and self-funding. Wanting more locations because you want more money is "not the right frame of mind." He advises leaving the wholesale business alone as long as it takes **zero** attention (it already runs on two employees) — its purpose is not to make more money but to preserve vertical integration ("your special beans"), so **no new wholesale customers**. The fix for retail is explicitly *not* one sexy bullet but "a hundred small things": deep financial review, staffing/hours optimization, rethinking team comp to save on payroll taxes while adding flexibility, menu engineering (reorder the menu to push higher-margin items; cut the ~50% of items rarely ordered that trap cash in inventory), cashier upsell scripts with an incentive structure to lift attach rates, and better customer acquisition. On ads: coffee/food marketing "almost always has to be grassroots," but there are high-impact tactics that genuinely drive foot traffic. His prescription is to **triple the profit of the three existing stores rather than open six more** — get retail margins to 40%+ — after which the expansion becomes inevitable and un-strapped. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's diagnosis for a multi-location owner feeling pressure to expand: "you're not over expanded, you're under talented" — the constraint is talent and unit economics, not footprint.
- As of March 2026, the correct frame for brick-and-mortar is to make existing stores so profitable you don't know what to do with the cash, at which point opening more becomes inevitable and self-funded; wanting more locations merely to make more money is the wrong frame of mind.
- As of March 2026, this owner's retail margins (~15%) must be fixed to ~40%+ before scaling to 20 locations; you should not scale a 15%-margin store.
- As of March 2026, a support/wholesale arm is acceptable to keep only if it takes zero of the owner's attention (already run by two employees); its purpose is vertical integration, not revenue, so it should take **no new customers**.
- As of March 2026, fixing retail margins is "a hundred small things," not one lever: staffing/hours mix, team comp restructuring (more flexibility while saving on payroll taxes), menu layout to push high-margin items, cashier upsell scripts with an incentive structure to raise attach rates, and acquisition.
- As of March 2026, roughly 50%+ of a typical cafe menu's items are rarely ordered and tie up cash in inventory — cutting them optimizes the cash conversion cycle.
- As of March 2026, marketing for coffee/food "almost always has to be grassroots" (word of mouth), though specific high-impact tactics can drive meaningful foot traffic; paid ads alone are secondary.
- As of March 2026, vertical integration compresses reported margins because coffee is the biggest COGS; buying at retail from a separate roasting company keeps the segments' books distinct.
- As of March 2026, Alex's recommendation is to triple the profit of the three current stores rather than open six more — do that and the expansion becomes inevitable with more than enough cash flow, removing the felt pressure.
