# 'Restaurant or Catering: Which Do I Keep?'

## Summary

barbecue — ~80% barbecue catering plus a restaurant open a couple of days a week. He
reports **$1.2M last year** (almost $1M of it catering) and wants **$10M in 3 years**;
he believes he's underpriced and says another advisor ("Ed") told him he has a broken

need to choose between the two businesses; either *could* scale, but the restaurant

constraint is the kitchen, Alex's verdict is that the catering business itself is
fine; he "just needs a bigger kitchen" plus more leads. **(2) Volatility is not
inherently a defect.** Seasonality is part of a food/celebration business; the job is
to print money in season to cover the off-season. Proof points: Harry & David makes

makes the vast majority of its profit in Q4. **(3) Reframe: this isn't volatility, it's

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **To scale far beyond current size, pick one of the two businesses.** Between a
  restaurant and a catering operation, catering is realistically the more scalable:
  either could scale, but the restaurant is limited by its four walls relatively
  quickly, while catering in a local area can make a lot of money without that cap.
- **A kitchen capacity constraint doesn't mean the model is broken.** Once the
  constraint is identified as production capacity, the catering business itself is
  fine — the fix is a bigger kitchen (plus more leads), not a model change. (Implicit
  pushback on the questioner's report that another advisor called the model broken.)
- **Volatility/seasonality is not by itself a business defect.** In a seasonal
  business you make sure you're printing money in the on-season to cover the
  off-season. Examples: Harry & David makes ~80% of revenue in December and loses
  money the other 11 months; See's Candies makes the vast majority of its profit in
  Q4. Food/celebration businesses naturally spike.
- **Diagnose before accepting "volatility": strong spring, fall, and Q4 means three
  of four quarters are fine** — that's not volatility, it's a shortage of leads; a
  couple more leads smooths the curve.
- **Beware artificial constraints that become self-fulfilling prophecies.**
  "March is always a bad month for us" often has no actual reason — it's historical
  pattern hardening into expectation.
- **The worst-month thought experiment.** If, in your traditionally worst month, you
  could scoop up everyone else's catering business and that would become your best
  month, then market demand exists year-round and your problem is market share, not
  seasonality. The move is to out-market the competition, which smooths the demand
  curve: "You don't have volatility. You just need better marketing."
- **Lead-gen prescription for local catering: PPC first, then Meta.** Paid ads
  (Meta) would probably work fine, but PPC is the best starting channel because the
  leads are highest-intent and warmest — people actively searching for a caterer.
  Expect to pay for leads for the first time.
- **Paid leads must be worked; referrals spoiled you.** Unlike word-of-mouth leads,
  paid leads won't immediately get on the phone and hand over money. The required
  machinery: a landing page, a lead-nurture process, getting leads scheduled, a
  short VSL before the call to frame what they're getting into, then closing on the
  phone. "You have to advertise."
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at
  acquisition.com/roadmap: ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies distilled
  into 10 growth stages with a quiz that locates a business and prescribes actions
  per function (product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR,
  finance); thank-you page books a call, with an in-person Vegas session if they
  can help.
