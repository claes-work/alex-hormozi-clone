# Why Most People Don't Win

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-screen host asks questions marked `>>`; Alex answers). The through-line is that most people don't win because they seek **permission not to** — they assemble a "reasonable" excuse that protects their ego, then look for someone to validate it. Alex's counter-move is deliberate agreement: when someone says "I can't grow my business because of my kids" or "I had a terrible upbringing," he answers "You're right. It's not your fault. You're never going to be successful" — and watches them rebel against the verdict. People fight for their success once it's denied, so the excuse they were defending becomes "the very reason you should do the thing." He concedes that childhood conditioning and ingrained neural patterns are genuinely hard to break, but insists belief is still a **choice**, and that a cruel upbringing is "a great reason to be successful," citing the many people with terrible childhoods who succeeded anyway. The second half develops his **doing-over-being** thesis: he rejects the personal-development mantra "be, do, have" as backwards. Being is a function of doing (we describe someone's identity by their actions — "he is a carpenter"), and having is an aftereffect of doing, so you can "cut out" both being and having and it "comes down to doing." Practical payoff: you don't need to feel confident, be in a good mood, or identify as a "shining knight hero" to send the 100 emails, make the calls, or ask the girl out — the same activity yields the same result regardless of mood. The host pushes back (wouldn't a good mood let you craft a 1%-better email?); Alex holds that if the *doing* is held constant, mood doesn't change the outcome, and his real target is "a person who's waiting to be in a good mood to work," because "sometimes a good mood never comes" while "the work still needs doing." He frames this detachment of self-worth from action as reassuring — it puts results back in his control and gives him "shelter in hard times." He also notes he believes very few things "through and through" and treats most of the rest — energy, manifesting, affirmations — as "noise" and "fluff," often from people who never did the thing they advise on.
