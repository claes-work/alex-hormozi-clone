# How to Make Decisions

## Summary
Solo monologue on decision-making, framed around **speed over deliberation**. Alex's core argument: by the time you have all the information, the opportunity is gone, so you have to make your bet. It's almost always faster to make a decision, be wrong, and correct the mistake than to painstakingly deliberate — so his practical rule is to weigh the cost of being wrong: if it takes 5 minutes to decide and 10 minutes to fix, don't spend 2 hours deciding. Either way you move forward faster than by waiting for certainty you'll never have. He reframes **uncertainty as "the job"**: certainty is only available on an "old dream" that already happened and won't happen again, so chasing a real dream means accepting uncertainty. The heaviest things in life aren't iron or gold but **unmade decisions** — they consume attention and brainpower, and simply deciding makes you feel yourself move faster. He stresses that **few decisions are actually irreversible** (even ones people pretend are): the real cost of a decision is the *delta* between what you could buy it for and sell it for, plus the (often large) cost of waiting. Bottleneck insight: people slow down at the *deciding*, not the *doing* — doing usually takes little time; deciding is the quagmire where people get stuck "trudging through mud." His speed lever: move 7x faster than others by shifting when you decide from end of week → end of day → end of hour → now. He models this with his own team, refusing to "circle back" or "kick it to next meeting" and instead forcing the choice on the spot (which vendor, which website design, which headline, which ad hook — "just pick"). It all comes down to bets: if you want the future you want, you have to get comfortable with risk. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
