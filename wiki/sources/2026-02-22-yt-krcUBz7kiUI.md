# Should You Focus on Cash Flow or Net Worth?

## Summary

In this teaching monologue, Alex Hormozi contrasts two identical-looking businesses ($10M top line, $2M bottom line) to argue that net worth beats cash flow: the owner who takes home ~$500K/year after taxes accumulates wealth slowly, while the owner of an equivalent but owner-independent business holds an asset worth a 6x multiple ($12M) that also multiplies every incremental dollar of profit. He frames building a sellable, self-running business as "the game of wealth," then walks through the operational steps to remove yourself from the day-to-day: a 15-minute time study, a self-inventory, and slotting each task into a project/process/person via red-yellow-green triage plus if-this-then-that decision rules with spending-authority caps.

## Key claims (cash-flow vs. net-worth position)

- Two businesses with identical top line ($10M) and bottom line ($2M) can differ **dramatically** in how rich their owners are — the difference is whether the owner is *required* to work in it or merely owns it.
- The hands-on owner ("frustrated Fred") nets $2M, pays ~50% tax, covers living expenses, and adds roughly **$500K/year to net worth** — "a slow way to accumulate cash."
- The absentee owner ("wealthy William") holds a business that trades at **6x profit** (6 × $2M = **$12M**), so he is "way richer" despite identical top/bottom line.
- On incremental profit the asymmetry compounds: an extra $500K of profit gives Fred ~$250K after tax, but gives William a 6x multiple — **another ~$3M** of net worth (his $12M becomes $15M).
- Building wealth off regular income is "**very, very inefficient**" because it is "taxed to oblivion" and gets **zero multiplication** — you get one year divided by two after taxes, not a multiple on years of work.
- The gap between a valuable, sellable company (10x) and one that can't sell is described as roughly a **20x difference**.
- **Step 1 to convert Fred into William: a self-inventory** — list literally everything you do, get maximally granular, and turn each item into something someone else can do so you exit the day-to-day "without breaking the machine."
- The step before that, if you don't even know what you do, is a **time study**: an Excel sheet with times every 15 minutes and a timer; note one word for what you did each interval. He claims it produces "the most productive week of your life."
- Each inventoried task gets slotted as a **project, process, or person**, then triaged **red/yellow/green**: green = can hand off now; yellow = a one-time project/process you know how to install; red = you don't know how or lack the needed person. Solve green → yellow → red.
- Beyond tasks, document **decisions** as "if this then that" — decision trees / rules of behavior for common scenarios. More duplicatable jobs and larger teams produce more standardized questions.
- Delegate decisions with **spending-authority caps** sized to company scale (under $500 / $1K / $10K / $100K), plus a total cap (e.g., decisions under $500 up to $5,000 total) so a person gets "10 shots to mess up." Financials at month-end catch anything out of whack.
