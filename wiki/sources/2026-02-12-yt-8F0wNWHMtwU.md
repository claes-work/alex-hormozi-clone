# How Much of Success Is Luck?

## Summary
In this short interview clip, a host asks Alex Hormozi whether it has become easier for people to become successful "by happenstance or by luck," and whether that has lowered the bar for excellence. Hormozi concedes that luck is real but says he's rarely seen it explain success among repeat and serial entrepreneurs; in his framing, luck acts like gasoline rather than the car itself — it accelerates people who were already going to succeed rather than manufacturing success on its own.

Asked for examples, he cites three historical windows of low-competition opportunity: selling on Amazon in 2013 when demand vastly outstripped seller sophistication, running Facebook ads in 2011 when it "wasn't hard to crush," and doing SEO around 2007–2008 when crude tricks (like white text on a white background stuffed with a keyword) could still rank a page because search algorithms hadn't caught up. He generalizes these into a definition: luck comes from a supply-demand discrepancy within a window of time, where demand is large and supply is small, and a person happens to be one of the few filling that gap. He adds a caveat that recognizing and acting on the opportunity is itself a skill, which blurs how much of the outcome is "luck," and offers a practical test: whether the success can be repeated is what distinguishes luck from skill.

The clip ends with a direct-to-camera pitch for Hormozi's free "10-stage roadmap from zero to $100 million+," which he says fewer than 1% of companies complete, broken down by eight business functions (constraint symptoms and the steps to graduate each stage) and validated across software, physical products, service businesses, and brick-and-mortar — available at acquisition.com/roadmap.
