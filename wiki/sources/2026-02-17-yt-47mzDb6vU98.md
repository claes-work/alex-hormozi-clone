# How I Train High-Performing Sales Teams

## Summary

Sales-training clip in which Alex Hormozi lays out the concrete mechanics acquisition.com
uses to turn ordinary reps into top performers. He opens with the common failure mode:
a team of ~3–5 reps where one star sells half the volume, suppresses the rest, and won't
follow the script or process — making the team impossible to scale because there's no
consistency across reps. Owners fantasize about cloning the star ("if I only had six
Sarahs"), and Alex's claim is that you actually *can* by (1) documenting what the star
does in far more detail than most businesses bother to, and (2) training far more
frequently with far faster reinforcement cycles. He stresses businesses radically
underestimate how much training a world-class team requires — acquisition.com's reps
train *every single day*, role-play every day, and get a weekly one-on-one with "game
tape" and their manager on what specifically to work on. He then details his signature
script-memorization drill (the black-out method), the "breathe the script" standard, the
interrupt-heavy roleplay method with "lock it in" reinforcement, and the philosophy of
process-above-everyone ("we are like the New England Patriots"). He closes on the culture
cost of the star: a cowboy/primadonna who ignores the process is a long-term cancer, and a
team of "all eights" on a 10/10 process will always out-perform one star with no process.
A second speaker (interviewer) appears once, only to answer "no" when asked if he knows how
to train a salesperson; all substantive content is Alex's.

## Key claims (2026-02-17, paraphrased, Alex's only)

- The core scaling problem: one star rep selling half the volume tends to (1) suppress the
  rest of the team and (2) ignore the script/process, so there's no consistency across reps
  and the team can't be scaled.
- ★ You *can* clone your best rep. The method: **observe the star and document the exact
  words they say and what they do** in far more detail than most businesses have → that
  documentation becomes the script → memorization + roleplay make every rep execute it.
- Businesses vastly underestimate how much training a truly world-class team requires; the
  volume of training is a "belief breaker" for most people — it's "a shitload."
- ★ **Training cadence at acquisition.com**: reps train *every single day*, role-play
  *every single day*, and have a *weekly* one-on-one with their manager reviewing "game
  tape" of what they specifically need to work on.
- Roleplay is the training method of choice because it's the *fastest feedback loop* you
  can give someone for training any skill.
- ★ **Script-memorization drill (the black-out method)**: put the script in front of the
  rep; they print a second copy; they read it aloud, black out one word, read again, black
  out another word, read again — repeating until the whole page is blacked out. By then
  they've said the script as many times as it has words, and they know it cold.
- ★ **"Breathe the script"** is the memorization standard — a rep must be able to deliver
  the entire blacked-out script from memory before roleplay practice begins.
- ★ **Roleplay method**: warn the new rep up front they'll be interrupted ~50–60 times
  before reaching the end — that's expected and correct. The two *worst* ways to roleplay
  are (1) letting someone recite the whole script uninterrupted, and (2) giving all
  feedback at the end — nobody remembers end-of-call notes; it's too late to matter.
- ★ **"Lock it in" reinforcement**: the instant a rep messes something up, stop them, say
  "try it like this," have them redo it immediately; when they get it right, say "lock it
  in" = do it again, again, again. Immediate change → immediate feedback is why the
  correction sticks (he compares it to how superstition forms).
- Goal of the drill is that **every rep delivers the script the same way**; most teams have
  a loose script nobody adheres to, and recruit by hiring a bunch, seeing who sticks, and
  cutting the rest.
- ★ **Adherence above everything / process above everyone** — "we are like the New England
  Patriots… no one's better than the process." The positioning to reps: thousands of calls
  and hours went into this script, "men died and gave their lives" for it, "you are not
  better than the script" — you say the script because these exact words close people.
- Sales is a highly *trainable* skill: an exceptional sales org can take almost anyone and
  make them "a savage" — it's only a question of how much effort you're willing to invest
  in a person.
- ★ The star rep as culture risk: a top closer who "does whatever he wants as long as he
  closes" is a cowboy/primadonna and a **long-term cancer**. Alex would rather have a team
  of "all eights" closing on a 10/10 process, which will always out-perform a team with one
  star and no process.
