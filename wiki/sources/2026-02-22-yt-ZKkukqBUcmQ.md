# Ignore 99% of the Advice You Hear

## Summary

Short solo clip (all statements Alex-attributed) on **whose advice and opinions to
weight vs. ignore**. Alex's filter: ignore critics and the average people around you,
because the average person's incentive is to keep you average, and many people quietly
want you to fail because your success indicts the risk they declined to take. The one
correction he gives to naïve "ignore everyone" advice is a discrimination rule —
**listen to the people closest to your goals, not the people closest to you.** He
closes with a mortality-framed nudge (your critics will die and their opinions won't
matter, so they don't really matter now) and a CTA for his free
`acquisition.com/roadmap` — a 10-stage zero-to-$100M+ roadmap broken down across eight
business functions by constraint, symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate each stage.

## Key claims (2026-02-22, paraphrased unless quoted)

- **Ignore critics** — easy to say, hard to do; most detractors are "fat, poor pansies"
  and shouldn't be allowed to deter you from doing whatever it takes to succeed.
  (paraphrase; note the deliberately provocative framing)
- The **average person will always try to keep you average.** If you want to be
  extraordinary, you'll necessarily do things an ordinary person sees as "extra."
  (paraphrase)
- **A lot of people want to see you fail because it justifies the risk they chose not to
  take** — this was a hard thing Alex says he personally had to come to terms with.
  (paraphrase)
- ★ The advice-filtering heuristic: **listen to the people who are closest to your
  goals, not the people closest to you.** Proximity/relationship ≠ credibility;
  weight advice by whether the person has reached where you want to go. (paraphrase)
- Mortality reframe: **your critics will eventually die and their opinion won't matter
  then — which means it probably doesn't matter now,** so you might as well do what you
  originally wanted to do. (paraphrase)
- CTA / self-reference: he offers a free **10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ that
  less than 1% of companies finish,** which he says he has done multiple times, broken
  down by eight business functions — what the constraint feels like, its symptoms, and
  the steps taken to graduate — validated across software, physical products, service,
  and brick-and-mortar businesses (at `acquisition.com/roadmap`). (paraphrase;
  self-reported / promotional)
