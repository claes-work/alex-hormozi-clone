## Summary
In this clip (interviewer "Nate" on the Acquisition.com channel), **Leila Hormozi** discusses the psychological low points of building a business — impostor thoughts, the feeling of being a "sham," and the period when the money left the account. Her central argument is that success is driven by behavior, not thoughts or feelings: you don't need to believe in yourself or feel confident to act, you just need to take the actions a successful person would take, and the confidence follows once you stack real-world evidence. She reframes self-doubt as "the downside of the upside" (a byproduct of caring), offers her mantra "prove yourself wrong," and stresses patience, a bias toward action over consumption, and the reality that everything takes longer and is harder than people expect.

## Key claims
- Impostor thoughts ("I'm a sham," "I'm not as smart as I think," "I'm in over my head") are universal among entrepreneurs and never fully go away — "it's always the same [thing], just different costumes."
- Self-doubt is "the downside of the upside": caring a lot about something makes you overly concerned with it; if you didn't have those thoughts you probably wouldn't care enough to succeed — so she treats it as a gift.
- During the hardest period, the internal dialogue was actively negative ("you suck," "this isn't going to work"), so she did not rely on it. She was 23, running an eight-figure business, had never managed people, and had no evidence she'd succeed.
- Instead of fixing her mindset, she committed to the process: take the actions a successful person without those thoughts would take, and "maybe I'd have a shot."
- Behavior dictates most results, not thoughts. Positive thinking is not required; she has plenty of negative thinking and succeeds anyway. Analogy: eating in a calorie deficit makes you lose weight regardless of whether you believe you can.
- Work backward from identity: ask "what actions would [the person I want to be] take?" — you can feel and think differently from that person but taking the same actions yields a similar result.
- Thoughts and feelings follow behavior, not the reverse. We mistakenly correlate a thought/feeling with an outcome, but confidence usually arrives *after* the action. Example: she feared a panic attack the first time she spoke on stage; she was fine, and only then believed she could do it.
- Confidence comes from having done enough things you didn't believe you could do — you accumulate evidence for "why I can" rather than "why I can't."
- Early on there was a delay: she over-consumed content instead of acting. Most things are learned best by doing ("throwing [stuff] at the wall and not being afraid to fail"); she sometimes reads no books at all.
- Prescription for beginners: shift from a bias toward thinking to a bias toward action — "get so busy trying things that you don't have time to doubt yourself."
- On collecting evidence that "isn't working": impatience is the problem — people want results on their timeline and have a skewed sense of how long things take. Give yourself grace; something not working now may work in six months; you may be one skill away from unlocking success.
- Once someone a few steps ahead confirms your strategy is sound, the main determinant of success is simply not giving up and continuing to try.
- Business is much harder than people portray. From a portfolio seeing hundreds of companies' financials weekly, growth is not linear — it's "[mess] the bed, grow, [mess] the bed, grow," in steps. "Everything's gonna take longer and be harder than you think it is."
- Meta claim: she is impatient that Acquisition.com is "not 10 billion yet" despite having the resources, but recognizes things won't happen on her timeline.
