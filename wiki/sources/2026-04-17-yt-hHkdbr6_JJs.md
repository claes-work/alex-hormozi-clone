# How Acquisition.com Makes Money

/ [[entities/acquisition-com]]

## Summary

Solo whiteboard walkthrough (originally an internal recruiting video) in which Alex Hormozi maps the full acquisition.com / ACQ business model as a flywheel: convert raw attention into contact information, convert that into low-ticket entry products, ascend qualified business owners into high-ticket advisory, then redeploy the resulting cash into real estate, ventures, and private equity. He frames the whole structure with a Disney analogy (media brand = Disney; ACQ Network = Disney+; advisory practice = the parks; Skool and future arms = the other franchise universes). The single non-negotiable premise underneath everything is the mission: make real business education accessible for everyone, keep the free content better than competitors' paid content, and hold "absolute premium brand credibility."

## Key claims (the Acquisition.com money model)

- **The flywheel in one line:** put money/effort/team into media → capture contact info → convert via low-ticket mechanisms → ascend qualified owners into high-ticket advisory → redeploy cash into real estate, ventures, and private equity. (self-reported)
- **Portfolio scale:** described as a "$250 million plus portfolio." (self-reported financial)
- **Mission as the root:** "make real business education accessible for everyone" — chosen after he and Leila sold their first company because business education is what "lights us up," and because few high-credibility people actually teach it. (self-reported)
- **Target eyeballs:** business owners, plus people one degree away — "wantrepreneurs," seekers. Wantrepreneurs are mostly helped for free; ascending business owners are the ones eventually monetized.
- **Top of funnel (media input):** content, courses on the site, podcasts, YouTube — created daily with money, effort, and team.
- **Conversion mechanisms (minor conversions):** the Mosey Minute email list (free; itself "a product in of itself" for further advertising); Skool ("one of our portfolio companies," the only publicly-made big brand association); and books (bought on the site or Amazon, ~$30). Framed as "a free trial into a $9 thing," "a $30 book," "a free email list."
- **The Advisory Practice (AP):** in-person workshops at the Las Vegas HQ with associates, analysts, directors. Three tiers (names "subject to change"): **L1 ≈ $5,000**, **L2 ≈ $35,000**, **L3 ≈ $135,000**; L3 is more detailed help / access to Alex. Customers ascend L1 → L2 → L3. (self-reported pricing)
- **Almost all advisory conversions happen over the phone** via a phone sales team; the ACQ Network may use checkout, phone, or both.
- **Cash redeployment bucket 1 — ACQ RE (real estate arm):** buys properties, "typically multi-family," via a partnership through Arc; has raised funds for deals and can solicit the audience to co-invest. Reason given: it is tax-efficient for the business. (self-reported)
- **Cash redeployment bucket 2 — ACQ Ventures:** either very small bets (1–5%) on "unicorn-style tech companies" with high growth potential, or seeding a company at onset that can be sold through ACQ's distribution base. Ventures is explicitly a career path for top advisory-practice operators to start/run new ventures (insurance, lending, sales AI, etc.).
- **Future arm — ACQ Network:** a more scalable business-owner association/membership, projected for 2026 at roughly **$5,000–$10,000/year**. Includes an AI business consultant "trained on all of this in-person experience," a community, and industry-specific calls (home services, healthcare, brick-and-mortar, professional services). Priced with enough barrier to entry to keep it business-owner-only. (self-reported / forward-looking)
- **Network as a buying-group moat:** the aggregate distribution base gives ACQ a stronger negotiating position with vendors (pre-negotiated agencies, credit-card providers, better rates for "thousands of businesses at once"), so accrued savings/value exceed the price and "no one would ever want to leave."
- **Private equity side:** rather than 1–5% chunks, ACQ may take a 30–50%, or up to 100%, stake in businesses and let them run independently, sometimes inserting ACQ talent. Future PE deals target companies that can benefit from / handle the scale of ACQ's distribution base, versus the "legacy portfolio" (companies they simply thought they could add value to).
- **Disney analogy:** big media brand = Disney; **ACQ Network = Disney+** (scalable B2B media/education + ancillary benefits); **Advisory Practice = the parks** (in-person, "meeting Mickey" in a B2B context); **Skool = a Star Wars-style franchise universe** for a specific avatar at a ~$9/month low barrier; real estate + PE = other spokes on the same machine.
- **Monetization avatar sweet spot:** business owners doing roughly **$500,000 to $50 million in revenue**. Sub-$500K eyeballs are still monetized, but "very distributed" — platform ad revenue, low-ticket products, book/Audible margins — with the real goal being to help them ascend into the sweet-spot category.
- **Revenue sources named:** content, book sales, Skool (plus equity value and distributions from Skool), advisory practice ("the parks"), future network memberships, ancillary services, and audience investment/capital raises.
- **The one premise everything depends on:** free stuff must be better than competitors' paid stuff, and the brand must maintain "absolute premium brand credibility" ("we are who we say we are, that we've done what we've said we've done"). This is framed as a "unique wedge" because higher-credibility people mostly don't want to do the teaching/media part.
- **A referenced launch:** a webinar that sold book packages at "6K" (context implies these book/bundle packages, treated as a notable conversion event). (self-reported)
- **Meta:** this was an internal recruiting video; Alex argues every company should have a "video sales letter that sells your company as the product to new candidates," and closes recruiting "A-talent," especially AI/tech-forward people.
