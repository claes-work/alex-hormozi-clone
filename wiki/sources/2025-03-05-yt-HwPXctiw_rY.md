# STEAL Ryan Reynolds' $1.3B Marketing Strategy [NO FAME REQUIRED]

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video that dissects Mint Mobile's Ryan Reynolds ad campaigns (video ads,
Facebook Ads Library entries, UGC creator ads, the landing/sales page, and the email
sequence) as a teaching case study for direct-response marketing. Alex walks through each ad
frame-by-frame, extracting transferable principles: the three-part ad structure (hook / value
"meat" / CTA), the value equation of "more good stuff, less bad stuff," Us-vs-Them positioning
for underdogs, levels-of-awareness targeting, offer wrappers, three-tier pricing psychology,
and page/email optimization. Ryan Reynolds and Mint Mobile are the example — only Alex's
marketing lessons are persona data.

## Key claims (Alex's marketing lessons drawn from the Reynolds/Mint Mobile example)

- **Every ad has three parts**: the hook (call-out), the meat (value), and the end (a CTA or
  a payoff). This mirrors the three questions of running paid ads: where to advertise, how to
  target, and what the ad should say.
- **Rep your own brand to build association.** Alex says he wears his brand (hat, shirt logo)
  for the same reason Reynolds puts Mint Mobile behind him: to link the thing people already
  like to the thing they don't yet know (acquisition.com). "101 strategy."
- **Us-vs-Them positioning is for underdogs, not leaders.** If you're the market leader, don't
  do Us-vs-Them against a competitor; instead frame it as "us vs. the alternative" (e.g. "us
  vs. your couch" for a top fitness brand).
- **Value creation = more good stuff, less bad stuff.** Write copy from those two buckets. The
  best ads make benefits look as big as possible and costs as small as possible — a "reverse
  value equation." Good stuff mapped to fast / easy / likely (risk-free); bad stuff to slow /
  hard / risky.
- **Cover "the don't."** Most advertisers only sell the positive; you should also sell the
  negative — what happens if the prospect does NOT buy (more delays, harder, riskier + less
  good stuff). Four vectors to mirror: more good / less bad / more bad-if-you-don't / less
  good-if-you-don't.
- **Contrast works two ways**: visual contrast (bright colors that pop) and conceptual contrast
  (Us-vs-Them). Analogy/simile ("your wireless bill is like chunky milk") is a key way to
  educate in ads.
- **Branding happens whether you like it or not** — associations form and you can't brand in
  the negative (the "pink elephant" problem). The chunky-milk ad hurt Mint by associating the
  brand with a disgusting thing AND by removing Ryan Reynolds, its biggest star — so it
  underperformed.
- **Levels of awareness (Eugene Schwartz)** determine your hook: unaware → problem-aware →
  solution-aware → product-aware → most-aware. Colder traffic needs curiosity-driven,
  solution-agnostic hooks; the most-aware just need an offer.
- **Branded/recognized advertisers (Reynolds, The Rock) run to a warm/most-aware audience**, so
  they can open by just saying who they are and making an offer. Any business — even one with
  50k followers — uses the same strategy; the only difference is the size of the warm audience.
  Recognition/relatability is one of the four elements of influence.
- **Offer structure: buy X get Y.** Alex predicts (from his upcoming book) that an asymmetric
  structure like "buy 1 get 2" or "buy 3 get 6" (get more free than you pay for) usually
  converts better than a mirror structure like "buy 3 get 3," and can save the business money.
- **Same offer, different wrapper (magic headline formula, $100M Offers).** Once an offer wins,
  don't change it — re-wrap it as the holiday / Super Bowl / spring / back-to-school promo. The
  reason for the promo creates novelty; customers only care about the offer. His "fraternity
  party" analogy: invent any excuse to run the promotion.
- **Urgency via a soft deadline.** "Ends soon" is subjective and lets you keep an urgency CTA
  running compliantly. Urgency = limiting time; scarcity = limiting quantity — they are
  separate, not interchangeable (Mint uses urgency, not scarcity, because it's unlimited).
- **Find winning ads via the Facebook Ads Library**: search the account; the ads running
  longest are the ones most likely working well (old-but-still-live = proven). New creative
  always beats old at first, so longevity is the signal.
- **Top-of-funnel needs far more creative than bottom-of-funnel.** Retargeting (bottom-funnel)
  can keep the same creative because the audience bucket changes; mass top-of-funnel needs
  constant new creative because everyone sees it.
- **Build the ad machine inside the business**: lean on UGC so customers generate ~80-90% of
  your creative and you only produce 10-20%. UGC gives near-unlimited, easily testable creative
  and looks native. The best ads don't look like ads; mimic trending organic formats (PSA,
  fake sales-rep conversation).
- **Read the comments** to learn prospects' (not customers') objections, then answer them in
  your next batch of ads.
- **Translate price into a small everyday cost** (e.g. "$15 = two coffees a month," "less than
  50 cents a day") to make it feel real; rotate the prop for different audiences.
- **Landing page vs. sales page**: an intermediate short landing page (coverage/ZIP check) adds
  qualifying friction that often lifts conversion vs. sending traffic straight to the sales page.
- **Congruence**: all traffic to one page must match the ad; the easiest way to kill conversion
  is a click that doesn't deliver what was promised.
- **Multiple CTA buttons**: going from one CTA to two, then three, usually raises conversion —
  until you have "too many referees on the field."
- **Three-tier pricing**: never split tiers evenly. Where you place the middle tier nudges the
  choice (the "small/medium/large soda" and movie-theater play). Pricing communicates which
  option the seller wants you to take; goal is to maximize revenue per buyer based on where the
  average customer enters.
- **Sales-page fundamentals**: headline + subhead + three bullets above the fold; attention +
  avatar cue at top; FAQ at the bottom almost always lifts conversion (each unanswered question
  = a few percent of buyers). Study Amazon pages for woven-in persuasion. Show a clear
  step-by-step signup and even how to cancel — de-risking the exit can increase purchases.
- **Page speed converts**: cites Neil Patel — roughly ~2.5% conversion lift per second saved;
  cutting load from 9-13s to ~3s can meaningfully improve conversion.
- **Email sequencing**: chunk objections — one obstacle per email, ordered by prevalence
  (most common concern first). Picture-heavy emails suit physical/low-ticket e-commerce;
  plain-text, low-formatting emails deliver and convert better for complex/high-ticket
  services. Personalization, micro-steps, cohort/likeness testimonials, and birthday/
  anniversary offers lift response.
- **Underdog can show side-by-side comparisons** (like Progressive in insurance); the top dog
  never shows competitors. Being explicit about no hidden fees reassures budget buyers.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "I'm Alex Hormozi, I'm the founder of acquisition.com. We spend millions of dollars every
  month on ads, we make tons of money and no one cares."
- "The best ads make benefits look as big as possible and the costs look as small as possible."
- "At the most basic level: more good stuff, less bad stuff."
- "The reason I rep my brand is to make that same association. If there's stuff that you use
  from my content and it makes you money, then hopefully you associate that value with me and
  acquisition.com."
- "Us versus them is best employed when you are not the market leader. If you're number one you
  don't want to do us versus them — if anything you'd use us versus the alternative."
- "You don't actually brand in the negative. I can't say 'hey don't think about pink elephants'
  — well of course you're going to think about pink elephants."
- "The longer the ad's been running the more likely that that ad has worked well."
- "The best advertisements are things that don't look like advertisements."
- "Once you nail the offer you just go endless variations and wrapping paper on it. Same
  bundle, different wrapper."
- "Scarcity is limiting quantity, urgency is limiting time. People talk about them
  interchangeably and that's not how it actually works."
- "If you want to know the objections prospects have, just read the comments — people will tell
  you why they didn't sign up."
- "When you have three tiers you almost never want the three tiers to be evenly split. Where you
  move the middle determines where you want to nudge the customer."
- "One of the easiest ways to drop conversion is to have someone click somewhere and then not
  get what they think they're getting."
- "Try and build the advertising machine within the business itself, so you only have to
  generate 20% or 10% of your ads and you let your community of customers generate the other 90."
