# Helping a $500K Coaching Business Get to $2M

## Summary

Alex coaches a music-lesson studio for neurodivergent kids. He frames the business by
LTV:CAC at the micro level and return on invested capital at the macro level, then
recommends semi-private delivery, premium specialist positioning, and at least 80%
gross margins for brick-and-mortar service businesses. For supply constraints, he gives
three levers: raise prices, change client-to-delivery ratio, and bring in people who can
do the work.

## Key claims

- Alex says LTV:CAC is the smallest version of the economic engine, while return on
  invested capital is the larger scaling lens.
- He says music lessons resemble the gym business and can use semi-private delivery.
- He prefers semi-private because it creates more loyalty to the brand and less
  attachment to an individual teacher.
- He says brick-and-mortar service businesses should have at least 80% gross margin,
  ideally over 90%.
- He says a specialist serving neurodivergent kids should be able to charge a premium.
- He says supply-constrained businesses should first raise prices, then change the
  client-delivery ratio, then add people who can do the work.
- He says stress lowers problem-solving capacity, so creating cash flow and capacity can
  make problems easier to solve.
