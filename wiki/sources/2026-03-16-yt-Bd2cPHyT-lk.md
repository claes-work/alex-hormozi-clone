# Helping a MedSpa Company Acquire Other MedSpas

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner runs two businesses: an in-person med spa doing ~$1.2M in revenue (on track for $2M this year), specializing in a single service — body contouring / fat-melting — across three locations, plus a $6,000/year consulting program that teaches other med spas to scale using their "pillars." The consulting cash is strong, and the owner is torn about which arm to scale and whether to exit the spas. Their real ambition surfaces mid-conversation: they don't just want to coach — the coaching pipeline feeds a **roll-up**, letting them cherry-pick the best clients into a "50 spa group, 100 spa group exit." Alex validates the roll-up as the smart, high-leverage play — "if you can do the roll-up, basically nothing else matters" — but pushes hard on two things. First, get the owner **out of the day-to-day** of the spas, because operating them is "not the opportunity at all." Second, he flags that deal structure is where the money is made: a couple of restructuring moves can make the acquisitions more **tax-efficient** for the buyer while letting the buyer **capture more upside** and still making the seller feel they got a better deal (he keeps the specifics "beyond the scope" of the clip). His concrete delegation prescription is his "cell phone talk": be willing to **eat margin** on the existing spas to install a high-"IQ per square foot" operator, compensated with roughly $100k/year plus 5–10% of profit vested over ~4 years and ~10% of that unit's EBITDA contribution at sale — in exchange for one rule: the owner's phone "doesn't ring." He role-plays crisis scenarios (an upset contouring patient, a leaking ceiling) where **any answer other than "call me" is the right answer**, and reinforces ownership psychology — put the operator's picture on the wall, "call it your facility, not mine," give them a real ownership position — so the founder is freed to "go and hunt" acquisitions. Ends with the standard roadmap promo.

- As of March 2026, Alex is willing to **eat margin** on existing units to install a high-quality operator ("intelligent person," "IQ per square foot"), treating reduced margin as the price of founder freedom to pursue acquisitions.
- As of March 2026, Alex's operator comp template ("cell phone talk"): roughly $100k/year salary plus 5–10% of the business's profit vested over ~4 years, plus ~10% of that unit's EBITDA contribution to the overall entity at sale — in exchange for the rule that the founder's phone "doesn't ring."
- As of March 2026, Alex's delegation test: for any operational crisis (upset patient, facility emergency), **any response other than "call me" is the correct one** — the operator must own resolution.
- As of March 2026, Alex ties delegation to **ownership psychology** — give the operator a real ownership position, "put your picture on the wall," "call it your facility, not mine" — so they behave like an owner and the founder can "go and hunt" deals.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes the coaching business and the spa roll-up "go together": the coaching relationships surface and vet the best acquisition targets, which is why the owner keeps the two arms coupled.
- As of March 2026, Alex observes there is **no exit in coaching itself** — a services/coaching business doesn't produce the same asset sale — whereas the spa roll-up is the exitable asset.
- As of March 2026, Alex praises the single-service focus (comparing it to "the Raising Cane's" of med spas — one thing done well) as an asset for scaling and roll-up.
