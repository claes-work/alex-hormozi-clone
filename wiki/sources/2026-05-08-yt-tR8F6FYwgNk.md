# Why Hiring a COO Usually Backfires

## Summary

Alex coaches a profitable hunting/fishing retailer whose founder is still the
"chief everything officer." He warns that hiring a COO can become a junk drawer unless
the owner first documents where time goes, trains current staff, and creates decision
trees and decision boxes for delegated work.

## Key claims (Alex's advice, dated 2026-05-08)

- Alex says the first step is a time study in 15-minute increments for one or two
  weeks.
- He says apparently random owner tasks usually fall into patterns once tracked.
- He says a COO or head-of-ops role can become a junk drawer unless the activities are
  clearly defined.
- He says the founder may already be paying people for tasks he is still doing because
  he has not trained them.
- He calls jumping in to solve employee problems a short-term fix that avoids the
  harder work of training.
- He says the founder must write decision trees for purchasing and customer-service
  decisions that currently live in his head.
- He recommends decision boxes: employees can decide up to certain money or severity
  thresholds before escalating.
- He says the founder may need to lose more money short term while the team learns, in
  order to build a sellable business.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The first thing I would do is be able to answer that question clearly with a time
> study."

> "Head of ops is not necessarily a bad role overall, but a lot of times it tends to be
> a junk drawer."

> "You're being lazy by jumping in to solve the problem for him."
