# "'I Do $3.5M/Year as a Men's Coach. How Do I Get to $100M?'"

## Summary

Coaching Q&A in which a business owner running a ~$3.5M/year digital men's sexual-health coaching business (group online therapy and e-learning) asks Alex Hormozi how to scale toward $100M and international markets. Alex rejects the owner's premise that a new business model is needed, arguing the constraint is brand, not model — and that AI would only change the delivery vehicle, not the underlying one-on-one consulting motion. He prescribes doubling down on organic-led brand growth, protecting a 70/30 organic-to-ads acquisition ratio, and — given how hard the taboo category makes referrals and endorsements — using an affiliate / centers-of-influence strategy to recruit adjacent businesses that already serve the same customer.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The owner does not need a new business model to scale; the real lever is building a big brand. "You don't need a new model, you need a new way of doing business."
- AI does not change the model — it changes the delivery vehicle. One-on-one consulting is an old model; AI just gives it more operating scale. The pricing might tweak slightly, but the motion of the business barely changes.
- Pricing, packaging, and better offers are "easy wins" worth capturing, but they are not the path to $100M — brand is.
- Large brand associations raise a company's perceived value and reach (illustrated with the hypothetical of a famous celebrity endorsement instantly creating a $100M brand).
- Failure-path prediction if brand is neglected: ad spend rises, organic share of revenue falls, margins compress, revenue grows to $5–6M but profit stays flat with more headache; owner then adds upsells, which add operational complexity, compress margins further, and lead to burnout.
- Keep the customer-acquisition mix at roughly 70/30 organic-to-ads. Let organic growth dictate how much you are willing to spend on ads.
- Growing the organic 70% should be the primary growth goal — a big enough brand can absolutely reach $100M.
- Celebrities have some of the weakest pull for endorsements; micro-influencers, live streamers, and long-form-audience creators pull harder — especially those who themselves struggled with the issue.
- The category is a hard referral and endorsement problem: people don't want to tell friends they have the problem, and many won't want the brand association.
- Best approach for a taboo category: an affiliate / centers-of-influence strategy — recruit businesses that already serve the same avatar, have a different money model, and acquired those customers for free, so they can refer over the services they don't want to deliver themselves.
- Ads and organic are "bread and butter" — keep doing them — but brand association is the ideal (if hardest) additional lever.
