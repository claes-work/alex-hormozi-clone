# 15 Brutal Truths I Know at 36 That I Wish I Knew at 20

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi distills a decade-plus of business and life
lessons into a set of mindset "truths" for people chasing big goals. Central thesis:
suffering is a fixed cost of life, so the only real leverage is picking a goal big
enough to be worth suffering for. He develops supporting ideas around volume, feedback
loops, long-term focus, decision-making as cutting off options, and reframing fear and
sadness as a "perceived lack of options."

## Key claims (the truths, condensed)

- **Suffering is a fixed cost of life.** Poor/rich, single/married, employee/entrepreneur
  — everyone suffers. Since the "how" (pain) is fixed, pick a destination meaningful
  enough to endure it. Cites Viktor Frankl: a big enough "why" bears almost any "how."
- **The pain has predictable flavors:** uncertainty (acting without knowing the payoff),
  criticism (people criticize whether you act or not), mistakes, failure, feeling stupid,
  looking cringe. Do things worth being criticized for.
- **The arbitrage of life** is the gap between your (fixed) suffering and what you get for
  it — and you get to choose how big the payoff is by choosing how big your goal is. Most
  people trade their "life savings" of suffering for a bag of chips / Netflix / sleeping in.
- **You can beat 99% of people** if you master the shame of rejection, the boredom of
  repetition, and the pain of feedback — willing to fail 20 times in a row and keep going.
- **~20 hours gets you base competence** in almost any skill, including money-making skills,
  because doing generates feedback that reading/watching cannot.
- **Learning = a change in behavior under the same conditions.** Consuming content without
  acting is entertainment, not education.
- **What you do next matters more than what you did last** — you can supersede everything
  that came before. (Illustrated with the "Chimp" tweet about wasting one's 20s.)
- **Everything can be solved with volume;** people radically underestimate the reps needed.
  Everyone learns eventually, so do enough volume that failure becomes unreasonable.
- **Extrapolate past success of any kind to your next success** via the transitive property
  (A=B, B=C → A=C) — the ability to generalize skill is what separates humans from animals.
- **If you're under 30 with no responsibilities, work as many hours as humanly possible** —
  you'll never be able to work this hard again, and a few years compound for decades. "Dig
  the well before you're thirsty." Those with responsibilities have trade-offs but more
  experience / strategic efficiency (the aging-pro-athlete analogy).
- **Every position on the board has an advantage.** Having nothing = nothing to lose =
  unlimited lottery tickets. Fear comes from having something to lose; nothing was ever
  yours to begin with. Capped downside + uncapped upside = opportunity.
- **Entrepreneurship is one of the greatest vehicles of personal development** because the
  market gives you truth where friends/family protect your feelings. 10/10 times the person
  limiting the business is you.
- **The hard part isn't starting — it's continuing when excitement wears off.** Your
  inability to work without reward for a long time hurts your potential more than lack of
  talent. Potential = how much uncertainty you can tolerate and for how long.
- **Long-term thinking is one of the last alphas** (cites Sam Altman) because so few will do
  it. But first get your "oxygen mask on" — escape the hole fast (work three jobs if needed).
- **Sadness = a perceived lack of options,** not an actual lack. It stems from arbitrary
  self-imposed rules and treating bearable consequences as unbearable ("I could never…").
  The question to ask: "What trade am I knowingly or unknowingly unwilling to make?"
- **Fear only exists in the vague, not the specific.** Walking a scary consequence down to
  concrete specifics dissolves the fear (the "disappointing my parents" worked example).
- **The price of wanting to be good at everything is being good at nothing.** Decision (Latin
  *dicadere*, to cut off) = removing paths. Fear of deciding → saying yes to everything.
  Giving customers everything they want creates their demise; simplicity/elegance wins.
- **You get lucky by staying in the game long enough for luck to find you.** You can't beat
  someone who has decided to do whatever it takes for as long as it takes.
- **The one belief you need to win is belief in your ability to figure it out.** Sometimes
  you have to be the only one who believes in you.
- **Everything you want is a few trades away** — but the trades are so embedded in your
  belief system you don't see them as options. Cites Orson Scott Card (paraphrased): we
  question all beliefs except those we truly believe and never think to question.
