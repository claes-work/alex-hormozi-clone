# How to make progress faster than everyone

## Summary

Solo talk by Alex Hormozi on how embracing "cringe" and enduring a long period of being bad is the price of getting good faster than everyone else. He reframes cringe as a defensive status play by others and a signal you are on the right path, and argues that caring hard is what separates people who progress from those who stay stuck. He shares his own archival "first everythings" (first ad, first post, first content piece, first YouTube video, first IG post, first podcast) to show that comparing your first chapter to someone's thousandth chapter is a made-up comparison, and urges viewers to document the struggle.

## Key claims

- Do not judge your first chapter by someone else's 25th or thousandth chapter — the comparison is not real, it is made up.
- Definitions: shame is breaking someone else's rules; guilt is breaking your own rules; cringe is supposed second-hand embarrassment.
- Calling something "cringe" is a defensive status play; interpret it as a signal the speaker's status is shifting and that you are on the right path.
- Anything can be made to sound cringe simply by caring about it (bodybuilding, chess, YouTube, writing a book).
- The cringiest thing of all is being scared of looking cringe (credited to Dom Mazzetti).
- People who mock trying hard have typically never cared deeply about anything themselves — and it shows.
- Advice to anyone on the journey: document more. Hormozi's one regret is not documenting the struggle because he was ashamed of it.
- He began documenting purposefully the day he had $1,000 left after losing everything, treating it as the start of his comeback story.
- "In order to get what you want, you must deserve what you want" — deserving means being good, and the only way to get good is to be willing to be bad for a very long time (deserving framing attributed to a Charlie Munger quote).
- The process: start, take feedback to get better, and never stop.
- Context / credibility markers stated: broke a Guinness World Record for fastest-selling non-fiction, $106M in sales in a weekend; portfolio did north of $250M aggregate revenue last year; 3.6M book copies donated by other entrepreneurs in his launch. (Self-reported.)
- He credits Dom Mazzetti as the inspiration for making this video.
