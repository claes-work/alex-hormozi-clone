# How to Make Better Offers and Lead Magnets

## Summary

Solo talk (all statements Alex-attributed) troubleshooting the two most common
reasons businesses can't get cheap leads: a weak offer and/or a weak (or missing)
lead magnet. On the offer side, Alex argues that when a product is easily
comparable (he cites home services — roofing, concrete, kitchen remodeling), you
must win on at least one of three **value vectors: faster, less risky, or easier**,
and raise prices to reflect the premium rather than staying a commodity. He gives
a concrete diagnostic question ("what would it take to get this outcome in
one-third the time?"), tactics for each vector (including buying an insurance
policy on the customer's biggest fear), and a fourth lever — wrapping the current
offer in [[topics/business/money-models|money-model]] attraction offers (giveaways,
win-your-money-back, decoy, pay-less-now-pay-more-later, buy-X-get-Y-free).

On the lead-magnet side he uses his own data — direct-to-workshop ads vs. the free
"scaling roadmap" lead magnet — to show that the lead magnet has higher ROAS over
a longer time horizon and scales better because it reaches colder
[[topics/marketing/stages-of-awareness|stages of awareness]]. He lays out the
**three lead-magnet types** (reveal a problem, trial of the solution, one-step-of-a-
multistep / splinter offer) and the splinter-offer mechanic of pulling one
component out of a bundle, giving it away at 80–90% off or free, and upselling the
rest. Ties directly to the $100M Offers / $100M Leads / Money Models frameworks.

## Key claims (2026-02-22, paraphrased unless quoted)

- When your offer is easy for customers to compare, you must win on at least one of
  three value vectors — **faster, less risky, or easier** — and you can win on more
  than one. This is the strategic core of de-commoditizing an offer. (paraphrase)
- There is *always* a way to be faster, less risky, or easier in any industry,
  including trades like roofing, concrete, and kitchen remodeling. (paraphrase)
- If a better offer costs more to deliver, **raise prices to reflect the premium** —
  you're no longer a commoditized product and people will pay for it. (paraphrase)
- Diagnostic question for the speed vector: *"What would it take to get this outcome
  for a customer in one-third the time?"* Answer it literally (ideally with your
  team) instead of saying "we can't." If delivering same-day/next-day costs (e.g.)
  50% more to contractors, ask whether you can sell that to customers — if yes, you
  have a winner; if no, **go up-market to rich people who value speed, risk, and
  ease.** (paraphrase)
- Risk vector: insure or guarantee against the customer's biggest fear via
  warranties or guarantees — or (a personal favorite he's implemented in many
  businesses) **literally buy an insurance policy on the customer's biggest fear**,
  fold its cost into your price, and tell the customer "we cover damages up to X."
  It's typically far cheaper than people assume. ★ (paraphrase)
- Ease vector: do pre-work for the customer ahead of time (intake, onboarding, prep)
  to make the experience seamless; so few businesses do this that customers notice
  and appreciate it. (paraphrase)
- Fourth lever (from the Money Models book): wrap what you already sell in an
  attraction-offer mechanism — **giveaway, win-your-money-back, decoy, pay-less-now-
  pay-more-later, or buy-X-get-Y-free** — to lower lead cost. (paraphrase)
- Matching mechanism to business type: **win-your-money-back** works well for
  service businesses where the customer has to do work; **decoy** works well for
  transactional businesses; **buy-X-get-Y-free** works across many models.
  (paraphrase)
- Buy-X-get-Y-free rule: **give away more free than the customer pays for, and adjust
  the unit price up accordingly.** Example: instead of $100/mo × 12 = $1,200, run
  "buy six get six" or "buy four get eight" but price the four units at $300 each —
  looks like a crazy deal, same total. (paraphrase)
- Giveaway follow-up tactic: after a giveaway (e.g. a kitchen-remodel giveaway), call
  everyone who *didn't* win and offer them a **second-place / "participation trophy"
  prize** — e.g. "second place is a $2,000 discount on your kitchen, you won." ★
  (paraphrase)
- Check advertising-compliance law with an attorney; giveaway/advertising rules
  differ by county and region. (paraphrase)
- Lead-magnet ROAS lesson from his own ads: **direct-to-workshop ads have higher
  ROAS upfront, but the free scaling-roadmap lead magnet has higher ROAS overall
  once you expand the time horizon — and it scales better.** A direct "buy a
  workshop" CTA only reaches the two warmest stages of awareness (a small segment);
  a lead magnet reaches far more people across colder stages. (paraphrase)
- "Lead magnets don't work" is a claim made by small businesses; if you only want to
  make a smaller amount of money, a direct "buy my stuff" ask will indeed give higher
  ROAS — but to reach much larger scale you must **soften the ask**, make it
  attractive, and build the conversion process around it. (paraphrase)
- Home-services businesses commonly run Google ads straight to a website whose only
  CTA is "request a quote" — **no one wants to request a quote.** Instead give
  something in exchange for contact info, and make it something they actually want,
  described in their own language (e.g. "11 frameworks we use to build wildly
  profitable gyms"). (paraphrase)
- **Three types of lead magnet:** (1) reveal a problem — e.g. "seven mistakes
  homeowners make when fixing their HVAC"; (2) trial of the solution; (3) one step of
  a multistep process (a **splinter offer**). (paraphrase)
- Trial-of-the-solution example: a company that gets any local business to rank #1–3
  in local maps offers to do it **free, then calls in seven days to ask if they want
  to keep it** — generates a ton of leads and converts well. (paraphrase)
- Objection-handling for one-step-of-multistep ("everything I do comes together, I
  can't split it"): there is *always* a piece you can pull out and do free or at deep
  discount — e.g. a door: put on a coat of paint, apply sealant, or just measure it.
  (paraphrase)
- Splinter-offer mechanic: given a bundle with components A, B, C, D sold for $2,000,
  it's often more powerful to **pull one component (B) out, give it away at 90% off
  or free, and once the customer has it they want to buy A, C, D.** This gets people
  in the door and produces cheaper leads. (paraphrase)
- Discount philosophy: **avoid 10–20% discounts — they don't change behavior. Use
  80–90% discounts on something that costs you very little**, because a discount
  should be big enough to change behavior and set up the upsell into the main offer.
  (paraphrase)
- Best lead magnets **give away something other people charge for**; the more real
  the lead magnet is (the more true cost it has), the more likely it works.
  (paraphrase)
- Summary diagnosis: cheap leads are hard because the offer promises nothing on
  speed, ease, or risk (you only need to hammer one well) and there's no compelling
  lead magnet preceding the offer to earn the prospect's contact info. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You need to either be faster than somebody else, you need to be less risky than
> somebody else, or you need to be easier... from a strategy perspective you want to
> focus on at least one of those three things."

> "Rather than saying we can't do it or we can't afford to do it, answer the damn
> question. What would it take? ... if the answer is no, then we just go up market to
> rich people who absolutely value speed, risk, and ease."

> "One of my personal favorites... is you actually just get an insurance policy on
> whatever their biggest fear is and you say don't worry we cover damages up to X
> because we already have insurance for it and you include that into your cost, which
> typically is not nearly as expensive as you think."

> "The key with buy-free... is you want to give away more free than someone pays for
> and you just adjust the price accordingly... you do like buy six get six or buy four
> get eight. That's like a crazy deal, but you just make the four $300 each."

> "It says request quote. For the love of God, no one wants to request a quote... give
> them something in exchange for their contact information. And how do we make it even
> more enticing? Give them something they actually want."

> "I hate the idea of a 10 or 20% discount. There's no point... I want a discount to be
> significant enough that it changes someone's behavior. And so a 90 or 80% discount,
> that will change people's behavior."
