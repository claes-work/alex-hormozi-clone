# Alex Hormozi Shares the THREE SKILLS Anyone Should Learn to Make a Million Dollars

## Summary
A short interview clip in which an unnamed host asks Alex Hormozi to name the three
skills someone should learn to reach their first million. Despite the framing, Hormozi
answers that reaching a first million really only requires one skill — sales — and
spends the entire clip on it. His core argument: learn to sell, then find the most
expensive thing you can and sell that, because expensive offers are *easier* to sell
(you deal with a "better" avatar) while the selling motion is roughly the same
regardless of price. He advises aspiring salespeople to build the skill in a
high-volume environment first (he cites his own four-to-five years of 20+ daily gym
consults) to develop "unconscious competence," then graduate to high-ticket roles
(buildings, jets, enterprise software) that can pay a seven-figure income. He closes
by noting that higher-ticket businesses generally carry less operational complexity
than high-volume, low-ticket ones.

## Key claims
- **Skill #1 — Sales.** To make a first million you "just have to sell something to
  someone." You don't even need your own product — you can do a million just selling.
  (The clip does not enumerate skills #2 and #3; the "three skills" title is not
  delivered in this transcript — see Notes.)
- To make more in sales, sell things that are more expensive: "learn how to sell, and
  then find the most expensive thing you possibly can to sell. And then sell that."
- Counterintuitive core claim: **the more expensive something is, the easier it is to
  sell** — because you're dealing with "better people" (a $50k client wires the money
  without friction; a $50 buyer nitpicks what they get).
- From a mechanics standpoint almost every sale is the same regardless of price, so you
  might as well do the sale that pays $5,000 instead of the one that pays $20.
- If developing the skill from scratch, **sell in the highest-volume environment
  possible** — fitness/gym sales are great training because of daily consult volume
  ("20 plus consults" a day for 4–5 years builds "unconscious competence" that becomes
  ingrained in your language patterns and gives you power in all areas of life).
- High-volume selling is training, not the destination: the goal is to move to selling
  expensive buildings, cars, yachts, jets, or enterprise software — roles that can earn
  "400 [thousand] a million dollars a year," and that's income, not gross.
- To just hit a million, reverse-engineer it to a daily number (~$3,000/day) and pick
  the deal shape that feels easiest: e.g. one $5,000 sale, three $1,500 sales, or
  thirty $300 sales — all reach the same number but suit different personalities.
- Speculative belief: businesses are an extension of the entrepreneur's personality
  ("their thumbprint is on it"); high-volume/low-ticket operators are a different type
  than ultra-high-ticket service sellers, and he's seen success on both sides.
- **The more expensive the ticket, in general, the less operational complexity.** If
  you're taking sales calls anyway, you might as well make them worth more.
