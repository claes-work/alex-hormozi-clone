# How to Win in Saturated Markets

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a compact two-part market-selection framework that may be worth promoting: (1) the "biggest fish in a puddle → pond → lake → ocean" progression reconciling "red oceans are good" with "the riches are in the niches," and (2) the **five directions to move your market** (up / down / adjacent / broader / narrower). Check whether existing topics already capture the five-directions model before synthesis.

## Summary

In this solo talk, Hormozi addresses the apparent contradiction between chasing big, competitive "red ocean" markets and his own advice that "the riches are in the niches." He argues that fierce competition signals large rewards (more fish where there's more blood), but that beginners should artificially constrain their market to become the biggest fish in a small "puddle," then progressively expand to pond → lake → ocean as their skill and experience grow. He illustrates this with his own path (personal trainer → gym content → business content) and with Gym Launch (single-location gyms → potential moves to franchisors, trainers, chiropractors, etc.). He then lays out five directions a business can move its target market — up market, down market, adjacent, broader, and narrower — and notes most small-business owners never consciously define their market at all, which confuses customers.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) A crowded "red ocean" is not a reason to avoid a market; the bloodier the water, the more fish are present, and the fiercest competition often carries the biggest rewards. Hormozi cites his own entry into the saturated business-advice niche as proof.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) This reconciles with "the riches are in the niches": early on you should artificially constrain the pond you compete in so you can win where the sharks aren't swimming — the goal is to be the biggest fish in a small body of water.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Market size is a progression tied to skill and experience: puddle → pond → lake → ocean. You outgrow each stage and move up as you get "big enough" to compete in a broader arena.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) His own trajectory demonstrates this evolution: he started as a trainer talking about nutrition, moved to gym content, then to business content.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) The upside in a niche is capped, but usually capped far higher than people expect; the trade-off is worthwhile because fewer competitors means more pricing power and profit — there are fewer alternatives to compare you against, so you can charge "niche prices."
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) A business (represented as a dot inside a triangular marketplace) can move in **five directions**: (1) **up market** — e.g. from single-location gym owners to multi-location owners and franchisors; (2) **down market** — e.g. to trainers, of whom there are far more but with less buying power; (3) **adjacent** — e.g. from gyms to chiropractors, reusing the same systems; (4) **broader** — e.g. from gyms to health-and-wellness generally (chiros, med spas, weight-loss clinics); (5) **narrower** — e.g. only spin studios or only dance studios.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) The marketplace is pyramid-shaped by volume and buying power: fewer franchisors than gyms, fewer gyms than trainers — moving down market trades buying power for larger population.
- (2026-02-25, paraphrase) Most small-business owners have never explicitly defined which of these five positions they occupy, effectively accepting anyone "with a pulse and a credit card." Failing to get clear on this confuses customers because they won't understand what you're really about.
