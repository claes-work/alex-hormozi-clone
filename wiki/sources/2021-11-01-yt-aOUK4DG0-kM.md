# This first hire nearly bankrupt me...but was worth it (2021-11-01)

## Summary

In this early (2021) scaling video, Alex Hormozi answers the recurring question of
*who to hire first* by introducing a "stack of time" tool: list how your working
day is divided across activities, then outsource the tasks that consume the
greatest share of time while contributing the least value. He argues the purpose
of any early hire is to buy time back for the least money, and that a bootstrapped
founder's first one or two hires are almost always either a jack-of-all-trades
admin/helper or someone to assist with fulfillment (only possible once the service
is productized). He states acquisition.com's portfolio was doing "$85 million a
year" at the time of recording (self-reported).

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- You will never *know* the "right" moment to hire; certainty is not the trigger. [2021]
- Use a "stack of time" model: map your day into activities (admin, sales,
  marketing, billing, customer-facing) and decide hires from two factors — which
  activity takes the greatest percentage of your time, and which of those is the
  lowest-value (lowest-cost) activity. [2021]
- Ascribe value to each activity — by market value, or (his preference) by which
  makes you the most money — and outsource the low-value, time-heavy ones first. [2021]
- Play defense early: don't outsource something crucial (e.g. sales) at an early
  stage when lower-risk tasks (admin, billing) can be handed off with little
  downside. [2021]
- The objective of a hire is to give you time back: "how can I get the most time
  back for the least amount of money?" [2021]
- Entrepreneurship is fundamentally buying back time and increasing your skill so
  you can do progressively higher-revenue activities. [2021]
- The endgame of this ladder: eventually you only make decisions, and beyond that
  you provide a vision/lens through which others make aligned decisions on your
  behalf. [2021]
- First hire, type A: administrative help or a "first follower" who believes in
  your vision — often a spouse or partner, who is more invested and out-works a
  normal employee. [2021]
- First hire, type B: someone to assist with fulfillment — possible only once you
  have productized the service (narrowed the customer type and outcome so the
  result can be reliably replicated by someone other than you). [2021]
- In the beginning the founder works "one and a half or double" — doing the
  business while also building the infrastructure so others can operate within the
  model. [2021]
- This generalization applies to founder-owned bootstrapped businesses with no
  funding, which he estimates is "99% of businesses"; VC-backed startups may hire
  differently. [2021]

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The first secret to this is that you're never going to know."

> "The objective of the hire should be to give you time. It should be to give you
> time back. And so the idea is how can I get the most time back for the least
> amount of money?"

> "The process of entrepreneurship is just buying back time and increasing your
> level of skill so that you can do higher and higher revenue activities."

> "You provide the lens through which people underneath of you make decisions in
> alignment so that they can move the entire organization forward on your behalf."

> "This is why spouses a lot of times end up being that first hire … they're going
> to be more invested. You get way more work out of that person than you would out
> of a normal employee."

> "What's taking me the most amount of time that I can buy from the marketplace the
> least amount of money?"
