# What Would Happen If You Lifted 6x a Day for 10 Minutes?

## Summary
Podcast-clip story from Alex's early-20s bodybuilding/strength era, told to an interviewer (interviewer's questions marked `>>`). A former roommate, **Greg Knuckles** — described as one of the two smartest people Alex has met (perfect SAT at 14) and "stupid strong and natural" (pulling ~800 lb in their 20s) — pitched a research-paper experiment: instead of one long session, **lift six times a day for ~10 minutes each**. Alex, who "lived at the gym," set a timer and every ~45 minutes did **one set on three exercises**, then went back to work. He notes this distributed cadence was "actually an amazing productivity hack" — a nervous-system "boost" every 45 minutes that woke him back up. He paired it with a diet Greg had also researched: a near **pure-carbohydrate, zero-fat calorie surplus** (~200 g protein, ~800 g carbs, essentially no fat), on the logic that dietary fat converts to stored body fat at near-100% efficiency while carbohydrate stores as fat less efficiently, so making the surplus all carbs minimizes fat gain. He gained "a tremendous amount of weight" and added strength to all his main lifts. Heavy caveats: he claims **elite genetics** (his friend Dr. Cashy, a biochemist and national strongman, told him he has "potato chip genetics" and shouldn't give diet advice); the protocol was **not sustainable** — shot knees, aching elbows, terrible sleep, bordering on overtraining. Afterward he **kept the split but dialed back total volume** (from ~7 sets per exercise down to ~5). The interviewer closes the frame: Alex carries this same precise, plan-then-attack, "of course it'll work" mindset from fitness into business — "Here's the plan, attack." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex describes an experimental training protocol from his 20s: **six short (~10-minute) lifting bouts per day** on a timer (every ~45 minutes: one set across three exercises), rather than a single long session — a paper-driven idea from his roommate Greg Knuckles.
- As of March 2026, he reports the distributed cadence doubled as a **productivity hack**: a regular nervous-system "boost" every 45 minutes that kept him alert during work.
- As of March 2026, he paired the training with a **near-pure-carbohydrate, zero-fat calorie surplus** (~200 g protein, ~800 g carbs, near-zero fat), reasoning that dietary fat stores as body fat at ~100% efficiency while carbohydrate stores less efficiently, so an all-carb surplus minimizes fat gain during a bulk.
- As of March 2026, he says the combined protocol produced **large gains in bodyweight and strength across his main lifts** — but only because he was "maxed out" on every variable simultaneously.
- As of March 2026, he stresses the result is **not generalizable**: he claims "elite" / "potato chip" genetics (per his friend Dr. Cashy, a biochemist and national strongman), and the protocol was **unsustainable** — damaged knees and elbows, poor sleep, bordering on overtraining.
- As of March 2026, his takeaway adjustment: **keep the training split but reduce total volume** (he moved from ~7 sets per exercise per day to ~5) for sustainability.
- As of March 2026 (interviewer's framing, agreed to by Alex), the same **precise, plan-first "here's the plan, attack" mindset** — and the absence of a self-limiting belief that it won't work — is what Alex carries identically from fitness into business.
