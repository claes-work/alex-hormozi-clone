# Stop Being Broke. Get Busy.

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex argues that "unscalable" tactics are almost never truly
unscalable — the founder is simply unskilled or lacks the context and resources to
scale them yet. He urges early/small businesses to embrace hard, manual work (1:1
calls, handwritten cards, personalized outreach) because in the beginning you learn
more from customers than you earn from them, and every unscalable process later
yields a "2.0/3.0" version that keeps ~80% of the effect at far greater output. He
closes with two frameworks: the sales-to-fulfillment continuum and the done-for-you /
done-with-you / do-it-yourself pyramid, advocating a top-down (Tesla-model) build.

## Key claims

- "Unscalable" is usually a misdiagnosis: unless physics literally forbids it, the
  thing is not unscalable — the founder is unskilled at scaling it (culture + training
  scale a service business).
- People refuse the manual/unscalable path because they fixate on an extreme future
  outcome (e.g. hitting $100M/year) while currently making zero — analogous to a
  gym client refusing to start because they fear getting "too bulky" before ever
  losing weight.
- You cannot be both "busy and broke" — pick one. If you're broke you have time, so
  get busy doing the unscalable work.
- Early on you learn more from customers than you earn from them: pain points,
  message hooks, offer/product tweaks that raise conversion.
- Two "magic questions" to distill an offer: (1) "If I eliminated every feature but
  one, which would you keep?" → power-ranks core value; (2) "If I eliminated one
  feature and it changed nothing in your life, which?" → surfaces the least valuable.
  Ask ~100 people and trim accordingly.
- Attributed to Paul Graham (and endorsed by Alex): nearly every business problem —
  marketing or product — can be solved by talking to your customer more.
- Onboarding even a $10/month customer manually increases retention and referrals and
  improves product + marketing.
- Scaling delivery path: 1-on-1 → 1-on-6 (semi-private) → group onboarding → 1-on-30,
  each roughly 5–6x-ing output while keeping most of the experience; you first build a
  presentation/deck around the ~6 recurring questions, then train others to run it.
- Alex claims he did 4,101 sales closes (not consults) over a multi-year period for
  his gyms — back-to-back 30-minute appointments, 2–3 booked per slot for show rate —
  as his self-taught "unscalable sales training."
- Every business incurs debt; the choice is which kind. Venture-backed firms incur
  financial debt (hire talent at market rate); under-capitalized founders incur
  "management debt" (do the job of 2–4 people). You must cross that "swamp" to reach
  the other side.
- A common advice failure: successful people extrapolate advice from their current
  (rich) context to people in a different (early) context — e.g. flying private is the
  outcome of being rich, not the path to it (Warren Buffett). Alex says he
  deliberately spends time with people at their first $10K / $100K / $1M-month to keep
  advice contextual.
- Tactical example: his sales teams send personalized voice notes / videos via
  iMessage from a personal phone (not automated green-text SMS) because it signals a
  real person and lifts response — leverage lets you buy reps iPhones later.
- Small business (defined here as $10M and under) advantage: you can do the profitable
  unscalable things that Apple/Microsoft-scale companies can't — that non-scalability
  is your competitive edge. Folding it is like being dealt pocket aces and refusing to
  play them.
- Framework 1 — sales-to-fulfillment continuum (from the offers book): the easier
  something is to sell, the harder it is to fulfill (main exception: technology).
  Done-for-you is easy to sell / hard to deliver; do-it-yourself is hard to sell /
  easy to deliver.
- Framework 2 — the pyramid: start top-down (Tesla model) with the most expensive
  done-for-you offer first (few customers, max revenue per customer, forces you to
  build SOPs), then move down to done-with-you (charge ~1/3–1/5, consulting/assist
  model) and finally do-it-yourself (hand over the SOPs). Top-down also price-anchors
  the brand and gets the best client results.
