# If you want to win in life, please watch this

## Summary

Solo mindset monologue framed around a conversation with a friend whose fitness-space
business failed and who never once asked Alex for help — because he wanted any failure
to be "on him." Alex uses this to argue that ego and pride keep people (especially those
who identify as intelligent) from asking for help, and that the world only remembers
whether you won or lost, never how or with whose help. He closes with several personal
mental frameworks for enduring a hard year (he says 2025 has been the hardest of his last
eight), including rooting identity in behaviors ("doing is being"), treating suffering as
mostly voluntary and mostly forgotten, and valuing determination over intelligence.

## Key claims

- "No one cares, just win" — until you win, effort goes unnoticed; people only remember
  the outcome, not who helped you or the circumstances.
- Fault is only ever assigned in the negative: no one asks whose fault a success was.
- Refusing to ask for help is ultimately ego / pride — inability to say "I don't know,
  please help." Many people would rather protect their ego and fail than win.
- This trap is most common among people who identify as intelligent, because their hidden
  self-image ("the smart one") makes humbling themselves harder.
- Humility is a core ingredient of learning fast: you can learn anything if you're willing
  to look dumb in front of others for an extended period.
- The most underrated money-making habit is being willing to look stupid; avoiding looking
  dumb / avoiding failure is one of the surest ways to never succeed.
- Risk is guaranteed and all great things carry it (ideas → ridicule, love → rejection,
  intimacy → vulnerability); every joy has paired suffering. "If you risk nothing you
  become nothing."
- Beginners think they have nothing to lose, but most of what they fear losing is in their
  head — social standing, looking smart to friends, a manicured identity.
- Root identity in your behaviors (things you control), not in what others believe you to
  be, to build an "anti-fragile identity." "Doing is being."
- Reframe: a bad event often changes nothing tangible about your day-to-day (losing $1,000
  of $10,000 changes nothing you actually do); most suffering is voluntary and chosen.
- Memory is wildly inaccurate; we remember life as discrete "moments," so you can choose to
  demarcate good moments and compress bad ones. Winners get to write a revisionist history.
- Example of humility in action: he joined a mastermind of gym owners before he owned a
  gym, to start "10 years ahead" instead of learning by mistakes first.
- His "secret": do exactly what a mentor/vendor tells you the first time, keep what works,
  toss the rest; aim to be the biggest success story of everyone who ever advised you.
- Ultra-wealthy people he's known stay curious about lower-status people because they hunt
  for what that person knows better and "ROI the time."
- Determination is rewarded more than intelligence, and determination just looks like a
  long string of failures until it works. Suffering is guaranteed; sticking it out is not.
