# Stop Caring About Other People's Opinion | Alex Hormozi

## Summary

Interview clip in which Alex Hormozi explains how he built mental "devices" to take
action despite his own insecurities and the judgment of others. His central frame is
mortality: in three generations everyone who knew you — including the people whose
opinions stopped you — will be dead, so "do it for you." He frames independent thinking
as a muscle that atrophies from resting on societal norms and can be re-trained, and
grounds the whole discussion in his own biography: growing up with essentially one voice
that mattered (his father's), realizing he was saddest at the moment his father was
proudest, and the six-month journey to quitting his consulting job and moving across the
country.

## Key claims

- He thinks about death constantly; it is the thing he thinks about most, and it shapes
  how he sees time and his own agency (which actions he's willing to take despite others'
  judgment).
- His core device against insecurity: in three generations he'll be forgotten, and the
  naysayers will also be dead — so "just do it for me." Then there's only one voice to
  listen to when he wakes up.
- The cost of that freedom: once you drop societal norms, role models, and expectations,
  you have to work out what to do from first principles, which is difficult in its own way.
- Independent thinking is a muscle — the more you flex it, the more it becomes your
  default, and everyone else's actions start looking more and more insane to you.
- Examples of "insane" default choices: living a life you don't want, not wearing what you
  want, dating who's socially accepted rather than who you want, staying in an "okay" job.
  Living an okay life on autopilot terrifies him more than the freedom to fail repeatedly.
- Taking agency / responsibility for decisions is a practiced skill; the more you practice,
  the more others' inability to act ("I just can't quit my job") seems ridiculous — he
  probes "physically, why can't you?" (move in with parents/friend, split rent).
- Perspective device he uses with Leila on small complaints: "if you zoom out far enough
  you can't see the earth" — it instantly reframes how trivial the concern is.
- Cites Sean Puri: don't follow what most people do, because you don't want the results
  most people get (average person is obese, likely divorced, less than $1K in the bank).
  Doing what everyone else does feels safe but is a terrible decision.
- Retraining independent thinking is very hard at first because you've rested on societal
  norms, past trauma, and parents' expectations for so long — like trying to move ear
  muscles that atrophied from never being used ("the ear muscle of personal growth").
- Biography: he had no siblings and basically no mother — just a father — so his father's
  approval was "literally everything," and his father disapproved of the path he wanted.
  His frameworks/"isms" came from combating that one booming voice.
- The turning point: the moment his father was most proud of him (consultant, graduated in
  three years, took the consulting job) was when he was the saddest — which taught him his
  father's approval wasn't the right way to feel good about life.
- That realization started a six-month journey from deciding to change his life to doing
  it. As you become higher-agency, the gap between deciding and doing compresses ("let's
  change it, done").
- The first big wall is by far the hardest; once you get past it, that wall becomes the
  evidence/proof point for clearing the next one. The bigger the dragon you slay, the more
  evidence you have that you can slay the next.
- He was so afraid of his father's disapproval that he didn't tell him he was moving to
  California until he'd physically left the state — five states over, in Ohio — before
  calling.

  becomes the default way that you think, and then everyone else's actions just start
  looking more and more insane to you."

  fail over and over again is still more fulfilling to me."

  slay the next dragon."
