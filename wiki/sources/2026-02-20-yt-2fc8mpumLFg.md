# How to Speak So People Actually Understand You

## Summary

In this short interview clip, Alex Hormozi explains why his content has done "disproportionately well": he communicates business at a "fifth grade level," defines terms most people leave amorphous, and anchors every explanation to "observable reality" instead of feelings or jargon. He argues that the moment a listener hits a word they don't understand, they lose the rest of the message — so clarity is achieved by aggressively simplifying vocabulary and by breaking vague concepts (like "value" or "brand") down into concrete things a person can actually see and do. The clip opens with him noting his book covers were deliberately kept simple so they'd be accessible even to kids.

## Key claims (2026-02-20, paraphrased unless quoted)

- His content outperforms largely because he speaks about business at a "fifth grade level" — plain, generic phrasing like "make it faster," "make it easier," "more good stuff, less bad stuff." (paraphrase)
- A second reason for the outperformance: he defines terms as simply as possible. People use words others don't understand, and the instant a listener hits an unfamiliar word, they stop understanding everything that follows. (paraphrase)
- ★ Communication mechanic — **anchor everything to observable reality.** Take amorphous words most people use loosely (e.g. "value") and break them down into things you can actually observe. All of his content is "always focused on the observable reality." (paraphrase — distinct, concrete lens beyond the general "clear, not clever" thread)
- Vague "feelings" (e.g. a designer's intuition, "I feel like it's this feeling") can be de-mystified as pattern recognition: you saw things that went well and things that went badly, and over time that "good / bad" cycle gets imprinted so you recognize patterns faster. Different words, same underlying mechanic. (paraphrase)
- The same reductive method generalizes: "how do I get good at brand?" resolves to — study what did well, study what didn't, and do more of what works for your industry. (paraphrase)
- His book covers were kept deliberately simple ("100%... I wanted to be accessible for like kids"), against a trend where "everything is just trying to be as loud as possible." (paraphrase, prompted by interviewer)
