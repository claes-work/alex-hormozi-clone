# Build Ramps, Not Walls

## Summary

A short live coaching Q&A clip (the transcript opens on the tail of a previous answer —
"Thank you, Derek" — before this question starts). The questioner runs a **B2B
Amazon/TikTok optimization agency** (both CRO-type optimization and ad management) selling
to TikTok/Amazon store owners ("B2C top 100,000"). His problem: **conversion drops off at
a certain price point** (he calls it ~$6,500), driven by a real **onboarding cost** —
account build-out, software integration, listing building, content — that depending on
catalog size can run up to **$20k–25k**, on top of ongoing services priced anywhere from
**$6,500 to $30,000 per month**. Prospects hear the onboarding number and say "that sounds
like a lot." He wants to lower the initial cost to get more yeses (because the internal
team upsells well) without going negative on onboarding. They've been experimenting with
term structures: higher price for month-to-month vs. lower price for a 12-month commit.

Alex's answer is a single offer structure he calls "one of my favorite offers ever" and
says is **coming out in the next book**: present two options. **Option A** — pay the
onboarding down (he uses $30,000 as the stand-in price), month-to-month, no commitment.
**Option B** — the seller **waives/stomachs the onboarding cost entirely**, in exchange
for the client committing to 12 months, framed as the client accepting that results take
time ("this is going to take at least 6 months or at least 12 months… for us to put all of
our systems in place"). The teeth: **if the client gets cold feet and cancels early, they
pay what they would have paid had they gone month-to-month** — i.e., the waived onboarding
comes due ("3 months from now you're like, 'Screw you.' Then I'm like, 'Then you pay the
onboarding.' And you're out."). He re-explains it with a gym example: $500 sign-up +
$99/month, or the $500 waived ("I'll onboard you myself") for a one-year commitment, with
the same early-cancel clawback. The economics as Alex states them: the onboarding cost is
real and "has to get paid one way or another" — the seller can stomach it only because a
12-month commitment lets him spread it over the term; without the commitment he refuses to
lose money bringing the customer on. A side benefit: the upfront fee **acts as a price
anchor** that positions the monthly rate as much lower, "which is nice for the sale."
The video ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift CTA (10 stages,
functions across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR,
finance; thank-you page books a call, in-person Vegas session if they can help).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **"One of my favorite offers ever," teased as coming out in the next book:** the
  waived-onboarding-for-commitment structure below. (Fits the money-model/offer-structure
  family in [[../topics/business/money-model]].)
- **The two-option onboarding offer.** Option A: pay the onboarding cost down,
  month-to-month, no commitment. Option B: seller waives/stomachs the onboarding cost if
  the client commits to 12 months — framed as the client committing to the reality that
  results take time ("at least 6 months or at least 12 months" to put systems in place).
- **The early-cancel clawback is what makes the waiver safe.** If the client cancels
  during the term, they pay what they would have paid month-to-month — the waived
  onboarding fee comes due. The onboarding cost is real and "has to get paid one way or
  another"; commitment lets the seller amortize it over 12 months, and without commitment
  the seller should not lose money bringing a customer on.
- **The gym version of the same offer:** $500 sign-up + $99/month, or waive the $500
  ("I'll onboard you myself") for a one-year commitment, cancel-early → pay the $500.
  Same structure at a consumer price point.
- **The upfront fee doubles as a price anchor.** Even when waived, the stated onboarding
  fee positions the monthly price as much lower — "which is nice for the sale."
- **The offer is a choice architecture, not a discount.** Both paths recover the
  onboarding cost; the client chooses between no commitment (pay it now) and commitment
  (seller eats it, spread over the term). "It's up to you."
- _(Audience, self-reported)_ The questioner's agency: B2B Amazon/TikTok optimization
  (CRO + ads) for store owners; conversion drop-off around a $6,500 price point;
  onboarding up to $20k–25k depending on catalog size; ongoing services $6,500–$30,000
  per month; internal team strong at upselling; has tested higher month-to-month pricing
  vs. lower 12-month pricing.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz
  that locates a business and prescribes actions per function (product, marketing, sales,
  customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance); thank-you page books a call, with an
  in-person Vegas session if they can help.
