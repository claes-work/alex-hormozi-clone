# What Is Reciprocity?

## Summary
Solo clip from Alex's negotiation/persuasion series (this segment covers reciprocity and rolls into a fifth principle, framing). Alex opens with a caveat: **reciprocity only works inside a culture where reciprocity is the norm** — mix cultures and one side may simply take the free thing and give nothing back ("Look at this idiot, he just gave me some free stuff"). Where the norm holds, his tactical insight is that people are highly sensitive to the *fact* of an exchange (they gave, you gave) but poor at judging **relative value** — so a trivial favor (grabbing someone's lunch) can be traded up for a large one (an airport ride). It "looks like, smells like reciprocity," but the concessions are wildly unequal, and that asymmetry is exploitable in your favor. His core method: **break every deal into as many separate variables as possible so you can trade concessions many times without moving your price.** Using a house-purchase example ($15M offer), he lists cash-vs-financed, closing period (30 vs 90 days), furniture, and — mapped onto his **value-equation** lenses — speed, price, risk, and ease. With five variables to their two, he can concede small things (ease, then risk, then speed) while the other side drops their price, staying "in the pocket" of reciprocity while keeping his own number. He claims a personal **"deal sheet" of ~80 variables** he can flex, which lets him satisfy the counterparty's unstated assumptions cheaply. He ties it back to earlier principles (multiple simultaneous offers, anchoring high on the initial and low on counters) as the setup that makes reciprocity horse-trading work. The clip then transitions to **framing** (principle five): position what you sell as an **investment/return, not a cost/overhead** — an employee as a commission on value brought, not a cost center; a vendor's "$5,000 cost" reframed as "a $5,000 investment for $15,000 in maintenance savings." He uses home-services examples (pool/patio/awning contractors): arm yourself with resale-value data so a $100k pool that adds $200k of home value becomes "half off — and you get to enjoy the pool the whole time," and always reframe the *other* party's offer back toward cost/overhead to anchor them down. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims reciprocity as a persuasion tool only functions within cultures where reciprocity is the shared norm; where norms differ, one party may accept the gift and give nothing back, so you must confirm you're operating inside a reciprocity-normed society before relying on it.
- As of March 2026, he claims people are far more sensitive to the *fact* that an exchange occurred than to the *relative value* of the two sides — so unequal concessions can be traded as if equal ("looks like, smells like reciprocity" while the values are wildly different).
- As of March 2026, his central tactic is to break any offer (a deal, an employee, a vendor) into as many discrete variables as possible so he can trade more concessions, conceding small variables while extracting price movement and keeping his own number.
- As of March 2026, he maps deal variables onto his **value-equation lenses** — speed, price, risk, and ease — and asks how each can be made faster/slower, riskier/safer, easier/harder to create more tradeable arrows "in the quiver."
- As of March 2026, he claims to keep a personal **deal sheet of ~80 variables** he can adjust, so he enters negotiations able to flex many terms and satisfy the counterparty's unstated assumptions without touching price.
- As of March 2026, he positions reciprocity horse-trading as the payoff of earlier principles: bring multiple simultaneous (compelling) offers as psychological power, **anchor high on the initial offer and low on counters**, then trade variable-concessions to stay near your initial number.
- As of March 2026 (framing, principle five, most important for employees and vendors), he claims you should position yourself/your offer as an **investment measured by return, not a cost measured by overhead** — an employee becomes "a percentage commission on what I'm bringing," not a cost center.
- As of March 2026, he claims many people don't understand framing and simply accept whatever frame is presented, so "$5,000 investment → $15,000 in maintenance savings" beats "this costs $5,000" even when functionally identical.
- As of March 2026, he advises arming framing with data (resale-value comparables): a $100k pool that adds $200k in home value is effectively "free" or "half off," and you should always reframe the counterparty's side back toward cost/overhead to push their anchor down.
