# You Need to Focus

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-screen host asks a few questions marked `>>`; all substantive speech is Alex — only his words are used as voice data). Asked what belief he held 10–15 years ago that he now sees as misguided, Alex answers: **not committing.** He defines commitment as "the elimination of alternatives," and says his misconception was that he'd work on multiple things and see which one worked — when in reality **any of them would have worked if he'd committed to it**, but by spreading himself thin he never reached critical mass on any. His central analogy is the **party**: starting one good party is hard (invite people, prepare, push manually early on), but past a threshold it takes off on its own — and trying to start four parties in one night with the same fixed resources (people, money, time) is nearly impossible. He argues the number-one reason people don't get what they want is **spreading themselves too thin**, because early on you have "functionally no money" and almost no time versus corporate scale, so the missing skill is **strategy = prioritization of resources**: deciding what to do and, more importantly, what *not* to do. The theme that succeeds isn't special ("pirate" vs "Greek" party) — it succeeds because it's the one you actually worked on; as soon as you pick, you eliminate alternatives and can move. He then tells the **origin story**: at ~26 he was making a few million/year in *revenue* (not income) across nine businesses because he thought that's what successful entrepreneurs did; he was the rainmaker in all of them, kept almost nothing after paying others, nearly died, got a DUI and a collision. Someone told him the stress would literally kill him and he had to make decisions; **Leila** pointed out that the one thing that seemed to work best had only gotten ~2 hours/week — what if he gave it all his time? Over 48 hours he called every partner, said "I'm out, you can have everything," and **fire-sold all of them** despite people telling him he'd worked years to build them. Over the next 6 months he made more money focusing on one thing than the sale of all those businesses combined would have — which is why he's such a focus advocate. Closes noting **focus never gets easier** ("like an addiction — you have to beat it every day") and the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
