# Build Your Business Like Harvard

## Summary

Alex Hormozi diagnoses why most education businesses (books, courses, coaching) are neither sticky nor sellable, using Harvard as the model of an education brand that has enterprise value. His central argument: education inherently produces one-time value, so recurring revenue and sellability come not from the teaching itself but from a scarcity-driven brand, from "consumable" deliverables that must be re-used every month, and from continued/recertification education. He walks through pricing the one-time value high and the consumable low, churn/retention benchmarks, and warns against bolting software onto an education business to fake enterprise value.

## Key claims

- **The Harvard analogy / brand-scarcity thesis.** What makes Harvard valuable as an education "business" is not the education (they also do education). It's three scarcity signals plus a brand: (1) they have standards — many apply, few get in; accepting "everybody who has a credit card" is not a high standard; (2) they make no promises — no income or timeline guarantees, you pay and might not get a result; (3) not everyone passes. Add in-person delivery and a curated network of high-quality people, and "fundamentally that's actually what they sell" — a curated experience with the Harvard brand slapped on top.
- **A sellable business needs reliable future cash flow, which requires a brand that guarantees future demand.** Harvard's brand means it isn't worried about demand (a persistent supply/demand discrepancy: many applicants, few slots). For your business to have value you must build a brand that guarantees future demand.
- **Education does not lead to continuity (except continued education).** If you teach a skill well, the student learns it and graduates — you no longer provide value. A payment plan is not continuity: selling a $12,000 program at $1,000/month for 12 months means people "graduate," they haven't churned; you have a payment plan, not recurring revenue.
- **Split every deliverable into two columns: one-time value vs. consumables.** Column A = things used once, value extracted, then no longer valuable (e.g. teaching someone to sell or generate leads). Column B = consumables that provide value again and again and must be re-consumed monthly.
- **Consumables are what drive stickiness.** Examples he gives: Gym Launch's tested winning ads (Gym Launch spent its own money proving ads in representative markets, then resold the 4-5 winners at ~100% gross margin to thousands of locations that need new ads every month); a Skool community teaching 3D printing that publishes a monthly "hot list" of trending products; a real estate/wholesaling community whose "bird dogs" scout and publish a fresh monthly property list; community itself (network effects); traditional recurring services (marketing agency, SEO).
- **Gym Launch's enterprise value was NOT from education.** People who profit from selling education businesses push the myth that Gym Launch was an education business; as owner he says the vast majority of its enterprise value came from the consumable/service components, not the teaching.
- **Pricing: charge more for the one-time value, less for the consumable.** Price the one-time education as high as the market will bear (it delivers "life transforming value" — increasing future earnings). Price the consumable at what people would pay for it standalone if the one-time piece didn't exist. The big mistake: pricing the consumable as a payment-plan slice of the upfront value (e.g. $2,500/mo), so once the one-time value is drained people leave because the ongoing value isn't worth $2,500. He advocates the "big head, long tail" model — big upfront ($10k), low ongoing ($100-$500).
- **Don't bolt software onto an education business to make it "valuable."** He has "yet to see" an education business successfully pivot to a true software exit. Software multiples come from the fundamentals (revenue retention, high gross margin, high incremental margin, less operational drag at scale), not from the fact that it's code. Adding software is usually a distraction that shrinks the business.
- **Continued education is the one education type with genuine continuity.** Professions requiring ongoing recertification / staying current with new tech let you build a membership that compounds and retains members for an entire career; that can sell at "almost a tech multiple." Harvard's own ladder (bachelor's → master's → PhD) is continuity via higher-level versions, each with drop-off and higher pricing.
- **Churn / retention benchmarks (Skool data).** Average Skool community churn is 18% monthly; 10% is roughly half that (decent/good); 5% monthly = 20-month LTV. Formula: LTV in months = 1 ÷ monthly churn. He cares most about M12 (12-month retention) and whether people who reach month 12 ever leave. Shopify (best-in-class) ~55-60% annual renewal (loses ~40%/year) but survivors stay long-term. High-end enterprise should see 80%+ annual renewal; beginners/"VSBs" (very small business owners) and prosumers are notoriously volatile — high churn there is a feature of the customer, not a bug.
- **The core metric: cost and return of a permanent customer.** All that matters is how many permanent customers you have. Model the funnel (e.g. of 10 education buyers, 3 move to the service, 1 never leaves) and compute permanent-CAC to permanent-LTV. Solving product-market fit so that post-milestone customers never leave "is the hardest question to solve"; once solved, growth is just math — find more of those people.
- **Feature-set discipline: add as few things as possible, delete freely.** Each feature must on its own justify 3-4x the price; if a customer can't say "it's worth it for this alone," it doesn't belong in the stack. Stacking calls, accountability, resource libraries, Q&A archives has not solved churn for anyone.
- **Service businesses sprout from education and are often more valuable than the education.** When customers ask the same question repeatedly, a service emerges; if done well, customers come for the service, not the education, and keep paying — making the service business worth more than the education one.
- **Three-marshmallow problem / the trade of future value for present cash.** Education/media businesses trade sellable-asset value for higher current cash flow. There's nothing wrong with an unsellable business making $10M/year — you enjoy the cash. But people who over-learn delayed gratification chase a "third marshmallow" that may never come; the magic is in the middle. His "hack" for a valuable business: "find stuff people already don't stop buying" and apply marketing/sales to it (what he did with private equity).
- **If you pivot from education to software/service, go all in — no half measures.** Examples: Sam Ovens sold and shut down Consulting.com to go all-in on Skool; Alex Becker sold and shut down Market Hero to go all-in on Hyros. Both succeeded. Trying to feed a software/service business with education cash flow usually fails because both businesses split your attention and you won't take the lifestyle cut.
