# Where Do I Find People Who Don't Exist on Job Boards?

## Summary
In this coaching exchange, a caller named Will — who runs a mentoring business for middle- and high-school kids that sells to parents and does $2.2M in revenue — tells Alex Hormozi he has more demand (especially on the boy side) than he can serve, and that his real bottleneck is finding male coaches. He has never systematically recruited and asks Alex where to look for 22-to-30-year-old mentors.

Alex reframes the search: instead of hunting for a job title, he maps the micro-behaviors the role requires and then looks for existing roles with the highest crossover, so the skill gap to train is as small as possible. He suggests camp counselors, rec coaches, and young underpaid teachers who would like to work from home. For actually reaching them he offers three tactics: search titles on LinkedIn and mass-message them, go into forums where teachers gather (pay the owner or join, since a job opportunity is a value-add to that community), and run recruiting ads. He warns that the next bottleneck will be volume, so Will needs a talent "attraction and conversion" system — a click-to-close funnel with metrics — and should target an LCGP-to-CAC ratio (lifetime gross profit per employee versus cost of acquiring talent), the supply-side equivalent of LTV:CAC.

On a second question about whether to chase audience requests like books and public speaking, Alex tells him not to add anything: he is already overwhelmed with demand, so the answer is simply more one-on-ones. He notes that productizing with an AI mentor would be the most valuable move for the business but would turn a service business into a technology business — not necessarily his recommendation — and guesses that a "pro-human" positioning will message well for a while. With great margins, strong demand, and a proven product, Alex's summary is that Will has everything and just needs to do more of what works.
