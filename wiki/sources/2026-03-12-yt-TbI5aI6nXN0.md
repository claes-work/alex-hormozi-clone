# Raise Your Bar for Excellence

## Summary
Short interview clip (an off-camera interviewer opens by observing that Alex's "bar for excellence" keeps rising year over year — what he'd have accepted as great work a year, five, or ten years ago was much lower). Alex's answer is a compact thesis on why standards rise and why patience compounds. His mechanism: **"the more you do, the more you realize you can do."** Producing more proves to yourself what you're capable of and creates a **positive-reinforcement loop** — spend ~50% longer on a piece of work and you get even more positive feedback, so you become "addicted to that loop": the more obsessive he becomes about anything, the more positive feedback he gets **on a longer time horizon**. That long-horizon part is what most people struggle with. His concrete proof point: he spent **two years** writing and editing his book, with no guarantee it wouldn't flop — and he states plainly that ten years ago he could not have waited two years for anything; the patience is a **muscle / skill developed over time**, not a fixed trait. He then reframes the classic marshmallow test: the standard version is binary (wait 15–30 minutes for a second marshmallow), but he wants a "third test" measuring *how long* a kid can wait — a day, a month. The kid who can wait a month "will win no matter what… whatever he does," because the amount of input they're willing to put in **before getting a result far exceeds everyone else's**. He acknowledges the analogy breaks at the extreme (the kid would starve), but the concept holds: tolerance for a long input-to-result gap is the differentiator. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
