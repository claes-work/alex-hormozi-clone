# After Closing 4000+ Sales, I Discovered a New Method to Close Deals Faster

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi teaches "reframing" — the one-to-three sentences a salesperson says after a prospect says anything but "yes" — as, in his view, one of the most important and least-taught closing skills. He presents a **3A framework** (Acknowledge, Associate, Ask/Attack) and **five rules** for using it ethically, arguing that the best closers ask for the sale the most times while maintaining rapport, and that the person asking the questions controls the conversation. He grounds the whole method in a stated ethical floor: only sell qualified prospects, "state the facts and tell the truth."

## Key claims (the new closing method)

- **Reframing defined**: the 1–3 sentences you say after a prospect says anything but "yes" that increase the likelihood your next statement gets them to buy. Instead of answering a prospect's question (a "trap"/"blind" question where the prospect becomes the judge of your answer), you ask a question about their question.
- **Whoever asks the questions is in control**; the moment the prospect starts asking, you are on defense. The best closers ask for the sale more times than anyone else — but doing it the wrong way makes prospects hate you, so every ask must preserve rapport.
- **The 3A framework**:
  1. **Acknowledge** — restate what they said back to them. Builds rapport, signals active listening, and buys you a few seconds to think.
  2. **Associate** — link their question/behavior to what your *best customers* do ("that's actually a question a lot of our best customers ask"), so a step away from buying gets reframed as a signal they should buy. Gives the prospect a positive label to live up to.
  3. **Ask / Attack** — ask your next question or "attack the frame" of their question ("which certifications are you looking for specifically?") rather than answering it.
- **Rule 1 — A prospect believes almost nothing you say and almost everything they say.** Don't tell them they're a good fit; ask questions until *they* conclude they are ("breadcrumb them to the logical solution"). Never ask "do you have any questions?" — it hands them the wheel and invites objections.
- **Rule 2 — No one can disagree with a question.** "Be like smoke" — you can't catch smoke; keep asking questions about their questions so they keep talking and you stay judge/jury/executioner of fit. Never voice disagreement: "you never win a sale by winning an argument."
- **Rule 3 — Tell them what their question means** (a zoom-in on Associate). Reframe the meaning of the objection (e.g. the private-wealth pitch: "90% of clients who shift over already have a wealth advisor, so you're already up the learning curve").
- **Rule 4 — Use straw men for tough truths.** Deliver harsh truths through a neutral third party so you don't insult the prospect: (a) reference a conversation with someone else earlier today, (b) reference a successful past customer ("makes you just like Sarah who had a similar plumbing business"), or (c) appeal to authority (attribute the point to an edified CEO/expert — you're just the messenger). Note: coach-hat / "worked well for me" framings can backfire with older, higher-status B2B buyers who see a young rep as a child.
- **Rule 5 — Retain childlike curiosity at all times.** Use a physical "huh" (tone follows posture even on the phone), keep a mirror/sticky note ("keep the human number one"), seek to understand not to win. "You don't close sales by being right; you close sales by making the prospect right."
- **Ethical floor**: bad selling = selling an unqualified prospect (lying that they'll get a result) OR not telling the truth. If you state the facts, tell the truth, and the prospect is qualified, you have an ethical *obligation* to ask as many times as possible.
- **Overcomes for "why won't you answer my question?"**: the *surgeon/secretary* analogy and the *mechanic* analogy ("it would be unethical to answer without looking under the hood first") — then: if anyone gives you an answer on the first call without assessing you, run the other way.
- **Universal deep-sale question**: "What are you most afraid of having happen?" (used later in a sale); for the first objection use "what's your main concern / what are the variables you're using to decide?" For stalls: "what would make it a no?" and "what would it take for you to say yes?"
