# I Have a 3.6M-Person Facebook Group. How Do I Monetize It With AI Coaching?

## Summary
In a live Q&A on @MoreMozi, a member who runs (with partner Valentine) the world's largest Facebook group for AI — 3.6 million people — plus an AI education/certification business explains he is badly under-monetizing the audience and asks for a content and monetization strategy. His stack: a $1,500 course, a $5K recurring AI-certified-consultant program, plus newly launched $5 front-end offers with $17/$27/$37 upsells funneling into a $49/month School community. Alex first reframes the "3.6M" vanity number down to what matters: ~1M are in Western countries and ~100K are monthly-active, and that active subset is the real "pseudo email list" to work. His core content prescription is declarative-over-procedural: show what AI can do (the cool built result), not how to build it — each build becomes a lead magnet for a different avatar and the ultimate proof, with CTAs pointing into the paid community. He argues creators wildly overestimate how much variety the market needs (audiences have "zero creativity"), so the winning move is to show the same framework applied across many settings — invoking Dave Ramsey's 45-year "spend less than you make" show and his own money-models webinar, where he used a "four ways to sell" reverse-labeling device so every listener self-identifies into the framework. The synthesizing recommendation: siphon everything from Facebook into School, and rebuild the webinar as a "choose your own adventure" — "here are five ways to use AI to make money; which one works for you?" — anchored to survey data showing 88% of buyers joined to learn how to make money with AI.

- (2026-02-09) Alex reframes the number: discount the ~2.6M non-Western; of the ~1M Western members, the active subset is what counts — probably ~100K monthly-active per the guest's estimate.
- (2026-02-09) The situation "isn't as bad as you think" — treat the ~100K active people as your real pseudo email list, because almost everyone else either can't afford it or isn't paying attention.
- (2026-02-09) Content strategy hinges on declarative vs. procedural: demonstrate what AI can do (show the cool thing you built and what it does), not the boring how-to that no one watches and then feels guilty about not watching.
- (2026-02-09) Each build you show becomes a lead magnet for a different avatar and is the ultimate proof; sprinkle in testimonials, but showing what someone can do across a zillion use cases does the heavy lifting.
- (2026-02-09) Structure content like a matrix: functions you know how to build (e.g. an answering service) crossed with business use cases it applies to (lawn care, spa, etc.) — same build shown across many settings.
- (2026-02-09) Creators assume the market needs far more variety than it actually does; the audience has "zero creativity" and cannot imagine applying a framework themselves, so you must show the same wheel going through mud, water, gravel, and concrete.
- (2026-02-09) Dave Ramsey is cited as the extreme example: a call-in show telling people to "spend less than you make," run three hours a day for 45 years — the same message re-contextualized endlessly (lottery winner, divorced person, etc.).
- (2026-02-09) In his own money-models webinar Alex deliberately recited a long laundry list of business types because people wait to hear "that one's me" before believing a framework applies to them.

- (2026-02-09) Before introducing each of the four, he had the audience raise hands for "who's number one?" etc., so by the end everyone had self-identified into one of the four and the framework "works for everyone."

- (2026-02-09) The marketing goal should be to siphon everyone from Facebook into School; show what you do applied in a new setting, don't teach how to build the whole thing — or if you do, keep it ~60 seconds ("we connected this to this, trained it on this, here's the voice that came out").
- (2026-02-09) The guest's survey found 88% of buyers, when asked why they joined, said they want to learn how to make money with AI. [guest-attributed] [self-reported]
- (2026-02-09) Alex endorses rebuilding the webinar as a "choose your own adventure": "here are five ways you can use AI to make money — which one will work for you?"
