# The 'Junk Drawer' Problem Every Founder Hits

## Summary

Alex Hormozi advises a direct-response ecommerce founder that "junk drawer" work is not
the real constraint if paid acquisition can still double. He recommends contractors or
VAs for sporadic niche tasks, then reframes the larger strategic issue: a portfolio of
random SKUs is a media-arbitrage business that tends to hit margin and defensibility
limits, while enterprise value comes from building a real brand around a defensible
product.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A pile of miscellaneous tasks is a complaint, not necessarily the business constraint.
- If paid spend can double profitably, customer acquisition is the higher-leverage focus.
- Sporadic specialized work should often be handled by VAs, contractors, or project-based
  help instead of full-time hires.
- Direct-response ecommerce with disconnected products is effectively media arbitrage.
- Media-arbitrage ecommerce can work into the low eight figures, but rising CAC, supply
  chain issues, compressed margins, and copycat products can create a wall.
- The founder should build one brand around a legitimate product rather than rotate
  through random SKUs.
- Influencers and affiliates work better when they believe in the brand and product.
- Brand completes the product loop because it creates memory, trust, and repeatability.
- Private equity buyers pay for brands more than isolated products.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "They buy brands. They don't buy products."

> "You run a media arbitrage business."
