# My Unconventional Diet Explained in 15min (2021-10-27)

## Summary

In a ~15-minute solo video, Alex Hormozi lays out the personal eating system he
says he has used for almost 20 years. He frames it against the biohacking, keto,
and intermittent-fasting fads he encountered at a mastermind of high-revenue
entrepreneurs, arguing that every diet works only because it creates a calorie
deficit. His method reduces eating to two numbers — a calorie target (bodyweight ×
a coefficient from 7 to 21 by goal) and a protein target (bodyweight in grams) —
with the leftover calories spent freely, including daily ice cream, cookies, and
alcohol. His central thesis is sustainability: a way of eating you can maintain
for life while keeping a six-pack.

## His diet

Two numbers are all he says 99% of people need (a third, training volume, is
deliberately skipped in this video):

**1. Calories = bodyweight × goal coefficient (range 7–21).**
- Coefficient scales by goal: low end (~7–10) = extreme/fast weight loss;
  high end (~21) = extreme weight gain; everything in between is proportional.
- Worked example for a 200 lb person:
  - Maintain (high side): 200 × 15 = 3,000 cal (he cites a maintenance range of
    ~2,600–3,000).
  - Fast weight loss: 200 × 10 = 2,000 cal.
  - Moderate weight loss: 200 × 11 = 2,200 cal.
- If weight loss is too slow, just lower the coefficient. He argues precise
  formulas don't matter because intake is easy to control while expenditure is
  very hard to measure.

**2. Protein = bodyweight (lb) × 1, in grams.**
- 200 lb → 200 g protein/day.
- Conversion "life hack": 100 g protein ≈ 1 lb of lean meat, so 200 g = 2 lb of
  lean meat per day. He says he eats ~2 lb of meat a day.
- Any lean protein counts and they're roughly interchangeable: shrimp, tilapia,
  chicken breast, and lean steaks (tenderloin, flank, top round, top sirloin).
  Can be split across different meats (e.g. half a pound of four things).

**3. Remaining calories = "whatever the hell you want."**
- Method to reserve protein calories: multiply protein grams × 5 (not the
  textbook 4 cal/g) to account for the fat carried along with lean protein
  sources. 200 g × 5 = ~1,000 cal from protein.
- Leftover = calorie target − protein calories. Example: 2,200 − 1,000 = 1,200
  cal/day of free/discretionary food (cookies, ice cream, alcohol, potatoes,
  rice, "nerds ropes," etc.).
- Rule: eat protein first, then "fill up to the lid" with whatever you want.

**Meal timing / structure:** completely flexible. Split the daily totals into
1, 3, or 5 meals — "it doesn't matter." To gain or lose, he keeps the protein
base fixed and only adjusts the extra/variable calories up or down.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-10-27)

- All diets (keto, low-fat, portion control, intermittent fasting) work by the
  same mechanism: they cut roughly one-third of calories. The specific macro or
  protocol is irrelevant; only total calories matter. (paraphrase)
- Keto "not working" is usually because people added excess calories (butter,
  ribeye for breakfast) that more than replaced the carbs they cut. (paraphrase)
- No one is immune to a calorie deficit — everyone loses weight in a deficit,
  only the rate differs. (paraphrase)
- Diet sustainability is the whole point: if he can't do something for the rest
  of his life, he sees no point doing it; being in shape for 8 weeks is
  worthless versus being in shape for life. (paraphrase)
- Never restricting any food is what prevents "falling off" — nothing is
  forbidden, so there are no cheat days and no bingeing. Eating ice cream daily
  makes it stop being special. (paraphrase)
- He comes from the fitness world and says biohacking, orange glasses, toe
  shoes, hydration shticks, etc. are "make believe" — good for selling, but a
  farce. (paraphrase)
- He claims he has had a six-pack continuously since age 13–14 and has stayed in
  shape for ~17–20 years eating this way. (self-reported)
- Body-type framing: he uses a fat/muscle matrix (high/low fat × high/low
  muscle); direction of desired change tells you whether to raise/lower calories
  and volume. (paraphrase)
