# The Problem with Getting Advice for Your Business

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says advice is hard to evaluate because advisors often lack context,
competence, or aligned incentives. He says founders should be willing to take advice while
also filtering it through first-principles reasoning and responsibility for the final
decision. He then discusses why people follow advice: status, credibility, power
("say-do correspondence"), and likeness.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Ignorance debt is reduced by spending time and money to talk with people ahead of you who
  can help you see around corners.
- Advice is often wrong because the advisor lacks context, lacks competence, or has
  misaligned incentives.
- A founder needs a parallel engine: be willing to take advice, but run it through a
  responsible first-principles filter.
- Some strong executors still fail because they switch directions too quickly after
  conflicting advice.
- People are more likely to follow advice from people with influence.
- Alex breaks influence into status, credibility, power, and likeness.
- Status comes from controlling reinforcers or scarce resources.
- Credibility comes from predicting something that then happens.
- Power is "say-do correspondence": someone follows the recipe, the promised good result
  occurs, and future adherence increases.
- Likeness increases baseline influence when people perceive similarity.
- He uses a logging-company founder as a self-reported example of unexpected likeness and
  "Mozy Nation" reach in that community.
