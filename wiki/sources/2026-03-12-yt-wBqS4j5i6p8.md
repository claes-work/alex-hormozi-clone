# Is It Wrong to Work Hard?

## Summary
Interview clip (off-camera host asks probing questions; Alex is the sole subject and all cited voice data is his). Alex defends hard work against the anti-hustle / "toxic hard work" framing that treats working a lot as unhealthy. His core argument: he works nearly all the time by choice — "I do as much as I can of the thing that I want to do with every minute of my day" — and he rejects the premise that there's an externally-defined "healthy" or "balanced" amount of work that everyone should hit. He reframes the debate as a fight against the word **"should"**, which he calls "the expectation motor of all of our psyches" (you should get a degree, marry her, be more balanced, work out less) — and argues that zoomed out on a cosmic scale, "there is no should… do what you want to do." When the host presses on what the real *measurement* is, Alex answers that the only measurement that matters is his own happiness/fulfillment: "I work because I enjoy working," and if he stopped getting reinforcement from work, he'd work less. He says people who criticize others' choices (too much work, no kids, working with your spouse) are usually casting judgment born of their own unexamined desire — they see a "delta" between someone else's life and theirs and either admit they want it or "cast stones at it so that we don't feel bad to our ego." He illustrates with an argument with his stepmother-figure who said "I would never want your life… it's so unbalanced," which Alex rebutted by asking where specifically he's unbalanced (physically? financially? romantically?) and landing on: it's *good* that we want different lives. He frames caring what others think as the one thing he'll "try and beat out of me until I die," reckoning he's ~30% better at it than 10 years ago, and cites concrete choices that freedom enabled: marrying in 8 days with no parents present, working with Leila every hour of every day. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex works "all the time" with no hobbies besides working out (~4x/week) and works until his output per unit of time "starts to drop precipitously," then takes a break (usually TV/Netflix) — and considers this healthy for *him*.
- As of March 2026, he rejects any externally-prescribed "healthy" or "balanced" amount of work: "why do you want me to do something that I would prefer to do less?" — he spends every minute doing what he most wants to do.
- As of March 2026, he is "a big advocate for destroying should, the word" — calling "should" the expectation motor of the psyche, and saying that zoomed out to cosmic scale, "there is no should. Do what you want to do."
- As of March 2026, the only valid measurement of whether an amount of work is "good" is his own happiness and fulfillment — "I work because I enjoy working," and if the reinforcement stopped, his work would go down.
- As of March 2026, people who criticize others' life choices are usually projecting their own suppressed desire: they see the "delta" between that life and theirs and either admit they'd want it or "cast stones at it so that we don't feel bad to our ego."
- As of March 2026, he recounts rebutting a family member's "I would never want your life / it's so unbalanced" by asking where specifically (physical, financial, romantic) — concluding it's good that people want different lives.
- As of March 2026, caring what other people think is the one thing he'll "try and beat out of me until I die"; he estimates he's ~30% better at it than 10 years ago and expects another ~30% over the next decade, and that gain has let him do what he wants.
- As of March 2026, examples of choices that freedom enabled: he got married in 8 days with none of his parents present, and works with his wife every hour of every day — dismissing "is that healthy?" with "we like it. Why do you care? How does it affect you?"
- As of March 2026, he says he thinks a lot about death but uses it as a frame "to give myself permission to do the things that I want to do" without hearing others' judgment.
