# I Built My $100M Business WRONG...

## Summary
In this short reflective solo video, Hormozi shares the biggest takeaways from a "half a billion dollar" mastermind of eight entrepreneurs whose combined revenue exceeded $500M/year, in which he says he was the second smallest (projecting his year at ~$32M while most others were above $100M and one over $250M). He frames the video as lessons from "my current chapter, my current season," explicitly self-reporting his own mistakes rather than issuing universal rules. Four lessons emerge: (1) "scale zero" — to grow he must make himself less and less important until only the biggest decisions are left to him, so he is never the bottleneck; (2) the bigger players all attacked "oceans" (large markets) while he was in a "lake" (a single niche); (3) focus on one primary business vehicle — running three businesses as CEO was, in his telling, a "terrible idea" that cost him roughly two years of personal development; and (4) everyone successful sold high-gross-margin, high-value offers. He closes by reframing "decision" via its etymology (the "-cide" root meaning "to kill") as choosing by elimination, and lays out the criteria for his next venture: an ocean market, one thing, high margin and high value, and a structure where high-level leaders remove him as the bottleneck.

## Key claims
- "Simple scales" — the biggest businesses in the room sold simple things; simplicity is what allows a business to scale.
- "Scale zero": to scale without becoming the bottleneck, the founder must reduce the business's requirement of him toward zero. "For me to get bigger I must get smaller within the company."
- As a founder moves up the skill set, they progressively outsource administrative work, then back-end customer service, then sales, then marketing, then messaging/positioning — until only the biggest, most important decisions remain for them.
- The people out-earning him in the room were notable for how *little* they were doing day-to-day — by deliberate design, which is hard, costs mistakes, and requires hiring genuinely good people.
- Everyone else was going after a bigger marketplace than he was; they were all in "oceans" while he was in a "lake" (a single niche).
- Niching down early to a single niche (gyms) was still the right call at the time; going after a lake when starting out is not a bad idea.
- Focus on one primary business vehicle. Being CEO of multiple businesses is, for everyone except someone like Elon Musk, not feasible.
- People who make a tremendous amount of money make it in one vehicle, then may own pieces of other businesses; if they are the main owner of multiple businesses, they are not active partners — they've hired a CEO and executive team to run and grow those businesses while they advise strategically.
- Almost every successful person in the room sold a very high gross-margin service and/or product; the two selling the lowest-margin offers were the smaller businesses.
- The ideal is a high-margin AND high-value offer, which is hard to find (it's easier to have high margin with low value).
- Talent hierarchy: doers → managers → leaders → decision makers; he wants high-level people who have already "run and won the Olympic gold," who plug in "pre-loaded with the solutions," "batteries included."
- "We need to be reminded more than we need to be taught" (myself included).
- Etymology of "decide": the "-cide" root (as in suicide, homicide) means "to kill," so a decision is choosing by elimination — actively killing off all other options.
