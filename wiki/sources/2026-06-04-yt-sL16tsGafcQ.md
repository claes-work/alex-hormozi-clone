# If You Want To Create Wealth In 2026, Watch This (4 Paths)

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out a 2x2 framework for building "mega money," defined by two axes: whose money you use (yours vs. other people's) and whose business you operate (yours vs. other people's). This yields four paths — bootstrapping, raising capital, investing, and fund management — each with distinct risk profiles, trade-offs, and ideal candidates. He backs the framework with the current Forbes top-11 list, walks through his own experience across all four paths, and works a numerical example showing how fund management leverages a small GP check into outsized returns.

## Key claims

- **The 2x2 of wealth creation.** Two variables — your money vs. other people's money, and your business vs. other people's business — produce four paths: (1) your money + your business = **bootstrapping**; (2) other people's money + your business = **raising capital**; (3) your money + other people's business = **investing**; (4) other people's money + other people's business = **fund management**.
- **Path 1 — Bootstrapping.** Fund the business from your own savings and cash flow, reinvesting profit; no outside investors. Typical for low-cost-to-start businesses (services, agencies, home services, education, e-comm, local businesses, increasingly software). Slowest path; you keep control and equity, decide the pace, and can exit on your own time or never. You incur more *non-cash* debt (management, leadership, technical, data debt) because money isn't available to solve those problems. Capital constraint limits which opportunities you can pursue. Recommended for a first business so you "pay off ignorance" without losing friends-and-family money.
- **Path 2 — Raising capital.** You run the company but sell equity slices to investors to fund fast growth. Fits businesses with huge upfront costs, later margin expansion, or winner-take-all/network-effect dynamics (tech platforms, marketplaces, pharma). Lets you hire top talent, outspend competitors, run negative acquisition costs, and pursue rarer opportunities with fewer well-funded competitors. Trade-offs: you serve two customers (end customer + investors), dilute equity, and risk losing control via board seats/liquidation preferences/ratchets — you can be voted out of your own company (cites Steve Jobs). Venture money is "grand slam money"; most founders who take losses have n=1 and end up with nothing.
- **Path 3 — Investing.** Use your actively earned cash to buy pieces of other people's businesses (cash-flowing companies, public stocks, real estate). You fund, don't run. Offers diversification but caps upside; the best investors are actually concentrated, not diversified. Easiest lifestyle (no boss), but the slowest path, and almost no one *makes* their initial money this way — they earn high active income first, then invest. Real estate is the #1 path to creating millionaires but not billionaires. Requires a very long time horizon.
- **Path 4 — Fund management.** Raise a pool of capital from LPs (limited partners) and invest it in other people's businesses, often adding debt for leverage. Highest-leverage path with the smallest personal checks. GP (general partner) often ends up richer than any single LP. Structure includes hurdle/pref rates (typically 6–8%) and a GP/LP profit split. Risks: enormous responsibility, very long feedback loop, accountability to LPs, regulators, entrepreneurs, and customers — "you can be rich on paper, but the entire time you almost feel like a slave."
- **Forbes top-11 proof points.** Musk, Ellison, Zuckerberg, Bezos, Page, Brin, Jensen Huang all raised capital; Ballmer (Microsoft), Michael Dell, and the Waltons (Walmart) bootstrapped; Warren Buffett is investing. Fund management doesn't appear in the top 11 but shows up a few spots deeper.
- **Capital follows deals, not the reverse.** "There is no lack of capital in the world, only a lack of good deals" (advice from a mentor). Ability to raise = founder track record × size and believability of the opportunity.
- **His own path.** Got rich bootstrapping (gym → Prestige Labs → Allan/software), invested that compounding cash into other companies via acquisition.com, co-founded Skool (venture-backed = raising capital), and does fund management via ACQ real estate as a GP on large buildings.
