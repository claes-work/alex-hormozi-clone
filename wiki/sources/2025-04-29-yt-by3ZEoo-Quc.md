# How to Buy Anything You Want Without Going Broke

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out four sources people buy things from — past money (savings), income money (monthly earnings), debt money (future earnings), and what he calls "new money" — and argues that the wealthiest, happiest people he knows fund discretionary "grandiose" purchases from new money rather than draining savings, income, or debt. New money means being resourceful: using existing, underutilized assets (spare time, spare capacity, idle rooms) to generate additional income earmarked for a specific purchase within a defined, usually short, time period. He illustrates the principle with his own 36,000 sq ft building — which he promised Leila would earn more than it cost, leading them to spin up advisory services to cover it.

## Key claims (his spending / buying philosophy)

- There are four ways to buy anything: **past money** (savings), **income money** (monthly earnings you can spend without touching savings), **debt money** (future earnings you borrow against and must pay off), and **new money** (income generated on purpose from underutilized existing resources for a specific purchase).
- The best-with-money, wealthiest friends he knows are comfortable buying grandiose things (big houses, cars, yachts, buildings) — but they fund them with new money, not by draining existing flows.
- His preferred behavior: "I want this thing, so I'm going to go make the money to buy the thing" — not use existing income, savings, or resources.
- Debt is acceptable only under strict conditions: he knows he'll pay it off within a year, there's no prepayment penalty, and he has a clear, time-defined plan to make the money.
- Advice to a business owner choosing between a sensible office and a bigger, pricier one (~2.5x the price): buy the big one you actually want, but pay it off within a year.
- His 36,000 sq ft building was a significant purchase less because of price and more because he didn't need it at the time — he wanted a premium home gym and a venue for meetups and portfolio-company meetings.
- He justified/funded the building via "sawdust money": using existing resources currently being underutilized to generate new money for a specific project (e.g., portfolio companies were spending ~$4M/year on event venues).
- He promised Leila the building would make more money than it cost; fulfilling that promise was part of why they spun up the advisory services.
- Concept of "creating a vacuum" / "make yourself poor": deliberately creating deprivation or a threshold that raises your demand for money and motivates resourcefulness.
- Stay ahead of spending not by keeping lifestyle low relative to income alone, but by increasing new money relative to the new purchase — which is typically defined and temporary (work overtime for a month, six months, or a year).
- The tactic scales down: a single mom at his gym couldn't afford membership, so she drove Uber one day a week to pay for it — became a long-standing member and lost 100 pounds.
- Caveat: this is for people further along. "If you are broke, don't do that... just focus on the new money stuff."
- On enjoying money: some wealthy people never enjoy it because of a scarcity mindset. If money is your goal, don't spend it. If money isn't your goal, use it as a tool — fund consumption you know isn't a good investment from underutilized assets, not core income.
- He personally dislikes spending his "core money" (business distributions) but has learned that for something crazy, he must be willing to do something crazy to earn it.
- Minimize regret: buying an expensive thing feels fine when the money didn't come from funds he'd rather use elsewhere. He says he has never once regretted funding a purchase this way.
