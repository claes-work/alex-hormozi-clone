# Helping a SaaS Company Fix 33% Monthly Churn

## Summary
In this short coaching clip, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a SaaS e-commerce fulfillment platform (specializing in drop-shipping fulfillment) whose customers churn at 33% per month. The owner reports that 99% of their traffic is brand-new businesses trying to start an e-commerce operation, and that these customers struggle to find products to sell and drive profitable traffic. Hormozi frames much of this as unavoidable "structural churn" inherent to serving the very-small-business (VSMB) segment, and prescribes reducing product overwhelm and funneling all resources toward the customer's activation point to lower churn.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex distinguishes "structural churn" — churn inherent to a market segment — as something founders should not lose sleep over; he notes Shopify (a comparable player in the same space) has higher churn.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes a market tiering: VSMB (very small business) → SMB → mid-market → enterprise, and says the VSMB space is "almost the same as consumer," meaning high churn is simply the nature of the game.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the VSMB trade-off as an "infinite market" — a downside of high churn offset by a nearly unlimited supply of prospects.
- As of March 2026, Alex points to Shopify's dual-sided monetization as a model worth exploring: a small fee to start, then upside from payments on the users who self-select as highest-and-best — making money on people who make no money, then more money once those people succeed.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the number one reason people cancel is overwhelm — too many things, not knowing what to do — and recommends improving product through elimination rather than addition: strip features down to the single next action a user needs, revealing more only after each step is completed.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises running a regression analysis on customer cohorts to identify activation points, then directing all resources toward moving people to that activation point — pausing growth initiatives and new features — on the logic that if the activation point is right, churn falls; if churn does not fall, the assumed activation point is wrong.
- Caller claim (unverified): the owner's SaaS drop-shipping fulfillment platform has 33% monthly churn, 99% new-business traffic, and a known "golden path" / activation for customers.
- Alex estimates the company could likely bring 33% churn down toward roughly 10%, referencing Shopify as being around that level.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "So you have something called structural churn, which is something to not lose sleep over. Like Shopify has more churn than you do."

> "That's just the game. Yeah, but the good news is there's a gazillion of them and so that's the trade. It's an infinite market almost."

> "They make money on people who make no money, and then once those people make a lot of money they make more money with them. And so it's kind of like a dual-sided monetization structure."

> "You want to think about product through elimination rather than addition. So it's how can I eliminate things from the product that people aren't using? The number one reason people cancel is overwhelm."

> "What is the one thing I need them to do right now? And then until they do that I'm not going to show them something else, and then once they have done that I will show them the next thing to do."

> "It's like the 100 golden BBs. There's no silver bullet."

> "If that is the constraint of the business then I don't care about getting new members... I'm not doing any growth initiatives. I'm not doing any new features. Everything that we're focused on is how do we get more people to that activation point, and I don't care about anything else."

> "If the activation point is right then churn will go down. If the activation point that you have is wrong then you have to find what it really is."
