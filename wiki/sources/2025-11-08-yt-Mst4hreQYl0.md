# Watch This To Generate 1000s of Leads (In Any Niche)

## Summary

Solo talk in which Hormozi lays out his lead-magnet framework: instead of sending
cold traffic straight to "buy my thing" or "submit for a quote," offer a low-cost or
free mini offer that fully solves a narrow problem, which then reveals the problem the
core offer solves. He walks through the three lead-magnet types (reveal a problem,
free trial, one step of many), the four delivery mechanisms (software/tools,
information, services, physical products), how to name a lead magnet (test headlines /
ask your audience), and how to close with a clear CTA. Explicitly framed as a companion
to his book `$100M Leads` (references page 31 and the book's own split-tested headline).

## Key claims (his lead-gen framework)

- Most businesses send traffic straight to a buy/quote/book-call ask; most visitors
  aren't ready to buy yet, so they leave and never come back.
- A "submit for quote" / "book a call" step is fundamentally just a **lead-capture**
  objective; you can capture far more leads by offering something first.
- Core mechanism: offer a **mini offer** — a complete solution to a *narrow* problem,
  low-cost or free — that reveals a further problem solved by the core offer. Leads
  who take a low-cost/free offer now are more likely to buy a related higher-cost offer
  later (cites page 31 of the leads book).
- Same 100 visitors: a direct "buy my thing" ask might convert ~1; leading with free
  help first can yield **3-5x** the hand-raisers. You don't get more traffic — you
  convert a higher percentage of it, so the gain drops straight to the bottom line.
- **Test the packaging (headline) of a lead magnet even more than its contents** —
  changing the headline alone can move demand for it by 2x, 3x, 10x. A bad lead magnet
  failing doesn't mean lead magnets don't work; "you just don't know that you suck yet."
- **Sell at the point of greatest deprivation.** The mini offer must not solve the same
  problem the core product solves; it should satisfy one need and expose the next
  (entree vs. dessert analogy). The moment after you satisfy a need is the worst time
  to sell that same thing.
- Three lead-magnet types: (1) **Reveal a problem / assessment** — his favorite;
  creates deprivation, best for problems that worsen while waiting (website speed test,
  posture analysis, termite inspection, financial audit). (2) **Free trial / sample** —
  low-barrier taste test; give the solution then remove it to create deprivation; limit
  uses, quantity, or time. (3) **One step of a multi-step process** — best for complex
  products (laser hair removal takes 6-8 sessions; first two videos of a course).
- Four delivery mechanisms: **software/tools** (calculators, assessment tools,
  templates), **information** (mini-courses, guides, expert interviews — infinitely
  scalable, no operational drag), **services** (free audits / done-for-you work — only
  for *qualified* prospects), **physical products** (books, branded items like a "CEO"
  shirt to qualify a list). These can be combined.
- Handle the "freebie seekers / tire kickers" objection by **qualifying** who gets the
  lead magnet (a dropdown routes qualified vs. unqualified). Only do free services for
  prospects with budget, authority, need, and urgency.
- Free-services math: if hard cost is $25/hr but the perceived value is $250+, and you
  convert 1 of 4, your CAC is ~$100 — worth it if the customer is worth far more than
  $100.
- **Naming**: how you name the lead magnet drives engagement more than anything;
  advertise the *result*, not the vehicle ("big booty boot camp," not "six-week deadlift
  and squat seminar"). Test names via small ad tests, story polls, texts, or comment
  A/B — but only let your target avatar answer. Naming conventions: number + outcome +
  timeframe; "how to [yay] without [boo]"; "the [adjective] [type] [that good thing]";
  "X mistakes that [prevent good thing]."
- **CTA / monetization**: the salespeople who ask the most times get the most deals, so
  ask often — but keep providing value between asks (increase value-per-second) so people
  keep letting you ask. CTA formula: (1) clear, not clever; (2) exact next action;
  (3) a reason to act now. Any reason beats no reason (the line-cutting "because"
  study); scarcity/urgency are bonus reasons. Service businesses have real capacity
  limits (78% of businesses are service) — leverage that scarcity, or use rolling cohorts
  if you're large.
