# Helping a Law Firm Get to $20M/Year

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A 25-year attorney runs a highly specialized Texas practice that serves as an officer of the court for judges statewide (~1,400 judges, 250 counties), delivering a debt/judgment-recovery remedy off proprietary software. The firm does $15M revenue at a 74% profit margin from a single building outside Dallas, is the dominant player (roughly half the entire state industry; #2, #3, #4 combined don't match it), and has locked-down relationships with five of the seven "whales" (the law firms that ask a judge to appoint a receiver). He wants to reach $20M and is at a fork in the road: expand to another state (e.g. Oklahoma — one hour north, where an existing Texas whale is inviting him, but it needs a ~2-year legislative change), or "whale hunt" to recruit new national high-volume firms into Texas. Alex's reasoning proceeds in layers. First, a **decision-making frame**: when multiple paths all lead up the mountain, don't agonize over the perfect one — both get you there, so deciding fast beats deliberating. Second, **risk-adjusted return**: recruiting whales costs little to attempt, but he dislikes any path where his growth "relies on somebody else opening their business and doing a good job," and prefers to "go where the fish already are." Third, his signature **20% cost-of-change** rule: any change to a business incurs a guaranteed ~20% performance dip before gains accrue, so he won't accept a change unless the upside is 50%+ or a doubling — this discipline stops the endless-tweaking habit that leaves businesses persistently below potential. Applying it: since no other state can match Texas's revenue (Oklahoma might add only ~10% incremental, "less than three" of the current 15), the answer is to **grow the pie in Texas, not chase geography**. Order of operations: (1) increase share of pie — go from ~40-50% to 70-80% via "monopolistic levers" that starve competitors out and massively raise business value; (2) grow the total Texas pie; (3) only after truly tapping Texas, look at the three-to-four other states where the remedy could legally make sense (e.g. New York). His constraint reframe: figure out why the other ~50% of the market is "allowed to breathe," then "take the oxygen out of the room." Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, when several strategic paths all reach the goal ("both ways will get you up the mountain"), Alex treats picking the single "perfect" path as a trap — obsessing over which route caused him to deliberate far longer than warranted; accepting that any of them works let him make big decisions faster.
- As of March 2026, he evaluates expansion moves by maximizing **risk-adjusted return**, not raw upside.
- As of March 2026, he dislikes growth paths that depend on a third party's execution ("I just don't want the growth of my business relying on somebody else opening their business and doing a good job") and prefers to "go where the fish already are."
- As of March 2026, his **cost-of-change** rule: any change to a business brings a guaranteed ~20% performance decrease before any gains accrue — a fixed cost he must pay regardless — so he won't take a change for a mere potential 20% gain; he needs to see 50% or a possible doubling to justify a 20% dip.
- As of March 2026, this rule is the antidote to compulsive tweaking: owners who chase a new 20%-upside idea every week drive employees insane and leave businesses "persistently far below potential," because next week brings yet another idea and nothing compounds.
- As of March 2026, for this client no other state can generate Texas-level revenue; Oklahoma is one of five whales and far smaller by head count, so it might add only ~10% incremental top-line ("less than three" against the current 15) — not worth the mental drag for an "overthinking brain."
- As of March 2026, the growth priority order is: (1) increase **share of pie** (least effort) — go from ~40-50% to 70-80% via "monopolistic levers" that starve out competitors and sharply raise the business's value; (2) **grow the total pie** of Texas; (3) only after genuinely tapping Texas, evaluate the three-to-four other states where the remedy could legally work (e.g. New York).
- As of March 2026, he reframes the market-share problem as a constraint: ask why the remaining ~50% of the market is "allowed to breathe," then "take the oxygen out of the room."
- As of March 2026, the client's remedy is Texas-specific because Texas is (per the client) the second-toughest state to recover unpaid debt/judgments in — it protects wages (no wage garnishment) and the homestead (can't force a house sale) — so the software-based tool only makes sense in a handful (three-to-four) of similarly tough states.
