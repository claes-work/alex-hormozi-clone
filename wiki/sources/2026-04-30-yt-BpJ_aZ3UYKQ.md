# You're Not Suffering From Imposter Syndrome. You're Just Lying.

## Summary

Alex rejects affirmation-style "worthiness" language and reframes imposter syndrome as a
signal that someone is claiming authority without evidence. He separates doing something
new from pretending to have done it: a founder can honestly say a project is a bet, a
new level, or uncertain, but becomes an imposter when they teach or guarantee what they
have not proven. His marketing rule is to state facts and tell the truth; if the truth is
not compelling, change the world until the facts become compelling.

## Key claims

- Alex says people who have not achieved what they want are not yet worthy of it, and he
  frames that as useful because it permits improvement.
- He distinguishes students learning from people pretending to teach what they have not
  done.
- Doing something new is not the same as lying; the problem is claiming certainty,
  authority, or guarantees beyond the evidence.
- Alex says he does not feel like an imposter when making factual claims backed by what
  he has done.
- His marketing rule is to state the facts and tell the truth.
- If the truth is not compelling enough, Alex says the answer is not to lie but to change
  reality until the facts become compelling.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Better to know you're bad for a season than pretend you're good for a lifetime."

> "State the facts and tell the truth."

> "If the truth isn't compelling, it doesn't mean you lie. It means you change the world to make the facts compelling."
