# Reopening the Right Way (2020-10-29)

## Summary

A Gym-Launch-era video coaching gym owners on how to reopen profitably under
COVID capacity limits. Hormozi argues that capacity restrictions force a
restructuring of the offer, price, and session model, and gives a simple margin
math for calculating exactly how many sessions a gym can afford to run. He frames
the reopening as a rare, society-granted chance to "click reset" and fix the
gym's economics from scratch.

## Key claims (paraphrase)

- (2020) Capacity caps (e.g. 10 people per session) break the large-group profit
  model; getting permission to reopen does not mean reopening is profitable.
- (2020) Price must reflect the level of service delivered — if forced into
  near-one-on-one service, charge $300–$400/month rather than letting people back
  in at their old group price. Letting old prices stand "doesn't make monetary
  sense."
- (2020) Reopening tilts the supply/demand curve in the owner's favor: the clients
  who return are the most eager, so demand exceeds available training time,
  justifying a price increase.
- (2020) **Rule of thumb: maintain a minimum 80% gross margin on services** (hard
  fulfillment cost only — excludes rent, admin, marketing). Every margin point
  matters; the average gym runs ~12.5% net annually.
- (2020) Margin math to size the schedule: revenue × 20% = fulfillment budget;
  budget ÷ cost-per-session = max sessions/month; ÷ 4.3 weeks = sessions/week.
  Worked example: $10k revenue → $2k payroll → $20/session → 100 sessions/month →
  ~23 sessions/week.
- (2020) Base decisions on *reality, not promises* — bill everyone, see which cards
  clear, then set the schedule from what actually goes through.
- (2020) Start with too few sessions (2–3/day, 90% margins) and add back, rather
  than over-building capacity you must later cut.
- (2020) Five reset levers: (1) switch unlimited → 2–3x/week as the main offer
  (doubles/triples capacity); (2) bill on a weekly/bi-weekly/28-day cadence (28-day
  = 13 billings/year, ~8% extra topline for the same service); (3) reprice to match
  service; (4) cut low-attendance sessions and space-hungry exercises; (5) shorten
  sessions 60 → 45 min (raises capacity, cuts payroll).
- (2020) Minimum reprice recommendation: $167/28-days ($39/week) bare minimum; most
  should be $49–$69/week. Smaller sessions raise perceived value, so you can charge
  more, not less.
- (2020) References the *Gym Launch Secrets* book, section two ("profit levers").

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Even though you get permission to open back up, it doesn't mean that you're
> necessarily going to have the profit to be able to do so."

> "You want to make sure that you're making an 80% gross margin minimum on your
> services... This has been a rule of thumb that we've used at Gym Launch forever."

> "Don't go off of promises. I'm begging you. Go off of reality."

> "Right now society is giving you permission to change all these things in your gym
> at once and then restart the right way with the right price points, the right
> hours, the right amount of people per session... you can click reset on your
> business."

> "You're not cutting sessions, you're adding value by making it more personalized
> because the sessions are going to be smaller and more controlled."

> "If I can give you one thing, just trust the math."

## Notes

Early, concrete articulation of Hormozi's later pricing/margin discipline — the
80% gross-margin heuristic and price-to-value-delivered logic prefigure the
offer-and-pricing frameworks in *$100M Offers*. The 28-day billing lever and
"size the schedule from margin backward" math are proto-versions of his later
money-model and unit-economics thinking.
