# I Spent $312,900 Living Like The 1% (Here's What I Learned) — 2021-05-12

## Summary

In this solo talk, Hormozi itemizes what an "ultra wealthy" Instagram-style lifestyle
(private jets, exotic cars, a penthouse, designer clothes, fancy restaurants) actually
costs per year — roughly $300k — and then, as someone who has bought all of it, reports
his verdict: almost none of it is worth the money. He reinforces his earlier frugality
stance, again citing regret over his Bentley, and closes with his "wealth is a ratio"
and "happiness equation" framings, arguing that lowering wants beats chasing a bigger
number.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-05-12)

- His father's repeated lesson growing up: "never be impressed by money." Hormozi frames
  the whole video as a transparency exercise to make luxury stop impressing viewers.
  (paraphrase, 2021)
- Itemized annual cost of the "ultra wealthy" lifestyle he walks through:
  - **Private jet** — ~$4,000/hour via a service; ~24 flight hours/year (six round-trips,
    ~4 hrs each) ≈ **~$100k/year**. Alternative first-class cost for the same six trips
    ≈ $6k–$10k. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Exotic car** — a very nice car ~$1,000/month; a Rolls-Royce lease ~$4,500/month ≈
    **~$50k/year**. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Penthouse** — a really nice non-major-market place ~$5k/month; he levels up to
    $10k/month ≈ **~$120k/year**. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Designer clothes** — Gucci belt ~$500, shoes ~$600, some $100 t-shirts ≈
    **~$3k/year** (he notes clothes are among the cheapest luxuries). (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Fancy restaurants** — ~$400 weekly date-night dinner ≈ $600/month ≈ **~$20k/year**.
    (paraphrase, 2021)
  - Total ≈ **~$300k/year** (the video's title rounds this to $312,900). (paraphrase, 2021)
- To fund ~$300k/year of spending after taxes you'd need to earn ~$550k/year, which he
  says is top-1% income. (paraphrase, 2021)
- His verdicts having done all of it:
  - **Private jet**: actually really nice, but wildly expensive, and he still feels
    guilty flying it. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Exotic cars**: "definitely not worth it." He owns two; says the incremental
    enjoyment vs. an old Prius or a pickup is inconsequential and you get used to it in
    a week or two. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Restaurants**: he gets a good return on date night with Leila, but from dressing
    up, not the food — he enjoys Applebee's, Cheesecake Factory, BJ's, Chili's and
    Chipotle just as much as $500 steaks. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Clothes**: he "get[s] nothing" from expensive clothes; most of his shirts are
    under $30. (paraphrase, 2021)
  - **Penthouse**: don't overspend on where you live; treat your primary residence as an
    investment (e.g. live in a duplex, rent the other side). (paraphrase, 2021)
- He only bought the exotic car because podcast host Brad Lee told him he was too cheap
  and to "just go get the darn car"; he now totally regrets it. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Claim about mass-market vs. luxury quality: the best quality often comes from big
  mass-market products because they do the most product testing. Example — a rented Chevy
  Suburban's interface was far easier to use than his ~$350k Bentley's. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Net-worth tiers he cites: millionaire = $1–5M; high net worth = $5–30M; ultra-high net
  worth = **$30M–$1B in investable assets**. At $30M investable, ~10%/year ≈ $3M/year of
  passive income — ~10x the cost of this lifestyle — so at that level it "kind of makes
  sense." (paraphrase, 2021)
- He says almost every genuinely ultra-wealthy person he knows has reached the same
  conclusion: almost none of this stuff is worth it — "you try the fruit and you realize
  this is just not worth it." (paraphrase, 2021)
- Reiterated core wealth mechanism: **wealth is a ratio of income to expenses, not a
  number.** A 10:1 income-to-expense ratio makes you wealthy; spend under 10% of income
  and within 2–5 years you can be financially free. (paraphrase, 2021)
- **Happiness equation**: happiness = haves ÷ wants (not "when I get X I'll be happy").
  Drive wants toward zero and abundance approaches infinity; decreasing desire raises
  your income-to-expense ratio and builds wealth. (paraphrase, 2021)
