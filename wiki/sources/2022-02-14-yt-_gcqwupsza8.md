# I Sold My Company at 31: My Detailed Breakdown of the Decision

## Summary

Alex walks through the 18- to 30-month decision process behind selling majority stakes in three companies and going all in on Acquisition.com. The video is both biography and decision-making: he narrates the Gym Launch / Prestige / ALAN sale context, the $46.2M valuation disclosure for two companies, his internal arguments for and against selling, and the final reason he chose to free attention for the next, larger vehicle.

## Key claims (Alex's advice/self-report, dated 2022-02-14)

- Alex says he and Leila sold majority stakes in three companies for over $50M in the prior year; two of the companies were publicly disclosed at a $46.2M valuation, while the third number is undisclosed.
- He self-reports that in March 2019 the business did $4.4M revenue and $1.7M owner dividends in one month.
- He says a fitness-space banker told him the business might sell for roughly $150M if growth continued and the brand became less dependent on his face plus one additional acquisition channel.
- He says shifting from customer goodwill to acquirer optics hurt the business; he lowered prices to reduce churn and estimates it cost about $5M in profit.
- He self-reports profit moving from $17M on $26M top line to $13.4M on $37M top line after that change.
- He says the business went to market in February 2020, was hit by COVID/gym shutdowns, and dropped as low as roughly $500K profit in Q4 2020.
- He says the company rebuilt for sale by removing him from product/client-facing work, building outbound to about 50% of sales volume, and promoting gym owners as the face of consulting.
- His decision variables were story/milestones, money, energy/headspace, team/resources, future vision, and best decision for the business.
- He says money would not materially change his life either way because he and Leila had already taken roughly $45M in dividends from the two businesses.
- He says he could own many things passively, but only one active thing; split attention is dangerous.
- He says the hardest decisions in his life were quitting his job, selling his gyms to start Gym Launch, and selling majority stakes to go all in on Acquisition.com.
- His final decision rule was that anything distracting from his "Berkshire Hathaway" would be a net loss.
