# When I Showed Up with $0 in California

## Summary

Alex Hormozi gives a visceral self-reported account of moving to California for fitness, arriving without housing, renting a room for $400/month, then sleeping on the floor of his first gym to save money and avoid the commute. He frames the early gym period as a long "Rocky cut scene" that becomes a short story later, but in the moment meant sleeping under a noisy parking garage, living on naps, dealing with homeless people outside, and selling the first 29 members mostly through pity rather than polished sales ability.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Origin stories become compressed sound bites later, but the actual hard period can last months or years.
- Visceral details matter because they create an undeniable stack of proof that the hardship was real.
- Early sales skill can be crude; Alex says his first gym members bought largely out of pity and belief that he would be there.
- Paying the first month's rent and watching the account return to zero made the repeat nature of business real.
- The pain of returning home as a failure and giving up authority over his life felt worse than continuing through hardship.
- Motivational content does not carry someone through the actual lonely stretch; the work still has to be done.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The rocky cut scene lasts 30 seconds in the movie, but it can last 5 years in your life."
