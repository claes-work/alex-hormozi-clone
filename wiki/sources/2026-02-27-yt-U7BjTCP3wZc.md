# You Need Longer Timelines for Your Goals

## Summary
Short interview clip on time horizons. Alex's headline lesson: **"the person with the longest time horizon wins"** — because competition has eroded most easy value, the only remaining alpha (value arbitrage) belongs to people willing to *wait and endure longer*. The caveat: that horizon must be "reverse engineered to some level of reality," not fantasy. His example is Elon Musk, whom he calls the richest man in the world precisely because Musk has wanted the same things (rockets, electric cars, space) since he was about seven and has pursued that single vision his whole life. Alex rates his own horizon as shorter — "3 to 5 years" — and half-jokingly ties that to being less rich than Elon; beyond ~5 years he says he genuinely can't see. He then makes a practical point: **"it's very difficult to have vision when you have bills to pay."** You have to get your personal finances "situated first," which buys "breathing room to think further." So when the interviewer asks whether *he* should be thinking 3–5 years out, Alex says no — his whole focus should be getting from $100k to $1M/year, which is doable in a year; once you clear that first level you gain resources and skills, and "your vision of the board will change." Alex closes on a reality-perception thesis: the people best at the game are the ones who "see reality the most clearly"; a business that's long failed to deliver usually means the owner sees "distortions," not reality; people move up in wealth because they're better at predicting what will happen. That's why he sees entrepreneurship as one of the best sources of personal development — "you get slapped really quickly to find out whether you were right."
