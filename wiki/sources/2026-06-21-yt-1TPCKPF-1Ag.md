# I've Put in My 10,000 Hours and I'm Not Making Enough Money

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a boat-membership operator doing about $2 million in revenue and roughly $600,000 in profit while carrying significant debt. Alex challenges the caller's assumption that lack of passion for boats means the business should be sold. He reframes the business as a skill-building vehicle and then diagnoses the economics as strong: low CAC relative to a $5,000 initiation fee and $500 monthly membership makes the problem more likely insufficient advertising, referrals, attribution, creative, targeting, and funnel conversion optimization than a bad business model.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - boat-membership operator. His revenue, debt, margins, licensing structure, lead costs, close rates, and contract terms are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A business does not have to be the source of emotional fulfillment; it can be an expression of excellence and values.
- Most businesses become similar at higher altitude because they share sales, marketing, IT, HR, legal, and personnel problems.
- The boat-membership business has attractive incremental economics once fixed costs are covered.
- The licensing fee described in the call does not appear to be strangling the business.
- Industry benchmarks are not a useful standard if the industry average is poor.
- The caller's CAC appears low relative to upfront and recurring revenue, so increasing ad spend is reasonable.
- The current bottleneck is likely more and better creative, keyword targeting, referral structure, attribution, and conversion-rate optimization rather than the core offer.
- Business owners can psychologically stop spending after fixed costs are covered, even when the next dollars would disproportionately increase profit.
