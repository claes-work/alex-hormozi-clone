# How To Give Feedback

## Summary

Alex Hormozi explains behavior through a behaviorist lens: people do things because they were rewarded for them in the past, so the productive move is to focus on what behavior to do instead rather than excavating the "why" from the past. He argues good feedback is fuel when it has low latency (given immediately) and high specificity (targets a precise behavior), and illustrates with a story about coaching a high-performing but disliked director to change specific behaviors instead of his character. An interviewer is present and prompts him but contributes no substantive claims.

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, Alex holds a behaviorist worldview: anyone does anything because they were rewarded/reinforced for it in the past.
- He distinguishes descriptive language ("Timmy stole a bike because he's dishonest") from explanatory language, calling the descriptive version a circular, non-useful definition.
- As of March 2026, Alex says understanding behavior as reinforcement history makes him better at business because it improves his ability to predict behavior (including his own).
- He gives an example: an employee threatening to quit if his title changed had a history of never actually quitting after prior title changes, so Alex predicted the threat was a low-probability bluff meant to influence the decision. (Second-hand anecdote, unverified.)
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that trying to trace behavior back to past trauma is usually futile because "you're never going to know"; the only lever available is changing current behavior.
- He defines trauma as a permanent change of behavior caused by an aversive (bad) stimulus, and says trauma can be "good" (a child touching a hot stove once) or "bad" depending on whether the behavior change raises or lowers the likelihood of achieving a goal.
- He frames trauma as an "accelerated learning event" where the brain rapidly and permanently changes behavior to avoid a repeat.
- He rejects the framing that experience is "stored in the body," saying "we're not computers" and that people just have a behavior set that can be changed.
- As of March 2026, Alex says what makes feedback useful is latency (how quickly it's given) and specificity (how precisely it targets a behavior); vague in-the-moment criticism like "that sucked" only makes someone less likely to act in front of you.
- He recounts coaching a competent but widely-disliked company director by removing personal judgment ("I don't care if you're a dick on the inside") and instead changing specific behaviors — not interrupting on calls, and asking permission before telling people how to do their jobs — after which people reported a night-and-day change within a week.
- He defines learning as "same condition, new behavior," and ties this to the "insanity" idea: unchanged behavior under unchanged conditions cannot expect a different outcome.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "My worldview on why anyone does anything is that they've been rewarded for doing that in the past."

> "If we actually try and find the explanation rather than the description of the event, we can get way better at predicting behavior, including our own."

> "It's so much more productive to just focus on what do we need to do instead, rather than trying to figure out why you did it, cuz the reality is, you're never going to know."

> "Trauma ... I define as a permanent change of behavior from an aversive stimulus, some a bad thing."

> "The reason feedback is fuel and what makes feedback good versus bad or rather useful versus non-useful is the latency of the feedback. How quickly do you get it? And how specific is it?"

> "I don't care if you're a dick on the inside. I would like to decrease the likelihood that other people describe you as one."

> "When you share it, share in terms of behaviors, criticism, not insult."

> "Learning means same condition, new behavior."
