# Ignore the Psychology Behind Everything

## Summary

Alex argues against over-explaining behavior with vague psychology, emotion, or
mysticism. He says the observable inputs and outputs matter more: what did the person do,
what happened, and which variables can be changed to increase the likelihood of the
desired outcome. A good teacher should be able to tell someone what to do with their
mouth or body.

## Key claims

- Alex says it is difficult to know why something works, but easier to observe whether
  it works.
- He recommends focusing on inputs and outputs rather than the unobservable "magic" in
  between.
- He says high-repetition inefficiency helps people get better by making them less bad
  over many reps.
- He says people often seek a narrative that scratches a psychological itch rather than
  one that changes performance.
- He uses a tennis-grip analogy: the coach should correct the grip, not investigate the
  emotional history of the wrong grip.
- He says if a teacher cannot tell someone what to do with their mouth or body, the
  instruction is not clear enough.
- He recommends observing behavior like a scientist and identifying inputs that increase
  the probability of the desired output.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Focus first on the inputs."

> "You just need to send this email, say these words."

> "Manage the variables prior to the desired outcome."
