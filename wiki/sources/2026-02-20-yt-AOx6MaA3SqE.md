# How to Find Solutions for Any Problem

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi short on turning problems into solvable, self-owned statements. His method: whenever you catch yourself blaming the market or other people ("there are no good salespeople in my market," "it's too crowded"), rewrite the complaint as a first-person skill gap you don't yet have — "I don't have the skill to get more leads / to hire and manage a good salesperson." Reframing it that way makes you the source who owns (and can therefore change) the outcome, instead of "casting your power outside of yourself." He concedes the external excuse might even be true — but argues that some prizes (like winning the "pity award" for worst circumstances) aren't worth winning, and that succeeding *despite* a bad hand makes a stronger, more useful story for the people who follow you. He closes with two decision heuristics he calls razors: the **story frame** (when torn between two choices, pick the one that makes the cooler story, because at 85 the story is all you're left with) and the **hard-thing rule** (between two options, take the harder one — because if the easy one were right, you'd have already done it, so your hesitation itself signals the hard one is correct).

## Key claims (paraphrased, 2026-02-20)

- Complaints about the market are usually mistranslations of an internal constraint — e.g. "it's really crowded" usually means "I can't get enough leads" or "leads cost too much."
- The fix is to reframe any external complaint as a first-person skill statement: "I don't have the skill to get more leads / to attract, hire, and manage a good salesperson / to get employees to behave according to the business's goals."
- Stating a problem that way transfers ownership of the outcome to you — you "become source," the one who can change it.
- It never serves you to cast your power outside yourself and blame external factors for the success you lack.
- The external excuse might genuinely be true — "and so what?" Some prizes aren't worth winning; you can win the "pity award" for worst circumstances, but the question is whether that prize is worth competing for.
- Winning *despite* bad circumstances is an equally or more powerful story — and the more difficult your past, the stronger that story is for the people following behind you who suffered the same or worse.
- Protecting your ego by attributing failure to an external cause robs you of that example-setting opportunity; being right about the adversity *and* winning anyway is the cooler story.
- ★ **The "story frame" razor:** when you're 85 and split between two life choices, pick the one that makes the cooler story — at the end of life the story is the only thing you're left with. (Named decision heuristic — L3-candidate.)
- ★ **The "hard thing = right thing" razor:** given two choices — usually a hard/right one and an easier one — always take the hard one, because if the easy option were right you'd already have done it; you're only hesitating because it's harder than expected. (Named decision heuristic — L3-candidate.)
