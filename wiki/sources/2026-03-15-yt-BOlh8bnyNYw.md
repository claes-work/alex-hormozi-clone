# Helping a Stem Cell Clinic Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The founder of BioXcelerator — a stem-cell/exosome clinic he describes as one of the top stem-cell companies in the world (~$24M revenue, targeting $250M–$500M, ~65% founder equity, raised angel/seed through a current Series B in $5–15M chunks) — wants to raise a much larger round (~$100M at a $500M valuation, i.e. selling ~20%) without losing control, and finds the conversation "much different" once you ask for $100M. Alex reframes the whole problem as a **sale, not a science pitch**. On control: you can absolutely stay in control as a minority shareholder — he cites Zuckerberg's dual-class 100-to-1 voting rights — so long as investors trust you and understand the business needs capital; doing a raise now might cost roughly half his current equity while giving him a fully-funded company. His fundraising method mirrors his customer-development method: talk to ~10x more potential investors than you need *now*, ask each "what would you need to see in order to believe?", collect the full list of "need-to-believe" items, then narrow to the realistic ones — sometimes running a tiny raise just to prove one belief and then laddering into the bigger round. Investors are all just **paying down risk**; that's manageable from a control standpoint without building someone else's company. The core of the raise is the **story**, not the facts (facts make good stories, but "don't be a scientist for the pitch" — investors' eyes glaze over on the science; they don't know it and just want to know they'll make money). Alex teaches VC valuation logic explicitly: a good VC only makes money on multi-billion-dollar outcomes, so a valuation is a **discount on a ~$10–20B end-state business** based on how many assumptions still have to be proven true — the fewer unproven assumptions, the higher the valuation. So the pitch must show what has *changed* since the last ($50M) round to justify a 10x step-up: here, the technology (already proven in-clinic for years) is now ready to scale into new manufacturing/laboratories — that de-risking wasn't present at $50M, turning it from an "assumption constraint" into merely a "capital constraint." He also notes regulatory risk remains but is trending green (Florida, Utah and other states shifting their stance on stem cell). Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames raising capital as fundamentally a sale: "this is just a sale… a sale on you, and a sale on the story" — think less about the facts and more about the story (facts can make good stories, but the story is what sells).
- As of March 2026, a founder can keep control while being a minority shareholder via dual-class / super-voting shares (he cites Zuckerberg's 100-to-1 voting rights); the requirement is that investors trust you and understand the business genuinely needs more capital to reach the goal.
- As of March 2026, his fundraising method: hold conversations now with ~10x more potential investors than you need, ask each "what would you need to see in order to believe?", gather every "need-to-believe," then narrow to the realistic ones — this is his customer-development loop (find your customers, find what they want, build that) applied to investors, without derailing your own vision.
- As of March 2026, investors are all just "paying down risk" — that is all they are buying down — which makes control concerns manageable if structured well.
- As of March 2026, sometimes the right move is a "tiny race" to prove one single "need-to-believe," get believed, then ladder into the larger round.
- As of March 2026, VC valuation logic: good VCs only make money on multi-billion-dollar companies, so any valuation is a discount on the eventual ($10–20B) business, sized by how many assumptions still must be proven true — fewer remaining unproven assumptions means higher valuation (higher likelihood of the outcome).
- As of March 2026, to justify a 10x step-up (from a $50M round to a $500M round) the pitch must name what has fundamentally changed — a proven, tested technology now ready to scale into new manufacturing/labs — so the business is now a "capital constraint," not an "assumption constraint"; that de-risking "was not there at 50."
- As of March 2026, on the medical/regulatory angle: stem-cell regulatory risk remains but is trending favorably ("all the directions are pointing green, not red"), citing Florida, Utah and other states shifting their stance.
- As of March 2026, pitch delivery advice: "do not be a scientist for the pitch" — investors don't know the science and get lost in the nuts and bolts; they just want to know they'll make a lot of money.
