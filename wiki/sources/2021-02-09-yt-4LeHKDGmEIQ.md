# This is Why You're Not Happy - Eye Opening

> Source: YouTube (@AlexHormozi), "This is Why You're Not Happy - Eye Opening", 2021-02-09
> [[../../index]]

## Summary

Aimed at younger entrepreneurs, Hormozi argues unhappiness comes from expectations: the
secret to happiness is "haves over wants," so you can raise your happiness simply by
expecting less. He reframes skill acquisition with a "bridge" analogy — each skill is a
brick laid across a valley — and warns against the binary "did I make it or not"
expectation that makes people abandon vehicles two steps from success.

## Key claims (paraphrase; dated 2021-02-09)

- **Unhappiness = wanting more than you have.** (2021) The "secret to happiness" many
  religions found is *haves over wants*: have one thing and want nothing, you're infinitely
  happy; have a million but want two million, you're unhappy. Lower expectations to raise
  happiness.
- **The bridge/bricks analogy.** (2021) Where you are and where you want to go are two sides
  of a valley joined by a bridge; each skill is a brick. Buying a course lays another brick —
  you may be closer even if you haven't "arrived."
- **Reject binary outcomes for a continuum.** (2021) Most things exist on a continuum
  (not "diabetic or not" but "how diabetic"), yet people psychologically bucket into yes/no.
  A binary makes it easy to score "zero out of one" and quit.
- **Ask "am I closer or further from my goal?"** (2021) Replace "did this work?" with the
  continuum question. He says this reframing has been tremendously helpful.
- **Don't disparage earlier teachers.** (2021) People trash a prior coach/franchisor once a
  later one advances them — like cursing your third-grade math teacher after calculus. Often
  step one was required to reach step two; saying "I've learned from many teachers" doesn't
  lower your status.
- **The college-expectations contrast.** (2021) People spend $200k and four years, graduate
  unable to get a job, and aren't upset — because they *expected nothing*. In the "make
  money" world, binary expectations breed frustration.
- **Success is misattributed to the last brick.** (2021) A client may need 38 skills, acquire
  24 elsewhere, and get the last 14 from Gym Launch — then credit Hormozi with everything. He
  says that's unfair to themselves and to prior teachers.
- **The business formula: raise expectations on inputs, lower them on outcomes.** (2021) Be
  absolutely intolerant of any lack of effort / anything but excellence in *activity*, while
  decreasing expectations of the *outcome*. Mirror of "haves over wants" for happiness.
- **Spending disclosure.** (2021) He mentions paying ~$50,000 for ~8 hours with one advisor
  and $120,000 for four hours with Grant Cardone — framing even a "this isn't a priority"
  takeaway as moving him closer to his goal.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The secret to happiness is haves over wants... you have one thing and you want nothing, you
> will be infinitely happy... you can simply change how happy you are by expecting less."

> "I like to liken each skill as a brick on the way to where you're trying to go."

> "It's not are you diabetic, are you not diabetic, it's how diabetic are you... we like to
> psychologically bucket things into yes/nos because it's simpler for us, but it misses actual
> reality."

> "Give myself... the question: am I closer or further away from my goal?"

> "Why is it that people will spend $200,000, four years of their lives, study Aztec
> literature... and not in any way be upset about the fact that they're not making money when
> they graduated?... The only reason I can come up with is that they didn't expect to."

> "Increase expectations on activity and actions, decrease expectations on outcomes. And I
> think that that is a formula for business success just as haves over wants is a formula for
> happiness in life."

> "You take two steps of progress and say, 'Oh, I'm not there. Binary outcome. Therefore, I
> will start something else.' ...it's only after failing three, four, five, six things that on
> the seventh thing, you had enough acquired skills from your first six failures that you were
> actually able to make it all the way across the bridge."
