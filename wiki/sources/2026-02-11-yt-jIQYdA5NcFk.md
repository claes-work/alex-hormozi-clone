# Learning to Live With Constant Suffering...

## Summary
Asked about the biggest operating obstacles in scaling Acquisition.com toward a billion dollars, Alex argues that suffering in entrepreneurship is constant — only its nature changes. He reframes scaling as less about how hard you can push and more about how long you can wait, how focused you can stay, who you can attract, and above all the quality of your decisions. He walks through a progression of shifting constraints: overcoming skeptical friends and family, then your own limiting beliefs, then learning to sell, then becoming a first-time manager of assistants and full-timers, then managing managers as a leader, then attracting and leading other leaders. He frames entrepreneurship as a "forcing function for pain" and simultaneously a "forcing function for progress" — you stay in pain until you progress, then you find new pain. The clip closes with a promotion for his free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) Suffering in scaling a business is constant; what changes is the nature of the suffering — "the hard is always hard."
- (2026-02-11) As a business develops, you end up trading things you didn't expect to have to trade.
- (2026-02-11) Beyond a certain point, success stops being about how hard you can push — sometimes it's about how long you can wait, how focused you can stay, what kind of person you can attract to the business, and, often, the quality of your decisions.
- (2026-02-11) The early stage requires overcoming environmental obstacles: friends and family who say it won't work or think the idea is stupid.
- (2026-02-11) After external doubt, you must overcome your own limiting beliefs.
- (2026-02-11) Growth forces a skill ladder: from doing it yourself, to using virtual assistants, to hiring full-timers and becoming a first-time manager, to hiring your first manager, to leading multiple managers as a leader, to attracting and leading other leaders — each a distinct skill you haven't done before.
- (2026-02-11) Leading leaders is a "totally different skill" from managing.
- (2026-02-11) Entrepreneurship is a forcing function for pain and also a forcing function for progress: you stay in pain until you progress, and then you find new pain.
- (2026-02-11) [promotional] He offers a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+, which he claims less than 1% of companies finish and that he has personally done multiple times, broken down across eight business functions (the constraint, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate), tested across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar businesses — available at acquisition.com/roadmap.
