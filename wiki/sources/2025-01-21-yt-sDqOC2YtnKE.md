# Skool: The Future of Business and Education

## Summary

Alex Hormozi explains why he invested in and backs [Skool](https://www.skool.com), framing it
as the simplest possible business model for beginners: drive eyeballs from social media to a
Skool community page and earn recurring subscription revenue with no inventory, integrations,
or technical skill required. He argues Skool pages convert better than self-built pages because
users borrow the platform's "inherent brand trust" (his analogy: his book converts at ~2–3% on
his own Shopify page vs. ~30% on Amazon), and that gamification plus native payments make Skool
structurally cheaper to run than a free Facebook group. He cites early School Games results and
a member testimonial (Evelyn) to support the thesis that Skool lowers the bar to entrepreneurship
and targets a "$10 trillion global nut of education."

## Key claims (his Skool / community-education thesis)

- **Skool is the simplest business model for a beginner.** "You don't need inventory, you don't
  need any third-party integrations, you don't need to know anything about technology, and you can
  actually make your money on the platform." One place processes the money, helps with delivery,
  and can run the entire business.
- **The model = eyeballs → Skool page → community → recurring revenue.** Get eyeballs any way you
  want (post, run ads, outreach, partner with an existing audience), drive them to your Skool page,
  provide value inside the community, charge a monthly fee (he cites a range of "$1 and $999" per
  month).
- **Skool pages convert better because you borrow the platform's credibility.** As people trust
  Skool and transact on it more, Skool pages will "convert better than any page that you could
  possibly build on your own." Supporting analogy (self-reported): his book converts ~2–3% on his
  own Shopify page but ~30% on Amazon — "10 times as efficient" — because of inherent platform trust.
- **Standardized page layout is a feature.** Amazon, Zillow, and Airbnb converge on the same layout
  (video, image carousel, reviews, buy button, bullet points) because their traffic/conversion data
  justified it; the Skool page mirrors this, removing design work and technical difficulty for
  beginners.
- **Skool is cheaper to run than a free Facebook group.** Native payment integration, automatic
  removal of non-paying members, and members-helping-members support (driven by gamification) cut
  the community-management cost that Facebook groups incur. Facebook also throttles paid reach —
  he cites the Evelyn testimonial's figure of ~300 of 6,500 members reached on average.
- **Gamification is core, built by gaming developers.** Members earn points for likes and comments,
  gain real status, and unlock abilities "like a game." "The core of the development team at Skool
  is actually all gaming developers because we wanted to make business fun."
- **The Skool Discovery / directory is organic customer acquisition.** Communities rank in a
  directory "just like Google ranks web pages"; people searching to learn a skill find and join
  groups, so Skool — not the group owner — generated that customer. (Evelyn testimonial: 234 net
  new members in a month, all organic, no paid ads.)
- **Pricing is deliberately simple, growth over profit.** Flat $99/month per group; monetization =
  number of groups × $99. He says they could add tiers to maximize profit but choose not to, to
  keep it simple and maximize students and teachers on the platform. Long-term, $99/month "is not
  the monetization place."
- **Skool is currently unprofitable by design.** The platform has been losing money due to
  aggressive reinvestment in engineers, designers, and developers to make the product "absolutely
  exceptional." He "wrote a big check into it."
- **Target market is the $10 trillion global education market** — one place to "learn whatever you
  want from people who are actually doing it," affordably, versus ~$50,000/year universities.
- **Marketing challenge:** the benefit (a platform that scales with you) is hard to sell because
  beginners "aren't suffering from the problem yet" — they only feel the pain of needing to scale
  after they succeed. Analogy: focusing on losing weight without thinking about needing new clothes.
- **He backed Skool because it was already growing on word of mouth without him.**
