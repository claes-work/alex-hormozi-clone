# Helping a Consulting Business Get More Leads

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A UK consulting firm that "makes optometrists richer" (helps clinically-trained optometrists who bought a practice actually run it profitably) is doing just under £1M revenue at ~58% EBITDA, wants to double, and frames its constraint as over-reliance on two lead sources: lens manufacturers who refer their customers (and initially pay the firm's fees, ~£7,000/yr per client), plus word of mouth and speaking on conference stages. Despite the "more leads" framing, Alex reframes the real problem as **massive underpricing**, not lead volume. Because the manufacturer is willing to cover the fee, and because the firm adds ~£50,000 to a client's bottom line, he argues the price is far too low: he floats charging the manufacturer ~£15k *and* the client ~£7k+ to get paid "both sides," and settles on a target of ~£35–45k as a one-time "fix the whole business" fee plus a smaller ongoing back end. He notes the avatar is exceptionally attractive because optometrists are highly credit-worthy and culturally trained to take loans and spend on things that don't pay back immediately. On the compliance problem (clients who won't do the work the firm gives them), his answer is simply to **charge more** — at £7k it feels free, so people don't act. His continuity recommendation is a **buying/discount association**: since all these practices buy the same lenses, frames, and displays, negotiate bulk discounts so members save more than the membership costs ("dollar for dollar… they get all the services for free"). The long-term play he names is a **roll-up**: get the practices "all under one flag" and sell the consolidated group. He affirms international expansion (US/Canada inbound already) with "I would accept money from people who speak English." Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes a consulting firm's "we need more leads" complaint as a **pricing** problem: if a manufacturer already covers the fee and the firm adds ~£50k to a client's bottom line, charging only ~£7k/yr is "wildly undercharging."
- As of March 2026, his pricing heuristic ties the fee to the value delivered: adding ~£50k warrants ~£15k; he floats getting paid on both sides (manufacturer ~£15k plus the client), and names ~£35–45k as the right price point for a full "fix the whole business" engagement.
- As of March 2026, structure the offer as a large **one-time up-front** fee for the heavy lifting (fix the business), with a smaller **recurring back end** (~£10–15k/yr) for ongoing value.
- As of March 2026, the best long-term continuity for a business whose clients all buy the same inputs is a **buying/discount association**: negotiate bulk discounts so members' savings exceed the membership fee, making the ongoing service feel "completely free."
- As of March 2026, the way to fix **non-compliant clients** who won't do the assigned work is to charge them more — at £7k the engagement feels "like free," so clients don't take it seriously and don't act.
- As of March 2026, this optometrist avatar is unusually attractive because they are highly credit-worthy (lend easily) and have been "trained for their entire careers" to take loans and spend on things that don't pay back immediately.
- As of March 2026, the ultimate play for a base of similar small businesses is a **roll-up**: consolidate them "all under one flag" and sell the combined entity.
- As of March 2026, on international expansion, Alex endorses accepting inbound demand from any English-speaking market ("I would accept money from people who speak English, for sure").
