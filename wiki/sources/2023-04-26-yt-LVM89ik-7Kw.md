# "I'm Broke, What Should I Sell?"

## Summary

Prompted by a guest lecture at the University of Texas, Hormozi lays out a taxonomy of the
seven fundamental things that can be sold — each in physical and digital format (14 total) —
then introduces his six-lens "delivery Cube" for scaling and repricing whatever you choose.
His practical advice for a beginner with little capital: sell your time (a service) to the
person you can help most for the least money, then enhance it.

## Key claims

- There are only seven fundamental sellable categories, each with a physical and digital
  form (14 total): products, services, access, attention, risk (insurance), money itself,
  and endorsement/brand. Offers are often combinations of these.
- What to sell comes down to two things: the resources you have and your skills/value
  experience — and everyone has more relevant experience than they think (from parents,
  people around them, and odd jobs / osmosis).
- With little capital (~$100 to start), physical products and software are mostly ruled out,
  so you should start by selling your time — i.e. a service.
- The "sweet spot" first offer is the person you can provide the most value to for the least
  amount of money to you.
- The delivery Cube has six levers to change value/scale/profit: (1) one-to-one vs small
  group vs one-to-many; (2) DIY vs done-with-you vs done-for-you; (3) level & medium of
  support; (4) consumption format (live/in-person = most valuable/least scalable, recorded/
  digital = most scalable/least valuable); (5) speed & convenience of delivery; (6) the
  "10x-to-1/10th test."
- The 10x/one-tenth test: ask what you'd change to justify charging 10x more, and separately
  what you'd add to still justify one-tenth the price — it forces bigger thinking about value.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "First off we have to define what are the actual things that can be sold, and this may
  sound crazy, but there's actually fewer of them than you think."
- "If you're a first-time entrepreneur then you're like, well I don't have any other
  experience — but you actually do."
- "Here's the person I provide the most value to for the least amount of money to me — to
  me that's where your sweet spot is as the first or next thing that I would package and sell."
- "Anything that's live is typically going to be perceived as more valuable, and if it's in
  person and live probably the most valuable and probably the least scalable."
- "If I charge 10 times more for my current thing, what would I have to change about my
  thing that I sell in order for it to be worth 10 times more?"
