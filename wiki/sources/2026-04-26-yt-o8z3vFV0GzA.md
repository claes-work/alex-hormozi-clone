# How to Form Opinions Nobody Can Take From You

## Summary

Alex responds to a discussion about copied opinions and original thinking. He says
original thinking is rare because thinking is hard, so most people delegate it. His way to
own a belief is to reason from observable facts and first principles; when he has built the
logic himself, he can sustain the course longer under social pressure. He also says it is
reasonable to delegate thinking in areas where he has not done the work, as long as the
expert's incentives are aligned.

## Key claims

- Alex says original thinking is rare and conceptual inertia can slow belief change across
  society.
- He says many people delegate thinking because thinking is hard and time is limited.
- He defines first-principles reasoning as starting with observable facts and building only
  from there.
- He says first-principles reasoning helps sustain a course for a long time because the
  logic is sound and owned.
- He distinguishes hills he will die on from topics where he delegates to an expert.
- He cites behavior change as a topic he has spent enough time thinking about to defend
  strongly.
- He says he delegates some fitness/nutrition thinking to Dr. Mike because he believes Dr.
  Mike has expertise and aligned incentives.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Original thinking is very rare."

> "Thinking is hard which is why the vast majority of people delegate it."

> "I know why I believe what I believe."

> "Until I have observable evidence that somehow contradicts... this is what I'm going with."
