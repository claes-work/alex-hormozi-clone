# A BILLIONAIRE told me how to make BILLION DOLLARS...here's my plan

## Summary
In this solo video Hormozi relays lessons from a private phone call he and his wife Leila had with an anonymous entrepreneur (whom he calls "Ben," describes as mid-70s, privately holding ~50 companies doing over a billion dollars a year in revenue). He frames the call as earned access — his own companies were pacing near $100M/year — and organizes the video around the billionaire's advice on talent, incentives, investing, and how the man's businesses grew from $100M to $1B. Throughout, Hormozi interleaves the second-hand advice with his own reflections, corrections he wishes he had made earlier in his career, and synthesized conclusions. Key threads include the "three superstars" rule for a self-sustaining business, aligning incentives via profit shares and buy-in equity, the billionaire's belief that "profits are unnatural," his real-estate philosophy of prioritizing return *of* capital over return *on* capital, and the punchline that the path from $100M to $1B was largely stepping back, incentivizing others, and luck. Hormozi closes by noting his own drive to make more money has diminished and shifted toward teaching and helping others.

## Key claims

**Billionaire's advice (second-hand; relayed by Alex, NOT Alex's own voice):**
- There are two levels of talent — stars and superstars — and every business that will grow and be self-sustaining must have three superstars: one over acquisition, one over delivery, and one over operations/shared services. (attributed to the billionaire)
- You can identify a superstar on sight: he has had people he thought were superstars turn out not to be, but has never had someone he did *not* think was a star/superstar later become one. (attributed to the billionaire)
- He set up profit shares only for the CEOs of his companies — 5% of profits — and let them truly buy in to progressively larger equity chunks (one CEO grew from 5–10% to ~30% ownership). (attributed to the billionaire)
- "I don't believe that businesses should make a profit" — he holds that profits are unnatural, non-naturally-occurring; a business left to its own devices roughly breaks even, and creating profit takes willpower and is the sole responsibility of the CEO. (attributed to the billionaire; Alex explicitly says he does not 100% agree)
- On investing: he and his partner put a couple hundred million into multi-family real estate through the company, sharing deal equity; he is "more concerned with return *of* capital than return *on* capital," and his rule of thumb was to never accept a cap rate below eight. (attributed to the billionaire)
- On going $100M → $1B: they were burnt out and stuck at $100M for 4–5 years, so they broke the company into ~100 pieces, gave profit sharing and phantom equity to superstars/drivers, and checked out; five years later they were at $500M, five years after that at $1B. "The way you get to a billion is not trying to get to a billion." He attributed much of it to luck and to key decisions his leaders made over his own objections (they were right, he was wrong). (attributed to the billionaire)

**Alex's own claims / synthesis (Alex-attributed):**
- Access to high-level people is earned by achievement, not bought cheaply; a rough cutoff is roughly 5x-to-10x (add a zero) beyond your current level. Buy-a-millionaire-lunch / work-for-free advice is unrealistic because you have nothing to offer. (Alex)
- The breakthrough from ~$30–40M (where he was stuck three years) to $100M was learning to focus on people; an entrepreneur's personal influence dilutes across management layers. (Alex, self-reported figures)
- His early-career mistake was partnering with people who shared his skill set (acquisition) rather than complementary skills (delivery, operations); you can pay for functions like an accountant as a vendor instead of giving away equity, and the most expensive equity you give away is at the beginning. (Alex)
- The gap from $30M to $100M is about giving up the next level of control — high-level decision autonomy — because superstars want that power, authority, responsibility, and credit for outcomes. (Alex)
- The size of a corporation is directly proportional to the number of superstars present; as a company grows it needs fewer "hunter" drivers and more "farmer" superstar tenders making incremental improvements. (Alex)
- Growth happens in a stepwise fashion (growth, plateau, growth) as constraints/bottlenecks are hit and then de-bottlenecked, usually by a person with prior experience in that area. (Alex)
- As you grow, decision-making becomes about downside mitigation — doubling net worth changes little, halving it changes a lot. (Alex)
- Acquisition.com / his companies were "doing just under 100 million a year" and "pacing probably about 85." (Alex, self-reported, dated 2021)
