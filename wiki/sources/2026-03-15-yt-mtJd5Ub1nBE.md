# Helping a Corporate Training Company Hire A-Players

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a corporate training company (communication, public speaking, presentations; sells both B2B corporate training and B2C open-enrollment courses; ~$2M revenue, ~20% margins, wants $10M in ~2.5 years) asks how to hire top A-player sales talent to drive growth despite limited upfront cash. Alex opens by reframing the question through his **debt thesis**: every business always incurs debt — the only choice is *which type*. Bootstrapped founders avoid financial debt but instead take on talent debt, infrastructure/tech debt, and data debt, and founders get "disproportionately compensated" because they must do many jobs better than most people for a long time to make up for that debt. He lays out the real options for hiring without cash: (1) take on financial/capital debt to buy all-stars up front; (2) **give away equity** to attract a better player without full cash comp — still levered on the founder's skill because they're betting on the founder ("I would rather have 10% of this guy's thing than 100% of my own thing"); or (3) work "third shift"/overtime yourself to grow cash flow until you can afford the person. With ~$400k free cash flow, he notes that's roughly enough for *one* good hire — a big personal bet that may not work out. Then he pivots to the deeper diagnosis: the client's real constraint isn't the missing rainmaker, it's the **lack of a reliable acquisition channel** (they run on SEO + word-of-mouth, which he warns is being eroded by ChatGPT). His counsel: don't hire the expensive "growth guy" (rainmakers aren't cheap — the truly great ones don't need you and can go do their own thing); instead **hire someone cheaper to backfill what you're already doing**, freeing your time and money to build the next acquisition channel yourself. He bets the business should focus on the higher-ticket B2B corporate motion, because there's no operational reason you can't sell 10-20 business engagements a week the way you'd sell 10 B2C seats — "the function is the same, but you just add zeros," giving far more operating leverage. Nods to his Money Models book for recombining cash-flow variables. Ends with the roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, every business always incurs debt; the only real choice is which type — financial/capital debt vs. operational, talent, data, and tech debt. Founders should take on the debt they can most easily pay back.
- As of March 2026, bootstrapping isn't debt-free: by declining financial debt you actively take on talent debt, infrastructure debt, and tech debt because a founder can't do everything alone — that debt must be paid back later.
- As of March 2026, founders are "disproportionately compensated" precisely because they do many jobs better than most people for an extended period, make bigger bets, and take bigger risks to cover the business's debt.
- As of March 2026, to hire above your cash level you can give away equity to attract a stronger player without full cash comp — but the deal is still levered on the founder's skill/vision, because the hire is betting on the founder ("10% of his thing beats 100% of mine").
- As of March 2026, with ~$2M revenue at ~20% margins (~$400k free cash flow), that's roughly enough to hire one strong person — a large bet on a single unproven hire.
- As of March 2026, the alternative to betting cash on a hire is to work overtime yourself to raise revenue/cash flow (e.g. to ~$2.5M) until you can pay ~$300k for someone who can actually drive growth; cash flow is "the oxygen for the business."
- As of March 2026, the client's true bottleneck is not a missing salesperson but the absence of a reliable acquisition channel — running on SEO + word-of-mouth, which is risky because ChatGPT is rapidly eroding SEO traffic.
- As of March 2026, the founder's first and primary function should be to build one reliable acquisition channel; then drive their own inputs through it, reinvest the disproportionate cash-flow return, and hire people to backfill themselves.
- As of March 2026, the better hiring question is not "how do I hire the person who'll grow my business" but "how do I hire someone to do what I'm already doing, so I can go grow the business" — the backfill hire is cheaper than a rainmaker.
- As of March 2026, "rainmakers ain't cheap," and the people genuinely best at growing a business don't need you — they can do it on their own; to attract them you must make the vision big and compelling enough that they choose to come.
- As of March 2026, he'd bet the training business should focus on the higher-ticket B2B corporate motion; once an acquisition channel is working, there's no operational constraint on selling 10-20 business engagements a week versus 10 B2C seats — "the function is the same, but you just add zeros," yielding far more operating leverage.
- As of March 2026, the core cash-flow move (per his Money Models book) is to recombine the money-making variables — get customers cheaper, get them to pay faster, get them to pay more — to de-constrain the business so it can grow (ads, team, talent, or infrastructure).
