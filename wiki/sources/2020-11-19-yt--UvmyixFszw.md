# 4 Steps to Maximizing Revenue Generated Per Customer (2020-11-19)

## Summary

An early (Nov 2020) solo talk introducing what Hormozi calls a **"cash flow quadrant"** — a
four-step sales-choreography process for increasing revenue generated per customer. He argues
that most businesses believe they have a marketing problem when they actually have a
monetization problem, and that a strong back end (high cash-per-show / LTV) is what lets you
outspend competitors on acquisition. He ties it to his in-progress book *Building a Better
Mousetrap* and to why Gym Launch stayed number one.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2020-11-19)

- Diagnostic question: if a business can't afford to pay $100–$200 to get someone in the door,
  it doesn't have a marketing problem — it has a **business/cash-flow problem** and can't
  acquire customers profitably.
- His cash flow quadrant is distinct from Robert Kiyosaki's (*Rich Dad Poor Dad*) cash flow
  quadrant — his is about increasing value per customer, not employment/ownership types.
- Three types of offers exist to get someone in the door: **free, discount, and value** (e.g. a
  free evaluation, a $19–$29 new-client special, or a straight value offer with no discount) —
  all can lead into the same "mousetrap."
- **The four steps (captured exactly):**
  1. **Products** — bolt on an immediate, high-margin physical product to the initial service
     package (gym → supplements; chiropractor → orthotics/pillow/brace; beauty → masks/serums/
     creams). Even a free front-end can break even on $100–$200 of product per person.
  2. **Add-on services** — additional services on top of the core offer, including a "menu close"
     showing everything else you offer (gym → accountability/nutrition/semi-private; med spa →
     coolsculpting/filler/microdermabrasion/skin tightening). Raises average LTV/investment.
  3. **Prepaid or financing** — drag future cash forward: use third-party financing (e.g. Care
     Credit in healthcare) and/or offer ~10% off to **prepay** (use the word "prepay," never
     "paid in full") to liquidate acquisition cost up front.
  4. **Continuity** — tie recurring revenue into the acquisition process via a method he calls
     **"downselling the upsell"**: charge more up front (acquisition/onboarding costs more than
     maintenance), then offer the same thing on a recurring basis at a decent discount; most
     people jump at it, locking in recurring cash flow and extending LTV.
- **Prepay math example**: of 10 walk-ins you close 5; four average $500 but one prepays $2,500
  — that single sale lifts the average ticket from $500 to $1,000 across all customers. A small
  percentage taking a big offer massively raises average order value / cash-per-show.
- **Full quadrant math example** (med spa, $19 offer): close half onto a $2,000 package (min
  $500) → +$200 product each → some add ~$1,000 more services → some add continuity — building
  toward ~$2,400 per person who walked in on a free/cheap offer.
- Capacity formula: business monthly ceiling ≈ (customers/month) × (LTV). Example: 20 customers
  × $2,000 LTV = $40,000/month. If a business isn't growing, either front-end inflow is too low
  or back-end capture (LTV) is too low — "that's all there is to it."
- Strong back end (high cash-per-show) is what unlocks growth: it lets you afford many
  acquisition channels (referral partners, cold traffic, affiliates, direct mail, social) — "it
  comes from the back, not the front." Everyone thinks they have a marketing problem; usually
  it's a monetization/choreography problem.
- Selling continuously is required, and justified: research on retention says LTV comes from
  **expansion revenue / ascension** — selling customers more over time; it's your duty to make
  the offers if you believe in the value. Frames the whole process as a choreographed "handoff /
  passing the baton" from sale 1 → 2 → 3 → 4.
- Says this quadrant is the process he walks businesses through in paid consulting days, often
  doubling or tripling per-customer revenue, and credits it for Gym Launch staying #1.

**[Proto-money-model / core seed]** — This "cash flow quadrant" (products → add-on services →
prepay/financing → continuity) plus "downselling the upsell," cash-per-show, and LTV-ceiling
math is a direct, named precursor to his later **Money Models** framework
(attraction/upsell/downsell/continuity). It predates *$100M Offers* (2021) and *$100M Money
Models*, and he explicitly ties it to the in-progress *Building a Better Mousetrap* book — the
same working title/concept referenced in [[2020-11-05-yt-pxVeOkOVr2w]].

> they don't have a marketing problem, they have a business problem."

> number three is prepaid or financing... and then number four is continuity."

> "A process that I like to call downselling the upsell... offer them the same thing that they've
