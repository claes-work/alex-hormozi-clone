# How to Scale a Service Business Past One Location

## Summary

Alex answers how he would scale a car garage or mechanic business. He starts with
maximizing utilization of the current location: bays full, mechanics efficient, job types
triaged by dollars per hour, and problems solved as quickly as possible. Only after the
first location is nailed does he recommend scaling. For expansion, he suggests buying
failing or retiring-owner shops, potentially using seller financing or long earnouts that
turn the business into an annuity for the seller.

## Key claims

- Alex says a mechanic shop should first maximize utilization of the existing space and
  team.
- He is not the biggest fan of pure hourly billing because it is simpler but may not be
  the most profitable model.
- He prefers selling the solved problem and then delivering it as fast as possible, so the
  business makes more money per worker per unit of time.
- He applies "nail it, then scale it": the current location should work without the owner,
  grow month over month, retain customers, and generate referrals before expansion.
- He says buying a failing or retiring-owner shop may be better than opening from scratch
  because the business already has lifts, zoning, and other capital-expense assets.
- He suggests seller financing or 15- to 20-year earnouts as a structure for older owners
  who want annuity-like payments.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "We have to look at dollars per hour."

> "Sell to solve the problem and then try as hard as you can to solve the problem as fast as you can."

> "Nail it then scale it."

> "Do these four walls work without you?"
