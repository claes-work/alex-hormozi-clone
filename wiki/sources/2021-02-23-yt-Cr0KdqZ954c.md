# Introduction To Marketing | Business Marketing 101

## Summary

In this early (2021) foundational marketing video, Hormozi argues that the sprawl of
marketing KPIs (CPL, CPC, CTR, etc.) can be distilled into three high-level numbers he
watches in any business he invests in or partners with: cost of acquisition (CAC),
lifetime value (LTV), and 30-day cash. He stresses that LTV should be measured as gross
profit over a customer's lifespan, not total revenue, because decisions must be based on
margin. He then defines two target relationships — LTV:CAC greater than 3:1 (proof of a
viable, growable business) and 30-day cash at least equal to CAC (break-even fast enough
to finance acquisition with other people's money / credit lines) — and frames using OPM
to acquire customers as the engine behind his companies' growth.

## Key claims

- (2021) The many marketing KPIs distill down to three "big buckets" worth watching at a
  high level: CAC, LTV, and 30-day cash. [paraphrase]
- (2021) CAC (cost of acquisition) should include *all* costs to acquire a customer:
  sales commission, ad spend, the marketing team that generated inquiries, associated
  software, etc. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Hormozi defines LTV as total *gross profit* over the customer lifespan, not
  total revenue — because all decisions should be based on margin. Example: $700 of food
  ordered at a 10% margin is really only $70 of LTV, so spending $200 to acquire that
  customer is a loss, not a win. [paraphrase]
- (2021) "30-day cash" is his term for what formal finance calls the payback period — how
  long it takes to pay back the cost of acquisition. He uses 30 days because virtually any
  business can access an interest-free-for-30-days credit line (credit card / Amex / bank
  line). [paraphrase]
- (2021) Target relationship #1: LTV:CAC should always be greater than 3:1 — i.e. generate
  more than 3x the cost of acquisition in gross profit — which signals sufficient profit to
  grow. Worked example: 80% gross margin on a $1,000 lifetime = $800, so CAC must be under
  ~$266. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Target relationship #2: 30-day cash to CAC of at least 1:1 (break-even within the
  window) means you can use *other people's money* (OPM) to finance acquisition — get the
  customer, collect enough in the first 30 days to repay the debt, and keep the customer
  plus back-end revenue "for free." [paraphrase]
- (2021) He attributes the big growth of his companies to not needing his own money to
  acquire customers — using someone else's money, recouping it within 30 days, then
  upselling into continuity. [paraphrase]
- (2021) If the metrics fall short, the fix is to raise 30-day cash: add an upsell, a
  first-week/first-two-week liquidation offer, ancillary products, or affiliate referrals.
  A bigger 30-day cash number lets you spend more to acquire and widen the acquisition
  net while still profiting. [paraphrase]

## Topic notes

This is an **early (2021) proto-version** of ideas Hormozi later systematized:

- The **LTV as gross profit (not revenue)** insistence and the **LTV:CAC > 3:1** heuristic
  are early statements of the unit-economics framing that runs through his later books.
  Relates to [[../topics/business/business]] money-models / pricing material — worth
  cross-linking to any LTGP:CAC page when one exists (note the later formulation uses
  **LTGP** — lifetime *gross profit* — which this video already describes in substance
  though not by that acronym).
- **"30-day cash" ≥ CAC** and financing acquisition with **OPM / interest-free credit** is
  an early version of the "client-financed acquisition" / payback-window concept that
  becomes central in *$100M Leads* (2023). This video predates that book by ~2.5 years and
  frames the same mechanic (break even fast → back-end is "free" → widen the net).
- The "add an upsell / liquidation offer / ancillary products / affiliates to raise
  30-day cash" fixes prefigure later monetization and acquisition-channel thinking; relates
  to [[../topics/business/acquisition-channels]] if/when that page exists. No Core Four
  channel taxonomy appears here — this video is purely about the *numbers*, not the
  channels, so it is a metrics-side precursor rather than a proto-Core-Four.
