# How to Make Hard Decisions

## Summary
Solo clip. Alex shares a **two-question decision frame** ("two razors") he uses for hard decisions and difficult conversations, especially interpersonal ones: (1) *What do I want to have happen?* and (2) *What increases the odds of that happening?* His point: the gut reaction to being wronged is a **countermeasure** — you want to counterpunch, punish, "counter-control" — but that reaction is usually *antithetical* to the outcome you actually want. Worked examples: someone attacks you online (you want them to move on and forget you exist → engaging only reinforces it and makes them hate you more); an underperforming employee (you want them to do a good job → yelling raises their anxiety and lowers performance, whereas rewarding/giving public status for what they did well creates a positive loop). He contrasts deliberate decision-making with how "most people live their lives" — gut reaction to counter reaction, "like seaweed in the ocean," directionless, never getting what they want, and concluding life is chaotic. A recurring output of applying the two razors: **doing nothing is often the most productive thing to do** — an inside joke he calls "the heavyweight champion of the world" (among all candidate actions, "do nothing" almost always wins). The right move is usually to keep doing your work, focus on your marriage, focus on good work, and ignore the rest — which is "physically efficient but emotionally painful." He frames this as one of the "artifacts" he's collected this year. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex uses a two-question frame for hard decisions and difficult conversations: (1) "What do I want to have happen?" and (2) "What increases the odds of that happening?"
- As of March 2026, the innate reaction to being wronged is a "countermeasure" — the urge to counterpunch/punish so the other person "dare not punish you" — and this gut reaction is usually antithetical to the outcome you actually want.
- As of March 2026 (online-attack example): what he wants is for the person to move on and forget he exists; interacting with them reinforces the attack and increases the odds they hate him more and try to damage him further — making the problem worse.
- As of March 2026 (employee example): the urge is to yell, but the goal is good performance; yelling raises anxiety and lowers performance, whereas rewarding or publicly giving status for the one thing they did well creates a positive loop that increases the odds of more good work.
- As of March 2026, the action that increases the odds of getting what you ultimately want "rarely is what I want to do," or at least is rarely the initial gut reaction.
- As of March 2026, most people live "gut reaction to gut reaction" and counter-reaction — purely responsive, "like seaweed in the ocean," directionless, never getting what they want, and therefore thinking life is chaotic.
- As of March 2026, a common output of applying the two razors is that "doing nothing at all is often the most productive thing to do" — an inside joke he calls "the heavyweight champion of the world," the option that almost always wins.
- As of March 2026, the usually-correct move is to keep doing your own work, focus on your marriage and good work, and ignore the rest — which is "physically efficient but emotionally painful."
