# Helping a Brick-and-Mortar Service Business Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client owns a dance school with two locations doing $2.7M/year (5 years in business), aiming for $3M this year — which would put them at ~80% capacity on core programs — and $10M+ long term, with facility space/capacity as the stated constraint. They want to eventually exit for at least $20M and don't want to franchise. Alex opts to give "the truth" rather than what they want to hear. His core message: high-service, talent-driven businesses are very hard to sell **for a lot of money**, and the $10–30M range is the hardest exit band of all — too small for buyers with real money, too big for buyers without it (a $100M deal is easier to close than a $25M one). Such a business can be *offloaded* easily to "some doctor who has no idea about business but thinks he does" — like a restaurant somebody buys because a friend told them their cooking is good — but not for a premium; you'd likely get "10 with a bunch of hair on it," earn-out clauses and strings attached. Alex says he can acquire gyms/dance/ballet studios essentially for free (that's how he expanded) because so many owners are in pain and want out — so the business model is good; the real question is the **expansion path**: M&A (buy retiring owners' studios), de novo (organic build location-by-location), or franchise/licensing. Only the franchise/licensing route is meaningfully sellable. His concrete advice: take the current model, ask "how could I cut the complexity by 80%?", then franchise the stripped-down version. He tells the **Alloy / Rick Mayo** story as the template: Rick had a $4M single facility with everything (smoothie bar, merch, PT, semi-private, large group), analyzed revenue per square foot, found semi-private was by far the highest, fired every client except semi-private (still profitable in the same 6,000 sq ft lease), and opened a pilot location running only the single most profitable service with a 3-person model (one manager doing mornings/evenings + sales, plus assistants) that does $500–600k/year at 50% margins and can be cookie-cuttered. He affirms the client is right to resist adding services on the front end; instead look at your most profitable times, pricing and packages, and strip down to the fewest people who could run it so someone else can operate it. Licensing is the same play with a different legal structure. He closes with a caution: he knows many fitness operators with 5–10 locations and essentially none sold the whole thing — they all piecemealed it (one location to a trainer, one to a customer, one to a competitor) when they got tired. Ends with the standard $100M scaling roadmap promo (ignored for claims).
