# Eliminate Should, Why, and Because From Your Life

## Summary

Alex says people guess at "why" and often answer with stories that have received approval
before. His current focus is eliminating "should," "why," and "because" from his thinking
because motives are usually unknowable. He says the practical move is to stick to what can
be observed: what someone said or did. This makes communication, relationships, management,
and persuasion simpler. He also defends Leila's preferences by saying people should spend
their own money their own way and not impose their preferences on others.

## Key claims

- Alex says people often invent answers to "why" questions from existing approved stories.
- He says the real reason for a behavior may be unknowable even to the person who did it.
- He is focused on eliminating "should," "why," and "because" as explanatory clutter.
- He recommends sticking to observable behavior rather than guessing intention.
- He says this helps communication, relationships, management, and persuasion.
- When asked why humans complicate things, he refuses to fabricate an answer and says he
  only knows that people do it.
- He says "don't analyze your pleasures" fits Leila's relationship with things she likes:
  if she likes something and can afford it in context, other people's objections are their
  preferences, not obligations.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Stick to what you can observe."

> "We just know that I said it."

> "I can make something up if you want, but like it'll just be made up."

> "No shoulds, no whys, and no becauses."
