# You're Setting Goals Wrong

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that most entrepreneurs stay "busy but broke"
not from working too little but from spreading effort across too many things at once.
Using a magnifying-glass and a "four parties on one Friday night" analogy, he reframes
strategy as the prioritization of limited resources against unlimited options, insists
that "priority" can only be singular, and quantifies the hidden cost of constant change
(~20% revenue hit per change). The prescription: pick the single highest-leverage
constraint, do that one thing well, and treat the vast idea backlog as things to *not*
do — because the real bottleneck is execution, not idea quality.

## Key claims (his goal-setting view)

- The reason people are busy but broke is not insufficient effort but effort spread
  across too many things — working hard "in the wrong ways." (paraphrase)
- Analogy: concentrating a magnifying glass burns through paper only at the focal
  "sweet spot"; diffuse light does nothing. Effort works the same way. (paraphrase)
- Analogy: throwing four parties on one Friday night means none reaches critical mass;
  combining everyone into one party makes it "take off." Entrepreneurs run "four
  parties at once" and wonder why none works. (paraphrase)
- Entrepreneurs get reinforced for quitting/starting things (job → own business), so
  they keep starting new "parties" instead of recognizing they simply moved to a
  *better vehicle*. (paraphrase)
- Strategy defined: the prioritization of limited resources against unlimited options —
  choosing what to do and what not to do. (paraphrase)
- "Priority" is inherently singular (from Latin *prior* = first); the 20th-century
  plural "priorities" neutered the word. If you have four priorities, you have none.
  (paraphrase)
- Biggest productivity hack: do nothing besides the task you set out to do. Most
  "productivity" is a guilt-free way to procrastinate. (paraphrase)
- Commitment / focus / deciding all mean the same thing: cut off alternatives; what
  remains becomes the priority. (paraphrase; "decide" from Latin *-cidere* = to cut off)
- People who move fastest don't literally move faster — they get more per move; higher
  leverage. (paraphrase)
- Prioritization test: if you had 100% certainty of accomplishing only one item a year
  from now, which gets you furthest? Contingent items reveal the true single priority.
  (paraphrase)
- Prefers doing things linearly/chronologically over in parallel, because focusing all
  company resources on one point makes everyone feel they're the bottleneck (they are)
  and the work gets done faster. Parallel effort means no one feels accountable and
  nothing gets done. (paraphrase)
- The cost of change: every new change costs ~20% of revenue up front before any
  uncertain upside. So a change must offer at least ~20% upside to justify a guaranteed
  ~20% downside. Most tests aren't worth running because they don't touch the
  constraint. (paraphrase)
- Businesses stuck for years at $1M / $3M / $10M are typically changing too many things
  and working on the wrong (non-constraint) thing; some changes actively make things
  worse. (paraphrase)
- Leverage lives at the two ends — a product so good people talk about it, or marketing
  so good everyone hears about it. Obsessing over the middle (e.g. conversion process)
  is low-leverage. (paraphrase)
- Rule of thumb: if a metric for the thing you love is at/above industry benchmark,
  leave it alone — free the bandwidth to fix the thing you're bad at, dislike, and have
  outsourced (usually the real constraint). (paraphrase)
- Small-business paradox: owners have the most ideas but the least capacity to pursue
  them; the best hope to compete is to do one thing well. (paraphrase)
- Patience = figuring out what to do in the meantime. Keeps a "mondo" list of 200+
  money/improvement ideas but has pulled only three from it in 18 months, despite having
  more resources than most. (paraphrase; self-reported)
- Most of your anxiety/stress comes from the self-created to-do list of undone things;
  dropping it and executing what you already do well will make more money — and depress
  you, because it proves the problem was execution, not ideas. (paraphrase)
- Cites Jack Dorsey ("make the details perfect; limit the number of details") and Warren
  Buffett's 20-punch-card investing analogy applied to one optimization "punch" per year.
  (paraphrase; attributed to others)
