# Making Money is a Game (Here's the Cheat Code)

## Summary

Solo promotional video for Alex Hormozi's upcoming book *$100M Money Models* (live
launch August 16th, 2025). He defines a "money model" as a deliberate sequence of
offers structured to generate the most upfront cash, the most leads, and the highest
lifetime value while accelerating cash flow. The core "cheat code": make more money
acquiring a customer than it costs to acquire them within the first 30 days, so you can
outspend competitors. He illustrates with a detailed walkthrough of the gym money model
that let him make ~$245/lead versus competitors' ~$53/lead from identical 100 leads, and
positions Money Models as the third book in the $100M series (after Offers and Leads).

## Key claims

- A money model is "a series of offers deliberately structured to generate the most
  upfront cash," get the most leads, and get the highest lifetime value (buy again and
  again), while accelerating cash flow.
- The cheat code: make more money acquiring a customer than it costs to acquire them
  within the first 30 days — this flips business from "hard mode" (spend to acquire, hope
  to recoup over time).
- The one guaranteed constant: the cost of advertising will only go up over time; a
  strong money model future-proofs a business against this.
- Money models are why century-old companies (he names JP Morgan, Northwestern Mutual,
  GE) survive — not trends/hashtags but a stronger money model.
- He claims money models are "responsible for the vast majority of my material success"
  and that "nothing else comes close."
- Origin story: his gym's customer-acquisition cost rose from ~$100 to ~$500 (placeholder
  figures) after an algorithm change, while charging a $99/month membership — unsustainable.
- A college acquaintance with two supplement stores (claimed $80k and $130k/month, not
  verified — Alex notes he didn't ask for bank statements) inspired him to sell supplements
  to gym members, getting a few hundred dollars per person in the first 48 hours, which
  fixed the economics and let him afford ads again.
- Four offer buckets in the book: (1) attraction offers (maximize conversion / pull cash
  forward), (2) upsells (maximize gross profit per customer), (3) downsells (turn nos into
  yeses without cannibalizing full-price buyers), (4) continuity (recurring purchases).
- "We don't get customers to make sales. We make sales to get customers."
- Downsells are risky: done wrong, customers who would have paid for the expensive thing
  buy only the cheaper thing.
- Business fundamentally requires only one thing: cash (to pay bills, payroll, incorporation
  fee). Business is an endurance game / marathon — staying alive and outlasting.
- The old gym-industry model was the "LBO" (low barrier offer, not leveraged buyout): a
  $21 21-day promotion front-end, then a $99/month continuity, with no compelling mechanic
  between them.
- Detailed math — competitor model: 100 leads at $20/lead = $2,000 cost; 25% convert at $21
  = $525; ~35% (industry trial-to-membership) → 8 members at $99/mo with ~5-month average
  stay ≈ $4,800; total ≈ $5,325, i.e. max ~$53/lead.
- His model: free/six-week challenge front-end (42-day detox/transformation), sold at the
  point of highest motivation (the moment they walk in the door, greatest pain) for ~$500.
  15% of 100 leads = 15 sales × $500 = $7,500 upfront (day one). Sell ~12 on supplements at
  ~$200 (≈80% gross profit) ≈ $2,000 gross profit. Roll the $500 forward toward a year of
  membership at ~$200/mo (raised from $99 via better pricing): ~70% take it → 10 members ×
  $2,000 = ~$10,000. Prepay-in-full discount (buy 10 months get 2 free) to ~20-30% → ~$5,000
  more. Total ≈ $24,500 from the same 100 leads, i.e. ~$245/lead — 5x the competitor.
- Most of that (~$19,500) lands in the first 30 days, so ads on a credit card return ~$8-9
  per dollar in, while he still keeps the lead and can sell more later.
- "People have different wallets" — membership money, supplement money, gym-clothes money,
  fitness-food money are separate buckets; the business that captures the highest percentage
  fastest wins.
- Customers are "fractal 80/20": ~1 in 5 will buy something significantly more expensive.
- The four offers he stacked, named: win-your-money-back (attraction) → supplement classic
  upsell ("can't buy X without Y") → rollover upsell (credit the $500 toward membership,
  upsell + continuity) → buy-X-to-get-Y (pay-in-full discount, buy 10 months get 2 free).
- "Auction of attention" thesis: many businesses (equipment, supplements, agencies,
  construction, apparel) advertise to the same avatar; the one with the best money model can
  outspend all others, raise CPMs, and hold an "ethical and legal monopoly" of the prospect's
  attention.
- Gym Launch was structured on a superior money model in two layers: a better model for gyms
  to run, and a better model at the Gym Launch level itself.
- Series placement: $100M Offers = create an offer so good people feel stupid saying no;
  $100M Leads = how to advertise / get customers; $100M Money Models = connects offer and
  leads, runs the water through the pipe (advertising in, money out). The three books were
  originally one 2021 manuscript titled "Lead Generation and Monetization Structures."
- You don't need to read Offers or Leads to use Money Models, but it helps; the components are
  additive/multiplicative "business enhancers." A money model is core to every business
  regardless of industry or size.
- Started writing the book in 2021 and saved every concept for this launch; had not discussed
  these concepts publicly before.
