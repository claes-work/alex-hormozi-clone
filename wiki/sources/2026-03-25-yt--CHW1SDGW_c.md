# How Do I Know Which Business to Go All In On?

## Summary
In this live Q&A, a caller who runs a TikTok Shop agency for e-commerce brands (doing ~$2.5M revenue, targeting $12M) asks whether to keep the agency, go all-in on a supplements brand he co-owns with a partner named Theo, or merge them and invest in client brands. Alex reframes the dilemma around the intended end state, diagnoses two structural weaknesses in the agency model (small volatile customers plus a fading TikTok Shop arbitrage), and pushes the caller to pick one path, serve only big brands via revenue shares, and resist opportunity-chasing FOMO through disciplined focus.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues there is no objectively right answer to which business to pursue; the choice should follow what the operator actually wants and the end state they envision, then be reverse-engineered into the present.
- As of March 2026, Alex says servicing small customers makes a business inherently volatile, harder to sell, less viable, and less fun to run.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames TikTok Shop as a "hot arbitrage opportunity" that will not persist; he estimates that in ~3 years the likelihood of comparable returns is low, so building on it alone does not create a long-term business.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises the caller to keep the agency's large marketing infrastructure but cut its clients and redirect that "marketing horsepower" toward the owned brand (Theo's supplements) to build one big brand — or, alternatively, pick the agency and serve only high-end customers. Either way, choose one.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends revenue-share deals over investing equity in client brands: cleaner, easier, and since most such brands never sell, cash flow is the only realistic return.
- As of March 2026, Alex says a $12M target is such a low bar that almost any of these businesses could reach it, so ambition size ("do you want a $100M thing?") is the real filter that would force serving higher-end customers.
- As of March 2026, Alex names FOMO as the worst driver for an entrepreneur (fear being its first letter); he says more skill means more good opportunities to say no to, and that doing one thing well for life makes it big while doing two makes neither big.
- Caller claim (unverified): his agency does ~$2.5M revenue and he wants to reach $12M; the top two client brands are very different avatars; the supplements brand and agency are both growing "like crazy" on TikTok Shop; and an info-product model does not yet exist in the UK market.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I like to think about things from like what's the end state going to look like and then kind of reverse it into the present."
> "You're selling to the wrong people and you're selling to short-term people a short-term opportunity. So it doesn't build a long-term business."
> "If I were you, I would take my existing very large infrastructure on the agency side, cut all my clients, and then take all of that marketing horsepower and put it towards Theo. And then own a big-ass brand."
> "Stop selling to small businesses and you'll build a bigger business."
> "The more skilled you are, the more amazing opportunities you have to say no to in order to do one opportunity all the way. Cuz if you just do one thing really well for the rest of your life, it'll get really big. But if you do two things for the rest of your life, you won't do either of them really well and neither of them will get big."
> "The last year was the first year that I noticed in my life that I didn't have FOMO... I can tell you that it is the worst drive as an entrepreneur. It's really fear. It's the driver. It's the first letter."
> "Everybody who's like, 'Hey, do you think you can make it big for me?' Wrong avatar. They're going to churn out and you're going to be their savior, which also means that you're their villain the moment it doesn't work."
> "This is a discipline thing. Like you have to learn how to say no."
