# I Discovered The Easiest Million Dollar Business to Start in 2024

## Summary

Solo talk in which Hormozi argues that coaching / information / education / digital
course / membership businesses are the easiest way to reach $1–3M/year but among the
hardest to scale into a sellable, exit-worthy asset. He frames the choice between an
education business and a traditional (local/service/software) business around two
questions — what is your goal (fast income vs. a $10M+ exit), and which set of problems
you are more comfortable solving — and insists on committing fully to one path rather
than straddling both. He speaks as both a customer and a supplier of the education
space, citing his own gym-launch history and the sale of his last such business for
$46.2M.

## Key claims

- **The business model he recommends (for fast income):** coaching / information /
  education / digital course / membership businesses. Easiest path to $100K/year,
  $100K–$200K/month, and $1–3M/year. No inventory, package existing knowledge and
  experience immediately, deliver outsized value to a niche audience.
- **The core tradeoff:** the same thing that makes it fast (low operational
  infrastructure) also caps it — "the reason that this thing is really fast is also the
  same reason why it's not going to make you a billionaire." Easiest to a million, hardest
  to $100M.
- **Two decision factors:** (1) what is your goal — fast income vs. a sellable asset /
  large exit; (2) which problems you are more comfortable solving.
- **Why education businesses rarely become assets:** low revenue retention (once you
  teach someone, they know it and leave — "four-year degrees are four years, not
  forever"), plus **three types of key-man risk** — the founder brings the customers
  (front-end/ads), delivers/maintains the product, and the team is personally loyal to
  the founder. This makes exit very hard.
- **Scale rarity:** of people who started an info business from scratch without a brand,
  he can count those at $100M/year "on one hand" (all with deep brands, 30+ years) and
  those at $30M/year "on two hands"; a massive band sits at $0.5–3M/year with a team of
  ~10, where most entrepreneurs cap out on operational/leadership skill.
- **Traditional business path (for a $10M+ exit):** slower at the start, faster near the
  end, more capital-intensive (must reinvest to open locations / hire), but builds a real
  asset that can run without you. Getting from 55 to 60 locations makes the most money for
  almost no time; most people quit "right when they reach a boss they don't know how to
  beat" — usually the inability to install the next layer of leadership.
- **Commit — don't straddle:** "you can't ride two horses." Whichever direction you go
  (traditional→education or education→software/service), cut/sell the other business; he
  has never seen anyone succeed straddling. A small side-business will still consume 50–100%
  of your mental bandwidth when a problem hits.
- **Test-kitchen rejection:** for education, don't keep a live traditional business as a
  "test kitchen." Instead test a new play the way you'd roll it out — sample 10 customers
  representing different markets and skill levels (2 from each 20% performance band), not
  the top or bottom 10.
- **Ownership over franchising / licensing:** cites buying out 30+ franchise locations of
  an acquired chain to take them corporate (8% royalty vs. owning the whole location for
  ~2x the work); Panda Express (Mr. Panda owns all ~2,600 locations, ~$4B sales, 27% net
  margins) as the payoff of sticking with one owned model instead of coaching competitors.
- **Goals change:** cites Alex Becker (info business ~$25–30M/yr → Hyros software), Sam
  Ovens (Consulting.com $30M+/yr → Skool.com, now year 5–6, less cash flow than
  Consulting but scaling faster) as people who switched and, crucially, stopped the old
  thing entirely.
- **CTAs:** Skool.com/games (free, guided business start — claims 54.1% of paid-community
  Skool Games participants make their first dollar online); Acquisition.com workshops for
  larger businesses wanting a big exit.
