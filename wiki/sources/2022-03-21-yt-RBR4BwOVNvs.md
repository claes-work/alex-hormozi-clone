# I sold everything (2022-03-21)

## Summary

Solo reflection, filmed ~a year after his 2021 exit, on why he sold his companies
and nearly all his material possessions, what living "with one bag" taught him, and
where he landed. Alex frames the exit as wanting "a reset," recounts ~9 months of
nomadic renting, diagnoses his own **overcorrection** (nothing felt as bad as too
much), and settles on a "middle path": 2–3 stocked home bases plus travel. Closes
with his purchasing philosophy — **function over form**, run as a pre-made "decision
algorithm" to conserve mental bandwidth. Alex is the sole speaker throughout.

## Key claims

- **Why he sold:** it "felt very heavy" — material things were weighing him down and
  taking his attention; he wanted a **reset**. (self-reported)
- **What he sold (2021):** a **$12M business**, **two businesses for just under $50M**,
  **two exotic cars**, and a **$4M house in Austin**. Kept equities/stocks but "no
  material belongings." (self-reported)
- Lives his life "in **seasons**" of 3–5 years: college→management consultant → running
  gyms → licensing gyms + starting "three kind of bigger companies which is what we just
  sold last year" → now full-time investing in service/e-learning businesses doing
  **$1–10M/yr in profit**. (self-reported)
- His going-in dream state: "an unlimited credit card and just consume my lifestyle with
  one bag" — he and Leila "more or less did that after we sold," giving away all clothing
  except one bag's worth.
- **Overcorrection learned:** going from "lots of stuff" all the way to "nothing" was too
  far; the ideal is **2–4 primary homes stocked with your stuff** so you keep flexibility
  *and* familiarity. Not having a home base "was not advisable."
- **Hedonic adaptation:** novel purchases (e.g. a new car) stop conferring value within a
  month or two; used to explain why more stuff doesn't buy lasting satisfaction.
- **On the Bentley Flying Spur** (~$300k+, brand new, one of the cars he sold): bought it
  as his one "status symbol" because people said he should "live a little"; only real
  benefit was better treatment when pulling up to places, but it "tainted" how he saw
  people. Verdict: not worth the money.
- **On private jets:** the one luxury he still spends on — because it "transforms transport
  from a negative experience to a positive experience," eliminating travel drain/recovery
  time. Justified via **relative cost**: he's "taken home more than a million dollars a
  month for the last half decade," so a $10k expense on $10M/yr income is proportionally
  like $50 to an average $50k/yr family.
- **Unifying purchasing theory: function over form** — buys on net utility, not status;
  spends up only where function improves (his flannels), buys cheap where it doesn't
  ($10 Amazon 5-packs of tank tops). Frames it as a **decision algorithm** ("if this then
  that") to pre-make decisions and save bandwidth.
- **Clothing rule:** excess clothing "actually decreases my quality of life"; keeps a
  fixed-size closet — to add a shirt he removes a lower-utility one; buys a liked shirt in
  ~6 colors and replaces older ones in bulk.
- Went **remote** over the prior year (decision made across ~2021–2022).
