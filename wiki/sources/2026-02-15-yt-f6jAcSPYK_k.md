# What Do People Remember About Us After We Die?

## Summary

Short interview clip in which a business owner asks Alex Hormozi to unpack what he means by "death creates clarity." Alex frames mortality as a forcing function for prioritization — the "ultimate razor" for deciding whether something will actually matter. He says writing his mother's eulogy was valuable because a eulogy exposes what makes the cut and what doesn't: a "hilarious amount" of both the things we stress about *and* the things we strive to achieve are simply not included. He endorses the "reverse obituary / reverse eulogy" exercise (which he attributes to Charlie Munger) — imagining your own eulogy and working backward. He notes eulogies run only 500–1,000 words because the audience is busy, so almost nothing survives the edit. At his own funeral, he argues, "titan of industry" or how much money he made would be one throwaway sentence covering 40 years; what a eulogy really transitions to is two things — **service** and **character**. From that he derives a life design: pick the causes meaningful enough to serve, then build the traits needed to serve them at the highest level, so that everything done between now and death is in service of that eulogy.

## Key claims (2026-02-15, paraphrased, Alex-attributed)

- ★ **Death creates clarity** by acting as a forcing function around prioritization — the "ultimate razor" for whether something will actually matter.
- Writing a eulogy or obituary reveals what's included and what's not; a "hilarious amount" of both the things we stress about and the things we strive to achieve get left out.
- Writing his **mother's eulogy** was personally valuable for exactly this reason.
- ★ The **"reverse obituary / reverse eulogy"** exercise — imagining what your own eulogy would say and working backward from it — is worth doing; he credits the concept to Charlie Munger ("Charlie Mer[ger]" in the garbled caption), while noting others likely said it before him.
- Eulogies are **only ~500 to 1,000 words** long, because the audience is busy ("rush hour, they've got places to be"), so almost nothing about a person's life survives the edit.
- Worldly accomplishments barely register: at a "titan of industry" figure's funeral, that status is **one sentence covering 40 years**; "no one's going to give a [expletive] about how much money I made."
- ★ Eulogies transition to **two recurring themes: service and character** — the common thread of what people are actually remembered for.
- Life-design implication: identify the **causes meaningful enough to serve**, then build the **traits/character needed to serve them at the highest level**, so that everything done between now and death is "in service of that eulogy."
