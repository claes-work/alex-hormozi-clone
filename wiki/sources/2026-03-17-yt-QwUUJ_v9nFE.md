# Advice for Anyone with 2+ Businesses

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A founder runs two businesses under one roof: a talent group (acting/modeling agencies) doing $2.4M in revenue — mostly from "preparation" workshop packages sold to applicants, plus a ~20% commission on booked work — and a newer chain of photography studios (~14 months old, two locations) doing a little over $1M. He wants to reach $6M "at a much higher margin and significantly less stress." Alex's diagnosis is singular and blunt: **pick one business and go all-in.** The two make roughly the same net profit (~$250k each; photo studio at 25–30% net margin, the agency at about half the margin), but the founder is CEO of both and only has enough time to *maintain* them, not *grow* them. The core cost is context-switching — not task-switching but full **business-switching**: mentally jumping from "people showing up for photo shoots, upselling portraits and packages" to "outbound to land a brand deal for a model" is running two completely different companies. Alex reframes the agency's real problem: nothing is wrong with the agency model (there are billion-dollar agencies) — the issue is the **avatar**. The founder is "bottom-feeding," taking talent from the bottom who can't book work and then churn aggressively; you can't control or guarantee their outcome. He notes the agency actually scales better (no brick-and-mortar), but the founder hates it, so he should pick whichever he wants, reclaim half his time, and commit. Tactical asides on the photo business: it will face ad fatigue in a local market with little recurring revenue, so he'd position it as models getting **updated photos once a year** to create an annual subscription. The through-line: "there's nothing I can tell you in this call that's going to matter except that" — pick one, commit, stick with it. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's single actionable directive for an owner running two businesses: pick one and go all-in — "if I get you to do one thing from today, it's that you pick one."
- As of March 2026, the binding constraint of running multiple businesses is time: split across two, the owner has only enough time to *maintain* each, not *grow* it — reclaiming half your time makes the problems in either business solvable.
- As of March 2026, the deepest cost of two businesses is **business-switching** (a level beyond task-switching): each requires a completely different operating context (inbound photo upsells vs. outbound brand-deal sales), so switching between them is switching whole companies.
- As of March 2026, the modeling agency's problem isn't the agency model (billion-dollar agencies exist) — it's the **avatar**: the founder is "bottom-feeding," signing talent who can't book work, so they churn and get aggressive because you can't guarantee their outcome. Fix the avatar (take from the top), not the model.
- As of March 2026, an agency scales more easily than a photo-studio chain because it doesn't require opening brick-and-mortar locations, so talent-per-location and local ad fatigue aren't constraints.
- As of March 2026, a local photo studio driven mostly by ads faces ad fatigue and little recurring revenue; to make it stickier, position it as models getting updated photos once a year and convert that into an annual subscription.
- As of March 2026, when two businesses earn roughly the same profit, pick the one you actually want to run, get all your time back, and commit — the decision matters more than which one you choose.
