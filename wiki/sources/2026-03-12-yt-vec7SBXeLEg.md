# How I Deal With Anxiety

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex's core answer to future-anxiety is a single principle: **"Action alleviates anxiety."** He recalls the anxiety he felt in his early gym years — "why haven't I made it yet? Why are these other people doing better than me? What if I'm on the wrong path?" — and says the only thing he could control was to work, and specifically to *work with the intention to improve*, not work for work's sake. He frames anxiety as arising from an unfilled gap between where you are and where you want to be: owning one gym never felt like "I've made it," it felt like "I'm so deprived of where I want to be, I have to fill this gap as fast as I possibly can." The bulk of the video is his learning method for closing that gap: pay to learn from others' mistakes (he bought into a gym-owner mastermind *before* opening his gym), spend weekends calling and driving out to other gym owners asking "why do you do that?", take everyone's ideas, test them, keep what works and toss what doesn't. He argues the people who "move faster than life learn faster," and that real learning means **"same condition, new behavior"** — if an identical customer walks in and your script hasn't changed, you didn't learn. He describes his current data-driven learning loop (look at the last 2,000 ads, compare the top 50 vs. bottom 50, do more of what the winners share, less of what the losers do, repeat with the next batch) applied to ads, sales calls, content, and meetings. He tells anyone under ~28 to exploit a status trick: humble yourself, ask people ahead of you to mentor you, and they'll give away advice "worth thousands of dollars, years of your life, for free." He closes on the meta-point his father gave him — **you are the asset you invest in**, the "permanent hold" you can't sell — and that skills compound and stack disproportionately (sales + finance → raise a billion; code + marketing → build and sell software), so two skills can be worth far more than ten times one. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's central anxiety principle is "action alleviates anxiety" — when anxious about the future, the antidote is to do the thing you can control, which is to work and to get better.
- As of March 2026, he frames the point of work as improvement, not effort for its own sake: do repetitions because volume makes you better "typically just by accident," but be deliberate about it to compound the gains.
- As of March 2026, he traces his early anxiety to an unfilled gap — owning a gym never registered as "I've made it" but as "I'm so deprived of where I want to be; I have to fill this gap as fast as I possibly can."
- As of March 2026, he paid for a gym-owner mastermind *before* opening a gym, reasoning it was "unfair" value to learn from others' mistakes (storefront, parking, don't go too big, keep rent under a threshold per foot) rather than make those mistakes himself.
- As of March 2026, after opening he spent weekends on calls and driving to other gym owners asking "why do you do that?", taking ideas, testing them, keeping winners and discarding losers — how he "quickly iterated the gym model" past older owners.
- As of March 2026, his definition of learning is "same condition, new behavior": if an identical customer walks in right after another and your script hasn't changed, you may not be learning.
- As of March 2026, doing the exact same thing every day is a sign you are not learning; the goal is to work hard *and* smart so the work itself improves as you tweak it.
- As of March 2026, his biggest gains now come not from advice but from data: e.g. review the last 2,000 ads, compare the top 50 vs. bottom 50, find shared traits, do more of the winners' traits and less of the losers', then re-run on the next batch — applied to ads, sales calls, content, and meetings.
- As of March 2026, he advises those under ~28 to give status to people ahead of them by asking them to mentor — humbling yourself ("I don't know anything, I'm happy to learn") unlocks advice worth thousands of dollars and years of time, for free.
- As of March 2026, he says he has spent "ego points," dollars, time, and social capital — every resource he had — to buy learning faster, because learning is the only thing that precedes the "scoreboard" that matters in business.
- As of March 2026, he credits his father with the belief that "I am the asset I am investing in" — you are the permanent hold, the one asset you can't sell, and you compound.
- As of March 2026, he argues skills compound and stack disproportionately: sales + finance lets you raise a billion dollars; coding + marketing lets you build and sell software; knowing only one skill may earn "not a tenth as much" as knowing two.
