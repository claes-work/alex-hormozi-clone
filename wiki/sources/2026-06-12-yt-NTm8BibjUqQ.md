# The 3 Types of Content (And Why Only One Builds a Business)

## Summary

In this solo teaching video, Alex Hormozi explains why he is shifting his own content strategy toward education for business owners and away from entertainment or broad edutainment. He divides content into entertainment, education, and edutainment by audience behavior, not creator intent. He argues that views from the wrong audience do not translate into books, email opt-ins, or portfolio applications, so his team is optimizing for business-relevant audience quality, ad revenue/RPM, and conversions rather than raw reach.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Content falls into three buckets by what the audience gets from it: **entertainment** makes people watch, **education** changes behavior, and **edutainment** tries to do both.
- Alex says he is going all in on education because not all views are equal, he enjoys teaching, and he prefers the audience attracted by education.
- Entertainment viewers tend to want more entertainment; education viewers tend to want more education.
- For his business, entertainment views did not meaningfully convert into book buyers, email opt-ins, or Acquisition.com applications.
- He wants content to be "for you" rather than "for us," meaning built for business owners rather than for insider familiarity.
- Packaging should become clearer, not vaguer: thumbnails and headlines should say what the video is about.
- Educational introductions should establish proof, promise, and plan: why to listen, what the viewer will get, and how the video will deliver it.
- The "meat" of education content is lists, steps, stories, and clear language rather than production razzle-dazzle.
- Pre-work matters more than post-production; research and preparation reduce editing waste.
- Ad revenue/RPM is a useful leading indicator because it combines audience quality and quantity better than views alone.
- Long-form content drives better business conversions than shorts, while shorts can still help find long-form viewers across platforms.
- Creators should assume each viewer is cold: introduce themselves, explain why to listen, and avoid relying on inside jokes.
