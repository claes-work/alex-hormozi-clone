# Stop Waiting for the Perfect Moment to Start

## Summary
Solo monologue triggered by the title premise — the perfect moment never arrives; it starts "the moment you decide that you're fed up with waiting and decide to take action," and only *looks* perfect in retrospect. Alex uses a conversation with a young man who claimed to "have demons" as the launchpad for an extended argument that reframes trauma, self-sabotage, and hardship as **skill deficiencies**, not mystical afflictions. His core move: strip vague self-diagnoses ("I have demons," "I self-sabotage," "I have trust issues") of their drama and redefine them in terms of concrete behavior under specific conditions — because "what you have is a lack of skills in how to behave in a certain condition." He defines trauma neutrally ("a permanent change in behavior from an aversive stimulus") and argues it is neither good nor bad in itself — only whether the resulting behavior is conducive to your goals matters (touching a hot stove = "good" trauma; winning the lottery / privileged kids = "good" events that produce bad behaviors). He attacks "voodoo" pop-psychology language (emotions "stored in your body," energy) as undefined and unfalsifiable, and warns that blaming external events is convenient but forfeits power: "wherever you point is where your power is… the only time that you have more power is if you say it's on you." He teaches a three-step diagnostic from **Dr. Cashy** — **Logic, Evidence, Utility** (define it as behavior → what data actually supports it → who cares / does it block your goals?) — worked through with the "I have demons in the kitchen / I touched the stove once" example. He closes on an identity/Stoic frame: everyone experiences their own 10-out-of-10 pain (pain is normalized across humans), hardship is the "origin story" of the hero you claim you want to be ("the bigger the monster, the more epic the hero"), and he shares a personal story of buying a lottery ticket in college and feeling *terror* at the thought of winning because it would rob him of earning his own achievements (invoking Epictetus). The antidote to a paralyzing past is building the "figured-out muscle" — "I don't know how to do this. I will figure it out." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

- As of March 2026, he argues that "demons," self-sabotage, "mom or dad issues," and inconsistency are not real afflictions but a **lack of skill** in how to behave under specific conditions — i.e., inexperience / skill deficiency.

Great salespeople never get cold" — being "on fire" is just behaving differently (pauses, tonality, questions), and those behaviors are learnable.
- As of March 2026, he defines trauma neutrally as "a permanent change in behavior from an aversive stimulus," and argues trauma is neither inherently good nor bad — only whether it produces goal-conducive behavior determines that (hot stove = good; winning the lottery producing bad behaviors = bad).
- As of March 2026, he claims parents punishing a child are *hoping* to "traumatize" them in this neutral sense — to permanently change behavior via an aversive stimulus.
- As of March 2026, he dismisses pop-psychology language (trauma "stored in your body," "it's energy") as undefined and unfalsifiable — "Show me on someone's body where it's stored. Give me an x-ray."
- As of March 2026, he claims blaming external events is often chosen because it is easier than admitting "I chose not to do anything about it anyways," and that "wherever you point is where your power is" — you only gain power by saying "it's on you."
- As of March 2026, he teaches a three-step framework attributed to **Dr. Cashy** — **Logic, Evidence, Utility**: (1) define the complaint in terms of behavior; (2) check what evidence/data actually supports it (often it's a handful of instances, or none — "basically made up" narrative); (3) ask "who cares?" relative to your goals.
- As of March 2026, he claims humans work in narratives and often attach stories to things that "don't actually mean anything" — convenient and fun, but not therefore true.
- As of March 2026, he claims all humans experience their own "10 out of 10" pain (a needle to someone who's never suffered is a 10/10), which normalizes pain across people and leaves only the question "what do I do now?"
- As of March 2026, he frames hardship as the hero's "origin story" — "the bigger the monster, the more epic the hero" — and argues the chapter you're in is a *requirement* for becoming who you say you want to be, so resenting it is self-defeating.
- As of March 2026, he shares that in college he bought a single lottery ticket (jackpot ~a billion) and felt "terror," realizing that winning would mean he could "never be able to achieve anything that anyone thought was my own."
- As of March 2026, he claims the most powerful muscle to build is the "figured-out muscle" — defaulting to "I don't know how to do this. I will figure it out," reasoning that if someone no smarter (two hands and a brain) did it, so can you.
