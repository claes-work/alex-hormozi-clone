## Summary

Alex Hormozi delivers an internal company training on personal finance, aimed at making
every employee "independently wealthy" regardless of their income level. His central
thesis: wealth is a **ratio of what you spend versus what you earn**, not a number, so
anyone with any income can become wealthy by controlling spending. He demonstrates
compound growth with a phone calculator, reframes everyday purchases as their retirement
value, attacks debt (especially interest, mortgages, student and car loans) as the
number-one drain on net worth, and lays out a **six-step plan** to financial freedom.
Leila Hormozi contributes on the "attention cost" of debt and possessions.

## Frameworks / method

### Core thesis: wealth is a ratio, not a number
Wealth = what you earn ÷ what you spend (framed as spend-vs-earn). Because it is a ratio,
income level is not the binding constraint — spending discipline is. He cites internal
observation that net worth in his company correlates with what people *don't* spend, not
what they earn (top-10% earners with $0 or negative net worth; bottom-20% earners with
higher net worth than top earners).

### "Gamify the bank account" behavior model
Reduce personal finance to one number — the bank-account balance — and make *not spending*
a game: try to beat last month's spending total every month. Do it with your spouse (both
must be bought in). Frames finance as a behavioral/character problem, "simple, not easy."

### The reframe: purchases as future value
Uses a saved compound-interest calculator to convert discretionary purchases into their
retirement-age value (S&P, invested to age 75). Examples he cites: $100 Jordans → $22,000;
eating out ~$140/mo → $31,000 (and ~$400k over a year of that behavior at scale);
$20 of weekend beers → $6,000. "No one does the … math."

### Compounding as "the eighth wonder of the world"
Positive compounding builds wealth while you sleep; **debt is the eighth wonder working
against you** (negative compounding). Credit-card debt at 10–16%/yr is framed as the bank
making *you* their high-return investment.

### The six-step plan to financial freedom
1. **Save a starter emergency fund of $1,000–$5,000** — *before* paying off debt, because
   an unexpected-but-predictable expense will come up, and if you break the habit of
   saving to cover it, you stop.
2. **Pay off all debt except the mortgage, smallest balance to largest** — explicitly NOT
   highest-interest-first. The point is the emotional victory and momentum, not
   mathematical optimality.
3. **Expand the emergency fund to 3–6 months of living expenses** — not invested; kept for
   security and to lower stress.
4. **Reduce lifestyle / risk basis** — trade in a leased car, buy a ~$5,000 car in cash,
   downgrade the neighborhood. **4B:** if you insist on staying, refinance to a **15-year
   fixed** so most of the payment builds equity, then overpay it (no prepayment penalty).
5. **Invest 15% of pre-tax income into the S&P 500** — via a free app (Robin Hood),
   connect bank, buy an index, no adviser (fees kill returns). Repeatedly disclaimed as
   "not investment advice."
6. (Implied "superstar" tier) plow excess cash into paying the 15-year mortgage off early
   (e.g. in 10 years), after which nearly all income compounds into wealth.

### Debt / risk philosophy (attributed to a wealthier mentor)
"Debt increases risk; risk over a long enough time horizon eats the returns the leverage
gave, because any number × zero is still zero." He and Leila hold **zero debt** — home,
cars, everything paid in cash — for the security that "no one can take it away."

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-09-29)

- Wealth is a ratio between what you earn and what you spend, not an absolute number;
  therefore anyone who earns anything can become wealthy by controlling spending.
- The average American has (or had) **negative net worth** — a newborn at $0 is already
  above average.
- A US minimum-wage worker accrues **>$1 million** in lifetime earnings; the median earner
  **>$3 million** — so meaningful money flows through nearly everyone; the outcome depends
  on decisions.
- $1–$5 million in assets puts you in the **top 1%** of America.
- **35% of the average American's paycheck goes to interest** — framed as the biggest drain
  on net worth after taxes, "3.5×" what a Subway franchise takes.
- On a 30-year mortgage, the first three years of payments go almost entirely to interest,
  not principal; the average American moves every ~3 years, so they effectively "lease from
  the bank."
- Student loans are singled out as predatory: banks lobbied for government-backed loans and
  hand 18-year-olds ~$200,000 with no earning potential and no discharge — a "royalty on
  your life."
- Material goods have not increased his subjective well-being; the value of money to him is
  **reduced anxiety / stress-free living**, achieved most easily by spending less (lower
  risk).
- Buying the **Bentley** was his single biggest financial regret; he got zero enjoyment
  from it and missed his dented, cracked-windshield **Prius** (which he drove while already
  a millionaire).
- A cash car bought at ~$5,000 and used 3 years can be resold for ~$5,000 — "you use it for
  free."
- The S&P 500 has returned ~9–10%/yr over the last ~80 years.
- Investing $500/mo from age 35 to 75 (contributing $240,000) can grow to ~$3 million.
- **Self-reported:** he was making $5,000 profit his second month in business, rising
  $5k/month ($10k, $15k, $20k…$35k), while sleeping on the gym floor, because spending
  earned income gave him anxiety.
- **Self-reported:** the "$1,000 left before Gym Launch" story omits that he *also* had just
  under **$300,000 saved in an index** that he mentally treated as non-existent /
  untouchable.
- **Self-reported:** when he first moved to Southern California he split one bedroom (in a
  6-bedroom house) with another guy for $400/mo while earning ~$20,000/mo.
- **Self-reported:** this year, he and Leila made **more money on their money than from
  their companies**.
- **Biographical:** his father was an immigrant who arrived with $1,000; the household
  reused paper towels (split in half), avoided napkins, and bought in bulk to save.
