# The anti-routine...[what billionaires use to win] (2022-02-07)

## Summary

In this 2022 mindset video, Alex Hormozi reframes the common "what's your daily
routine?" question as the wrong question, arguing that people who reach very high
outcomes succeed not by *adding* rituals but by *subtracting* — an "anti-routine"
built on the single word **no**. He recounts the roughly 18-month stretch (framed
as "half a decade ago," when he went from looking up bankruptcy lawyers to taking
home over a million dollars a month at age 27) and lists the concrete things he
*stopped* doing so that "all that remained was the work to be done." He states he
owns acquisition.com, a portfolio of about six companies doing about $85 million a
year.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2022)

- The premise behind "tell me your routine" is wrong: people assume champions
  *do* something extra that you don't; in reality champions *lack* something you
  have — e.g. an "off switch" — and it is that lack that drives their success. [2022]
- The most productive routine possible is an "anti-routine": stop doing the things
  you currently do so that only the essential work remains, which also makes it
  clear why you weren't being effective. [2022]
- Real change came from things he *stopped* doing, not things he started. [2022]
- Priorities mean two things cannot be equally important; if you call two things
  both important, neither actually is, which is why people who can't say no can't
  get things done. [2022]
- Prioritizing literally means saying no to everything else; if something is the
  priority you do not do the other things. [2022]
- The theme of the whole method is the word "no" — a temporary "season of no"
  ("not forever, just for right now"). [2022]
- Every incoming communication (text, email, call request) contains an implied
  take: a request to reply. You are not obligated to answer. [2022]
- People conflate *having the time* to reply with being *obligated* to reply; the
  "500,000 people text you" thought experiment shows the obligation never existed.
  [2022]
- During the 18 months he simply stopped replying to people altogether — difficult
  for him as a self-described people-pleasing, social person. [2022]
- Events, masterminds, and coaching programs mostly served social needs, not
  problem-solving needs; he attended one mastermind, got what he needed, skipped it
  for 18 months while executing, and later became one of its success stories. [2022]
- Diagnostic question: do I need more *information* or more *execution*? Once you
  know what to do, anything other than doing it is in the way. [2022]

**Tactics he implemented (2022 account):**

- Changed his phone number: gave the old number to his executive assistant, kept a
  new one, and only reached out to people *he* chose to contact rather than being
  reactive to everyone who had his number. [2022]
- Stopped going to events/masterminds — he needed execution, not information. [2022]
- Took no meetings from 4:00 a.m. to noon, working the first ~8 hours completely
  uninterrupted on the priority only. [2022]
- Stopped treating weekends differently from weekdays — same bedtime and wake time
  every day so he could protect the 4-to-12 block. [2022]
- Worked out only about twice a week — his "minimum effective dose" just to
  maintain, not to progress on multiple fronts at once. [2022]
- Fasted one or two days a week (caffeine and bone broth only) to avoid time lost
  to eating, preparation, and cleaning — with fat loss as a side benefit. [2022]
- Deleted all social media from his phone; since he only needed to *post* (not
  consume), he did that from his computer, reclaiming the "three to five hours a
  day" most people lose to phone consumption ("the new age of watching
  television"). [2022]
- On leverage: early on (zero to ~$1M, up to ~$3–5M) you lack team leverage and
  must still do a lot of the doing, so leveraging your own time is what moves the
  needle; later a team of ~20 can execute more than he could alone. [2022]

## Topic notes

**Relation to the 2021 anti-routine video ([[2021-11-08-yt-ErWpi_91b70]]) —
refinement, not repetition.** Both videos attack the "what's your routine?"
question and both share the anti-dependence spine. But they argue on different
axes:

- The **2021** video is *anti-rigidity / anti-crutch*: routines and rituals
  (coffee, sleep aids, orange glasses) make you *fragile* and non-resilient; the
  argument is about identity ("I am" vs. "I have a tendency toward"), cycling
  inputs so novelty keeps conferring benefit, and being "source, not reaction."
  The enemy is *dependence*.
- This **2022** video is *anti-addition / pro-subtraction*: the enemy is *doing
  too many things*. It introduces named machinery the 2021 video lacks — the term
  **"anti-routine"** as an explicit concept, the **"season of no,"** the
  priorities axiom ("two things cannot be equally important"), the **implied
  take** in every communication, the information-vs-execution diagnostic, and the
  **minimum effective dose** framing for health.

So this is a **refinement/extension**: 2021 tells you routines can weaken you;
2022 supplies a concrete subtractive *method* (a catalog of things to stop — phone
number, events, meetings before noon, weekend/weekday parity, minimal workouts,
fasting, social media off the phone) unified under "no." Note also a partial
tension with the 2021 anti-rigidity stance: several 2022 tactics (fixed 4 a.m.–noon
block, identical bedtime/wake time every day) are themselves *consistent daily
structure*, which fits the reconciliation already noted on the 2021 page — he is
against dependence and clutter, not against low-cost decision-eliminating habits.

Also note the recurring **acquisition.com "$85 million a year"** figure appears
here (2022-02) as in the 2021-11 video; a synthesis pass tracking that number over
time should include this data point (here described as "about six portfolio
companies").
