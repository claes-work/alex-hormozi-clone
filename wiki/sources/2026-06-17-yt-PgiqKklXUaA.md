# Scaling a $15M Consulting Firm to $100M by 2029

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A in which the managing partner of Eclipse Consulting (a boutique
NYC data-management, risk, and regulatory-change advisory firm serving regulated,
data-intensive financial-services clients) describes his sales/lead-gen constraint and
Alex Hormozi prescribes a lead-generation system. The owner's firm peaked over $30M,
fell to ~$15M after the banking crisis hit concentrated clients (Credit Suisse, Silicon
Valley Bank), and targets $100M by 2029; operations, people, and finance are strong but
lead flow is the bottleneck, currently almost entirely founder-led warm outbound. Alex's
advice centers on a three-part enterprise acquisition engine — conferences, affiliate
partners, and cold outbound as the front end that feeds the other two — plus treating
affiliates as a "second customer base" that must be activated via customer success.

## Key claims

Alex-attributed (persona data — his advice only):
- For an enterprise/B2B firm like this, acquisition should be a combination of three
  things: **dominate trade shows and conferences** ("that's where all the fish are in one
  barrel"), **affiliate / center-of-influence partners** via joint-venture deals that
  share deal flow, and **cold outbound**.
- **Cold outbound is not the end goal — it is the front end.** You use cold outbound to
  land conference slots and to close affiliate/JV deals; the conferences and affiliates
  then become the primary, repeatable lead sources.
- Runs a **conference playbook** for all enterprise sales; a properly set-up event has
  "a bunch of different conversion points" you can squeeze for far more volume than
  simply attending.
- Case example (Sharran Srivatsaa / "Sharon", president of publicly-traded Real
  Brokerage): grew the company ~6x from $200M to $1.2B primarily by doing **220 speaking
  events a year**, staying two days after each to sell everyone who approached — cited as
  proof the "scalable" move can be brute-force volume of stage time.
- The acquisition cycle: outbound → events; events feed leads directly, surface potential
  affiliate partners (triaged on-site) for ongoing between-event lead gen, and produce
  direct deals.
- Adding "more zeros to the thing you sell" (higher price point) lets warm outbound go
  much further than it would for a low-ticket product like gym memberships.
- **Treat affiliates as a second customer base.** The common failure is not too few
  partners but **un-activated** partners; having "too many alliance partners" that don't
  refer is an activation problem.
- Affiliate structure lesson from his software company (ALAN): three tiers — SMBs, then
  agencies (who actually needed the software to serve SMBs), then agency influencers /
  "super affiliates" who could push 100–500 agencies with a single email — each requiring
  its own tier of service and leverage.
- **Customer success must be installed as a function on top of the affiliate base**, on a
  regular communication cadence, because you only make money when partners activate and
  actually use/refer.
- Reverse-engineer activation: quantify the demographics and behaviors of the partners who
  *do* send leads versus those who don't, then bake those differentiating actions into the
  regular activation cadence.
