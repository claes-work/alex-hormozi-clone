# The first product you must sell in a new business

## Summary
In this solo video Hormozi argues that most small businesses over-complicate their product line to serve every budget, creating operational drag and confusion, when the better path is to sell one dream solution and accommodate different budgets through payment terms rather than different products. He prescribes a product-count-by-stage rule (one thing from $0–1M, two things from ~$1–10M), explains why "one dream to sell" beats a low/medium/high ladder, and shares pricing hacks around monthly-cost framing and large up-front payments to extend contract value and increase stickiness. The unifying claim: sell one thing, then let payment options — not product options — do the accommodating.

## Key claims (2021)
- The goal is to maximize revenue per customer with an "accommodating buying curve": wealthier buyers spend more, lower-budget buyers still buy but for less.
- The common mistake is meeting that with many products/services; this creates operational complexity and distraction. If your own employees don't understand all your offerings and price points, your customers certainly don't.
- Instead, simplify the product offering and reach the maximum buying threshold per prospect by varying payment terms.
- Product count by stage: $0–1M = one product/one service, one avatar, one channel; going from ~$1–3M toward $10M = introduce a second product line (a front-end and a back-end thing).
- "Sell one dream" beats a low/medium/high (good/better/best) product ladder: with three products the salesperson can't decide what they're selling and defaults to indecision, so give them the single most valuable thing that solves the customer's problem.
- "A confused mind doesn't buy": too many options mean the salesperson won't know what to sell and the prospect won't know what to buy; the question should shift from "which product will you buy?" to "now that you're buying, how would you like to pay?"
- Money hack — buyers watch the monthly cash-flow expense, not the total contract value, because "no one is good with their money"; for the business owner the total contract value matters most.
- Stickiness hack — "the bigger the head, the longer the tail": the more someone pays up front relative to their recurring payment, the stickier they are (e.g. $5,000 down + $99/mo is far stickier than $99/mo alone).
- A large up-front payment lets you liquidate ad cost, cover sales commissions, and cover the higher up-front fulfillment/onboarding cost of a new customer.
- More payment options raise conversion rates ("proven"): offer e.g. pay-in-full, split-pay, 3 payments (~20% higher), or 6 payments over a six-month implementation. But more options may lower cash flow because reps default to the lower plans.
- If the offer is structured with very high gross margins, it barely matters whether a customer pays $1,000/mo or $100/mo — it's almost all margin either way, so you capture higher LTV by getting higher-budget buyers to prepay.
- Prepaying customers ascend into the second (back-end) product faster, letting you solve the next problem in a separate sales conversation instead of cramming everything into one.
- Scoping caveat: this is a broad-brush prescription for entrepreneurs below ~$3M/year whose operations are weak.
