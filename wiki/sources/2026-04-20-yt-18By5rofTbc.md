# Helping a $500K Financial Planning Firm Get to $5M

## Summary

Alex coaches a financial-planning firm serving young big-tech employees. He says the
constraint is likely acquisition, not service capacity, and recommends workshops as a
trust-building lead magnet. He also says the owner is blending personal-finance coaching
and wealth management while pricing too low for the monthly delivery load.

## Key claims

- Alex says financial-planning businesses are usually demand constrained rather than
  supply constrained.
- He recommends virtual or in-person workshops because a half day or two days can build
  more trust than a long time online.
- He says the firm is probably undercharging and doing too much by meeting clients
  monthly.
- He distinguishes personal finance coaching from wealth management and says the owner
  is delivering both while pricing like one lower-value offer.
- He says operators are usually either volume players with tech-enabled cost efficiency
  or premium providers; the middle is difficult to perceive as valuable.
- He says acquiring younger clients through personal finance could be a good ascension
  model, but the delivery and pricing should not be blended with private wealth
  management.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You're doing 12 times the work and billing less."

> "There's really not a lot of room in between."

> "I think you're undercharging."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: financial-planning offer split; workshops as trust lead magnet; premium vs volume positioning; personal finance front end to wealth-management ascension.
