# The Offer Structure a Stranger Pitched Me in a Parking Lot

## Summary

In this solo teaching clip from the Money Models material, Alex Hormozi explains the "discount plus one-time fee" offer structure. He traces the idea to a 2015 parking-lot pitch at his La Habra gym, where a personal-training manager proposed using Alex's facility, offering a discounted first month, and monetizing through an enrollment fee. Alex rejected the partnership but kept the structure: use a behavior-changing discount to attract customers while using a made-up one-time fee to liquidate acquisition costs, increase commitment, and reduce churn.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- "Discount plus one-time fee" combines a discounted first period with additional fees such as setup, onboarding, or enrollment fees.
- Discount front ends surprise fewer people than free front ends because the customer already expects to pay.
- The same discount can be framed multiple ways, such as percentage off, dollars off, or reduced first-month price.
- Alex prefers discounts of 50% or higher because a discount must change behavior; smaller discounts often just give away margin.
- If the owner does not want to discount the whole service, Alex suggests peeling off one component, discounting that heavily, and upselling the rest.
- One-time fees can liquidate commissions and acquisition costs while the discounted period lowers the barrier to entry.
- Higher one-time startup fees can reduce churn because the customer is more invested.
- When a customer must participate to succeed, charging an upfront fee can increase attention and compliance.
- A one-time fee needs a name, price, reason why, and a decision to charge, discount, or waive it.
- Alex distinguishes pre-set promotional discounts from haggling; he says never present a price and then lower it reactively.
