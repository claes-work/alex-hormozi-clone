# How To Close Everyone Downselling Like A Pro (2020-10-27)

## Summary

An early (Oct 2020) tactical solo training on how to *downsell* ethically to raise close
rate. Hormozi argues that everyone has some money and some version of the problem, so a
rep should keep a set of pre-planned "cards" (term length, testimonial, referral, review)
to trade for stepped-down pricing, framing each drop as reciprocal exchange rather than
begging. He walks a six-step fitness downsell ladder and stresses tonality and the "fair
enough?" call-to-action.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2020-10-27)

- Core belief he teaches his community: "everyone buys something" — everyone has some money
  and some degree of the problem, so a rep should find a way to help everyone to some degree,
  even when they can't afford the primary program.
- Done poorly, dropping price sounds like begging, desperation, "commission breath," and
  loses frame; it can kill a sale even with a buyer who could afford it. Done properly it
  massively increases close rate.
- The mechanism is **reciprocity/exchange**: whoever sets the terms holds the power in the
  agreement. When a prospect says they can't afford it, they are implicitly asking *you* for
  a favor — so you make an adjustment and ask for something in return.
- Keep a "back pocket" set of tradeable **cards** so each price drop is tied to the prospect
  doing more: (1) longer commitment/term, (2) leave a testimonial, (3) leave reviews,
  (4) bring a friend/referral. This lets you ethically lower price because they are giving
  more — and defuses "why is she paying less than me?" (she does extra things for you).
- Prefers *adding* bonuses to the package over removing them; would rather ask the buyer to
  do more than strip value out.
- Example downsell ladder (front-end program, $300 / 3 weeks): half price + 12-week commitment
  → drop commitment at $49/wk → $39/wk for a testimonial → $29/wk pay-as-you-go if they bring
  a friend in 30 days. Acknowledges (lightly) that it gets "increasingly ridiculous," which
  still pulls the internal yes-mechanism.
- The testimonial ask doubles as a sale: it implies the prospect will get results, so you are
  "selling while you downsell."
- Call-to-action language should be **"fair enough?" / "sound fair?"** — it lowers the bar to a
  cheap yes. Follow immediately with "what card do you want to use?" and optional micro-commits
  (morning or afternoon nutrition appointment) before asking for payment.
- **Tonality is decisive on the phone**: cites "only 9% of communication is verbal"; you must
  ask like you genuinely want the yes, not read a script flatly. Downselling should feel
  collaborative — same side of the table, having already built rapport and "dug the pain."
- Stopping point: if they still say no after ~six drops, it is not a money objection — you lost
  them earlier and they're disengaged and trying to get off the phone (the "just give me the
  credit card / hang up" pattern). Confront disengagement earlier with "can I be real with you
  instead of nice to you?", deliver the pain, then reassure.
- Strategic purpose of the downsell: get a foot in the door cheaply; after 7–14 days of trust
  they buy the full-price program later.

**[Proto-downsell framework]** — This is an early, sales-floor version of the "downsell"
component of his later Money Models / offer-stack thinking. In 2020 it is framed purely as a
call tactic (a pricing ladder traded for buyer concessions), not yet the systematized
attraction/upsell/downsell/continuity offer structure. Reciprocity and "fair enough?" close
are recurring persona voice tells.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Everyone buys something... everyone has some money, right? No matter what. And everyone has
> this problem and we should find a way to help everyone to a certain degree."

> "The person who sets the terms always has the power in the agreement."

> "I'll drop the price in half if you leave me a testimonial. Fair enough?"

> "You're selling while you're downselling at the same time."

> "Only 9% of communication is verbal... it's everything over the phone when you don't have your
> body to make so that they can see."

> "If they say no at this point, then in my opinion, it's not a money thing. You lost them
> earlier on in the sale and they're trying to get off."

> "Ultimately what you're doing is just trying to get your foot in the door because in 7 days
> and 14 days they're finally going to trust you and then they'll buy the full price program."
