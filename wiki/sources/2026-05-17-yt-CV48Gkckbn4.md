# How I Got 9 Months of 16-Hour Days Done

## Summary

In this short monologue, Alex Hormozi lays out his "12 by 30" challenge — working 12 hours a day for 30 days straight with no weekends — and the six lessons he says it teaches, chief among them that you have a harder "gear" in you when you need it. He argues that being "on" (on stage, on camera, meeting customers) is far more draining than working alone, and recounts a self-reported nine-month stretch early in his gym career of 15–16-hour days that produced "the kind of tired a good night's sleep couldn't fix." He frames enduring such seasons as how you acquire the trait of being relentless and the veteran's calm under pressure. The video closes with his standard acquisition.com/roadmap CTA.

## Key claims (Alex's content only)

- The **"12 by 30"** challenge: work 12 hours a day for 30 days straight, no weekends. He calls it not as unsustainable as people assume.
- By the last day you learn six lessons: (1) you aren't made of glass; (2) how much more you could be working; (3) how much faster you could be achieving your goals; (4) how much you could be getting better every day; (5) you don't need to work like this forever, but you now have that gear if a season or reason demands it; (6) there are people working more than you who are beating you.
- The payoff: getting more done in a month than you probably did last quarter.
- The point of the challenge is to **push through** — taking a couple days off defeats it.
- You can scale it up to "16 by 30" if you want; he describes himself as effectively **"12 by 100"** (six-to-six most days, with 15–16-hour days when events or dinners run late).
- There are **levels** to intensity: working alone all day is less exhausting than being on stage or on camera all day, because "every second that you are not peak, you are losing" and you can't take a break when on stage, meeting customers, or making content.
- Being "on" means people actively demanding things of you all the time, not just sitting at a computer. A team behind the camera is counting on him every second.
- Enduring a hard season teaches you that you **have that gear**, which gives "supreme confidence" entering any new endeavor because you've acquired the trait of being **relentless**.
- The difference between the newbie and the veteran: the veteran looks back on what they've endured and says "I've seen this before, this is another one of those" — which is why they don't feel the anxiety, stress, or elevated heartbeat in high-pressure situations.
- The only way to earn the ability to say "I've done it before" is to do it now.

## Biographical facts (dated)

- **Self-reported:** Early in his career, while running a gym, Alex says there was "a season of my life" of roughly **nine straight months** of 15–16-hour days. His described routine: wake at 4:00 am, get to the gym, open the doors at 4:30, start classes at 5:00; the last class ran until 8:00 pm, after which he did billing, reached out to old customers, and confirmed next-day lead appointments — "sometimes until 10:00, 11:00 at night" — then woke again at 4:00. (No specific calendar year is given in this source.)
- **Self-reported:** He describes the resulting exhaustion as "the kind of tired a good night's sleep couldn't fix," saying he needed "weeks to basically come back to normal."
- **Self-reported (current routine):** He describes his default as roughly six-to-six, with frequent 15–16-hour days when events or dinners extend the evening past 9:00.
