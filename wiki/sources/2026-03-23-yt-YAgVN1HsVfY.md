# Are You Shadowbanned or Just Making Bad Content?

## Summary
In this coaching exchange, Alex Hormozi advises a caller named Jonathan, a solo operator who sells dating services to single men via YouTube live streams and whose views and sales have declined sharply (the caller attributes this to being "shadow banned"). Hormozi reframes the problem: either the content got worse/less relevant, or the environment/algorithm changed, and he urges focusing on the controllables rather than an uncontrollable shadow-ban theory. He recommends diversifying beyond live streams (clipping streams into shorts, doubling down on the caller's un-monetized Instagram, adding webinars/phone calls, pitching more often) and improving content packaging using research tools. Only the caller's statements are attributed to the caller; the recommendations below are Hormozi's.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that when metrics decline, at the simplest level one of two things changed: either you unknowingly changed something (content is no longer as good or compelling), or the environment changed (content sounds tone-deaf now, or the algorithm changed how it distributes content).
- As of March 2026, Alex prefers to diagnose declines as a content problem rather than a shadow-ban, because a content problem is "super fixable" while shadow banning is an uncontrollable.
- As of March 2026, Alex says short-form distribution has been "largely democratized" and is now driven far more by an interest graph than by followership, so a brand-new account with zero followers can post a clip that gets millions of views.
- As of March 2026, Alex says that because of this democratization, being shadow banned is "not nearly the death sentence that it used to be" and that a plausible fix is simply to start a new account.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises not to rely on a single distribution "trick"; when a tactic stops working, keep doing it enough to keep the lights on but move into "hyperactive mode" to find a new way to promote.
- As of March 2026, Alex calls Instagram the most monetizable platform of all because you can directly DM every follower/customer; he recommends a ManyChat integration to DM new followers and repliers and route them to live broadcasts.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends going live on Instagram simultaneously with YouTube (using a second phone if needed) and using Instagram's broadcast/add-to-broadcast feature to notify followers.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends the content research tool oneof10.com, describing it as a repository that surfaces the highest-performing headline packaging and thumbnails across YouTube channels for a given topic, to ideate adaptations for one's own niche.
- As of March 2026, Alex interprets a channel going from ~300k to ~100k views as a platform-wide shift over time (YouTube changing) plus content becoming less relevant, not shadow banning; he notes red-pill/manosphere topics are less in demand than before.
- As of March 2026, Alex suggests pitching more frequently than once every 12 weeks and adding higher-friction offers (phone calls, webinars) as additional monetization levers.
- Caller claims (unverified): Jonathan says revenue ranges ~$250k–$900k and he wants $1.2M to net ~$100k/month; his best launch was ~$250k in 5 days at ~85–90% profit; his shorts fell from 15 million views per clip to sometimes 800–1,000; he has 58,000 Instagram followers, does not monetize Instagram, pitches once a quarter, and has a $10k high-ticket immersion offer.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "At the simplest level, one of two things changed. Either you changed something unknowingly, which is the content is not as good, or it's not as compelling — or something else changed, which is either the environment changed... or the algorithm changed in terms of how it's pushing out lives."

So somebody who has a brand new account that has zero followers can make a clip that gets 10 million views.
