# Advice For Anyone Who Feels Stuck

## Summary

A Skool community Q&A/mastermind clip in which Alex Hormozi answers how to stop feeling
stuck and overwhelmed by the endless churn of new tools and tactics (learn Claude Code?
crypto? NFTs?). His core diagnosis: "scattered braininess" comes from fear — fear of being
left behind, of becoming irrelevant. His antidote is trust in a simple, stable model:
gather attention, point it somewhere, make a compelling offer, deliver on the promise. For
most operators (especially under ~$1M), the only real variable is the input — advertising
(content + paid, now merged) — because conversion and delivery are largely fixed/automated.
He prescribes a "thirds" workday (promote / build / deliver), defines focus as doing one
thing, and preaches "repeat successful actions" — illustrated by giving the exact same
book-launch presentation on day two and doing $25M in sales. He contrasts first-party data
(from actually doing the thing) with third-party data (other people cheering what they
think worked), argues strategy is the partition of limited resources, and — with a likely
second speaker (Skool founder Sam Ovens) — discusses "cowboy testing," how hard it is to
beat a control in an A/B test, and how leverage is far bigger early in the funnel (creative
and traffic) than late (about-page conversion).

## Key claims

_All paraphrased from 2026-02-21; Alex-attributed unless noted. See Notes on attribution._

- Feeling stuck / "scattered braininess" comes from a place of fear — fear of being left
  behind, of your stuff no longer being relevant. (Alex)
- Trust as the antidote: believe that when something is significant enough to actually
  affect your business, you'll change — until then, keep doing what you know works. (Alex)
- The whole business reduces to four moves: gather attention → point it in a direction →
  make a compelling offer → deliver on the promise. (Alex)
- For most operators below ~$1M, what makes money is only: letting people know about the
  stuff, delivering the stuff, and making the stuff you deliver. (Alex)
- Conversion is usually automated (an about page) or a fixed, curated performance (a sales
  call/webinar you don't need to keep remaking once it converts); delivery is usually
  leveraged. So the only part of the system that actually needs volume is the input —
  advertising. (Alex)
- Content and paid have merged, so "it's just content" now — find the good content, then
  put money behind it so more people see it. (Alex)
- Time allocation as thirds: e.g. of a 12-hour day, first 4 hours promoting, next 4 hours
  building the stuff, last 4 hours delivering — set a timer and stick to the block. ★
- Focus = doing one thing; anything that isn't that thing is, by definition, less focused
  (the most focused video-game player would only play, never sleep or eat). Anything that
  isn't the one thing is a distraction. (Alex)
- "Repeat successful actions": once something starts working, do not stop doing it — and
  especially don't stop out of fear it will stop working. If it keeps working, keep doing
  it. (Alex)
- Book-launch example: on day two he gave the exact same presentation (against advice to
  do a different "re-pitch"), because success is about asking again — and did ~$25M in
  sales on day two, to many of the same people who simply watched it again (we watch reruns
  of shows we already know the ending to). ★
- The right question is not "what's a new business model this week?" but, given a working
  input→output equation, "how can I do 100× the inputs?" — via work ethic plus leverage
  (systems that put one piece of content in ten places). (Alex)
- First-party data (from actually doing it) is far more accurate and relevant than
  third-party data (other people cheering what they think worked for them). (Alex)
- The more first-party data you have, the stronger the signal and the smaller the noise —
  you can tune out almost everything, because anything that truly matters will show up in
  your first-party data. ★
- When your conversion rate (or other metric) is the problem, one consumed video gives you
  ~six things to try; there's no point consuming more until you've actually tried them.
  (Alex)
- Leila's reminder: "don't change things when a solution is on the way" — don't abandon a
  live split test mid-flight (change mid-flight and "everyone dies"); some things just need
  a certain number of repetitions before the data is trustworthy. (Alex, quoting Leila)
- Strategy is the partition of resources; the core of entrepreneurship is being able to
  choose — of a hundred possible actions with limited time/money/energy, which has the
  highest likelihood of the biggest outcome. Most people don't even have enough data for
  that choice to be meaningful yet. (Alex)
- "Cowboy testing": rather than always running formal A/B tests, just change the thing and
  watch whether the number goes up or down; only set up a proper A/B test when the change
  is very sensitive. (attribution uncertain — likely Sam Ovens / Skool; see Notes)
- Beating a control is genuinely hard: e.g. 16 A/B tests on the Skool games, 14 lost; on a
  high-traffic sign-up page, most attempts made it worse. (attribution uncertain — Skool
  internal)
- Leverage is bigger earlier in the funnel: taking ad CTR from 1% to 2% doubles the whole
  business, whereas you're unlikely to double a page's conversion rate (maybe a 10–20%
  lift). A great creative can stack double/triple CTR, double/triple conversion, and ~1/6
  the CPM into an ~18× improvement — the biggest levers are creative volume at the front
  and price at the very end. (attribution uncertain — see Notes)
- Most businesses don't have an about-page conversion problem; they mostly need more
  traffic or a fixed churn problem — though a genuinely non-converting page is possible.
  (Alex/shared)
- Audience coaching moment: a member's tiered offer ($9/mo, $77/yr premium, $249/yr VIP)
  saw premium conversion drop from ~20% to ~5% when the third VIP tier was added; advice
  was to trust his own first-party data over the general "high anchor works" heuristic and
  revert to two tiers. (Alex/host to audience member)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The scattered braininess comes from a place of fear. Is, I'm afraid I'm going to be left behind. I'm afraid my stuff's not going to be relevant."

> "I trust that when something is significant enough that it actually affects my business, I will change. Until then I will continue to do what I know works, which is that I will gather attention, I will point it in a direction, I'll make a compelling offer, and then I'll deliver on that promise."

> "Focus is doing one thing, which means that anything that's not that thing is less focused... As soon as they introduce any activity that's not playing video games, they become a less focused video game player."

> "You will do something and as soon as it begins to work, do not stop doing that thing until it stops working. Do not stop doing that thing because you fear it will stop working."

> "I did the exact same presentation day two and we had did like 25 million dollars in sales day two. Same presentation... They just watched it again. We watch reruns of shows that we already know happened."

> "The more first-party data you have from doing it — that's obviously so much more accurate and relevant to you than third-party data, which is other people cheering what they think worked for them."

> "The core of entrepreneurship is being able to choose. I have these very limited resources in time, money, energy, and there's a hundred things I can do, but which of these hundred things, if I were to implement, has the highest likelihood of yielding the biggest outcome?"
