# Why I chose to disappoint my dad

## Summary

In this interview clip Alex Hormozi recounts the pivotal early-20s decision to
quit a prestigious management-consulting career and disappoint his immigrant
father in order to pursue his own path (ultimately fitness/gyms in California).
He frames the choice through his recurring mental model — using mortality and a
"die to myself or die to my father" calculus as a decision-making tool — and
digresses into his skepticism about legacy, his preference for "joy" over
"happiness," and the idea that most perceived rules and expectations are
self-imposed and can be relinquished. Much of the value is biographical: father
relationship, family origin in Iran, and the timeline of leaving consulting.

## Key claims

- Mortality is the single biggest driver behind his biggest life decisions; he
  reuses it as a repeatable "decision-making algorithm" because it provides
  clarity and shows most decisions don't matter. (self-reported)
- Subjective well-being over a lifespan looks like a smiley face: high at ~7,
  drops sharply between 20–30, bottoms around 45, then rises again from ~55–60
  and spikes near the end of life as people stop worrying about others'
  judgment. (his paraphrase of research he cites)
- His early drive was fear-based — fear of failure, disappointment, and other
  people's judgment / seeking his father's approval and validation.
- Legacy is largely irrelevant on a long time horizon: he argues you can't even
  name your own distant ancestors, and revolutions/governments can erase
  material legacy overnight (as happened in Iran).
- Advice from parents/elders should be weighed against the context (time, place,
  lifestyle) in which it was given; good advice can be simply "out of context"
  for a new life rather than wrong.
- It is easier to improve life by inversion — identify what you hate and reverse
  it — than to first answer what brings you joy.
- He prefers the word "joy" over "happy": joy is internal (you can "mourn
  joyfully"), happiness is external/happenstance.

## Biography facts (dated, self-reported)

All from this 2022-06-23 clip; all self-reported by Alex unless noted.

- **Family structure:** Only child, raised by a single father.
- **Father's origin:** Father was born in Iran, a Middle Eastern immigrant to the
  US.
- **Father's immigration:** Came to the US with about $1,000 and a medical
  degree, and did not speak English; built everything from there. Alex says
  "what he did was harder than what I've done."
- **Father's motivation for the pressure:** Education "saved his life" — he was
  only able to leave the country because he was educated; roots of his emphasis
  on credentials run deep.
- **Distant ancestor:** His great-great-grandfather was in the ruling class in
  Iran, had ~400 children (multiple wives) and great wealth; family land,
  buildings, houses and bank accounts were seized by the government after the
  revolution in Iran. Alex says he can't even remember the ancestor's name.
- **Early career:** After college he was a management consultant; did "space
  cyber intelligence" work for the military with a top-secret clearance (which he
  downplays as sounding better than it was). Graduated in three years; was
  president of "all the clubs"; got "the good job" — a career his father wanted /
  had helped set up.
- **The turning point (~early 20s):** He faltered for ~6 months, not wanting that
  life but not wanting to let his father down; at his lowest he was contemplating
  not living. Resolved it by deciding he would rather "die to" his father than to
  himself.
- **The break:** After ~2 years in consulting he quit at age **23**. He estimated
  the two years / degree were worth ~$200,000 economic equivalent and bet he'd
  learn more starting on his own. Sold everything, packed his car, drove to
  California ("the land of fitness opportunity" — fitness being the only thing he
  truly enjoyed), mentored under a guy for a few months, and started his first
  facility, sleeping on the floor for the first nine months.
- **Father's reaction:** Father thought he was stupid, wasting his life, wasting a
  degree he'd paid for and a job he'd set up.
- **Reconciliation:** Later they "came to terms." The only time his father ever
  apologized to him, the father said: "to be fair, in my time I would have been
  right." Alex agrees — the advice wasn't inappropriate, just "out of context"
  for a different time.
