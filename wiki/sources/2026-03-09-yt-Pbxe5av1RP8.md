# Just Get the First Dollar and You'll Win

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host opens by asking how important it is to have "that moment with the check" — the first dollar earned; everything after is Alex). Alex says the first dollar is "in some ways everything and in other ways nothing." Everything, because the first dollar is by far the hardest — "the second dollar comes so much faster after that" — and it's why he invests in Skool, citing that 30.44% of people who finish their first month of Skool make their first dollar online. But nothing, in the sense that it's the goal, not a prerequisite to start: he reframes progress as **experience points** with three levels *before* the first dollar — (1) accept "it's my fault," (2) "use what you have, not what you wish you had," (3) aim only at the next step, not 100 steps forward. The back half is a mindset argument against the "positivity mantra": Alex says had he waited to feel happy or "follow his passion," he never would have started — his passion was simply "to not be poor" and "not be here in Baltimore." He defends winning from negative emotion (anger, sadness, shame), quoting a mentor: "Money does not buy happiness, but it can help you avoid pain." He describes long identifying with the villain and not believing he deserved the "happy ending," and the reframe that rescued him — you can still get "the good guy's ending" if you just do the things the good guys do, with impure intentions. Hence his "ruthless focus" on action over emotion: "Throw the emotions out… What do you do?" He closes on future-pacing pain as a tool — Tony Robbins' "change happens when the pain of change is less than the pain of staying the same" — using a vivid weight-gain example (imagining the snide holiday-party comments) to make staying the same more painful than moving. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
