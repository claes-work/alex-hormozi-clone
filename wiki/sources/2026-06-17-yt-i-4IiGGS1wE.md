# How to Scale Two Companies to $100M

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a ~$11M/year commercial construction company (18% profit) who has also launched a fast-growing elevator company that did $3M in its first year at 30% margin. The owner, splitting his attention between the two, asks how to go from $10M to $100M. Alex reframes it as a pure opportunity-cost decision: the elevator business is the orders-of-magnitude-better opportunity, so the "unreasonable" but correct move is to fire-sell the construction firm fast — even below its worth — to free up full attention and compound the recurring-revenue business far faster than the sale price is worth.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The situation is a sunk-cost / opportunity-cost problem — you can't un-start the second company ("you can't like not have the kid, it's already out there running around"), so decide purely on where future time is best spent.
- The reasonable advice is to hire operators and let the construction firm keep running without you (it might take ~$800K in construction profit to buy back the two or three leaders needed to run it absentee).
- The elevator company is the better opportunity: higher margin (30% vs 18%), it's recurring revenue, super-niched, the owner already has the domain "ins," and it grew faster while getting only partial attention.
- The "quote-Alex" (unreasonable) advice: **burn it down** — exit the construction company now for far less than you think it's worth, take the headache off, and pour full attention into the elevator business.
- He evaluates almost exclusively on opportunity cost: the time spent winding down or dressing up the old business for sale is worth more deployed into the new one — he can rebuild the equivalent enterprise value in recurring revenue in a single quarter.
- Split attention caps growth: the owner did $3M split; with full attention he might have been at $8M.
- Precedent from his own life: he fire-sold six gyms in 90 days, made in total on all six what he "should have made on one," and within six months was doing $1M/month on the next thing.
- Social proof/pain warning: everyone (spouse, family) will call you crazy when you burn a business down — but the people who've told him he was crazy are none of them wealthier than he is; no one wealthier than him ever told him that.
- Reframe the sunk effort as not wasted: the skills learned building the old business are transferable — "I was the asset."
