# Helping a Landscaping Business Scale

## Summary
Live consulting clip. The client owns a landscape design/construction company in the Florida panhandle selling mostly to homeowners (~$2.2M revenue / ~$400k net this year, up from ~$1.5M / ~$300k; one main crew of seven plus ~five subs; ~$300k cash on hand). His goal is a $50M+ enterprise-value landscape/construction firm with multiple divisions, and he asks where to place his next big capital bet for the highest compounding return. Alex first reframes the math: at a ~6–6.5x multiple, $50M enterprise value needs ~$8M EBITDA, so he must roughly **20x** the business (to ~$44M topline). Then he pivots to the short-term principle he repeats throughout: **capital and effort always go to the constraint**, because that's where the return is highest — so the real question is "why can't you do more of what you're already doing?" The client diagnoses himself as stuck "in the swamp" ($1–3M range, hands in everything, putting out all the fires); he says if all he had to do was market and sell he could hit $10M+, but can only spare ~20 hrs/week for consultations. Alex diagnoses two missing **VSLs** (video sales letters) — one between booking and the first appointment, one between the first and second appointment — as likely the single biggest lever on his 30% close rate, potentially lifting it to 50–60% and enabling a 25–35% price increase that more than doubles profit. His two-step plan: (1) build both VSLs (a day's work, pure upside), and (2) immediately recruit a **crew leader** (~$60k) to support the one strong operator (a $75k lead already spread thin), debottlenecking talent so the owner reclaims ~20 hrs/week to sell. On capital allocation, Alex tells him to hold the cash for trucks/equipment (the next constraint after talent) — allocate the $400k as ~$340k kept / ~$60k to talent. On enterprise value, Alex endorses adding **recurring revenue** (maintenance/mowing) but only *after* getting out of the weeds: the high-ticket install ($75k backyard redo) acquires customers profitably and price-anchors cheap ongoing maintenance (~$800/month), and the installing crew already knows the property (pipes, hoses) so continuity is natural and sticky. On lead sources he says there's no single best one — every channel is a different sales motion; Google inbound (intention-based) should convert ~30%, Meta ads (interruption/invasive, colder) maybe ~10% — and content plus paid ads is "an and, not an or." Ends with the acquisition.com 10-stage roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the goal in reverse from enterprise value: at ~6–6.5x EBITDA, a $50M enterprise value needs ~$8M EBITDA, implying the client must roughly 20x (to ~$44M topline) from ~$400k net today.
- As of March 2026, the best short-term allocation of resources is always the constraint, because that is where the highest return lives; the operative question is "why can't you do more of what you're currently doing — what stops you?"
- As of March 2026, an owner stuck "in the swamp" ($1–3M, hands in everything, putting out all the fires) is bottlenecked by his own time, not by demand.
- As of March 2026, two missing VSLs — one between appointment-set and first appointment, one between first and second appointment — may be the single largest lever on close rate; adding them to an already correctly-priced offer could lift a 30% close rate to 50–60%.
- As of March 2026, once close rate rises via the correct sales motion, the client could raise prices ~25–35%, which more than doubles profit and frees cash to hire the needed crew leader.
- As of March 2026, hiring a crew leader (~$60k) to support the one strong operator (a $75k lead, spread thin across the main crew, small projects, and subs) is easier than replacing the main person and debottlenecks the talent constraint, returning ~20 hrs/week to the owner for marketing and sales.
- As of March 2026, capital allocation follows the constraint chain: talent now (~$60k), then trucks/equipment/mowers next (~$150k), then talent again — so hold the cash reserve for the equipment need that is coming.
- As of March 2026, adding recurring revenue (maintenance/mowing) meaningfully raises enterprise value and should be done, but only after the owner is out of the weeds.
- As of March 2026, the winning model for home-service: the high-ticket one-time install acquires customers profitably and serves as a price anchor for cheap ongoing maintenance/continuity — e.g. a $75k backyard redo anchors ~$800/month upkeep, and the same crew already knows the property (pipes, hoses), making continuity natural and sticky.
- As of March 2026, there is no single "most effective" lead source — every channel is a different sales motion; Google search inbound (intention-based) should convert at least ~30%, while Meta/Facebook ads (interruption, invasive, colder) might convert ~10%.
- As of March 2026, organic content and paid ads are complementary — "an and, not an or"; don't stop the content that is already gaining branding traction.
