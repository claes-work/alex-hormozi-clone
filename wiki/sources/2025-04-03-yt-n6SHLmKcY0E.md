# Building a $3,000,000 Business for a Stranger in 57 Mins

## Summary

Live-build/coaching episode ("Cash Guys" format) where Alex Hormozi diagnoses and rebuilds
the funnel, offer, and sales process for Ben, co-founder of Unfiltered Hospitality — a
cocktail/beverage-program consultancy ($1.1M revenue, $123K profit in 2024) that sells a
$30,000 consulting package cold and struggles with feast-or-famine lead flow. Alex's core
prescription: stop selling a $30K service cold off a bare call funnel; instead add a lead
magnet, call out the exact avatar in ads, lead with pain, and insert a cheap ($5,800) "pricing
booster" front-end offer that ROIs immediately, then upsell the customer into the $30K program
and a quarterly retainer via a rollover credit / waved-fee structure. Only Alex's advice is
persona data; Ben's business specifics are context.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **The right offer at the wrong time is still the wrong offer.** A $30K service sold cold to
  strangers on a bare call funnel is a very hard sale; premium pricing for premium service is
  fine, the problem is *how* and *when* it's sold.
- **Lower the bar with a cheap front-end offer to convert prospect → customer fast.** Build a
  front-end product (~$5–6K) that solves one specific problem well and delivers a large ROI
  (e.g. a $5K thing that makes them $50K), purpose-built for a one-call close instead of
  arm-wrestling people through three calls to a $30K sale.
- **Pick the front-end that is fast, easy, and immediately profitable.** For Ben, a "pricing
  audit / pricing booster" beats DIY or sales training because it's easy to deliver (they just
  reprint menus) and shows value immediately — "you'd have made an extra $100K last year."
- **Cold ads need a lead magnet.** Going low-trust straight to a sale (leads book, p.30
  mismatch) leaves money on the table; a valuable lead magnet moves people low-trust → high-trust
  first. The three lead-magnet types: reveal a problem, give a sample of the solution, or give
  one step of a multi-step process.
- **Call out the avatar in the first line of the ad** — e.g. "$4M+ per year restaurant owners."
  Once the right person self-identifies ("this is for me"), ~80% of advertising's job is done.
- **Lead with pain, not features.** The more precisely you articulate a prospect's pain — more
  accurately than they can articulate it themselves — the more they believe you can help them,
  better than any promise. Pain is also more ad-compliant.
- **Use the value equation and the who/what/when framework** to generate copy variations across
  dream outcome (fast, likely, easy) and nightmare/pain angles; write to varied "histories of
  reinforcement" (some avatars want profit, others want to be cool / get recognition).
- **Sell in "moments," not abstract features.** Describe the concrete moment the customer
  experiences the benefit (or the relief of a pain) rather than a process word like "proven
  results" that requires mental effort to unpack.
- **Above the fold is the most valuable real estate.** Remove the logo/brand on cold traffic —
  they don't know or care who you are. Headline = clear reason to move forward; sub-headline
  handles the single biggest concern ("without X, Y, Z"); everything below the fold only exists
  to give skeptics more relevant reasons to proceed.
- **Marketing is finding the sweet spot on friction.** Bad friction (repeat opt-ins, slow loads)
  loses good and bad leads alike; good friction (qualification) filters. "The bigger the plane,
  the longer the runway" — pricier products generally need more friction.
- **Business owners should walk through their own funnel regularly.** A classic mistake is
  setting it up once and never clicking through it; make a habit of testing it.
- **Front-load the sales pitch into a video (VSL) before the human call.** The perfect pitch
  delivered every time in automated format; the live call is then just customization to a
  decision. Gate the booking behind the video so prospects actually watch it.
- **Front-end/back-end split is mostly about pulling cash forward** — shortening the payback
  period so you recoup ad + labor spend fast, then everything after is gravy. Whether you need
  it "depends" on how much info a buyer needs and on cash-flow constraints.
- **Ascension via rollover credit + waved-fee offer.** Seed the upsell up front, credit the
  $5,800 toward the annual/quarterly back-end. Prefer spreading the discount across quarters
  (e.g. knock $1,500/quarter) over a big first-payment discount, to get long-term commitment
  rather than an easy yes that churns. Wave-fee: "I charge $30K for this; commit for the year
  and I'll wave it — cancel early and you pay it" (turns the close into an A/B choice).
- **Fix churn before pouring more into the top of the funnel** — "why pour more into a leaky
  bucket." Ben's retainer churns after two quarters because value isn't being demonstrated
  (unfilled scorecards); fix: get a login to the client's system and do a monthly numbers review
  so "this guy knows all my stuff."
- **Not everything should be recurring.** Education/consulting naturally "graduates" people;
  one-time-service businesses can make a lot of money, they just have less enterprise value.
  Either engineer stickiness (consumables, always-changing needs like new-staff training) or
  accept it and price appropriately.
- **Founders should sell in the beginning.** They're the most convicted, outperform salespeople,
  keep you close to the customer for rapid feedback, and remove the salesperson variable; the
  saved commission frees cash flow. Ben should take calls until he literally can't — by then
  he's blown past his revenue target.
- **Test ad spend at 2× allowable CAC per campaign.** Budget 2× the cash collected from a
  customer in 30 days when testing new ads; stomach early inefficiency to find the winning
  hooks/creative, then double down and scale. Ben was spending too *little* to move $30K tickets.
- **Never ask for money you haven't already made them.** Make the customer $100K first, then the
  next offer costs a fraction of what you already made them — "I've already made room for you to
  afford me."
- **Ask for the upsell at milestone moments**, within the value window: right after the first
  post-change result, again the next week, and a last-chance ask — three shots at the sale.
