# No BS Advice to Get Rich Like the 1%

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi (net worth crossed $100M at age 31, now 35)
distills recurring themes he has observed among billion-dollar-plus operators. The
through-line is a set of pairings: rich people use money to buy time; ambitious people
use time to buy skills; poor people buy stuff; lazy people buy distractions. He argues
that success comes down to focus (saying no to everything but one thing), extreme work
volume (his "12x30" prescription), collapsing the gap between decision and action,
paying for education as an investment, and accepting trade-offs / hard work as the
price of an exceptional life. Ends with a School (skool.com/games) promotion.

## Key claims

- Four pairings frame the whole talk: **rich buy time, poor buy stuff; ambitious buy
  skills, lazy buy distractions.** Generational wealth transfers through education and
  skills, not assets.
- Cites a **Sanskrit proverb**: "If you have a good son you don't need to accumulate
  wealth; if you have a bad son there's no point in accumulating wealth."
- Lazy people distract themselves to avoid the pain of admitting they're inadequate;
  entitlement kills gratitude, and gratitude is what raises subjective well-being.
- You can be **ambitious and distracted, which keeps you poor.** At his peak he ran
  nine businesses (chiropractor agency, dental agency, gym-turnaround business, six gym
  locations) because he'd heard "seven income streams make you wealthy" — but he was
  still the rainmaker in all nine, so spreading himself thin was a mistake.
- **Focus is measured by the number of things you say no to.** The most focused person
  says yes to only one thing.
- **"It's 100x not 2X."** Growth comes from an order-of-magnitude increase in volume,
  which builds skill, which increases leverage. Example: a friend grew a company from
  ~$200M to ~$1.2B market cap in under two years by doing 66 in-person events a quarter.
  He himself makes ~450 content pieces a week versus a billionaire friend's 7.
- Defines **work = volume x leverage.** More reps build skill; skill raises leverage per
  rep.
- **12x30**: work 12 hours a day for 30 straight days, no weekends. Lessons: you aren't
  made of glass, you could work more/faster, you have that gear for a future season.
  He personally runs "12x100" and often does 15-16 hour days. Recalls a 9-month gym
  stretch (up at 4am, gym doors 4:30, billing/outreach until 10-11pm) — "the kind of
  tired a good night's sleep couldn't fix."
- Being "on" (stage, camera, customers) is more draining than working alone because you
  can't take a break — "every minute that is not recorded is basically a minute wasted."
- **You get better at working by working more.** Cites a French internship at "ARA"
  where a legal partner told him "you don't even know what work is." Productivity is a
  skill built by reps.
- **Focus is a muscle**: pushing past the urge to stop (like a 20-minute diet craving)
  builds the ability to "whip yourself back in." First 100 days of the year he took no
  days off.
- Rejects "do what you love and you'll never work a day." His saying: **"I will do what
  is required; how I feel about it is relatively irrelevant."** Desire, not passion, gets
  you through hard times. Work is **labor** — painful, like giving birth.
- **Work begins once motivation stops** (bodybuilding "working reps begin at failure").
  Cites Muhammad Ali: "I hated every minute of training but I would tell myself, suffer
  now and live the rest of your life as a champion." The "champion wall" is where most
  people quit and thus don't win.
- **"They cannot copy your proof."** People can copy your promises, services, logo,
  name — not what you've done. Advises young creators to **document, not teach**.
  Business titans (Musk, Bezos, Jobs, Gates, Buffett, Jensen Huang) built big businesses
  first; the personal brand followed. **"The proof comes before the pudding."** Trust is
  the scarce commodity; index hard on trust. MrBeast's early videos worked as proof of
  doing what others wouldn't.
- **Overnight success is a perception illusion**: the public sees the viral output
  moment, not the years of input. People model the plateau (private jets, getting weird
  after getting rich), not the rise. "You don't get rich by flying on private jets; you
  get rich, then you can fly on private jets."
- **Act with urgency — shrink the gap between thought and action.** The "god figure"
  extreme: thought and reality are one. Tactical hack: ask "can I get it done by end of
  today?" instead of end of week — a ~7x increase in pace. Move month→week→day→hour.
  Leila's rule (from a mentor): "if it's a 5-minute task and you have 5 minutes, do it
  now." The word *decide* comes from Latin *decidere*, to cut off — decide by eliminating
  alternatives.
- **Tomorrow isn't guaranteed.** Only 3 in 10,000 live to 100; median male US lifespan
  is ~74, so at 37 you're at the halfway point. Adjusting for perceived acceleration of
  time (a year is 20% of a 5-year-old's life vs 2.5% at 40, ~9x faster), the subjective
  midpoint is closer to 21.
- **Hard is for now, not forever.** Three outcomes to a hard time: you quit, it gets
  easier, or you get harder — two of three improve, so you only lose by quitting. Problems
  that stressed you 5 years ago are trivial now: you got better, so nothing is inherently
  hard. Hard times become "the good old days" in reverse.
- Growth = pain. "They're called growing pains, not growing joys." Alternatives to
  growth stress are stagnation stress and decline stress — stress is a fact of life;
  thinking that having problems is itself a problem is the biggest stressor.
- **The blacksmith/steel parable** (creator-of-the-universe dialogue): a good life isn't
  an easy life; it's one that makes you into a good person, which is a hard life. "We are
  both the steel and the blacksmith." His life thesis (crediting Jesse Itzler): **hard
  work is the goal — nothing left in the tank, emptying the tank every day is the point.**
  After selling Gym Launch he took ~a year off and was miserable because work was the
  most reinforcing thing in his life.
- **"Exceptional" = the exception**: to be exceptional you can't expect to be accepted;
  if everyone agrees with you, it's the wrong path. When he quit a Vanderbilt-track
  consulting job to be a minimum-wage personal trainer, everyone told him it was a bad
  idea. **Listen to people closest to your goals, not people closest to you.** Your real
  **reference group** is whoever's voices influence your decisions when you make a big move.
- **Outbound case study (Gym Launch)**: ~70%+ of customers came from paid ads; he built
  outbound as a second acquisition channel to de-risk before selling (avoid "one-itis").
  A book said it'd take a year; he thought 12 weeks; it took 12 months. 90 days to first
  sale, an executive "intervention" calling it a shiny object, then it grew to 50% of
  revenue. Wall Street saying: **"It happens slower than you expect, then faster than you
  can imagine."**
- **Masters have more ways to win**: expertise = the number of ways/times you can measure
  progress and win; beginners only see the final output and feel nothing is happening.
- **Education is an investment, not an expense**: "the only thing more expensive than
  education is not being educated." He paid **$350,000 for a dinner with a man worth $7B**
  (bid up from 100k→200k→250k→350k). The value of education = the gap between where you
  are and where you want to go; spend $50k to learn a $1M/yr skill and every year you
  delay costs $950k. Use investor language, not poor-people language — you are the asset.
- **Learning process**: (1) do everything they tell you to the tee the first time,
  (2) take what works, toss the rest. "Only take in that which makes you stronger" (Sword
  of Gryffindor analogy). You learn as much from bad examples (what not to do). Best
  students never come out negative.
- **The bridge/bricks model**: education gives you bricks (foundations); a program
  "failing" often means you were missing a brick someone else already had. Fault only
  matters in the eyes of the law; only the outcome, which is under your control, matters.
- Rejects the "it's not fair / it's easy for you, you're a white guy" excuse: **fairness
  doesn't exist in the universe.** You can't control your starting point, only your rate
  of progress. **Education = a change in behavior; intelligence = rate of learning.** The
  NFL wide-receiver-vs-fan race analogy: starting position matters less than speed.
- **Trade-offs / price tags**: "privacy is the price of fame, loneliness is the price of
  ambition, pleasure is the price of discipline, novelty is the price of loyalty,
  discretion is the price of trust." People want the benefit but won't pay the price —
  and the ideals are matters of degree, not binary.
- Most people do "10/10 work for a $100k goal" while their goal needs 10x that — a 1/10
  effort for a $1M goal. **The pipeline/valves model**: outcomes don't occur until every
  condition is met; three of four valves open still yields no water.
- **Four levels of trade-off understanding** (Nike-shoes metaphor): (0) think you should
  get it for free / steal it, (1) willing to pay but bid far too low ($30 for $300 shoes),
  (2) bidding — keep raising until sufficient ("it takes what it takes," a Leila-and-Alex
  internal saying), (3) **source** — you set the price/standard yourself. At the source
  level, standards become internal; meeting the market's standard actually lowers your own.
  "The person who has the highest standards is the one who should be making the decision"
  — not the most senior or the largest shareholder.
- **Standards / mastery**: Tolkien worked on The Hobbit for a decade and didn't submit it
  (a friend grabbed it off his desk). Because excellence takes so long, "you can only do
  three to four things in your whole life." Steve Jobs / Henry Ford: customers would have
  asked for faster horses, not cars.
- **Iteration**: "it's 19 drafts not two; it's 500 minutes of editing not 50; it's a
  thousand phone calls a week for two years not two months." Any of many paths can work,
  but none works unless you pick one and make it work.
- Denies natural talent: his own first content pieces (gym podcasts ~13 years ago) were
  bad and he took shots before filming out of nervousness; he was "good at languages"
  only because he grew up bilingual — talent is forgotten prior work that generalized.
