# Why You Don't Find Your Passion, You Create It

## Summary

Alex rejects the idea that people "find" passion before action. He says passion is
created by becoming competent: first a person does something they are bad at, then they
get better, then they like the competence and status that come from improvement. He uses
his own early options of fitness, test prep, and frozen yogurt to argue that interests
are not the same as profitable work.

## Key claims (Alex's self-report and advice, dated 2026-05-12)

- Alex says people create passion rather than find it.
- He says passion is built by getting good at something that initially feels bad because
  the person is unskilled.
- He recalls an early "wantrepreneur" phase when he considered fitness, test prep, and
  frozen yogurt as possible passions.
- He says those were better understood as interests than as business-ready passions.
- He says starting a gym made his passion disappear because it became work.
- He rejects the advice never to create work around a passion, calling it false because
  work behavior must change to make money.
- He says competence creates enjoyment, status, and a positive feedback loop.
- He prefers setting the expectation that everything will be hard and then becoming the
  kind of person who can do it anyway.
- He says the condition in which someone starts is the perfect condition because starting
  itself is what matters.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You don't find it, you create it."

> "Starting is the perfect condition."

> "Everything will suck."
