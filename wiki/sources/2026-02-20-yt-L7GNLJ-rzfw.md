# How to Make Content People Can Understand

## Summary

Live event Q&A clip. An audience member — an owner of a ~$1M/yr language-course
business (private lessons + digital products) — asks Alex about his "writing hygiene"
and protocol for producing quality content and books. Alex answers solo (the only
other voice is the questioner).

Alex's core method: he doesn't reread and refine as much as **reason from ground zero /
first principles**. He starts as though he knows nothing, isolates only statements he's
sure are true (fast beats slow, fewer steps beats many, low risk beats high risk), and
builds up from there — throwing out the received "mythology" of the field. He collects
his own anecdotal findings of what works, then hunts for **through-lines that unify them
into principles that hold across time**, and retroactively stress-tests them. Every book
starts by defining the thing ("What is an offer? What is a lead? What is selling?"), and
he actively tries to **break his own definitions** with counterexamples until one
survives.

The content-specific engine — why he thinks his content over-performs — is that he
**writes as if speaking to an autist, a court reporter, or an alien**: he only uses
things that are *observable*, never things you "feel" or read between the lines. Everyone
can agree on what they can observe; no one agrees on what they feel. He argues most
business education is woo/mysticism/superstition born of **fast reinforcement loops**
(the sneeze-before-a-sale, the baseball player's pre-swing ritual) — fast feedback is
great for learning but you often learn the wrong thing. His fix: only talk about what
can be observed. Worked example: defining **tonality** (which he had to crack for the
sales handbook) down to 3 constants (loud enough to hear, articulate enough to
understand, slow enough to understand) and 2 variables (pitch high/low → solicits a
response; pause → solicits attention). He also writes to a **third-to-fifth-grade reading
level**, because "people stop learning the moment they hit a word they don't get," and
defines any word above that level.

Overlap note: the observable-reality / clear-not-clever thread is shared with the sibling
video "How to Speak So People Actually Understand You." **Distinct to CONTENT here:**
(1) the first-principles *writing/authoring* process — collect anecdotes → find
through-lines → break your own definitions → retroactively test; (2) the book-by-book
"define the thing first" structure; (3) the reading-level constraint as a content filter;
(4) the fast-reinforcement-loop theory of why bad education spreads. The tonality example
and the "speak to an autist/alien" framing are the connective tissue between the two.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-20, paraphrased from Alex unless quoted below._

- His real content/book method is to **reason from ground zero** — start knowing nothing,
  admit only what's provably true, and build up from first principles rather than
  rereading and marinating in prior material.
- The provable base cases he trusts: **fast > slow, fewer steps > many steps, low risk >
  high risk** — components that raise the likelihood of a purchase decision.
- He **throws out the received mythology** of a field. Example: the "emotional vs.
  logical buyer" dichotomy — he parroted it until he realized you can't actually know a
  buyer's type (they don't even know why they bought). He replaced the binary with a
  **continuum**: the more information someone consumes, the more likely they are to buy;
  you just move people along it (which also syncs with how branding works).
- Process: gather **anecdotal findings** of what tends to work → find **through-lines
  that unify them into principles that apply across time** → **retroactively test** each
  principle to confirm it's true.
- He actively tries to **break his own models and definitions**. He hunts for a
  counterexample; if the definition survives, it becomes his working definition until
  something breaks it. Examples given: discipline ("action or not action?" — countered
  with a pleasurable action that takes no discipline) and influence ("the likelihood
  someone complies with the request").
- Every book **starts with a definition**: What's an offer? A money model? A lead? An
  engaged lead? Advertising? Selling? Define the thing first.
- ★ **Write as if speaking to an autist / court reporter / alien** — use only what is
  *observable*, never what you "feel" or imply between the lines. Because everyone can
  agree on what they can observe (but not on what they feel), grounding claims in
  observables makes far more people understand you — there's nothing left to question.
- Much of business education is **woo, mysticism, mythology, and superstition**, and it
  spreads because of **fast reinforcement loops**: quick feedback (the pre-shot ritual,
  the lucky sneeze) is powerful for learning but frequently teaches the *wrong* lesson
  (correlation mistaken for cause). Antidote: only assert what you can observe to be true.
- **Tonality, operationalized** (cracked for the sales handbook): reduce voice to what a
  human can physically do — go up/down, faster/slower, pause — then split into constants
  vs. variables. **3 constants:** loud enough to hear, articulate enough to understand,
  slow enough to understand; violate any and you lose comprehension, and by extension
  persuasion. **2 variables:** pitch (high solicits a *response*) and pause (solicits
  *attention*). It follows that the moment you want maximum attention *and* response is
  when you **ask a question and shut up**. A sales script therefore only needs two marks:
  where to stop talking and where to raise your voice.
- **Reading level as a content filter:** he targets a **third-to-fifth-grade reading
  level or below** and runs his material through that check, because people stop learning
  at the first word they don't understand. Any above-level word gets defined (in
  observable terms).
- The **Leads book** was by far the hardest he's written — **19 drafts** — because media
  changes so fast that durable, time-invariant principles were extremely hard to isolate.
- His stated summary of how he makes content: **"act as if I know nothing and act as if I
  cannot perceive or understand anything that I cannot define."**

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Real, real answer is that I actually try and reason from ground zero how something
> works. I start as though I know nothing and then try and, like, what are the only
> statements that I know for sure are true, and then I just try and only reason up from
> first principles."

> "I retroactively test stuff to make sure that it's true. That's why the leads book was
> so hard because media changes so much. Leads was by far the hardest book I've ever
> written... it was 19 drafts."

> "The reason that I think the content that I have disproportionately performs is that I
> act as though I'm speaking to an autist... an autist can only observe what they can
> see... So I have to define things in terms of things that are observable. And when you
> do that, way more people get it because everyone can agree on what they can observe,
> not on what they feel."

> "Whenever you have a fast reinforcement loop, that's where superstition occurs... Fast
> feedback loops means very good for learning, but you don't always learn the right
> thing. And so for me, I have to think what things can I observe to be true and then
> only talk about those things."

> "When you make your voice high, it solicits a response. When you pause, it solicits
> attention. And so it would make sense that the point in the sale where you solicit the
> most attention and a response is where you ask a question and shut the [__] up."

> "People will stop learning the moment they get to a word they don't get. And so I try
> to speak at a third grade reading level or fifth grade reading level or below and I run
> all my stuff through that."
