# What is Love?

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host, "Jack," asks the questions; only Alex's words are used as voice/belief data). Alex lays out his philosophy of love and long-term partnership through the lens of his marriage to Leila. He rejects the romantic view of love as an "ephemeral," indescribable thing that just "occurs between two people," arguing instead that love is **conditional, not unconditional**, and that a durable relationship is run on explicit **agreements** rather than mystique. Because every marriage is "n equals one" (each person, and each pairing, is one-of-one), he refuses to prescribe universal rules and only shares what has worked for him. His and Leila's model: both are highly independent, high-agency people who "don't like to be controlled," so they make clear agreements about what each will and won't do, keep them until they no longer fit, and revisit them when something comes up — which gives them "exceedingly clear expectations of one another" and fast, low-drama conflict resolution ("we don't argue much"). He frames even marriage as contingent: if Leila ever no longer wanted the same things, he would not hold her to the commitment, because it was founded on shared mission, values, and interests. His compatibility test: aligned destination (mission), aligned way of getting there (values), and aligned use of time in between (interests). He critiques people who "look for hookups and then think hookups will turn into life partners" — you screen for a spouse differently than for a hookup. He also describes their unusual division of labor (Leila is CEO and "absolutely runs the business," while he gets the public credit), his "strong woman needs a stronger man" view of male-female dynamics, and his belief — crediting Esther Perel — that couples, especially in remote-work life, "spend too much time together" and must "create space to be missed" ("motivation's the equal opposite of deprivation"). He closes on the concrete ritual he and Leila still practice: a 45-minute end-of-day walk where they pull out their phones, go through their calendars, and "download" each other's day to re-sync.
