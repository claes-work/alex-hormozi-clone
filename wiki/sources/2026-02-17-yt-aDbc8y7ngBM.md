# Why You Should Give Away All Your Secrets for Free

> Source: YouTube (@MoreMozi), 2026-02-17 — id `yt-aDbc8y7ngBM`.
> Solo Alex Hormozi teaching to a listener (the "Mhm." interjection); all substantive
> content is Alex. Caption `>>` marks are turn-breaks in one continuous monologue.

## Summary

Alex answers the recurring fear among content creators and info-product sellers: "If I
give away all my secrets in my free content, no one will have anything to buy." He argues
the opposite — 99% of people will never buy regardless, but they *judge* you by the
quality of your free content, so give the information away and sell the implementation.

The distinct mechanism (see ★): the "how much do I teach vs. pitch?" question is the wrong
frame. What matters is *which kind* of knowledge you teach at each stage. He splits
education into **declarative knowledge** (knowledge *about* something — how it works, what
outcomes are possible) and **procedural knowledge** (knowledge of *how to do* something —
the step-by-step tactics). Declarative is what you use to **sell**; procedural is what you
**sell**. Free content should be predominantly declarative: break the audience's beliefs
(old model vs. new model), show them what's possible and why it works, and everyone in the
room gets value just by *being there*. Reserve the procedural "here's exactly how to set it
up" for the paid offer.

## Key claims

All dated 2026-02-17, paraphrased unless quoted.

- ★ **Declarative vs. procedural is the real teach/pitch dial.** Two types of education:
  declarative knowledge = knowledge *about* something (how it works, outcomes, what's
  possible); procedural knowledge = knowledge of *how to do* it (tactics/steps). Declarative
  is "what you use to sell"; procedural is "what you sell." (Alex credits this to standard
  education theory of the last ~100 years, not his own invention — "people just don't talk
  about it.")
- **Free content should be predominantly declarative.** Teach how things work conceptually
  and break beliefs (old model vs. new model), not the step-by-step execution.
- **Declarative knowledge delivers value instantly; everyone in the room gets it just by
  being there.** No action required to extract the value — so it lands immediately.
- **Procedural/tactical teaching delays the value and kills present-tense buying intent.**
  With tactics, the listener has to *go do* the tactic before extracting value; the value
  is at a delay, "which means they don't want to buy right now." Teaching tactics also leaves
  people "with a shitload of to-dos" so they walk away thinking "I'm good" — self-served,
  not sold.
- **Don't fear giving away the information — sell the implementation.** 99% will never buy;
  the rest judge you on free-content quality, so giving away the "what/why" builds the trust
  that sells the "how."
- **Example of good declarative teaching (illustrative, numbers are his example, not a
  verified claim):** a business at $2M/yr crossing to $5M crosses "the institutional
  threshold," where the valuation multiple jumps ~3x → ~7x — so ~$3M of added revenue can
  move enterprise value from ~$4M to ~$35M "because institutional buyers…". Explaining *why
  that works* fascinates the audience without teaching them *how to* do it.

**Overlap note:** This sits on the existing "give your best stuff away / knowing ≠ doing /
execution gap" thread. What's genuinely new here is the **declarative-vs-procedural
vocabulary** as the operating rule for *what to give away vs. what to sell* — a sharper,
namable mechanism than the general "give it away" advice already in the corpus. The
"value at a delay because tactics require action" point also connects to the existing
knowing≠doing/execution-gap idea but reframes it as a *pricing/buying-intent* lever.
