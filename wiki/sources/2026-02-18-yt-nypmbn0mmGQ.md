# Why Should People Believe You?

## Summary

Short clip (channel @MoreMozi) from a panel/roundtable setting — there is audience
[laughter] and Alex "piggyback[s] on Sam's point," but **only Alex speaks in the captured
transcript**, so all material below is Alex-attributed. The topic is proof/believability:
how to make a skeptical stranger believe your advertising claims.

Alex opens with a team mnemonic — **WISBY, "Why Should I Believe You?"** — which he keeps
posted above the creative room so his team always designs content assuming the viewer
arrives with maximum skepticism ("everything's fake, everyone's lying"). His prescription:
**lead with your single most extreme piece of proof**, then let the prospect *degrade it
down* to their own smaller use case. His examples: show yourself landing a 1200 (an extreme
skateboard trick) before you teach the ollie; a jacket that survives Everest gets
mentally discounted to "it'll keep me warm from the car to my front door"; an off-road
vehicle becomes "so if it snows I can still get to work"; **Red Bull's Stratos space jump**
becomes "it'll maybe wake me up when I'm tired." The extreme demonstration is how brands
and authority get built: if the guy "vibe coded a $10 million app, he can probably help me
automate my email." The prospect scales the giant proof down to their modest need — and
believes. Closes with an ad read for his free 10-stage $0→$100M+ roadmap at
acquisition.com/roadmap.

Overlaps heavily with [[../topics/marketing/proof]] (proof is the biggest lever; the
Belief Continuum from doubt→certainty; "Big Results > Small Results"). What is **distinct
here**: (1) the **WISBY mnemonic** as a default operating assumption of total skepticism,
and (2) the **"extreme proof, degraded down"** mechanic — lead with the maximal capability
demonstration and let the prospect self-scale it to their own smaller job. That extreme-to-
authority move is a fresh framing not spelled out in proof.md's 13 dimensions.

## Key claims

_All claims dated 2026-02-18. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Alex-attributed only._

- ★ **WISBY — "Why Should I Believe You?" — as a default content-design assumption.** He
  keeps the acronym posted above the creative room so the team always thinks like a maximally
  skeptical viewer who assumes everything is fake and everyone is lying. Every piece of content
  should preempt that skepticism.
- ★ **Lead with your most extreme proof, then let the prospect degrade it down to their case.**
  People shop for the capability that handles the extreme (a jacket for Everest, a car for
  off-roading) and then mentally discount it to their modest real need (car-to-door warmth,
  driving to work in snow). So open with the hardest demonstration; the audience self-scales
  it downward and believes it will handle their smaller task.
- **Extreme demonstrations build brand and authority.** "These extreme examples are so
  important to enforce your brand[], your authority." If someone did the extreme thing, the
  viewer reasons "if he did that, he can probably help me with my much smaller thing" — e.g.
  "if that guy vibe coded a $10 million app, he can probably help me automate my email."
- **This is fundamentally how brands are built** — his cited case is **Red Bull's space jump
  (Stratos)**: a man parachuting from the edge of space translates, in the customer's mind, to
  "it'll maybe wake me up a little when I'm tired." The stunt is proof-of-capability that halos
  onto the everyday product.
- **Proof can precede the teaching.** In the skateboard example, you start by showing yourself
  landing a 1200 ("you could just start with that"), *then* say "anyways, here's how you do an
  ollie" — establish believability with the extreme feat before delivering the basic lesson.
- **(Ad read) Free 10-stage $0→$100M+ roadmap** that "less than 1% of companies finish," which
  he says he's now done multiple times; broken down by 8 business functions with, per stage,
  what the constraint feels like, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate — across
  software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar. At acquisition.com/roadmap.
