# How to Successfully Launch Your Skool Community — Alex Hormozi

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi argues that a valuable paid community does not require a large
audience — the opposite is usually true, because huge communities struggle to
maintain value amid chaos. He cites platform data that a community only needs
**10 true regulars** (people who post daily) to be valuable, and tells people to
**start with just 3 paying members**, since "three is on the way to 10." He grounds
the low-audience thesis in Kevin Kelly's *1,000 True Fans* and a niching/affinity
argument (the Joe Rogan cross-interest example), then gives the concrete first
tactic: text everyone in your phone contacts and invite them, reframing the barrier
as fear rather than lack of know-how. The clip is a short launch pep-talk / VSL
segment for a Skool-related program, not a full end-to-end operations manual.

## Community-launch playbook (detailed bullets)

**Audience size — you need far less than you think**
- Do NOT wait for "zillions of fans or followers." A big audience is not the
  prerequisite for a valuable community.
- Large communities are actually the *hardest* to keep valuable — "so much chaos
  going on inside of it."
- Per platform stats (self-reported), the value threshold is **10 true regulars**:
  10 people who contribute/post to the group regularly.
- Model example: his own 13-person powerlifting group where everyone posted daily
  (lifting videos, what they struggled with, how others broke through plateaus) —
  he "looked forward to seeing the post every single day" and would have paid for it.

**First members — start with three**
- **Start with 3 paying members**, not 10 — "three is on the way to 10."
- Getting 3 paying members is framed as the achievable first milestone that proves
  the model and builds momentum toward the 10-regulars threshold.
- (Program-specific promotional hook, self-reported) In this program, landing 3
  paying members also unlocks a bonus: access to the full recorded day when the
  "top 10" contest winners fly out — "as though you had won it too."

**Positioning / niche — narrow to raise affinity**
- Invokes Kevin Kelly's *1,000 True Fans*: you don't need to be Mr. Beast or Logan
  Paul; ~1,000 people is enough, and against 8 billion you can afford to be extremely
  specific.
- Stack interests to niche down: a "vegan mom powerlifter who loves cats" has another
  thousand of the same — cross-pollinating interests *narrows* the audience but makes
  its affinity with you *much higher*.
- Joe Rogan example: JRE sits at the center of aliens, fitness, conspiracy, fighting,
  and comedy. Fans who share one topic like him; two topics → like him more; all of
  them → hardcore fans. Build your community around the *multiple* interests you
  personally share, and the people who overlap will join and bond harder.

**Getting the first members — work your existing contacts**
- You already have the seed audience: friends, Facebook friends, Instagram followers.
- Concrete tactic: pull out your phone, recognize you have 400–800 known contacts, and
  **text them to personally invite them** to your community.
- Claim (self-reported): invite ~800 / everyone in your contact list and "I promise
  you you'll get three… probably get more than 10 just from doing that one thing."
- The blocker is emotional, not technical: "it's not because you don't know how. It's
  because you're afraid." Reframe: "all we're doing is inviting people to a party."

## Key claims

- Good communities all start small; you do not need a large following to launch.
- Massive communities have the hardest time maintaining value because of internal chaos.
- Platform stats say **10 true regulars** (daily contributors) are enough to make a
  community valuable. *(self-reported platform data)*
- The right starting target is **3 paying members**, because 3 is on the way to 10.
- Kevin Kelly's *1,000 True Fans* thesis: ~1,000 people is enough to sustain a
  creator/community; you don't need millions.
- Niching by stacking multiple shared interests narrows the audience but raises its
  affinity — cross-pollination beats breadth.
- Inviting your entire phone contact list (~800 people) will reliably yield at least 3,
  and probably 10+, members. *(self-reported)*
- The real obstacle to starting is fear, not lack of knowledge.
