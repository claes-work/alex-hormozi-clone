# $100M Price Raise Playbook (2025)

**What it is.** A short Acquisition.com playbook by Alex Hormozi, subtitled "How to Raise Prices Without Losing Customers" (PDF p. 1), roughly 28 pages including front matter, table of contents, and appendix. It teaches how and when to raise prices, the RAISE letter framework, and includes two full price-increase letter templates plus a checklist. Raw text: `raw/books/2025-100m-price-raise-playbook.md` (cite `PDF p. N`).

## Thesis

Raising prices is one of the fastest, highest-leverage profit moves a business can make: "the easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff" (PDF p. 3). Most owners are trapped in a "vicious price cycle" / race to the bottom copying broke competitors; raising prices instead triggers a "virtuous cycle" where higher prices lift emotional investment, perceived value, results, service level, and conviction (PDF pp. 6–8). Done wrong it can kill a business; done right (with the RAISE letter, an expiring loyalty discount, and testing on new customers first) it retains most customers while multiplying profit.

- **Raise Prices / opening story** (PDF pp. 4–5) — Trey, the North Carolina gym owner charging $49/mo, near bankruptcy; Alex has him triple prices; result: $2,000/mo loss → $4,000/mo profit in a week.

- **My Rules For Raising Prices** (PDF pp. 8–9) — no grandfathering, no lifetime deals, raise ≥1x/year, test on new customers, meet if >50%, pair within 90 days of a launch, do the math.
- **How To Pick Your Price** (PDF pp. 9–12) — worked click→conversion→churn→LTV chart across $10 / $20 / $100 price points; test, test, test.
- **The Perfect Price Raise Letter (RAISE)** (PDF pp. 13–16) — Remind, Address, Invest, Soften, Explain; the three types of people; pulled-forward churn.
- **What This Letter Looks Like All Together** (PDF pp. 16–17) — assembled template letter; what to do next (send it; stair-step option); handling incoming customers.
- **My Gym Price Increase Letter** (PDF pp. 20–23) — the longer, real letter Trey sent (Patrick Campbell of ProfitWell collaborated on the concise version).
- **Why You Should Actually Do This** (PDF pp. 24–25) — closing exhortation; revisit pricing annually.
- **Price Raise Checklist** (PDF p. 26) and **Appendix: Price Raise Letter Template** (PDF pp. 27–28).

## Key claims

- Trey's gym (2019) charged $49/mo, had the worst customers, lost money despite more signups; Alex told him "in 12 more weeks, you won't have a gym" and had him **triple** all monthly rates via a letter used as an explainer-video script (comments turned off, questions referred to him). Result in a week: from bleeding **$2,000/mo to $4,000/mo profit** (PDF pp. 4–5).
- The core lever: "the easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff"; copying competitors is a race to the bottom because "everyone else is BROKE" (PDF p. 3).
- **Don't grandfather existing customers; don't do lifetime deals.** Value depends on price; locking a price limits future options. Never sell a "one time price" for lifetime access unless fulfillment truly costs $0 (PDF pp. 8–9).
- **Raise prices at least once per year.** Warren Buffett's See's Candies: over 50+ years he raised prices on average ~10%/year, some years up to 17% — the one decision he reserved for himself (PDF p. 9).
- **Test price raises on new customers before rolling out to the whole base** — gives data and confidence; shows the base the market already agreed (PDF pp. 9, 12).
- **Meet 1-on-1 if you raise prices more than 50%** (usually the result of a big early undercharging mistake) (PDF p. 10).
- **Do the math**: know what percentage of customers you can lose and still make more money before raising (PDF p. 10).
- Pricing worked example: $10 price → 5 sales, $100 LTV, $500 total. Doubling to **$20** → conv. drops to 4%, LTV doubles to $200 → $800 total (**+60%** from losing one customer; on 30% margins this ~triples profit). Going to **$100** (10x) → conv. 2%, churn 33%, LTV $300 → $600 total (worse than $20). Best known price is $20; next test $39–$59 (PDF pp. 9–12).
- **The RAISE letter**: R-emind them of value delivered; A-ddress the price change directly (rip the bandaid, one sentence); I-nvest in their future (frame the extra money as investments already planned, paired with "more good stuff / less bad stuff", never lie); S-often with an expiring loyalty discount (3–6 months); E-xplain away concerns via a PS line (PDF pp. 13–16).
- **The expiring-discount reframe**: raise the price now, then immediately offer a discount that expires in 3–6 months, ideally shown as an account credit — "people are more 'okay' with a vanishing discount than a raise in price" (PDF p. 12).
- **The PS statement** disarms replies: B2C "if this will materially affect your ability to buy groceries, let me know"; B2B "if this materially impacts your business, let me know and we'll work something out" (PDF pp. 15, 18).
- **Three types of people**: (1) see the value and comply; (2) actually affected → extend the discount another 6 months; (3) were going to cancel anyway → churn spikes month one, dips below normal month two, returns to baseline month three ("pulled-forward" churn) (PDF pp. 15–16).
- **Incoming/new customers**: get them to the new price immediately; sales line — "96% of our customers agreed… this is the cheapest it'll ever be" (PDF pp. 18–19).
- **Stair-step option** for big raises: drop the discount off at intervals (e.g. "$200/mo off for 4 months, then $100/mo, then $50/mo") to ease customers into the new price (PDF pp. 18, 26).
- ProfitWell's Patrick Campbell oversaw 100 price increases directly and analyzed 500+; he collaborated on the concise template (PDF p. 20). A Shopify competitor took ~300 customers from $30 to ~$3,000/mo (100x) and lost only one (PDF p. 10).

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by a tenth of a cent, then you've got a terrible business." (PDF p. 9, attributed to Warren Buffett)

> "The easiest way to make more money is to charge more for the same stuff. Brilliant, I know. And yet, no one does it." (PDF p. 3)

> "There's no strategic advantage to being the second lowest price in a marketplace. But there is for being the highest." (PDF p. 6, attributed to Dan Kennedy)

> "Don't grandfather existing customers. Don't do lifetime deals. Value depends on price. If your value goes up, so should your price." (PDF p. 9)

> "People are more 'okay' with a vanishing discount than they are a raise in price. And - they mind even less when another discount appears in the future." (PDF p. 12)

> "Rip off the bandaid. Don't waste time. The rest of the letter will soften the blow. It's usually a single sentence." (PDF p. 10)

> "Actually. Send. This. Don't be a chicken. Send it." (PDF p. 15)

> "People moan, but they still buy. Just because someone wants something for less doesn't mean they won't buy it for more." (PDF p. 19)

> "Raise prices one time. See that you don't die. Notice how you just make more money. Get addicted to price increases." (PDF pp. 24–25)
