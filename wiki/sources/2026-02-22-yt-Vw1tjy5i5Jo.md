# What It Takes to Get Good at Anything

## Summary

Short clip (interview format — an off-camera interviewer asks the questions; Alex Hormozi answers). The interviewer asks what separates unstoppable, great writers from everyone else "other than the work." Hormozi's answer: there isn't a separate thing — but the work only counts when it gets **expert feedback**. Volume alone does not create mastery; he cites creators who have posted for 10 years and have the same following because they never learned or improved. He reframes the purpose of reps: the work is required to change *you* (build the skill), not to produce the output. Once the skill exists, the output does the work (his example: if Mr. Beast's account were cancelled, he could rebuild to millions because he owns the skill). His practical prescription — "100 reps a day" — is deliberately *not* the same 100 reps; each rep should improve, guided by an end-of-day self-review ("what could I do better?") and outside expert eyes catching stumbles to drill. Clip ends with an ad read for the free acquisition.com/roadmap (10-stage zero-to-$100M roadmap).

## Key claims

All claims dated 2026-02-22, paraphrased unless quoted. Solo Alex (interviewer only prompts).

- **Volume without feedback doesn't build skill.** The differentiator between great and mediocre performers is not just doing the work — the work needs feedback from expert eyes to gain proficiency. Posting the same short every day for 10 years without changing anything produces no progress. (paraphrase)
- **He knows creators who made content for 10 years with the same-size following** because they didn't learn or improve. (paraphrase)
- **The work is for the person, not the output.** "The work works on you more than you work on it." Reps are required to build *your* skill, not because the output inherently needs years of labor. (paraphrase + quote)
- **Skill is portable; output is downstream of skill.** Someone with the skill can reproduce the result quickly — e.g., if Mr. Beast's account got cancelled he could start another and grow it to millions because he has the skill. The 5 years built the skill, not the specific account; after that the output does the work. (paraphrase)
- **"Do 100 reps a day" ≠ the same 100 reps.** The prescription is to get *better* with every rep, not to repeat identical reps. (paraphrase)
- **Two feedback mechanisms:** (1) end-of-day self-review — ask "what could I do better? how could I improve?"; (2) outside eyes flagging specific stumbles ("you stumbled here on this call") and drilling them a couple of times. (paraphrase)
- **His "Doctor Money" work prescriptions target skill, not activity.** He doesn't care that you made 10,000 phone calls — he cares that you got better 10,000 times. (paraphrase)
