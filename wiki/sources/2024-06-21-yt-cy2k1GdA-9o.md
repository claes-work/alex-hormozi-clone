# Sales Was Hard Until I Understood These 9 Concepts

## Summary

Solo Hormozi video laying out the traits that separate elite salespeople, organized under
his claim that a salesperson has three jobs: maximize opportunities, convert the highest
percentage of them, and sustain that consistently over a long period. He walks through
~13 concepts (he admits "I said I had nine but I have way more than nine") sorted into
those three buckets, drawing on having built multiple nine-figure sales teams and
personally closing 4,000+ sales. Much of the closing material overlaps his 2023 logical-
closing talk; the operational/leads-maximization and meta-skill (consistency) material is
the freshest content here.

## Key claims (the concepts, by bucket)

**Framing: a salesperson has three jobs** — (1) maximize the number of opportunities,
(2) convert the highest percentage, (3) do it consistently for a long time. Every concept
falls into one of these buckets. He also defines sales itself: "increasing the likelihood
that the prospect buys" — a perfect sales process = 100% conversion.

**Bucket 1 — Maximizing opportunities/leads:**
- **Most available hours.** The rep who works the most hours sells the most; availability
  was the single strongest predictor of total sales across his software company Alan's data
  (4,000+ appointments/day). Be available when the prospect is available — weekends, Sundays
  ("businesses pay rent on Sundays too"). Fewer time slots → prospects book at less
  convenient times → show rates and buying-mood drop.
- **Pull up calls (and two SOPs).** Drag later-week appointments into today (higher show
  rate, more interest, higher close, more velocity); if a prospect goes quiet, push them out
  and refill the slot with a responsive lead. He learned the "pull-up" tactic from a solar
  rep who immediately called every new booking to qualify + either close now or fill a later
  slot — that rep sold 4x the next best; adopted portfolio-wide. Best reps have **two
  checklists**: an on-call SOP (what you say) and an off-call SOP (what you do between calls);
  most companies lack the off-call SOP and lose money.
- **BAMFAM — "book a meeting from a meeting."** Never end a call without the next contact
  time set; never let a prospect fall into "no man's land." Learned the acronym from his
  friend Chiron.
- **High activity + don't take rejection personally.** More outreach/follow-up attempts
  (visible in the CRM) correlate with more calls; it's a numbers game. Keep a **"kill list"**
  of whales/high-value prospects somewhere visible for daily extra attention.
- **Create your own opportunities via referrals.** Ask "**who do you know** who is as awesome
  as you and would benefit from this?" (forces a name, wraps it in a compliment). Referral
  close rates are ~80–90%; even a 1-in-3 or 1-in-4 referral rate roughly halves or thirds
  your customer-acquisition cost. A brand-new rep who simply asked "who else wants to come
  with you?" outsold the #2 rep by 50%.

**Bucket 2 — Maximizing conversion (on the call):**
- **Preparation.** Five minutes of research makes you "sound like a genius." Rule of thumb:
  prep ≈ 10% of call length for a routine call (12 min for a 2-hr call); reverses for rare
  high-stakes meetings (6 hrs prep per 1-hr quarterly). Coming with problem + solution
  already identified switches you from salesman to consultant. In his early gym-turnaround
  days he pieced together social profiles from just an email so his outreach wasn't
  "sketchy": "the big guys had the reputation, the small guys win through preparation."
- **Take notes.** Public prep (what you observe) + on-call prep (what you learn on the phone)
  → reference it on the next call so the prospect doesn't repeat their story; proves your
  recommendations are contextual, pre-handling the "will it work for *me*?" concern. Turns
  a salesman into a consultant/helper.
- **Listen more than talk.** Best reps listen ~2x as much as they talk (measured via AI
  call-analysis across the portfolio). "The person answering the questions is the one getting
  interrogated." Be "like smoke" — answer questions with questions (hot potato) unless you
  already know your statement aligns with what they want. Educate the prospect so they reach
  their own conclusion; "I need them to say it's a good idea, not me."
- **Breathe the script.** Memorize it "like the Bible" so you can be 100% present and listen
  instead of rehearsing your next line. Concision matters — don't take 100 words for 5;
  longer scripts creep in via "positive reinforcement" (the rep who coughs before a close and
  it closes starts adding coughs and extra questions). Managers must **role-play daily**,
  drilling **one** part of the script at a time (no one improves multiple things at once).
- **Kill zombies up front / diffuse the bomb (concept #10).** Distinguish **obstacles**
  (raised before price — move around/through them) from **objections** (after price — the bomb
  timer is now ticking). Handle everything before you mention price. All obstacles reduce to
  three sources: **circumstance** (time / money / fit), **other people** (spouse / partner /
  decision-maker), and **self** (doubt they can do it). Break beliefs with memorized stories
  and metaphors; use **best-case/worst-case, "let's play it out"** ("I've probably closed more
  sales on best-case/worst-case than any other close"); ask **"what are you afraid of
  happening?"** Confront the ugly thing in front of you, not after they get home. Bonus:
  the **"ghetto tone"** (learned from Sam Bakhtiar) — humor/tonality to defuse hard moments.
- **Ask for the sale again.** "If you do not ask for the sale you will not get it." Loop:
  resolve the concern, then ask again — repeat unlimited times *as long as you resolve the
  concern* (asking again without resolving just annoys). "Permission not support"; the
  seasonal/"when-then" time fallacy; "resourcefulness not resources."

**Bucket 3 — Consistency (meta-skills):**
- **Sustain performance / enthusiasm as skills.** On-off cycling (motivated/demotivated) is a
  skill deficiency, not a trait; champions sustain output. Enthusiasm is a learnable skill.
- **Kill for sport.** Mike Ary's dogs/horses/tigers analogy — dogs need feeding, horses need
  whipping, tigers kill for sport even when full; you want tigers who love the hunt and would
  do it for free. Treat every lead (even unqualified) as free practice: "you either practice
  the skill or you close — either way you win"; low-quality reps warm-up-rep instead of
  practicing at full intensity.
- **Track data.** Skill is judged by the quantity/quality of metrics tracked. Close rate is
  a lagging outcome; track booking rate, show rate, offer rate, call-to-close average, cash
  collected. Raising schedule rate 20% = raising close rate 20%.
- **Give the best leads to the best closers.** Score leads first (requires tracking), match
  best leads → best reps, worst leads → new reps (business loses less; new reps sharpen on
  hard leads). A timeshare rep making $3M/yr in commission won this concession from the CEO,
  5x'd his income, then it rolled out nationwide and 5x'd the business. Creates survivorship-
  bias appeal that 10x's recruiting.
- **Never blame circumstances / take 100% control.** His biggest pet peeve is reps blaming
  bad leads/comp. Best reps (and best marketers) take 100% of the blame for every outcome —
  "if everybody is taking absolute blame for every outcome, you win."
