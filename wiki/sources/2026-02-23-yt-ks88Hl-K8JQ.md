# Helping a Custom Apparel Business Get More Qualified Leads

## Summary

A "hero hotline" coaching call on @MoreMozi. The caller, **McKenzie** (the questioner — NOT Alex; treat all his business figures as self-reported), runs a custom-apparel business at ~$100k/month topline with ~35% gross margins. He acts as a middleman: his team handles design, outsources manufacturing, and marks it up. He acquires customers via cold outbound — scraping contacts (mostly on Instagram) for colleges and college organizations (fraternities/sororities) and reaching out cold. His stated constraint: cold outbound works economically but generates too many unqualified leads, and he can't predict which prospects become high-LTV customers (some spend $20k/year, others $1,500/year).

Alex's core coaching move is to **reframe the "unqualified leads" problem as a market-selection problem**. Rather than perfecting qualification within colleges (AOV ~$12,500), Alex pushes him toward the segments McKenzie already knows are far bigger — **construction and summer camps** — where a single order can be ~$50,000 vs. 50-shirt college orders. Alex argues the operational effort is identical (he's a middleman either way), so the only new thing to solve is acquisition for the bigger avatar, and the leverage difference is ~100x. Alex explicitly flags this as advice he does NOT normally give (his default bet is "just do more of what works, 10x the outreach"), but the magnitude of the value gap plus low churn justifies the pivot. Guardrails: commit to ONE new vertical (not many), keep the existing college business running under an operator, and validate reorder/revenue-retention in the new market as fast as possible before over-investing.

## Key claims (2026-02-23, paraphrased unless quoted)

- Picking the customer is the first and most important decision in a business; the offer is reverse-engineered from the customer. (paraphrase)
- Alex's stated regret about the "$100M Offers" book: he would have made "your first customer" (a lost chapter) the FIRST chapter, because you must start with the customer, then reverse-engineer the offer. (paraphrase — biographical/self-reported about his own book)
- Getting customer data right (knowing who your customer is) is non-negotiable leverage for messaging, offer, and channel selection; don't delay it to chase volume. (paraphrase)
- Variance in customer value (some duds, some big) is "a feature not a bug" of cold outreach — list segmentation and list enrichment are part of what makes outreach effective. (paraphrase)
- The north-star metric for this business should be **absolute profit per order**, not per-unit margin or per-shirt price — a 5,000-shirt order at 20% margin beats a 50-shirt order at 50% margin because the middleman work is the same. (paraphrase)
- If you target 5,000-unit orders, even your "small" customers scale up proportionally (e.g., 500-unit orders instead of 50). (paraphrase)
- Default/"for sure" growth bet for a business with low churn and stacking gross profit: just triple/10x the existing outreach — lower risk than switching markets. (paraphrase)
- The condition under which he'd stay in colleges: if those customers were reliably lifetime/recurring (predictable annual gross profit retained at 80–90%), you'd build a monopoly as "the default t-shirt guy" for colleges. McKenzie reports he's largely already there (very low churn). (paraphrase; McKenzie's churn claim is self-reported)
- Alex maps the decision onto his **five Ms** framework (referenced from a prior livestream): the caller had diagnosed *data/metrics* as the constraint; here Alex walks metrics → market as the next M. (paraphrase — the caller invokes "the five Ms")
- Do NOT build many parallel vertical websites/funnels; commit to one new avatar. Every vertical has different acquisition, sales, and nuances you must relearn. (paraphrase)
- The pivot is only justified by the combination of ~100x value difference AND virtually no difference in operational effort — the middleman mechanics stay identical; only customer acquisition must be re-solved. (paraphrase)
- Risk is reversible: if construction fails, you can always fall back to the "for sure" path of 10x-ing college outreach. (paraphrase)
- Retention/reorder is the one thing to validate fast in the new market — with reorders you get revenue retention and there are more than enough construction firms to reach $20M/year. (paraphrase)
- ★ Notable as a possible L3-candidate: Alex openly breaking his own "more/better before different" heuristic when a ~100x leverage gap exists at same operational cost — a clean, quotable exception to a core rule. Also the "$100M Offers first-chapter regret" is a citable authorial statement worth capturing in beliefs/biography on synthesis.
