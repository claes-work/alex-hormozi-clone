# A Funny Story That Humbled Me

★ L3-candidate — canonical volume/level-of-effort teaching told through the "300 flyers" origin story; ties Alex's gym-marketing beginnings to his current content playbook (350 pieces/week) and the "consistency not complexity" thesis. ## Summary
In this short monologue (with a brief interjection from a second speaker, likely his chief of brand), Alex Hormozi tells a self-deprecating origin story from his early gym days to make a point about volume and level of effort. A mentor told him flyers worked, so Alex — broke and unable to stomach more advertising — printed and distributed 300 flyers and waited. The single call he got was from a man complaining Alex had dinged his Mercedes; Alex panicked and hung up. When he confronted the mentor, the mentor laughed and explained that his own team tested with 5,000 flyers and considered a half-percent response acceptable — meaning 300 flyers could never reveal whether anything worked. The lesson: Alex "might have been doing the right thing, but was not doing nearly enough of it." He generalizes this to early entrepreneurship (people give up on content after one unprofitable month) and to a recent conversation with a successful friend who was humbled to learn Alex's team publishes ~350 pieces of content a week. Alex argues newcomers think in "doubles and triples" when the real gap is 30x or more, and closes with his thesis that great work takes great struggle — the barrier to the biggest rewards is not complexity but consistency over time.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-08) When Alex was starting his gyms, a mentor told him flyers were how they got customers and suggested he try them.
- (2026-02-08) Advertising was expensive and Alex was broke, so 300 flyers was the most he could "stomach"; he put out 300 total.
- (2026-02-08) The only call he got was from a man saying Alex had dinged his Mercedes; Alex panicked and hung up without a word, and the man never called back.
- (2026-02-08) When Alex complained to the mentor that the flyers were supposed to work, the mentor asked what his "test size" was — the first batch you test before scaling.
- (2026-02-08) The mentor's team tested with 5,000 flyers: a half-percent response was "okay," 1% meant scaling hard, 1.5% was cause for celebration.
- (2026-02-08) At a half-percent, 300 flyers would yield ~1.5 people — too few to know if anything is working.
- (2026-02-08) Alex's takeaway: he may have been doing the right thing, but not nearly enough of it.
- (2026-02-08) The story applies to early entrepreneurship: people get disheartened when their first month of content hasn't made money, but the issue is usually not doing enough or for long enough — time is a proxy for doing enough volume.
- (2026-02-08) People (including early Alex) underestimate the volume it takes to be successful.
- (2026-02-08) A successful friend (described as "B-level" successful) wanting to build a personal brand asked Alex why his content outperformed; when he learned Alex's team puts out ~350 pieces a week, he said "thank you for resetting my minimum standard."
- (2026-02-08) In the flyer analogy, the mentor scaled to 5,000 flyers per day — 150,000 in 30 days — while Alex put out 300 in the same 30 days, roughly a 1/500th level of effort.
- (2026-02-08) People think in doubles and triples when the required difference is often 30x the work.
- (2026-02-08) The same volume/effort gap distinguishes mediocre from world-class across advertising, product iteration, and video editing: an experienced editor spends far more than the two hours a newcomer thinks is "the whole thing."
- (2026-02-08) Alex personally put 2,000 hours into his forthcoming book — about six hours a day for almost two years — and wrote 19 drafts before it would come out.

- (2026-02-08) Great work takes great struggle: if it were easy it would be low-hanging fruit, and low-hanging fruit has already been picked by others. [partly voiced by second speaker]
- (2026-02-08) The big wins come from hard-to-reach fruit; the "key" to most treasure chests is in plain sight, but there is a time-lock on it (e.g. "wait five years"). What makes it hard is not complexity but consistency.
