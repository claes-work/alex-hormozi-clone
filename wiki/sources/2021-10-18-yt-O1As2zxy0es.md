# The Only 4 Ways To Scale A Service Business (PICK ONE)

## Summary

In this 2021 talk, Hormozi walks through what he calls the only four ways he knows of to
"package" a service business — its labor, knowledge, and IP — into a scalable, valuable
enterprise. He frames the choice as a trade-off between cash flow / margin / operational
drag on one side and enterprise value (exit multiple) on the other, and says he personally
owns businesses in three of the four categories. The framing question comes from a real
conversation with a profitable single-location salon owner asking which "vehicle" would
make them the most money. Each vehicle is evaluated on build-out cost, margin, cash-flow
timing, defensibility, and exit value.

## The 4 ways (exactly as stated)

1. **Privately held chain ("private chain")** — you open location after location, retain
   all ownership, take all the risk, front all the capital, hire all the labor, control
   the brand. You own everything end to end.
2. **Franchise** — a single proven model franchised out to outside investors who buy in.
   A franchise has three components: **name + business systems + fee**.
3. **Licensing** — packaging your IP/knowledge for others without being a (legally
   defined) franchise. A licensing model typically carries only **two of the three**
   franchise components (e.g. name + fee but no business systems, like CrossFit; or fee +
   business systems but white-labeled with no brand). Using all three while calling it a
   license makes you an "illegal franchise."
4. **Software / tech-enabled service** — replacing or augmenting the service with software,
   where the type of service allows it (you can't replace haircuts, but you can replace
   some services).

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- **The four vehicles trade cash flow/margin against enterprise value.** Some give more
  cash flow, higher margin, or lower operational drag; others give more enterprise value
  but are harder to create. (2021)
- **Private chain — when it makes sense:** low build-out cost relative to the profit a
  location generates. Rule of thumb — if a new location costs **less than ~6 months of its
  first-year profit** to open, owning the whole thing privately makes sense. Example given:
  Insomnia Cookies (tiny ~800 sq ft units, high margin on flour and sugar). (2021)
- **Private chain scalability depends on low skill/service requirements.** The more
  specialized the skill needed to deliver value, the harder it is to scale privately
  because of service inconsistency. Subway works because anyone can make the same sandwich
  with the same recipe and process. **Productize the service** to minimize variability and
  reduce labor-skill requirements. (2021)
- **Franchise — cons:** very litigious, expensive to start (~**$750,000** in setup, legal,
  and filing fees before it is even running), and slow — usually **1–2 years** to see
  profit because the franchisee's upfront fee (~$50K) typically only covers the cost of
  acquiring that franchisee. You only really make money on **royalties off top-line** once
  they open. (2021)
- **Franchise — pros:** very high enterprise value, but the multiple only applies to the
  corporate franchise revenue (you are selling LLCs to other owners who own most of the
  profit). (2021)
- **Franchise math rule of thumb:** you generally need **100+ locations** for a franchise
  to make real sense — and **90–95% of franchises never exceed 100 locations**. "The
  franchise is not the end goal, it is the beginning of the next journey." (2021)
- **Licensing — pros:** lowest cost (almost nothing to license out a model), very high
  margins, high cash flow, and you can make money essentially day one. Best when you are
  newer and want cash flow sooner. (2021)
- **Licensing — cons:** not very defensible, low enterprise value / low exit multiples,
  unless the licensing agreement is extremely sticky. To get an attractive exit (~6x+
  multiple) you would want high yearly retention on licensees. (2021)
- **Software — cons:** high build cost, long time, very low cash flow. Do **not** outsource
  software to a third-party dev team unless you already have a technical software co-founder
  — he says he lost "$2–3 million" learning this. (2021)
- **Software — pros:** usually the **highest enterprise value on exit**, but only if you
  hit **80%+ yearly retention**; below that, buyers value it like a normal service with a
  tech element, not as software. (2021)
- **How he selects a vehicle as an investor:** based on the IP/value, required cash flow,
  upfront investment, time horizon, the entrepreneur's risk appetite, and how structurally
  sound the return is. If they know they'll win and build-out is cheap → own it privately;
  if they'll win but build-out is expensive (e.g. McDonald's ~$1M/location) → franchise to
  crowdsource the capital risk; if the IP isn't protectable and it's offer-driven (easy to
  copy) → license for speed and market share. (2021)
- **Self-reported portfolio figures:** he moved one portfolio company from a licensing model
  to owning locations (private chain) and grew it from **~$500K/yr to ~$12M/yr in 14
  months**, opening a new location every month (12 locations). His first business, Gym
  Launch, was a **licensing** business — chosen because he lacked capital and wanted cash
  flow sooner. Acquisition.com portfolio cited at **~$85M/yr revenue**. (2021)

## Topic notes — relation to the later "5 ways to scale"

This 2021 "4 ways" model is **a different framework from** the 2023 "5 ways to scale"
enumerated in [[../topics/business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]] §2
([[2023-07-01-yt-M2VP64Tc60w|5 Ways to Scale ANY Business]]). It is **not an earlier
4-way version** of that list:

- **The 2021 "4 ways" are monetization / packaging *structures* (business models or
  "vehicles")** for a service business: private chain, franchise, licensing, software.
  They answer *"how do I structure ownership and replication to build enterprise value?"*
- **The 2023 "5 ways" are TAM-expansion *moves***: up-market, down-market, adjacent,
  broader, narrower. They answer *"how do I expand who I can sell to?"* Neither list maps
  onto the other one-to-one.

Where it **does** connect within the topic page:

- It is a much earlier articulation of the same **enterprise-value / exit lens** that later
  crystallizes as **EV = customers × lifetime gross profit ÷ risk** ([[../topics/business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]] §5,
  2023). Already in 2021 he ranks the four vehicles by exit multiple vs. cash-flow timing,
  and already invokes the **80%+ retention** threshold for software value — a precursor to
  the "reliable recurring revenue / net-negative churn" criterion (§5b, #5) and the risk
  denominator.
- The three replication vehicles (**private chain, franchise, licensing**) directly
  foreshadow the **"three expansion paths"** in §8 (2026,
  [[2026-06-21-yt-S1Lq8ITwtHQ|Why a $25M Exit Is Harder Than a $100M Exit]]): M&A / de novo
  (organic) / franchise-licensing — where he again notes **franchise and licensing are far
  more sellable** and that **licensing ≈ franchising, just a different legal structure**.
  The 2021 talk is the earliest source in the corpus defining that legal distinction
  (three components = franchise; two = license).
- The **"productize the service to reduce skill/variability"** point aligns with the
  "get the owner/specialist out of delivery" throughline (§1, §3) that recurs across the
  scaling page.
