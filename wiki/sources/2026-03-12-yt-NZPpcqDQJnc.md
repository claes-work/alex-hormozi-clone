# Helping a Catering Company Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. Matthew runs a premium catering company in Sydney selling high-end
experiences to private clients for milestone events (~$2.8M this year, 40% net profit,
typical events of 60–100 people). He wants to reach $8M in two years and frames his
constraint as **seasonality**: ~65% of annual revenue lands in a six-month busy season
(roughly September–February), driven by a corporate end-of-year party rush that pushes
him beyond capacity, while winter is slower. He fears being supply-constrained/overstaffed
and is tempted to launch a separate year-round corporate prepackaged-delivery business to
counter-cycle the events side. Alex reframes the whole problem: seasonality is **"a feature,
not a bug"** of the industry (like lawn care, snow plowing, pool services, seasonal food
stores), and Matthew isn't actually as seasonal as he thinks since catering happens
year-round and he stays profitable in the offseason. Alex's core teaching is the distinction
between **volatility and risk**: something can be volatile without being risky if it's
*predictable* — Harry & David (a US chocolate company earning ~100% of annual profit in
December and losing money the other 11 months) and insurance (steady profits punctuated by
a Katrina-scale loss every several years) are both volatile but not risky because they're
predictable, and what's predictable can be planned for. Since nothing structurally stops
Matthew from simply doing *twice as much* of what already works, Alex tells him he has no
real problem to solve — he should stop chasing the counter-cyclical new business ("don't
chase the woman in the red dress") and instead focus on **doubling PPC and cracking Meta
ads**, which alone would hit his goal. He grounds this in **theory of constraints = focus**:
in a small business the scarcest resources are the owner's time, effort, and mental
bandwidth, and the discipline is saying no to shiny objects while competitors get distracted.
Ends with the community/Skool and roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames seasonality as a **feature, not a bug** of certain industries
  (catering, lawn care, snow plowing, pool services, seasonal food) — not a defect to be fixed.
- As of March 2026, a catering business that stays profitable year-round with a predictable
  busy/slow cycle does not have a real problem to solve — it has a predictable cycle, which is
  preferable to an unpredictable one.
- As of March 2026, Alex distinguishes **volatility from risk**: something can be highly
  volatile (large month-to-month swings) yet not risky if it is *predictable*; what is
  predictable can be planned for.
- As of March 2026 (examples of volatile-but-not-risky): Harry & David earns roughly 100% of
  annual profit in December and loses money the other 11 months; insurers profit steadily then
  absorb a rare catastrophe (e.g. a hurricane) — both predictable, therefore not risky.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises removing "the economy" from consideration as a growth lever —
  it is outside the owner's control, so it should be ignored.
- As of March 2026, if nothing structural stops an owner from doing twice the volume, the
  answer to seasonality is simply to **do more of what already works** rather than invent a
  counter-cyclical new business.
- As of March 2026, Alex's constraint recommendation for this business: **double PPC spend and
  crack Meta ads** — doing that one thing alone would double the business and hit the goal, so
  there is no reason to add three more initiatives ("why do four touch").
- As of March 2026, **theory of constraints is fundamentally about focus** — the scarcest
  resources in a small business are the owner's time, effort, and mental bandwidth, and growth
  comes from what you say no to.
- As of March 2026, a seasonal business owner should be grateful the "feature" distracts
  competitors with shiny objects while he keeps executing on the one thing that works.
