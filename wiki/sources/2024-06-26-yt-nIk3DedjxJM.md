# "'I'm Broke, What Business Do I Start?'"

## Summary

Solo teaching video in which Alex Hormozi walks a total beginner through a five-step
framework for choosing what business to start, defining who to serve, and crafting the
message to land the first five customers. He frames it around his role as co-owner of
Skool (school.com), citing that "one out of two" people who start a paid community on
Skool make their first dollar. The five steps are What (three Ps), Who, How part one
(good stuff / value equation positive), How part two (bad stuff), and Combine (a "money
Mad Lib" one-sentence offer), plus two bonuses: the unique mechanism and a warm-outreach
system for the first five customers.

## Key claims

- Businesses are born from one of three **Ps**: **Pain** (something you went through and
  overcame), **Profession** (your day job / past work skill), or **Passion** (what you
  read, watch, and listen to in your spare time).
- You don't need the perfect pick on the first try. Picking anything is just the first
  step that starts the feedback/iteration loop. The goal is your "**best bad idea**" as
  fast as possible — make a bad idea "less wrong" over time until it becomes a good idea.
- The **Who** falls into three categories: people like you, people you've helped before
  (paid or as a favor), or an underserved/emerging market. Hormozi recommends "people
  like you" because you already know their pains, demographic, and aspirations without
  research.
- Starting purely from market/trend analysis "tends to make mercenaries"; helping people
  like you "tends to make missionaries" who care more and stay in it for the long haul.
- Narrow the avatar using **five frames** — age, gender, profession, problems/pains,
  interests/passions — and pick at least three to be specific enough that "they know
  you're talking to them."
- **Specificity raises price.** A generic time-management PDF might sell for $19; time
  management for salespeople ~$199; for outbound salespeople ~$1,999; a full outbound
  time-management system for garden and power tools could be ~$10,000. More specific
  prospect = more premium price and near-zero competition ("blue ocean").
- **How part one** = the good stuff, which maps to the four elements of the value
  equation stated positively: easier, more guaranteed, faster, and exactly the dream
  outcome (and exactly the way they want to get it).
- **How part two** = the bad stuff. You motivate people only two ways: remove bad stuff
  they don't want, or give more good stuff they want. Frame the inverse of the value
  equation — risks avoided, slowness avoided, pain/sacrifice avoided — plus good things
  they normally give up but don't have to, and bad things they'd normally start doing but
  don't have to.
- **Specific pain beats generic claims.** "Lose weight fast" no longer works; a specific
  pain like thighs chafing in the sun passes people's "banner blindness" and makes them
  think "I can't believe they know this about me."
- To source pains if the avatar isn't you: the "**three-letter method**" — ask people you
  want to sell. Interview them like a sales call (problems, how long, what's painful,
  ideal experience, what they'd have to stop/start doing).
- **Step five (Combine)** = a "money Mad Lib": *"I help [who] get [good stuff] without
  [bad stuff]."* Examples given: helping 45-year-old women who just had kids get back
  into their high school jeans without giving up family time; non-fiction business authors
  get on the WSJ bestseller list without looking cringe.
- **Bonus 1 — unique mechanism**: a named, proprietary process (a checklist or ordered
  sequence of steps, then wrapped and named) that differentiates you and makes prospects
  feel "if I just had this thing I'd already be successful." Examples: P90X "muscle
  confusion," Weight Watchers points system.
- **Bonus 2 — first five customers**: greet someone, give a specific compliment, drop in
  the Mad Lib offer sentence, and ask if they know anybody who'd benefit — done 4 hours
  per day or until 100 outreaches. This is the "warm outreach" method from chapter one of
  the Leads book.
- Content framework mentioned: **promise, proof, plan**.
