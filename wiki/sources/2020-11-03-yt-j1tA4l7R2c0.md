# How To Close Prospects By Saying 'No' First (2020-11-03)

## Summary

A short (Nov 2020) sales training built around a story from Hormozi's teenage job at
Schwartz/Mano furriers in Baltimore, where he learned an "assume close" upsell technique:
phrasing an upsell as a negative question ("you don't want anything else, do you?") so that
contrarian prospects say "no" — which actually agrees to the sale. He argues this converts
small, high-margin bolt-on upsells at 90%+ instead of the typical 20–30%.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2020-11-03)

- Credits Richard Schwartz (fourth-generation furrier) as an unwitting early mentor whose
  business he studied and did "back-of-napkin math" on at 17–18; says Schwartz dominated the
  Maryland/Pennsylvania market "simply doing the boring work and being better at the everyday
  things."
- The technique applies best to **bolt-on / upsell** sales, not complex core-offer sales:
  supplements, meal plans, a scarf with an outfit, orthotics, special toothpaste, night serums
  — one or more high-margin things that go with the core offer.
- Typical upsell take rate is ~20–30%; the negative-framed assume close can push it to 90%+
  "because they don't know they're being sold."
- The **fur-coat example**: customers came in for a free pair of fur ear muffs offered with
  summer coat storage; the "storage + conditioning" was itself the upsell, presented as if it
  were the default. Staff would ask "you don't want anything else, do you?" and customers,
  primed to refuse being sold, said "no" — closing the conditioning upsell (a couple hundred
  dollars per coat across thousands of coats). The free ear muffs then "needed" storing and
  conditioning too, and a quick coat inspection surfaced further small paid fixes (loose button,
  loose seam).
- Mechanism: people are **contrarian** and pre-programmed to resist buying; a negative question
  routes their reflexive "no" into a yes at the subconscious level ("they're not even making the
  thought process").
- Usage patterns given: "You don't want to buy anything else, do you?" (confirms the
  recommendation) and "You don't want to pay full retail for that, right? ... so you want me to
  send it with this discount to the address on file?" (closes via a no).
- **Fries-and-Coke profit principle**: you don't make your money on the burger, you make it on
  the fries and Coke — small high-margin bolt-ons are huge, often-missed profit pockets,
  especially for brick-and-mortar service businesses; they liquidate acquisition cost and drive
  bottom-line profit.
- Mindset close: "winners" ask how they could use a tactic in their business; "losers" say
  "that wouldn't work in my business, my business is different."

**[Proto-upsell / assume-close framework]** — Early sales-floor version of the upsell component
of his later Money Models thinking. The "fries and Coke" and "liquidate acquisition cost via
high-margin bolt-ons" language is an informal precursor to his codified profit-maximization /
money-model concepts.
