# Why Alex Hormozi Doesn't Invest in Companies Under $1 Million

## Summary
A short interview clip in which Alex Hormozi explains why [[../entities/acquisition-com|Acquisition.com]]
sets a minimum profit threshold (~$1M in profit) for the businesses it will consider,
and why he actually prefers businesses at $2–3M+ in profit as a comfortable starting
point. His core argument is that more profit gives a business cushion — both for
volatility and for reinvesting in growth — whereas a small business can be halved in
profitability just by hiring two good people. He then pivots into the psychology of
small, founder-run businesses: because first-time founders extract all their money from
the business and equate the business's performance with their self-worth, both the
business and the founder's emotions become volatile, making the partnership more
stressful. He closes on the owner-vs-operator distinction — that owning a business and
running a business are two separate roles founders often conflate — using a painting
analogy to argue that worth should sit in the effort/craft under your control, not the
volatile output (sales/revenue/profit).

## Key claims
- Acquisition.com's stated minimum is roughly $1M in profit; Alex frames it as a genuine
  floor, not a soft target — "if you meet the minimum you're still probably not big
  enough, but we will take a look above the minimum." Moving the number to 900k would just
  make 900k the new minimum. (self-reported firm policy)
- He prefers businesses at "two three plus million in profit as a comfortable starting
  point," because more profit can be redeployed to accelerate growth faster.
- Everything is perspective on scale: a $100k/month profit sounds huge to someone in a
  nine-to-five, but from a business perspective a company can "hiccup" and lose $100k/month.
- A small business (e.g. ~$80k/month profit) lacks cushion for two things: volatility, and
  reinvesting in growth. Hiring two good people can "cut the profit in half."
- Founders at that size typically make all their money by taking all the money out of the
  business, so a profit dip directly hits their income and their self-worth — making both
  the business and the founder's emotions more volatile, and the partnership more stressful.
- First-time founders are the hardest case: their only entrepreneurial experience is this
  one business, so "this business equals my success" and all their eggs are in that basket.
- Owning a business and running a business are two separate roles that are "just often
  combined into one person because they just don't know how to separate them." The person
  who runs the business need not be the person who owns it.
- Painting analogy: if your worth is in the effort, craft, and attention to detail you put
  into the work — which is under your control — rather than in whether the painting sells
  (correlated to profit/revenue), you're insulated from the volatility, "especially when
  you're small."
