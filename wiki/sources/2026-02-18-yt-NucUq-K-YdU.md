# You Need to Find Your Constraint

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi talk (from a live Q&A / workshop setting, single speaker) laying out
his method for identifying and solving the single constraint that is limiting a
business. He frames constraint-thinking via the theory of constraints — a manufacturing
bottleneck analogy (a four-lane highway that narrows to one lane; widening the four-lane
sections does nothing to throughput) — and asserts every business has exactly **one**
constraint, but most owners either work on the wrong thing or work hard without thinking
about the constraint at all.

The core of the video is a **six-part "de-constraining" decision tree** (the "Mosy 6" —
all M's) that he says he distilled from doing ~10,000 Q&As and that he personally runs to
decide "what should I do next?" It starts from one question — *"Why can't we do more?"* —
because doing **more** of what already works is the highest risk-adjusted-return move
("new almost never works"). Every objection to doing more falls into one of the M's:
**Market, Metrics, Model, Money, Manpower** (he notes the natural order is Metrics →
Market, and that it's a cycle that loops back to the top). He drills into **Model**
(features-vs-bugs: every business has something hard, and the hard problem is precisely
what you get paid to solve; long time-horizons make almost any vehicle viable), **Money**
(splinters into leads-too-expensive / sales-too-low / LTV-too-low, plus churn), and
**Manpower**, for which he introduces a second diagnostic — the **"management diamond"**
for why a person (or you) isn't doing a thing.

Overlaps heavily with the existing constraint / theory-of-constraints thread in the wiki;
what is distinct here is the explicit **named 6-M decision tree** and the **management
diamond**, both presented as concrete, repeatable diagnostics.

## Key claims

_All claims dated 2026-02-18. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section._

- ★ **The "Mosy 6" de-constraining meta-framework (a fresh, concrete diagnostic).**
  A six-part decision tree for finding any business's constraint, framed as answering one
  question — "Why can't we do more?" — and sorting every reason "I can't do more" into six
  M's: **More, Market, Metrics, Model, Money, Manpower.** He says it's a newer framework,
  distilled from ~10,000 Q&As, that he runs on himself to decide what to work on next.
- Constraint defined two ways: quantitatively, the area giving the **highest return on
  effort/improvement**; from manufacturing, the **bottleneck** (the one-lane stretch that
  caps total throughput — improving anything else changes nothing).
- Every business has exactly **one** constraint. Most owners work on something they only
  *believe* is the constraint, or don't think about the constraint at all and just work
  hard hoping the business grows.
- **"Do more" is almost always the best move** because it's the highest risk-adjusted
  return: it already works, so more of it very likely also works. Experimentation is a
  high-probability path to *not* working — there are unlimited ways for something to fail
  and finite ways for it to work. "New almost never works."
- **Market constraint** is real but rare. Objection dissolves by checking saturation
  (e.g. servicing chiropractors with 100 customers out of ~50,000 available — not
  saturated, just unknown) or by duplicating what works into another geography.
- **Metrics constraint**: "I don't even know what *more* is." You must measure what you're
  currently doing before you can do more of it — so getting data becomes the constraint.
- ★ **Model constraint → features-vs-bugs.** (Credited to Suzanne Shifflet, his CFO at
  Gym Launch: "all businesses have [something hard]".) Every business has a hard component;
  people switch vehicles because another business's hard thing is hidden and its easy thing
  is visible. The hard problem is exactly **what you get compensated for solving** — solving
  it (e.g. a cleaning company building a recruiting/training engine for low-skill labor)
  can add $10–50M of enterprise value. Reframe: "for $50M I'd be willing to solve it."
- **Time-horizon claim (Model).** "Most of the goals you have are probably attainable —
  just not on the timeline you gave yourself." Almost any vehicle can make you
  exceptionally wealthy over decades. Example: his neighbor owns Panda Express — 5 locations
  × $3M topline, 27% net margins; over 45 years scales to ~$3.7B/yr, ~$1B/yr cash, tax-free
  via owning the real estate. Teaching him to "sell chicken courses" would be terrible advice.
- **Low-opportunity vehicles have an advantage**: unsophisticated competitors. Chasing
  hot vehicles (e.g. AI) means competing against the best-capitalized, smartest people.
- **Money constraint ("I can't afford to") splinters into three**: leads cost too much,
  you sell too few (sales too low), or LTV is too low. Requires pattern-recognition to
  find which. Common pattern: a break-even front-end with **no back-end** (an LTV problem —
  "sell the thing to all those people" and the business prints). Or a **churn** problem
  (churning in 3 months makes CAC irrelevant — fix churn; sub-causes: messaging, avatar,
  onboarding). Lead-cost levers: creative quality/quantity, funnel CRO, better lead-magnet
  offers. Sales levers: messaging, scripting, price points, follow-up, training, onboarding,
  sales-rep comp.
- **Manpower constraint**: not enough technicians/coaches/reps. Getting manpower loops
  back through the same tree (metrics on recruiting → market for talent → offer for talent
  → money per rep), i.e. the framework applies to **supply-side (talent) just like
  demand-side (customers)**. Making the right hires and being able to train them is one of
  the **highest-leverage** things a business owner can do.
- ★ **The "management diamond"** — why someone isn't doing something, in order of true
  likelihood (and the order is the *reverse* of where entrepreneurs jump): (1) they didn't
  know you wanted it done (→ write it down + set a deadline); (2) they didn't know how
  (→ train); (3) they didn't know when / by what deadline; (4) something is blocking them
  (competing priorities); (5) they didn't want to do it — which entrepreneurs assume first
  but is almost always last, because most people prefer to keep their job and to feel
  they're doing it well.
- **Apply the management diamond to yourself.** "I know I need to do more — why am I not?"
  Walk the same rungs (didn't know / don't know how / not urgent / something's blocking me).
  Unblocking example: customer-service "onesie-twosies" eat 80% of your time and cost $60k/yr
  to offload; profit is $400k. Dropping to ~$340k profit to reclaim 80% of your time is worth
  it — reasoning via **second- and third-order consequences** to the natural end.
- **Personal example**: post-launch he freed bandwidth, ran the tree, went "six questions
  down," and found his current constraint is **recruiting executives** — where ~80% of his
  time now goes (executive meetings, follow-ups). Once they're onboarded he'll run the tree again.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Every business has just one constraint. But the reality is that most business owners are
> not working on that constraint. They're working on something they believe to be the
> constraint. Or even more commonly, they're just working all day long, not even thinking
> about the constraint, [just] hoping the business will grow."

> "There's an unlimited amount of ways that something doesn't work. There's a finite amount
> of ways that it does. … New almost never works. And so that is why more is a significantly
> better return on assets if you look at risk adjusted."

> "The hard problem that you have in front of you is the thing that you get compensated for
> solving. Like that's the game."

> "Most of the goals that you have are probably attainable — just not on the timeline that
> you gave yourself."

> "Everyone is so concerned about getting really rich in the next 36 months [when] almost all
> vehicles can get you super rich in the next 36 years, and many, many orders of wealth along
> the way. We just don't think in those time horizons."

> "[Not wanting to do it] in my experience is the first place that we go to as entrepreneurs,
> and typically the last one that's reality. Most people, when they have a job, prefer to keep
> it. … And so it's usually one of these other issues."
