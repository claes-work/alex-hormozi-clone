# You Just Aren't Doing Enough...

## Summary
Solo storytelling clip on the **volume of effort** required to win. Alex opens with an origin story from his first gym (started at 23): wanting to fill it, he asked a mentor with tanning salons how to get customers, was told "we just put flyers out," and printed **300 flyers** because "paper was expensive and I was broke." The only response was an angry man claiming a flyer had scratched his Mercedes; Alex panic-hung-up and got zero members. When he called the mentor to complain, the mentor was incredulous at the number: he *tested* with **5,000 flyers** just to see if a single flyer worked, and if it worked he ran **5,000 every day** — roughly **150,000 flyers a month**. Alex realized he had been doing "one 5,000th" of the mentor's effort — the mentor wasn't working twice as hard, he was "working 5,000 times harder." From this Alex builds his core argument: beginners (which he defines here as anyone under **$1M**) carry a "big misconception about the volume of effort that is required to win," and it "takes so much more volume and for so much longer than you expect." His prescription: find people actually succeeding, learn how much volume they're doing, **and then double it** — double because they're probably more skilled than you. He illustrates with a skill-vs-volume model: if you both make 100 calls, the experienced person converts far more (skill = leverage on input), so the beginner must do 200 both to match output *and* to close the skill gap over time. He closes with a competitiveness anecdote — seeing a competitor post family photos on Facebook and thinking "I'm getting ahead of him" by working — and the caveat that unlike a 2-minute Rocky training montage, the real-life grind "can last 5 years." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, beginners hold a major misconception about the sheer **volume of effort** needed to win; it "takes so much more volume and for so much longer than you expect."
- As of March 2026, Alex defines a "beginner" for this volume concept as any business owner making **less than $1M**.
- As of March 2026, his recommended method: find people who are actually succeeding at your goal, learn how much volume they do, **and then double it** — double because they are probably more skilled than you at it.
- As of March 2026, **skill is leverage on input**: equal input (e.g. 100 calls each) yields unequal output because the more experienced operator converts more (10 appointments vs. 1 or 0); doing double the volume lets a beginner both match output now and close the skill gap over time.
- As of March 2026, there is "the work you do to match someone and then the work you do to catch up" — beating a more-skilled competitor requires volume beyond parity.
- As of March 2026, a fast shortcut is getting around someone already operating at high volume, because proximity lets you "leapfrog levels."
- As of March 2026 (origin story, self-reported): at his first gym he put out **300 flyers** expecting them to fill it, got only an angry call about a scratched Mercedes, and zero members; his mentor tested with **5,000 flyers** and ran **5,000/day (~150,000/month)** when one worked — Alex was doing "one 5,000th" of that effort ("rounded to zero").
- As of March 2026, the competitive-mindset framing: people over-identify as a "special snowflake" and can't comprehend an operator working literally thousands of times harder; from an output perspective a low-effort beginner is "a rounding error."
- As of March 2026, the grind is longer than motivational imagery suggests: "the Rocky cut scene lasts like 2 minutes in a movie and it can last 5 years in real life."
