# I Own a Hail Damage Repair Business. I Don't Know Who to Hire

## Summary
Live business Q&A on @MoreMozi in which a founder ("Joseph") who runs a $5.5M auto hail-damage repair business across four locations asks Alex Hormozi how to build a leadership bench and design its compensation to scale without eroding owner control or margins. Hormozi reframes the ask: at ~$5M the founder needs a VP/director-level operator (not true C-suite titles), and the single biggest constraint is finding a right-hand operator so problems stop routing back to the owner. He advises sourcing via LinkedIn outreach and Indeed ads, favoring targeted outreach (~500 people) to operators in similarly-sized auto businesses, with relocation packages as signing bonuses. He stresses hiring leaders of people over process-lovers, since a strong operator is "two levels above" and can run almost any business. On compensation he lays out a full framework: a 10–20% profit-share pool distributed among leaders (the percentage stays fixed while the pie grows, making leaders think like owners); the four things equity actually carries (cash flow, sale, risk, control) — most people want cash flow and upside, not risk or control; phantom profits-interest that vests over time and pays out on a sale only if the person is present and tenured; and reverse-engineering compensation from a target on-target-earnings (OTE) figure rather than picking an arbitrary percentage. He also argues that private multi-unit expansion requires an on-the-ground operator in each store with no tie to the bottom line, and profit-share is what lets you truly decentralize.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed, self-reported] The questioner does $5.5M/year in auto hail-damage repair across four locations, wants to reach $15M next year, and says his biggest constraint under $10M is people leadership.
- (2026-02-11) At ~$5M revenue a founder is probably mistitling roles: he needs VP/director-level people, not an actual C-suite (COO/CMO); correct titling also eases the compensation question.
- (2026-02-11) The single biggest hire right now is an operator — a right hand — because everything currently routes back to the owner on operations.
- (2026-02-11) Anyone with auto / auto-repair experience is helpful, but industry fit is a bonus, not the core requirement.
- (2026-02-11) For sourcing, the two most fruitful channels are LinkedIn outreach and (surprisingly) simple Indeed ads; Indeed yields lower-quality candidates but you can still get lucky.
- (2026-02-11) This hiring level sits at the transition between running ads and doing targeted outreach; for the caliber wanted, expect to reach out to ~500 people.
- (2026-02-11) Targeted-outreach pitch: find people already in that role at auto businesses of similar size and say "you want to do that thing you did there, over here"; offer a relocation package as a signing bonus if the local talent pool is thin.
- (2026-02-11) Hire a leader of people, not a process-lover; entrepreneurs get tricked by candidates who love "processes and systems" but hate talking to people — that is not the relief the owner needs.
- (2026-02-11) You want someone your team would admire and look up to in character, so that when something bad happens the team goes to them, not you.
- (2026-02-11) A really good operator is "two levels above" — they don't fix cars, they fix people — and can therefore come into almost any business and run it.
- (2026-02-11) Compensation is like a "reverse sale": the offer is proportional to the leverage the candidate has; less experience means less leverage and less to give up, while someone who has scaled a business 5x-to-50x multiple times will justifiably want more.
- (2026-02-11) Giving a leader a small tie to overall profitability is a good idea; run a profit pool of 10–20% (he likes ~10–15%) distributed among the leaders — the percentage stays fixed while the pie grows, so leaders think like owners and accept diluting their slice to bring in more leaders who expand the total pie.
- (2026-02-11) Equity carries four things — cash flow, sale (exit), risk, and control; most people say they want equity but don't actually want risk or control, only cash flow and exit upside, so the right structure gives them the latter without the former.
- (2026-02-11) A phantom profits-interest structure (illustratively 4% vesting at 1%/year) comes back if the person leaves, and pays out their vested percentage only if a sale occurs while they're present and after a minimum tenure (e.g. 12 or 24 months); the whole grant can be accelerated at sale if desired.
- (2026-02-11) The downside of that phantom/option structure for the employee is paying regular income taxes on the sale bonus; the alternative (real equity) would mean paying taxes on the granted equity up front with no other benefit, which most people won't do.
- (2026-02-11) He has yet to see anyone expand well in private multi-unit (brick-and-mortar) expansion without the equivalent of an operator with boots on the ground in each store who has no tie to the bottom line.
- (2026-02-11) Profit share for store operators is not about handing current staff a cut, but about identifying the caliber of person needed to run a location without handholding — which lets you truly decentralize and expand.
- (2026-02-11) Two structures for operator upside: (1) for fast-growth, geographically-unconstrained businesses, give a high percentage (e.g. 30%) of year-over-year growth, which sets a new high-water mark each year; (2) for geographically-constrained local businesses (like his), avoid growth-based pay — growth is hard and gets people discouraged — and instead give a profit-share percentage of profit (not revenue).
- (2026-02-11) The percentage itself doesn't matter — reverse-engineer it from a target OTE: decide what the caliber of person should earn (e.g. $150K/year), then back into the percentage from the store's profit.
- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed, self-reported] Cumulative profit across the four stores is close to $200K/month (~$50K/month per store), with margins around 40%; roughly 80% of current revenue comes from one storm-related location.
- (2026-02-11) Worked example: at $50K/month profit per store, a 10% share pays $5K/month (~$60K/year) extra — turning a $100K base into ~$160K OTE.
- (2026-02-11) Set three lines in the sand: bad performers get fired, mediocre performers hit on-target earnings, and top performers make a defined higher number; take those figures and reverse them into percentages to present.
