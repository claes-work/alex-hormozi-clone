# How to Turn Your Job Into a Business

## Summary
Interview clip (host asks short prompts marked `>>`; all substance is Alex). Alex answers how to turn a **profession** into a service/side-hustle/business, and calls the profession "by far the fastest angle" because you already own something valuable — a skill people already pay you for — so the only two things left to add are **promotion** (letting people know) and **conversion** (getting them to pay). For promotion he lays out the **Core Four**: the only four things one person can do to get customers, mapped on a 2x2 of {strangers vs. people you know} × {one-to-one vs. one-to-many} — warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, and posting content. Everyone must start with the Core Four; higher-leverage moves (affiliates/partners, employees, agencies) unlock only after it. For conversion he walks his **CLOSER** sales framework end to end: **C**larify why they responded, **L**abel their problem, **O**verview past experiences (the "pain cycle" that raises short-term motivation by expanding felt deprivation), **S**ell the vacation (the outcome, not the mechanics; keep the pitch tight — he targets under ~320 words — and structure any pitch in three points each with a back-pocket anecdote), **E**xplain away concerns (the five objections: time, money, decision-maker, stall/"let me think," and preferences — each a decision-making fallacy: time is priorities, money is value, decision-maker is support-not-permission), and **R**einforce the decision (added years later; the 24 hours post-purchase disproportionately set the whole relationship). He then closes the loop for the professional: use the Core Four, run CLOSER, sell your service by **moonlighting** — and reframes that people blame their "9-to-5" when the leverage is their **"5-to-9"** (5am-9am and 5pm-9pm = eight prime hours, a full second workday). Risk-averse himself, he advises keeping the job if you have a family and mortgage, supplementing income, and only jumping once off-hours income surpasses the full-time income and holds for ~6 months (because there's volatility). Two capstone principles: **"volatility is a symptom of insufficient volume,"** and his **Rule of 100** (100 reachouts, or 100 minutes posting, or $100/day on ads, daily). Once you know your input→output ratio (e.g. ~700 efforts per sale), scaling is just multiplying volume — the "compression process" that separates small from large businesses. He illustrates with his own start in content: a big influencer told him he was "doing too little" (Alex posted every other day; the influencer ran 70-80 posts/day across platforms). His improvement loop: run large volume, isolate the top 10% by response, replicate what was different about them in the next batch.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, the profession is the fastest of the three angles into business because you already exchange the valuable thing for money — you only have to add promotion and conversion.
- As of March 2026, there are only four things one person can do to get customers (the Core Four): warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, and posting content — a 2x2 of strangers-vs-known and one-to-one-vs-one-to-many.
- As of March 2026, everyone must start with the Core Four; higher-leverage channels (affiliates/partners, employees, agencies who promote on your behalf) only unlock after it — even partner-driven growth started with a message you sent.
- As of March 2026, conversion follows the CLOSER framework: Clarify, Label (the problem), Overview past experiences, Sell the vacation, Explain away concerns, Reinforce the decision.
- As of March 2026, the "pain cycle" (overviewing past attempts) raises short-term motivation by expanding felt deprivation around the desired outcome, which increases its priority enough to act.
- As of March 2026, sell the outcome not the mechanics ("sell the vacation, not the plane flight"); most people talk too much — he targets a pitch under ~320 words, structured in three points, each backed by a short anecdote.
- As of March 2026, there are only five objections — time, money, decision-maker, stall ("let me think about it"), and preferences — and each is a decision-making fallacy: time is really priorities, money is really value, decision-maker is really needing support not permission.
- As of March 2026, the "R" (Reinforce) was added years later; people decide about a business in the first 24 hours post-purchase, so that window preframes the whole relationship.
- As of March 2026, professionals should start by moonlighting; the "9-to-5" isn't the obstacle — the "5-to-9" is, meaning 5am-9am and 5pm-9pm give eight prime hours, a full second workday.
- As of March 2026, he recommends (being risk-averse himself) that people with a family and mortgage keep the job, supplement income, and only jump when off-hours income surpasses full-time income and holds for ~6 months, because of business volatility.
- As of March 2026, "volatility is a symptom of insufficient volume" — inconsistent leads/sales mean not enough Core Four activity.
- As of March 2026, the Rule of 100: do 100 per day — 100 reachouts, 100 minutes posting content, or $100/day on ads (or all three).
- As of March 2026, once you know your input→output ratio (e.g. ~700 efforts per sale), scaling is just multiplying volume; this "compression process" is the main thing separating very small from very large businesses, and small owners badly underestimate the volume leaders put out.
- As of March 2026, his improvement loop for any system: run large volume, take the top 10% by response/views/clicks, identify what was different about them, and replicate that in the next batch.
