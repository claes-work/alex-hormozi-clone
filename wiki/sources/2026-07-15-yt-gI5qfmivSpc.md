# 'Demand Isn't My Problem. Delivery Is.'

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A. The questioner, **Will**, runs a **teen life coaching / mentoring
business** (mentors for middle- and high-school kids, sold to parents) at ~$2.2M revenue
with a goal of ~$5M by end of next year. His situation inverts the usual pattern: demand —
especially for boys' coaching — exceeds what he can deliver, churn is low, and his
bottleneck is **coaches** (he's only tried to scale for ~16 months and has never tapped
recruiting pipelines for 22–30-year-old male mentors).

Alex treats the supply side exactly like the demand side. First, the **hiring avatar**: when
sourcing for a role, he maps **behaviors rather than titles** — list the micro-behaviors a
teen life coach must do, then find existing roles with the **highest behavioral crossover**
so the remaining skill gap to train is as small as possible. Off the top of his head: camp
counselors, rec-league coaches, and young underpaid teachers who would love to work from
home. He then asks "what stops you from doing that?" — and Will admits nothing does; he
never even tried. Alex gives **three sourcing channels**: (1) LinkedIn title search plus
mass outreach, (2) teacher forums — pay the forum owner or post free since job
opportunities are a value-add for the community ("one or two forums would be literally all
you need"), (3) ads targeting teachers. He predicts talent volume won't be the issue; the
*next* constraint will be building a **talent attraction-conversion system** — a
"click-to-close" funnel for coaches mirroring the customer funnel, with metrics — driving
toward a supply-side **LTGP:CAC**: **cost of acquiring talent vs. lifetime gross profit per
employee**, "the same as LTV to CAC but on the supply side for team."

Second question: Will's personal-brand audience is asking for books and public speaking.
Alex's answer: no — **do more of what works**; he's already overwhelmed, and he mocks the
logic of "I have so much demand so I think I'm going to go get more." When Will reframes it
as "more scalable products for the demand while they wait," Alex elevates it to the
strategic question — **what business do you want to build?** The most *valuable* version is
productizing with an **AI mentor**, but that transforms a service business into a
technology business, which is "not necessarily my recommendation"; he guesses that being
**pro-human** will do really well as messaging to parents "for a while." Verdict: great
margins, demand, and product — "you have everything. We just need to do more." The video
closes with Alex's standard promo for the free **$100M Scaling Roadmap**
(acquisition.com/roadmap: 10 stages, functions across product/marketing/sales/customer
success/recruiting/IT/HR/finance) and a call-booking CTA.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-15. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Hiring avatar = behaviors, not titles.** When sourcing for a specific role, map the
  micro-behaviors the role requires, then find existing roles with the **highest crossover**
  with those behaviors, so the skill gap you must train is as small as possible.
- **Worked example for teen-mentor recruiting:** camp counselors, rec coaches, and young
  underpaid teachers (who "probably got into teaching" for mission/lifestyle and would love
  to work from home). Those three buckets alone are "endless."
- **"What stops you from doing that?" exposes inaction.** The owner concedes nothing stops
  him — he never even tried. _(The "nothing stops me / never tried" admission is the
  questioner's, audience-attributed.)_
- **Three talent-sourcing channels:** (1) LinkedIn — search the titles, "mass blast" them;
  (2) teacher forums — pay the forum owner or post free, since job opportunities are a
  value-add to the community; one or two forums would be all you need; (3) run ads
  targeting teachers. All three will work; finding talent won't be the issue.
- **After sourcing volume, the constraint moves to conversion: build the talent
  attraction-conversion system.** Training/onboarding exists; what's missing is the
  "click-to-close" funnel for recruits, built out with collected metrics — the recruiting
  mirror of the customer funnel.
- **Supply-side LTGP:CAC.** The target metric is lifetime gross profit per employee vs.
  cost of acquiring talent — "the same as LTV to CAC but on the supply side for team." Once
  you know dollars-in/dollars-out on both the demand and supply funnels, you can just
  scale. _(Matches the LTGPE:CAT framing already recorded from the 2026-06-30 trades clip —
  see [[../topics/business/scaling-and-enterprise-value]] §6.)_
- **Decline audience-requested product lines; do more of what works.** On books and public
  speaking requested by his audience: "No, I would just [do] more of what works. You're
  already overwhelmed, why would you do more?" He ridicules the reflex of stacking new
  demand channels while delivery is the constraint.
- **"What business do I want to build?" is the strategic filter for productization.** The
  most valuable version of the business would be productizing coaching with an **AI
  mentor** — but that would transform a service business into a technology business, which
  is **not necessarily his recommendation**. The founder must pick who he wants to be in
  terms of service delivery.
- **Pro-human as a messaging bet (dated guess).** Alex guesses that staying pro-human
  (human mentors, not AI) "will probably do really well from a messaging standpoint for a
  while" with parents. Explicitly framed as his guess, as of 2026-07-15.
- **Verdict: no missing piece — just volume.** Great margins + demand + a working product
  means "you have everything. We just need to do more."
- **Standard outro promo (marketing, not coaching):** free "$100M Scaling Roadmap" at
  acquisition.com/roadmap — self-described as ~200 hours of analysis over portfolio
  companies, 10 growth stages, per-function guidance (product, marketing, sales, customer
  success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance) — plus a CTA to book a call with acquisition.com.
  _(Self-reported promotional claims.)_
