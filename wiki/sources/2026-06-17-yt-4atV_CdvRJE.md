# He's Charging $7K. I'd Charge $22K

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises an optometry-practice consulting firm that helps optometrists improve profit and receives customers from lens manufacturers. Alex says the firm is wildly undercharging if manufacturers can afford to pay the current fee, anchors possible pricing around the absolute profit improvement created, and suggests a higher upfront transformation offer plus a continuity product built around bulk-buying / discount association economics. He also points toward a longer-term rollup / common-flag opportunity.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - optometry-practice consultant. His revenue, EBITDA, referral sources, manufacturer relationships, and client impact are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If a manufacturer can cover the consulting fee from wholesale-margin gains, the consultant is probably undercharging.
- Pricing should be tied to absolute customer profit improvement, not just relative percentage improvement.
- If the average client gains roughly $50,000 in bottom-line profit, a fee around $15,000 may be more justified than $7,000.
- A structure where the manufacturer pays and the client later pays can become a "both sides" monetization path.
- Optometrists are attractive avatars because they are creditworthy and accustomed to financing practice investments.
- The higher target price could be $35,000-$45,000 if the service can reliably create a larger profit gain.
- A useful model is a large upfront "fix the business" fee plus an annual continuity offer.
- The continuity offer can be framed as an association / bulk-buying discount program where savings offset membership cost.
- The long-term strategic play could be organizing practices under one flag and eventually rolling them up.
