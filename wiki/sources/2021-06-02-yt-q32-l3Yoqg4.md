# I Taught 116+ Salesmen My Closing Framework (2021-06-02)

## Summary

A guest sales training Hormozi delivered (via screen-share presentation) to a room of
high-ticket closers, hosted by "Mike." He walks through what he frames as the three
frameworks behind Gym Launch's sales results: (1) the **CLOSER** closing framework, (2)
a **conviction/tonality** framework for people newer to sales, and (3) scaling a sales
team (touched on only briefly for time). Notably, the CLOSER acronym is already fully
named and structured here in mid-2021 — this is not a rough proto-version but the
essentially-mature framework, with Hormozi noting the final "R" was a piece he "added
years later." Only Hormozi delivers substantive content; the host mostly introduces and
prompts.

## The closing framework (CLOSER, as taught)

Hormozi says he arrived at it "after pouring over hundreds of scripts" and noticing that
hitting the same milestones consistently produced the sale. He stresses it works B2C and
B2B, and for $500 tickets as much as $100,000 tickets — "the process is the same." The
acronym:

- **C — Clarify why they're there.** Establish the objective/problem: "why are you
  here, why'd you take the call, what's the problem, what are we trying to solve." Must
  be nailed early; if the prospect only "wants more information," dig: "what did you want
  the information to solve?" You do not ask for the sale until they've clarified they
  need a solution ("sit in the pain").
- **L — Label them with a problem.** Restate it back so they own it: "so what I'm
  hearing is you're struggling with this, does that sound about right?" They have to say
  it and own it — "I can't cure cancer until you admit that you have it."
- **O — Overview their past pain.** "What have you tried so far, how long, how did that
  work out, what else have you tried." He calls this running the **pain cycle**
  repeatedly to exhaust options and gather "ammo." Key move: explain it's **not their
  fault** — they were missing one piece of the equation, which you'll provide.
- **S — Sell the vacation** (sell the destination, not the mechanics). Use short
  **anecdotal stories** to break the prospect's limiting belief; keep the pitch under
  three minutes; avoid jargon/"technobabble" about modules and features because "all of
  that sounds like work." Sell the result (Maui), not the plane flight (TSA lines,
  layovers, baggage claim = your meal plans, macros, funnels, targeting). You always sell
  the same destination; the tiers only differ by level of service / how they get there.
- **E — Explain away their concerns.** Handle the three big obstacles (see claims).
  Memorize the obstacle-overcomes in advance because "that's the red zone — not where you
  start thinking about what you're going to say." Rely on the prospect's past agreements
  (with spouse/partner/self), sometimes "forgiveness rather than permission," and delayed
  closes (card down now, tear up the contract if the partner says no). For "I need to
  think about it": "what's your main concern? Let's think about it together." The three
  things a prospect actually needs a yes on: do you like the product / do you like us /
  do you have (or can access) the money.
- **R — Reinforce the decision.** Added "years later" once he realized "the sale
  continues throughout the entire customer relationship." The first 48 hours after
  purchase decide long-term retention: personalized welcome video, handwritten card,
  strong onboarding. Clients must be re-sold at onboarding and each check-in.

The three surrounding frameworks he lists: **(1) closer framework** (questions that get
yes), **(2) conviction framework** (control tone so a believer beats a seasoned rep),
**(3) scaling** (duplicate the skill in others — teaching to sell is more valuable than
selling).

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-06-02)

- Only three things are ever said on a sales call: **questions, restatements, and
  anecdotal stories**. Talking too much about the product is "the number one mistake"
  even his own reps make.
- Train salespeople on **the prospect, not the product**. A rep can know the product
  perfectly but lose the deal by misarticulating the prospect's problem, so it doesn't
  feel "for him."
- The reason people buy is **how understood they feel** — hence restating the pain back
  accurately and "shutting up when they're talking."
- There are essentially **three obstacles**: price, decision-maker, and the stall ("I
  need to think about it"). People stall because they fear making a mistake and prefer to
  "let life make the decision for them."
- Sell "**three pillars**" of the solution — the human brain can't hold more than three
  (e.g., fitness / nutrition / accountability; or timely / qualified / exclusive leads).
- On a call **you always have the upper hand** — the prospect admitted a problem they
  haven't solved, so they must be missing a piece: "you are in an unlosable situation
  unless you choose to lose the frame."
- **Words are only ~10% of a sale; tonality is ~90%.** Even the most tested script fails
  if delivered flat. He credits Jordan Belfort's book for sharpening his focus on
  tonality (teaching *how* to ask the question, not just the words).
- **Conviction corrects tone** — the shortcut for newer reps who can't yet train
  tonality. Believing in the product makes you sell it the right way because you actually
  want to help.
- The **most important part of the sale is the product**, because ethical people can't
  sell hard on something they don't believe in; if a business has no testimonials, "find
  another business."
- Conviction tactics: re-read testimonials out loud daily as a team; owners should fix
  everything possible about the product and never blame the customer for lack of success
  ("what could I have done to make the next person like them succeed" → plug the hole).
- Belief is not binary but a matter of degree: "would I bet $1,000 on this product… would
  I sell my mother this product?"
- Sales reps burn out "not because they can't handle no, but because they can't handle
  how they feel about themselves" from not putting the work in.
- Training regimen for his closers: 60 min/day, 5 days/week — 25 min reading the script
  aloud, then drilling obstacles ("I need to think about it," "I need to talk to my
  partner," "this is expensive"), plus reviewing call recordings.
- Business/context stats cited: lifetime **36:1 return on ad spend**, ~**$3M** total ad
  spend; ~**4,000** one-on-one fitness sales, **20–25 consults/day for ~4 years**; over
  **1,000 clients** to $100k/year and **100 gyms to seven figures** using this framework;
  ~**$1.7M** donated over three years. His origin pivot: "attach the phone to the funnel"
  (sell expensive stuff on the front end) turned a 2:1 loss into a 10:1 return.
