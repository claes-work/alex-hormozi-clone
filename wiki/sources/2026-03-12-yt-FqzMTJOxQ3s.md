# How I Think About Growing on Social Media

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-screen host asks the questions; Alex is the guest — only Alex's words are voice data). The host opens on how Alex is "compounding" across Twitter/LinkedIn/etc. and whether he's tracking month-over-month growth. Alex reframes growth entirely: he treats **compounding as a consequence of the work, not the goal**. His operating principle is **"too good to fail"** — if a piece of content is high quality and he's proud to have it out there, he'll push it even when he knows it won't go wide or perform, because doing the basics well *every time* over a long enough horizon produces the result on its own. The conversation then narrows to his **Twitter workflow**, the only channel he posts to directly (no ghostwriters). Tweets come from two places: he **wakes up with three or four in his head** and won't get out of bed until he's put them down (so his feed is front-loaded and refined early in the day), and ideas that surface naturally in meetings — the team says "that's a tweet" and he fires it off. There's no cadence: two days silent, then eleven tweets when he's "in the zone." He argues **Twitter is the single greatest tool for learning to write** because it forces word concision and gives immediate, language-level feedback; alongside the **Hemingway app**, it's improved his writing more than anything (Hemingway when drafting books, Twitter for concision/feedback). He recounts a tech entrepreneur with two unicorn exits pressing him for his "Twitter strategy" / engagement-group playbook and refusing to believe the real answer: "I tweet [shit] as I think of it." Finally, he explains *why* his tweets read as "violent" and themed — he's usually **tweeting to himself**, writing in a moment of discomfort to bang an idea into his own head ("be patient," "relax," "you don't need to care about this person's opinion") to get past the moment and move on. Ends with the $100M scaling-roadmap promo.
