# How to Increase Cash Flow in a Consulting Firm

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a financial-planning business serving people 55+ that is growing quickly but feels cash-flow constrained. Alex says the unit economics make sense and the problem is mostly growth-throttle math: the business can either pull cash forward with an upfront planning fee, raise equity to accelerate, or grow at the rate its bootstrapped cash flow allows. He emphasizes risk tolerance, reputational risk, and not letting service quality collapse while chasing speed.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - financial-planning business owner. His revenue, AUM model, organic acquisition, headcount, advisor capacity, and growth goals are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If fast growth is constrained by front-loaded hiring and onboarding cost, the model can be put into a spreadsheet and throttled deliberately.
- One way to grow faster is to pull cash forward with an upfront planning fee that can be credited toward the first year of service.
- Another way is raising equity if the opportunity is large and returns on capital are strong.
- If bootstrapped, the business cannot go below zero cash; the buffer depends on projection confidence and risk tolerance.
- There is no universal minimum net-income percentage; it depends on the founder's tolerance for risk.
- Alex prefers the Keith Cunningham-style test: upside, downside, and whether the founder can live with the downside.
- The biggest risk of growing too fast may be reputational rather than fiscal if service quality drops and organic lead flow dries up.
- Alex frames the opportunity as "yours to not mess up" rather than something that requires reckless acceleration.
