# The Biggest Mistake Community Businesses Make With Offers

## Summary

In this interview clip Alex Hormozi lays out a complete playbook for how community/membership businesses (Skool-style paid communities) should think about their offer. His core thesis: the biggest mistake community businesses make is treating the offer as purely the "grand slam offer" (the value/product) while ignoring the single most underrated component of a community's offer — **the qualifications of who is allowed in**. Who is in the room *is* part of the offer. He argues communities should behave like a party with a doorman: segment aggressively, draw a hard line about who belongs, and be willing to kick out members who don't fit the vibe — even high-paying ones (he says he removed members paying $3,000/month). The payoff of holding that line early is compounding: more self-policing, higher retention ("stick"), higher gross margins, and more affiliate referrals, because members feel "these people are just like us."

He frames the hard part as being willing to **say no to money** for roughly the first six months in order to curate the culture and build the community's *brand* during its "gestation period" — using a garden/rose-bush metaphor (protect it, weed it). He and the co-host acknowledge the chicken-and-egg problem (when you're brand new it's hard to get even one person), and Alex resolves it with the **"accordion"** model: oscillate between quantity and quality — first get quantity, then select down, then overcorrect and let more in, back and forth. He prescribes **objective qualification standards** (B2B: revenue, customer count, headcount, investable assets; B2C: demographic filters like "a woman over 45 with at least two kids"), notes that **Skool's own data tells you who your most active members are** so you can go find more of them and narrow your messaging, and describes the end state: the community becomes a self-reinforcing **snowball / network effect** that stops needing you to provide value and instead becomes the magnet — the shift "from me to you, from broadcast to network." Short term, he says, it's fine to sell services and one-on-one calls to make money; long term the value comes from the quality of the people inside. Ends with his standard acquisition.com/roadmap CTA.

## Key claims (2026-02-16, Alex-attributed, paraphrased unless quoted)

**The mistake and the reframe**
- The biggest thing most communities are missing is **qualifications** — they lean too hard into the "grand slam offer" (the value/product) and forget that **who is in the community is part of the offer**, and arguably its most underrated component.
- A community's offer is not just the content/access; it's the *people in the room*. Nobody thinks about this.

**The doorman / segmentation principle**
- A community should work like a real-world party with **someone at the door** deciding who can and can't come in. Let everyone in and you get "a weird party" — many paid communities feel weird precisely because not everyone there should be there.
- The whole point of a community is to **segment** — "you draw a line in the sand," everyone else out, this group in — creating "a tiny little world" you then have to protect.
- Be **more aggressive about kicking people out**. Frame it as fit, not judgment: not "you're a bad person," but "this isn't a fit for you, that's okay — we have a different vibe in here."

**Why holding the line early compounds**
- Hold the line **earlier** and the work required to run the community later drops dramatically. What goes *up*: self-policing by members, **stick/retention**, **gross margins**, and **affiliate referrals** (members say "these people are just like us — you've got to join").
- The difficulty is that, like most great things, it takes longer at the beginning — because **you have to say no to money**, which most people won't do.
- If you're willing to **say no to money for at least ~6 months**, you can curate the community and build its culture. You literally **create the brand of the community during that gestation period**, and that brand then proliferates — but you must keep protecting it.
- **Garden / rose-bush metaphor**: growing a good community is like a garden — it takes a while before the good plants come in, and even then you have to protect it and weed it. A whole rose bush with weeds all over it ruins the entire view.
- People aren't aggressive enough about pruning because **they like watching their MRR go up** ("MR numbers"). Alex says he has kicked out members paying **$3,000/month** because they were "messing up the garden" — he doesn't care about the revenue if they hurt the culture.

**The chicken-and-egg problem (co-host raises it; Alex resolves it)**
- Co-host's caveat: this advice is complicated for beginners — "if you're a noob it's hard to even get one person." You want to get *some* people in early; only once you can get a lot of people do you control quality. Rule of thumb: "the harder it is to get in, the better it is" (like all good teams and clubs) — but that's hard to start with.
- Alex's resolution — the **accordion**: it swings back and forth between **quantity and quality**. First get quantity; then select down to a small, high-quality subset; then you overcorrect and realize you need more people, so you let some more in — and you keep oscillating.

**Objective qualification standards**
- Use **objective, stated standards** for who gets in:
  - **B2B**: a revenue threshold, a minimum number of customers, a headcount, or investable assets (for an investing-oriented community).
  - **B2C**: demographic filters are legitimate too — e.g. "you need to be a woman who's over 45 and have at least two kids."
- **Let the platform's data guide you**: Skool tells you who your **most active members** are. Ask "who are they?" then "how do I find more of those people?" — this in turn **narrows your marketing messaging**.
- Qualifying does **not** shrink your business if you have enough traffic — there are "more than enough" of any given avatar (e.g. 45-year-old moms with two kids). With enough traffic it eventually **snowballs**.

**End state: snowball → network effect**
- The goal everyone wants: the **community itself becomes the magnet** for more of that same type of person. At that point **you stop having to provide the value** and the community becomes a **true network effect**.
- Sequence over time: **short term you sell services / one-on-one calls** to make money (nothing wrong with that when starting out — you should do it). Then **shift from "me" to "you," from broadcast to network** — and that shift comes from **the quality of the people inside** the community.
