# How to get customers to pay forever

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi argues that fortunes are built by getting customers to keep paying rather than by chasing one-time sales. He frames retention as harnessing time: a modest, steady flow of customers who never leave compounds into a large recurring base, and the way to earn that is to relentlessly improve the product via customer feedback until people don't want to stop paying. He uses his own Gym Launch (5,000 gyms) as a cautionary case where failing to retain cost him an estimated ~$900M in enterprise value.

## Key claims

- The core distinction: be in the "reorder business," not the "selling business." Most people have one-time sales and no compounding vehicle, so they are always selling for tomorrow's paycheck.
- Retention lets you "harness time." For the wealthy, time is an asset (the longer you wait, the more you win); for the poor, time is a liability (constant rush for the next sale).
- Compounding math: even at a flat rate of ~1 customer/week who never leaves, in 10 years you have ~500 paying customers/month. At 10 new customers/month retained, in 5 years you have ~600 paying/month.
- If you must sell 100 new customers a month just to maintain (because they churn), you never build wealth. Retained customers turn each new sale into permanent revenue — "a node of revenue that pumps money your way month after month."
- Recurring vs. reoccurring revenue: the principle applies to both. Subscriptions (recurring) and repeat one-time buyers (reoccurring) both count — Facebook has no subscription but advertisers reorder eyeballs over and over.
- Acquisition test question when evaluating a business: "Is there a way we can get someone to buy this and never stop paying for it?" Make value so high that when a customer's credit card changes, they call you first — like people do for their phone, utilities, or rent, but not for Netflix.
- The product test: "If tomorrow you could get no new customers except by referral and word of mouth from your current ones, how differently would you treat them / how much better would your product be?" Thinking that way is thinking like the wealthy.
- "You don't get customers to make sales. You make sales to get customers." Transactions exist to create the relationship so revenue recurs.
- The real bottleneck is that the product isn't good enough, not that customers are lazy. If people don't consume your product/service, "it means that you suck" — onboarding is too hard, so they never reach the benefit. (Amazon doesn't complain about service tickets; it fixes the products.)
- Three-step scaling model:
  1. Promote just enough to get first customers → get the first "C," consumption.
  2. Run the improvement loop: survey customers, study client experience and results, make it faster and easier, iterate the same product until people refer others. Don't market more or launch new products to escape a plateau.
  3. Only then put "gas on it" — spend heavily on marketing, because retained customers give you higher lifetime value, so you can outspend competitors for the next customer.
- To finish, make it harder to leave: in B2B, add collateral or control the money flow (e.g., Stripe controls the money and has leverage). "The person who controls the money has leverage."
- Price vs. value: "Price is what you pay, value is what you get." Aim for a customer surplus / an insane bargain (a $500k house sold for $50k, learn to build it for $10k). Even if you provide more value up front and it's less profitable at first, iteration drives your cost down over time.
- Self-reported figures: crossed $100M net worth at 32; acquisition.com portfolio does $200M/year; Gym Launch has sold licensing to 5,000 brick-and-mortar gyms; company now worth ~$100M but would be worth over $1B if all 5,000 still paid — an estimated ~$900M missed. Says the lesson took him 7 years to internalize.
