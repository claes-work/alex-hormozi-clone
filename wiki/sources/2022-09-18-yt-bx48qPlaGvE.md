# My Best Sales Tactic (to Make a TON of Money)

## Summary

Alex Hormozi shares what he calls his most powerful sales tactic, learned by
accident while selling supplements at his gym: deliberately telling a customer they
can get a needed product cheaper elsewhere (e.g., Costco or "down the street") to
prove you're acting in their self-interest, which rapidly builds trust and dramatically
lifts close rates on the items you do want to sell. He names this device "ghost
products" (originally "sacrificial lambs"), pairs it with a prescriptive close that
piggybacks product usage onto an existing habit, and closes with advice to get "reps"
by injecting yourself into a high-volume, low-ticket sales process to master selling.

## Key claims

- **The tactic — "ghost products" / "sacrificial lambs":** as a salesperson, openly
  tell the customer they don't need to buy a given item from you (or can get it cheaper
  elsewhere). This concession makes them trust you faster because you acted in their
  self-interest, not your own; they then buy what you actually recommend.
- Discovered by accident: he ran out of a supplement during a challenge launch, told a
  customer to buy it cheaper at Costco after the program, and found she bought
  everything else he recommended. He leaned into it and began closing 100% of people.
- He taught the tactic to salespeople who had never sold before and they closed 80–90%
  of walk-ins in a retail environment selling physical products.
- Over time he stopped even carrying the "ghost" products because they were more
  valuable as trust-building tools than as inventory.
- **Which products to give away:** make the ones you "send across the street" your
  lowest-margin products; give away the low-margin stuff to gain trust, keep the
  high-margin stuff for yourself.
- Manipulation vs. help is a matter of *intention*: changing behavior with positive
  intent (you believe in what you sell) is helping; with negative intent it's
  manipulation.
- **Prescriptive close:** give a vision of the benefit, then explain exactly how to take
  the product *before* asking for the sale — e.g., "put it next to your toothbrush,"
  piggybacking usage onto an existing daily habit rather than creating a new one.
- **One-click upsell:** because the card is already on file, the customer only has to say
  yes — no wallet, no new purchase decision. Combines the prescriptive close with ghost
  products for trust.
- **Budget objection handling:** ask if they're on a budget, then offer to order items by
  importance; remove the lowest-priority item, but never signal that what you removed is
  unimportant (or it looks like you sell things that don't matter). Reintroduce the
  problem it solved ("you'll have to eat a pound of broccoli") to bring it back.
- **Finding "found money":** ask what old-identity behaviors (drinking, smoking, going
  out) they'll stop; reframe that freed-up spend toward the new purchase.
- **Get reps:** attach yourself to a big business that does many low-ticket transactions
  (car washes, massage, nails, hair) to get rep after rep — teaches selling better than
  any course and makes higher-ticket selling feel easy later.
- **Services version:** for a service business (e.g., web design), send low-priority work
  "across the table" (e.g., "there's a guy in the Philippines who'll do it cheaper") to
  make the salesperson seem to be on the customer's side while still selling the core
  offering.
- **Goal of selling:** move from across the table to shoulder-to-shoulder — both looking
  at the decision together with the same information to make the best decision for the
  buyer.
