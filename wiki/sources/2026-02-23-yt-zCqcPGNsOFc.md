# Ads Have Changed Forever

★ **L3-candidate** — a dated, notable shift in Alex's paid-advertising stance: he declares the "singularity" of paid and organic media (his paid team and media team are now merged into one), and prescribes a specific new creative workflow (run your best-*saved* organic content as ads with only a click-to-grab banner overlay). Trend-sensitive; date-stamp before promoting to persona/topics.

## Summary

Short clip (likely from a live mastermind/room Q&A, since a second speaker participates) in which Alex answers a group of ad-buyers about what is working in paid advertising *right now* (as of 2026-02-23). His thesis: the future of media between **paid and content has completely merged** — "the singularity" — and at his own company the paid team and media team are now a single team.

Because his company publishes ~450 pieces of content per week, he lets the algorithm surface the best/most-interesting organic pieces, then simply runs those as ads. Notably, he says they no longer append CTAs to the content (they did that ~a year prior); instead they take the winning content as-is and put a **banner/overlay** on it ("click to grab a thing"). If the content was good, the viewer takes whatever action the ad platform allows (workshop, scaling roadmap, books, lead magnet, etc.).

Tactical signal for the audience: don't optimize ad selection by *views* — look at the organic content with the most **saves** (and the content that gives the most *follows*, not most views), and run those as ads. He ties this to Meta's **Andromeda** system, noting "you lose so little running a bad ad," so even a creator posting only 20 pieces/week should just run all 20. His rationale: a perfect platform would have no ads, so if your ads *look like content*, the platform rewards you ("Nirvana for them") — content creates purchase intent, and paid + organic simply hadn't been combined before.

A second speaker reports they tried the **reverse** (taking their best *ads* and posting them as organic content) and it did not work. Alex/the group reconcile this: the direction that works is organic → ad, not ad → organic. Ads buy you *reach* — the platform won't distribute a "hooky," right-offer piece for free because it's not good enough on its own; you pay for that reach, and if content is genuinely good, paying money behind it "super distributes" it. The second speaker adds their best ads were made by short-form creators.
