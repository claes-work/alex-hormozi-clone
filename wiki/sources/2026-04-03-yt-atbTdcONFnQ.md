# Why Doctors Need a Sales Role

## Summary

Alex diagnoses a direct-primary-care practice's lead follow-up, rather than advertising,
as its immediate constraint. He proposes a low-priced, self-scheduled assessment tied to
a common customer problem and argues that the office needs a dedicated setter or sales
role to contact and convert leads.

## Key claims

- The physician self-reports about $2 million in revenue, 15% profit, 2,000-2,300
  patients, and seven or eight new patients per month; these figures are unverified.
- As of April 2026, Alex says local paid advertising remains plausible because the
  practice already generated leads; the missing process is rapid, persistent follow-up.
- His proposed front end is a $19-$99 paid assessment or screening linked to a frequent
  ideal-customer pain, with self-scheduling and phone follow-up for non-buyers.
- He recommends revealing the diagnosed business problem and then prescribing the
  membership as the next step, rather than asking administrative staff to improvise sales.
- He argues that a medical office needs a dedicated setter/sales role because reception
  staff commonly prioritize existing-patient administration over outbound lead work.
- Alex says the owner's highest-leverage work is recruiting, interviewing, training,
  and managing that role until the acquisition process works.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I don't think there's anything you could possibly do with your time besides interview and train a setter."

> "Doctors need salespeople."

> "You collect their card and then you call all the people who didn't schedule."

## Notes

This is business-process advice, not medical advice. The physician's medical and COVID-related claims are guest-attributed and excluded from persona training.
