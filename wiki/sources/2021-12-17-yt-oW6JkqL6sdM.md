# A DOER's approach to PROCRASTINATION (2021-12-17)

## Summary

In this early (2021) mindset talk — a re-released clip from his gym-owner community — Hormozi argues that the single trait separating the top 1% of entrepreneurs is the *speed* with which they execute after making a decision. He frames procrastination not as a motivation problem but as an unnecessary delay between deciding something is a good idea and it actually happening, and prescribes a concrete fix: collapse deadlines from "end of week" to "end of day" or "the next two hours," interrogate every competing priority, and start the moment the decision is made. He ties this to the military OODA loop (observe, orient, decide, act) and to running teams, insisting the owner sets the cadence for the whole organization.

## Key claims

- (2021) The trait Hormozi sees consistently in the top 1% of entrepreneurs (not the top 10%) is the speed with which they execute *after* making a decision. [paraphrase]
- (2021) He defines power as the gap between thoughts becoming reality — the smaller the gap between deciding something and it occurring, the more powerful/potent the entrepreneur or organization. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Speed is a core tenet ("speed is king") of his companies; the gap between deciding an idea is good and executing it is the loop through which a business improves. [paraphrase]
- (2021) He invokes the OODA loop (observe, orient, decide, act) as a military term for the iteration cycle; the faster you can run that loop, the more responsive you are and the faster you seize opportunities and discover failures. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Compressing deadlines from end-of-week to end-of-day literally speeds up the organization 7x; even that single change can 7x how fast a business grows. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Speed is not doing things fast — it is not being distracted by things that don't matter; it is prioritization. Most people delaying a task aren't doing something higher-priority, they're often doing nothing. [paraphrase]
- (2021) The owner sets the tone/cadence: when someone asks to do something and the owner says "let's start right now," that sets how quickly the whole organization orients and how quickly thoughts become reality. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Higher-revenue entrepreneurs have a much shorter delay between gaining information and acting on it; he cites peers doing $5–15M who read a recommended book immediately and sent back notes and a plan. [paraphrase]
- (2021) He warns against consuming irrelevant "education" (e.g., motivational blogs) instead of executing an already-tested playbook that removes the need to re-test. [paraphrase]
- (2021) Example: "Joey," whose gym he calls the #1 gym in Gym Launch, implemented every system in the "Legacy" program by week eight — not more hours in the day, just higher efficacy in execution because he doesn't let himself get distracted and feels an "itch" to execute until the task is done. [paraphrase]
- (2021) In competition, speed-of-first-to-market matters less than loop speed: a competitor who can observe, execute, and improve faster than you can even understand what's happening gives you no chance to catch up. [paraphrase]
- (2021) We procrastinate "for no reason" — the problem doesn't go away, so the delay is pure waste. [paraphrase]

## Topic notes

His core anti-procrastination method (2021): shrink the deadline. Instead of "end of week," demand "end of day" or "the next two hours," then challenge every competing task with "what are you doing that's more important than this?" and start the moment the decision is made. The mechanism is compressing the OODA loop (observe–orient–decide–act) so thoughts become reality faster. Reframes procrastination as a prioritization/distraction failure, not a motivation problem, and as an owner-set organizational cadence rather than a purely personal habit.
