# Passive Income Is a Lie

## Summary
Short solo monologue reframing the active-vs-passive income debate. Alex's core argument: passive income is a false category. Because every minute you're alive time passes, everyone on Earth has an implicit **dollars-per-hour** rate — so "passive" and "active" aren't a binary, they're a spectrum of *how active the activity is relative to the time invested*. He would scrap the labels entirely and ask a single question: **"What is my return on time — how much do I make per hour?"** People resist this because "no one should trade their hours for money," but Alex insists everyone already does; the money is simply "not denoted in hours." He then draws out the two behavior changes this forced on him. First, he had been **obsessed with finding quick, low-effort, "passive" money**, sinking cash into those bets while underinvesting time in the active work already making him money — and dismissing anything non-scalable as not worth his time. Second, when he actually did the math — his annual income divided by hours — one activity was earning **15x his average hourly rate**, so the correct move was to do *more* of that until all his hours were that, then find something even higher and trade time for that. The insight that changed his behavior: stop diverting money from active income into the hunt for passive income, and instead **reinvest into the active engine you already know works, to multiply it.** He offers a "humbling" exercise: divide last year's income by 2,000 for a 40-hour-week hourly rate, or by ~8,000 (or the 2,000 figure divided by four) for your true per-hour-alive rate — then judge any opportunity against that number. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
