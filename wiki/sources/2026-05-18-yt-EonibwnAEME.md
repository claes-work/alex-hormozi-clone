# How to Catch Up In Life (Using Logic)

## Summary

In this solo talk, Alex Hormozi argues that when you're ambitious but don't know what to do, the correct move is always to **build capacity** so you can recognize and seize opportunities when they arrive. He lays out six actions — save money, add and stack skills, build an audience without a product, build a wait list, and grow your network — framed with a baseball metaphor (be at the plate with a practiced swing when the "fat pitch" comes). He ties the work to his stated mission of helping the next generation make their first $100K, and cites the Princeton "Good Samaritan" study to show that having capacity (not being rushed/depleted) determines whether you can act on what's in front of you.

## Key claims

- When you're not sure what to do, the answer is always the same: **build capacity** so you're ready when opportunity strikes.
- Opportunities present themselves to everyone, but **only people with capacity can recognize and capitalize on them** — don't be in the bleachers when the fat pitch comes; be at the plate with a practiced swing.
- The Princeton "Good Samaritan" study: seminary students' self-rating as moral had **zero correlation** with stopping to help a fallen person; how rushed/late they were had the highest correlation — a **6x difference** between those 10 minutes early and 10 minutes late. Lesson: lack of capacity blinds you to opportunities right in front of you.
- **Save money** when you don't know what to invest in: money buys time, and time buys optionality. Practical cuts — don't eat out, buy from discount grocery stores, wear what you already own for 2 years (trade/Goodwill), live as cheaply as possible (with family, or bunk up 6 to a house / split bedrooms).
- Treat **time as a financial asset**: stop doom-scrolling and use the ~8 hours outside work (roughly 5–9am and 5–9pm) to get ahead — you live for today to pay for today, but plan and prepare for tomorrow in those other hours.
- **Add skills and practice them**: spend all excess cash acquiring skills until you can't possibly spend on more. You are your highest cash-producing asset; increasing your capacity to earn increases your value.
- **Skills are inflation-proof** — "whether we're trading in Bitcoin or seashells," value can always be exchanged. The only logical response to disruptive technology is to double down on skills and make yourself more valuable.
- **Learning is always additive** — even bad experiences make you better if you learn what not to do and change your behavior; "winners win no matter what."
- **Skills stack**: each new skill multiplies the value of the ones beneath it (Jay-Z: rhythm → rap → lyrics → sell → market → build a label → recruit artists; or math → bookkeeping → accounting → taxes → insurance → M&A). You still need the foundational skill to have the advanced one — like needing arithmetic before calculus.
- **Build an audience without a product**: you don't need a product to start, you need attention, because attention gives you leverage and builds potential energy. Document the work you do — "epic proof or epic effort."
- **Build a wait list** before you build the thing: a person who pays with their time now is more likely to pay with their money later.
- **Build your network / luck surface area**: going out (coffee shop, gym) instead of staying in expands your luck exposure; the fastest way to change your life is to change the people around you. Move to the hub for your field (finance→NY, film→Hollywood, politics→DC).
