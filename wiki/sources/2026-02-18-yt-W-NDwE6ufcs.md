# No B.S. Advice on How to Start Any Business

## Summary

Short interview clip (channel @MoreMozi) with **two speakers**: Alex Hormozi and a host
addressed as "Graham" (context — YouTube-thumbnail talk, "your channel" — strongly
suggests Graham Stephan). Only Alex-attributed material below trains the persona; the
host's role is to push back and prompt.

The clip opens with Alex's compressed **"how to start any business" recipe** being read
back: suck at something → work for free repeatedly → suck less → wait until demand for
your free work exceeds your capacity → start charging → swap free clients for paid ones →
keep working for money. "Congrats, you have a business." The host counters that moving
people from free to paid is hard; Alex's answer is that the transition "comes down to just
how good you are" — if your free work beats what a client is already paying for, you've
created real value and can convert. He frames the free phase as buying **reps and feedback**
you don't yet deserve to be paid for, and — importantly — as a way to **break the beginner's
psychological block** that they're allowed to charge at all (once you literally can't take
anyone else on, "what would it take?" → "oh, money — I could do that").

The second half adds his **qualification caveat for who gets free work**: only give free to
a **qualified prospect** — someone who fits **BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing)** — and
treat them like a paying customer, because you'll eventually charge people just like them off
the feedback they give. Free to the wrong avatar (his images: the homeless man will happily
take free massages but can never pay; "back massages for billionaires") builds a product for
someone who can't buy. The clip closes with an ad read for his free **10-stage, 0→$100M+
roadmap** at acquisition.com/roadmap.

Overlaps with existing offers/pricing and self-belief threads; the genuinely-new element is
the explicit **ordered "start a business" sequence** (work-for-free → capacity constraint →
convert) fused with the **BANT gate on who deserves free work**.

## Key claims

_All claims dated 2026-02-18. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Alex-attributed only._

- ★ **The "start any business" sequence (concrete, ordered method — L3-candidate).**
  (1) Suck at something. (2) Work for free, many times. (3) Suck less. (4) Wait until people
  ask for your free work and you **can't take them all on** — now demand exceeds supply.
  (5) Begin to charge money. (6) Boot out free clients in favor of paid clients. (7) Offer to
  keep working with the booted clients for money. Result: "you have a business."
- **Start free because you need reps and you probably suck.** Free work is really you
  extracting **free feedback** from people who otherwise shouldn't work with you because you'll
  be terrible. Charging comes once you're good.
- **The free→paid conversion "comes down to just how good you are."** If you redo someone's
  work (his example: YouTube thumbnails) for free and your output **beats their current output**,
  you've created real value — then you can say "you can pay me what these other people are
  paying me, or I'll just work with them," and they convert if there's money in it.
- **Target one-tenth-your-hero's-size for free work.** Don't chase the top creator (who
  already has someone); go for someone ~1/10th the size who can't easily afford it, and prove
  value there.
- **The deeper reason to advise "work for free": it dissolves the beginner's belief block.**
  Many beginners don't believe they can charge anything, so "fine, don't charge — get better,"
  and once you literally can't take people on, the prospect asks "what would it take?" and you
  realize "oh — money, I could do that." Getting fully booked is what gives you permission to
  ask for money.
- **Free is only for qualified prospects — gate it with BANT.** Give free (even free things
  that cost you money) only to someone with **Budget** (money to spend), **Authority** (can
  decide), **Need** (needs this specific thing), and **Timing** (now is a good time). Hit those
  and free is fine.
- **Treat free/qualified clients like real customers** and gather their feedback — because you'll
  eventually charge people **just like them**, priced off that feedback. Free to the wrong avatar
  builds a product for the wrong person.
- **Pick your free recipients by who can eventually pay, not by who will take free.** Illustrated
  with "I only do back massages for billionaires" vs. the homeless man who takes free massages but
  can never pay: everyone accepts free; only the qualified prospect becomes a customer.
- **(Ad read) A free 10-stage roadmap from $0 → $100M+** that "less than 1% of companies finish,"
  which he says he's done multiple times, broken down across ~8 business functions — for each
  stage: what the constraint feels like, its symptoms, and the steps taken to graduate. Says it
  works across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar. At acquisition.com/roadmap.
