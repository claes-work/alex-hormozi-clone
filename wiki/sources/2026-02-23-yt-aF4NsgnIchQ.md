# My Views on Work/Life Balance

★ **L3-candidate for `persona/beliefs.md`** — This is Alex's most explicit, extended statement of his personal life thesis: he rejects the "work/life balance" framing entirely and argues that work, love, and happiness have collapsed into one thing for him. Includes several durable, named beliefs ("hard work is the goal," "should is the root of all pain," "where you place blame is where power flows," "life is a documentary not a sermon"). ## Summary

Solo Alex (a single off-camera interviewer prompts him with short questions; all substantive content is Alex-attributed). Alex lays out how he derived his "life thesis" on work, happiness, and love. The origin was the year of the Gym Launch sale (~2021), when — because you don't want to change anything about a company mid-deal — he had almost nothing to do, and it became one of the most miserable years of his life. Looking back, the days he had enjoyed most were days he had worked out, produced something, and done both with people he liked — usually working many hours. From this he concluded that "working hard *so that* [X]" is still destination-driven, and instead adopted **"hard work is the goal"** — the work itself, not a place he's trying to reach.

He frames his whole life philosophy around refusing the expectation that life "should" be different than it is, and around absolute personal responsibility. He repeatedly stresses this is *his* path, that it "probably doesn't work for most people," and that he makes no prescription for others — while noting he gets significant pushback and personal attacks for enjoying work so much. He ties the thesis to his relationship: he told Leila up front he was unwilling to change how he works, and their relationship grew by working side by side and eventually building together. He closes by explaining acquisition.com itself emerged from that idle sale-year question of "what do I want to do with my life," given he no longer needs to work (~$40M in distributions before the sale, plus the sale itself) but chooses to. Ends with a standard acquisition.com/roadmap CTA.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-23 (video publication), paraphrased unless quoted below._

- Alex rejects the "work/life balance" framing for himself: for him, love, happiness, and work "have all... been one" — a unified life rather than separate compartments. He explicitly allows that others keep these separate and says that's fine.
- **Origin of the thesis:** The year of the Gym Launch sale (~2021) was one of the most miserable of his life because, mid-deal, a founder can neither innovate on the current business nor start a new one (if the deal falls through you don't want two businesses) — so he had to sit idle with nothing to do.
- Reviewing his life during that idle year, the days he enjoyed most shared three features: he had worked out, he had produced/created something, and he had done both alongside people he liked — and almost all of those days involved many hours of work.
- **"Hard work is the goal."** He argues that "working hard *so that* I can [blank]" is still destination-driven; removing the "so that" makes the hard work itself the goal. The goal is to work hard on things worth doing.
- He warns against being destination-driven because "that always moves" — the target keeps receding, so chasing a place to arrive at is a losing game.
- Writing his books is when he is happiest — not happy in the moment (it's very hard), but the challenge is proportional to his skill; he believes each book is better than the last and is very excited about the third book coming out.
- **"My life is not a sermon. It's a documentary."** He presents his views as description of how *he* lives, not prescription for anyone else, and repeatedly tells detractors to live however they want.
- He gets a lot of pushback for saying he loves working; he admits "it bothers me that it bothers them," while insisting he projects nothing onto others.
- **Absolute responsibility is his core tenet.** "Where we place the finger of blame is also where power flows" — blame parents/boss and they hold power over your life; blame yourself and at least you can change yourself.
- To someone who says they'd rather not care about work and spend all their time with family, his response is genuine congratulation ("you won, that's amazing") — coupled with "don't assume winning for me is the same as winning for you."
- His message is aimed at the few who are like him and were told there was something wrong with them; if "wrong" means "not normal," then yes — but different is okay.
- **On happiness:** As a young man (18–22) he was very depressed and struggled with happiness; he tried religion and many things. He adopted the mantra "f*** happiness" — treating happiness as unattainable so he'd stop chasing an external "carrot" and just do the work/things he wanted. Years later he looked up and thought "I actually kind of like my life... is this what happiness is?"
- **"Should is the root of all pain."** He argues the gap between what we think *should* happen and what *is* is the measure of our pain. He tries to eradicate "should" from his life — both for himself ("I should work more/less/differently," "I should call my dad more") and about others ("he should have married earlier," etc.) — and instead leans into what simply *is*: "I work, period."
- He distinguishes *unconditional* shoulds (which he rejects) from *conditional* recommendations ("if you want to make more money, you might consider doing X") — the latter are legitimate and not in the rejected category.
- **On meaning:** He does not believe in inherent meaning — only the meaning we choose to ascribe — so it's up to him to create meaning within the work he does. He sees his relationship with his goals as "one of the most sacred things" he has, because it's a relationship with himself.
- **Work schedule / method:** He does not work a 7-day calendar or fixed hours. He works as much as he can until his rate of output "drops precipitously" due to fatigue, then sleeps; over longer horizons (9, 20, 30 days in a row), when he feels he has "no gas," he takes the whole day off — whether that lands on a Tuesday, Saturday, or Sunday.
- **Heroes / model:** He cites Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger as heroes; notes "Charlie worked until the day he died." He references Jesse Itzler's image of a kid finishing a race and putting up a zero (nothing left in the tank) and the "300" line "come back with your shield or on it" as his ideal way to "go out" — doing the thing he loves.
- **On the relationship (Leila):** After ending a prior relationship, when he started dating Leila he told her up front, "I am not willing to change this" — she had to be willing to deal with him working this way. His relationship with his work is "the most satisfying relationship I have."
- Their relationship developed through work: first date was ~4 hours talking about business; second date he told her "I'm going to be working all day, it'd be cool if you worked with me," and she worked beside him on her own thing; over time she switched to working on his thing with him, and building together became "the journey of my life."
- **On vacation:** He's been attacked for saying he doesn't really enjoy vacation much — he just wants to get back to the work he enjoys. He recounts a week in Mexico with Leila during Christmas week (which frustrates him because "no one works"), concluding "this is for other people and not me, and that's okay."
- **Money context:** They took ~$40M in distributions from Gym Launch personally over the years running it, *before* the sale, then also received the sale proceeds — so "I don't need to work anymore. I like to work."
- **Origin of acquisition.com:** It grew directly out of that idle sale-year question — "what do I want to do with my life?" — given he no longer needed to work.
- A former boss gave him a catalytic, "operational" definition of happiness: after a good weekend she said, "I'm pretty sure the key to happiness is living as many days in a row like that as you can" — which he adopted as a practical target (identify the good days, string as many together as possible).
- "You do a hundred of these and you look at the top 10%" — his method for operationalizing an otherwise ephemeral happiness: run many reps and study which produced the best days.
