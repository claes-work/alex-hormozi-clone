# The Leader Is Always the Problem

## Summary
Short solo clip stating Alex's diagnostic razor for organizational problems: whenever a department, function, or line of revenue underperforms, look first at its leader — "the leader is always the problem. Not sometimes." His logic: a mediocre function means a mediocre leader, full stop. It is either the leader plus the team, or just the leader, but never just the team — because a mediocre leader will drag even a great team down to mediocre, while a great leader can lift a mediocre team up to great (he invokes the "good coach turns around a bad team" movie trope). He illustrates with a business owner who had four locations, two doing well and two doing poorly; after "a zillion questions" the root cause surfaced — a long-time-friend operator who was an addict showing up to work inebriated. Alex's point: no tactical advice (pricing, sales) matters while the leader problem is unfixed, because no one respects or follows a leader like that ("Why don't you show up to work sober? Why don't we start there?"). That is an extreme case, but the same pattern holds at every level — weak media team, weak sales team → look at the leader. Once you pull the leader out, he says it is roughly a 50/50 shot whether the team is also broken: a low-bar leader who hired low-bar people leaves a bad team behind, whereas a strong leader can convert a mediocre team into a great one. He frames the razor as a time-saver: early in his career he spent too long hunting for pinpoint problems; going straight to the leader "is so much faster." Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap scaling-roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's razor: if any organization, department, or line of revenue has a problem, the leader is *always* the problem — "not sometimes."
- As of March 2026, a mediocre function means a mediocre leader; the failure is either the leader and the team, or just the leader, but never just the team.
- As of March 2026, a mediocre leader will turn a great team into a mediocre team, while a great leader can turn a mediocre team into a great one.
- As of March 2026, no tactical business advice (pricing, sales) matters until the leader problem is fixed, because people will not respect or follow a leader who cannot meet a basic standard (his example: an operator showing up to work inebriated).
- As of March 2026, once you remove a bad leader it is roughly a 50/50 shot the team is also broken: a low-bar leader tends to have hired low-bar people; a strong leader put over a bad team can turn it around.
- As of March 2026, going straight to the leader is a faster diagnostic than hunting for pinpoint problems — a mistake he says he made too much early in his career.
