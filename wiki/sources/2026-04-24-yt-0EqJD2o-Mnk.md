# Helping 6 Business Owners Scale in 33 Minutes

Rapid-fire live coaching where Alex Hormozi diagnoses six brick-and-mortar / trades
business owners (roofing, electrical, HVAC, junk removal, commercial construction) who
want to scale from single-digit millions toward $10M–$100M. The owners' situations are
context; only Alex's advice is persona data.

## Summary

Alex works through six trades-business owners one at a time, each stuck between their
current revenue and a much larger goal. His recurring diagnosis: scaling constraints are
almost never a single thing — they are multi-step chains (usually price → cash flow →
hiring redundancy → growth). He repeatedly reframes problems around trade-offs, the "who"
game (hiring better talent), separating inbound from outbound sales, doubling down on the
channel you already have skill in, and — in the extreme — burning down a lesser business
to chase a clearly superior opportunity based purely on opportunity cost.

## Key claims

Alex's advice across the six cases (paraphrase unless quoted):

- **Regret is usually illusory.** We imagine the upside we missed without accounting for
  the downside we avoided paying — "the trade-off" — so the regret "isn't real."
- **Dissatisfaction comes from wanting both.** On work-life balance vs. growth, there is
  no right answer; it's preference. "Either want less or trade more."
- **To grow you must change something** — "if you change nothing, nothing will change."
  Decide which thing you value least; short-term profit can be made up later, family
  time cannot.
- **Scaling is the "who" game, and talent comes in levels** — the best talent is always
  ahead of you, not behind. To bring in high-level talent you may take a short-term hit
  to profitability.
- **Keep passive investments passive.** Making passive money active just incurs cost;
  active income beats squeezing 5% better returns out of passive. "Keep active active,
  keep passive passive."
- **Constraints are multi-step.** If it were one thing you'd have seen it already; the
  fix is usually a second- or third-order change you reverse-engineer back to the core.
  Typical chain: bump price → cash flow → hire redundancy (2–3 backups per role) → stay
  above the business → keep advertising to backfill.
- **Guarantee-backed premium pricing.** Raise price 20–40% and back it with a guarantee
  ("if we don't meet these qualifications, I'll give you all my profit back"); if you
  miss, you fall back to today's price, so the downside is capped and competitors who
  won't guarantee reveal they lack confidence.
- **Ignore price complaints; watch behavior.** "I care more about what people do than
  what they say." Customers will always want it cheaper, faster, and guaranteed — that
  never changes.
- **Reduce dependence by adding "wells."** For a contractor dependent on a few referral
  sources, do outreach to competitors' clients to gain leverage and independence.
- **Key-man risk is the #1 risk of every business.** No one wants to buy it, and you'll
  eventually want to kill it yourself. Do a 15-minute time study for a week, rank
  activities by revenue/uniqueness, and delegate the bottom half.
- **Serving premium customers funds premium hires** — excess margin should buy you
  better people; lower your "tolerance for mediocrity."
- **Recruit via national ads + generous relocation/signing bonus** (e.g. $50k, split $25k
  now / $25k at month six) rather than only recruiting firms.
- **1099 vs. W-2:** if they must show up at set times and work your way, they're
  employees — "go more legit, not less legit," keep it W-2.
- **Inbound and outbound sales must be separate teams.** Inbound (fed, qualified leads)
  is what top closers graduate to — higher volume, lower per-deal, more reliable; the
  commission structure differs because you pay for those leads. Funnel expensive leads
  only to your best closer.
- **Own sales and marketing in-house** — "your entire business is sales and marketing so
  you should have the core elements of the business in house."
- **Reject the premise that one channel is "the" channel** — you make one work. Pick the
  channel with the highest overlap with your (and your team's) existing skill set, then
  do far more of it before learning new ones.
- **Burn things down on opportunity cost.** When one opportunity is orders of magnitude
  better, exit the lesser business fast even below its "worth" to reclaim full attention;
  the time saved can generate more value than the sale would.
