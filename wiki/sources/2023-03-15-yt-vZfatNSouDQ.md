# Hardcore Business Lessons I Learned From A Dealer

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi frames six business lessons around his first job as an 18-year-old working summers in the warehouse of a fifth-generation fur coat dealer (owner "John"), a business that survived two world wars and multiple recessions. Through the dealer's playbook he teaches complaint handling, inventing a continuity/insurance product, no-based selling, using free "sawdust" scraps as bait, manufactured scarcity, and the discipline of relentlessly doing the boring basics. The dealer analogy is a rhetorical device — the lessons are Alex's own paraphrased business principles.

## Key claims

- **Handling angry customers — the "angry boat."** There can only be one person in the angry boat; if a customer is upset, you must get in and then help them out of it. Validate their anger rather than downplay it. Saying "it's not a big deal" reads as refusing to take responsibility, which makes the customer escalate to force validation. Even if a complaint is ridiculous, it is still your responsibility, and taking ownership is how the business gets better.
- **Invent an off-season / continuity product ("insurance").** The dealer had no summer demand for coats, so he offered to store and condition coats over the summer — repairing, sewing, conditioning the fur to make it "last two, three, five times longer." This is effectively an insurance product: you can charge people for the safety/assurance that what they already bought keeps delivering value longer. Cost to deliver was tiny (one 18-year-old, air conditioning, a storage facility).
- **Use an analogy to sell an unfamiliar product.** John compared insuring a fur coat to paying ~$100/month insurance on a $50,000 car — mapping something people didn't understand (fur coat care) onto something they did (car insurance).
- **No-based selling — get a "yes" by getting a "no."** Storage was the default core service; conditioning was the upsell. Phrasing was "we're going to store and condition the coat — you don't want anything else, do you?" The customer says "no, I don't want anything else," which implicitly confirms yes to both storage and conditioning. Because consumers are trained to reflexively say no to upsells (and saying no usually saves them money), you align the script so their automatic "no" actually becomes a "yes" for you.
- **"Sawdust" — free scraps as bait.** Sawdust = anything a business creates in excess that it normally throws out (IKEA furniture from lumber-mill sawdust; whey protein, once a dairy waste product). The dealer turned leftover coat scraps into small fur items (little purses, earmuffs) and gave them free to anyone who turned in their coat — something that cost nothing but was still perceived as valuable, used as bait to get people in the door and take the upsell. Once in, they'd get charged to store the free item too.
- **Selling is not evil.** Capitalism is the only system where both people say thank you after an exchange — meaning both parties are better off. Consensual sale does no harm; a threat is different (that's robbery).
- **Manufactured scarcity doubles purchases.** The dealer used dealer-only pricing perception ("dealers aren't allowed"), a "limit two coats per customer" sign, multiple price strike-downs ("final price," "all sales are final"), and single-file entry with a fixed number of people in the store at once — all creating a huge perception of demand so buyers feel they must get theirs.
- **Become the dealer "for life" via what you do between sales.** A continuity product (annual insure-and-fix) makes customers return to you first instead of price-shopping all over town. You become the salesman for life, not the one-time seller.
- **For one-time / big-ticket products, add cheap repeat offers.** Even if you don't profit on the little add-ons, if they get customers to buy on average two more products from you, it more than makes up for the cost and effort.
- **Do the boring work, for a long time.** The dealer weathered five generations (and PETA-type pressure) by never stopping the boring basics; what they do hasn't fundamentally changed in years — they just got better and better at it. The difference between small and big business owners is that big owners do the same right things (blog, consistent email follow-up, content, building a sales team) every day for a very long time. Building a big business takes big time but creates bigger outcomes.
