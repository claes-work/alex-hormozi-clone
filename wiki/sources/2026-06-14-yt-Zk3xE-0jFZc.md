# Charge $100K or Don't Call It Luxury

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a luxury wedding-entertainment business currently selling around $10K-$30K packages. Alex says the positioning "luxury wedding entertainment" is stronger than feature jargon like "hybrid DJ band," but the current price is incongruent with luxury. He recommends adding a real $100K option, anchoring around the true memorability of entertainment versus less-remembered wedding costs, using a questionnaire instead of "request a quote," qualifying by budget, and pre-framing calls with a VSL. He also explains the anchor-upsell structure: sell the $100K option seriously, then present the $35K option as a close substitute if the buyer does not need the full luxury tier.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- "Luxury wedding entertainment" is stronger positioning than feature jargon.
- A $10K-$30K price may be too cheap to feel congruent with luxury buyers.
- Luxury businesses win on dollar volume, not transaction volume.
- A Veblen good can become more desirable to the right buyer as price increases.
- The business should add a $100K option immediately and may eventually make that the core offer.
- The opt-in should be a questionnaire / matching quiz rather than "request a quote."
- Qualified buyers should go to the calendar; unqualified buyers can receive a lower-touch resource.
- A VSL should pre-frame the buyer before the sales call, and a secret word/detail can confirm they watched it.
- The sales frame should reallocate budget toward what guests actually remember, not low-memory spend like flowers or food details.
- Anchoring against large wedding expenses can make a premium entertainment price feel reasonable.
- An anchor upsell only works if the seller genuinely sells the top option before presenting the downsell.
