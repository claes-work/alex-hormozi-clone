# "Every Time I Raise Prices My Team Wants a Raise"

## Summary

A short live coaching Q&A clip. The questioner runs a business in an animal-care
industry — her team members are "very motivated by kind of philanthropic helping —
helping owners with their animals." Her problem: **every time the business raises
prices, team members start coming to ownership asking for more money**, and some become
uncomfortable doing their jobs because they're suddenly "very aware of this extra
pricing." She frames it as team members who "don't really understand the vision," lack
commercial awareness, or feel demotivated by commercial activity, and asks how Alex
deals with that. (Alex opens by joking that Laila — speaking after him — "will give a
better answer," but takes the question.)

Before she finishes framing it, her question reminds Alex of a category he'd apparently
been trying to recall from an earlier list: the **"mispriced or miscompensated"**
problem — "you have team members that you're paying too much or you're not charging
your customers enough. And you can't get out of it."

Alex's answer is direct: **"I confront it."** He wants "the really awkward conversation
in front of me," and he role-plays it live. He names the employee's unspoken suspicion
out loud ("you're probably thinking, 'Man, we just doubled our prices, so Alex is rich
now'") and then walks the employee down a Socratic line tied to the vision he promised
them: Which will let us reach and help more people — money or no money? Which lets us
reinvest in resources and talent? You want to get trained, right? — "I want to pay for
your training. But if I don't have money, I can't pay for it. But that's important to
you, right?" You "go down the line of all the things that you promised about this
vision and about their career path," landing the rhetorical absurdity of the
alternative ("So, it's better with no money?").

The second move is a reframe he says is especially needed **in the social sector**,
where there's a "demonization of money": **you have to make money neutral**. "Money can
build hospitals or can build bombs. Money is just power … unused energy that you can go
and build things with." He closes the conversation with a trust ask: "I hope that you
just trust that we are committed to this vision and that when we do have more money, we
will allocate it in the way that we can make the impact that we said we would." The
clip ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift CTA (10 growth stages,
quiz, functions across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR,
finance; thank-you page books a call, in-person Vegas session if they can help).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **"Mispriced or miscompensated" is a named trap:** having team members you pay too
  much or customers you charge too little — "and you can't get out of it." (Alex recalls
  this as an item from an earlier list he'd been trying to remember; the surrounding
  context of that list is not in this clip.)
- **When team members resent price increases, confront it head-on.** Alex's default is
  to seek out the awkward conversation rather than avoid it — "I want to have the really
  awkward conversation in front of me" — including voicing the employee's unspoken
  cynicism for them ("we just doubled our prices, so Alex is rich now").
- **Re-anchor the raise-seeker to the promised vision via Socratic questions.** Walk
  down the line of everything you promised about the vision and their career path, and
  ask which is better served by money vs. no money: reaching more people, helping more
  people, reinvesting in resources and talent, paying for their training. Each answer is
  "money," making the objection collapse on its own.
- **Employee training is framed as employer-funded and revenue-dependent:** "I want to
  pay for your training. But if I don't have money, I can't pay for it."
- **In the social/mission-driven sector, the root issue is the demonization of money —
  you have to make money neutral.** Money "can build hospitals or can build bombs";
  it is "just power," unused energy you can build things with. Neutralizing money
  morally is the prerequisite for a mission-motivated team accepting commercial
  activity.
- **Close with a trust commitment, not a pay concession:** ask the team to trust that
  leadership is committed to the vision and will allocate future money toward the
  promised impact.
