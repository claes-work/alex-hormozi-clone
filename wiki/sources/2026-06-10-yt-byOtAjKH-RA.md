# Move the Factory or Fix Sales First?

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a family manufacturing business that sells lightweight equipment to contractors. The company has two P&Ls, has narrowed its avatars, and faces a future production-facility constraint in 24-36 months while sales and marketing are the current constraint. Alex first treats the facility move as important but not urgent, then changes the recommendation once the owner says the move would consume roughly half of annual free cash flow: fix sales and marketing first, free up cash, then fund the move with stronger cash flow.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A future facility problem can be an inevitable/existential constraint even if the current constraint is sales and marketing.
- The decision changes if a facility move is a major capital constraint rather than only an operational project.
- If improving sales and marketing can create enough cash flow without immediately hitting the supply constraint, sales and marketing should come first.
- The owner should use the increased cash flow as reinvestment capital to complete the move and recapture EBITDA.
- A facility move can be project-managed by a dedicated operator rather than distracting the sales/marketing team.
- If a project manager handles the move well, that person may be valuable long-term.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

> "You have an upcoming constraint in 24 to 36 months."

> "This is definitely like Eisenhower matrix of non-urgent and important."

> "If we crush marketing and sales in a good way, it's like we're just going to exacerbate how quickly that next constraint's going to come."

> "Free up cash, deploy the cash as the reinvestment strategy."

> "Marketing and sales first."
