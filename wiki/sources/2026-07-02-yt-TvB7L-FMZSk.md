# Watch This If Your A Players Are Great But Churn Is Killing You

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Chase Hazelwood, whose $50M pest-control company wants to reach $1B but churns lower-level delivery employees while A players quickly promote. Alex gives a tactical career-ladder fix: take a sparse three-step path and expand it into many smaller rungs by alternating title, pay, perks, and objective performance milestones. The goal is to keep employees always three to six months away from the next visible win.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Chase Hazelwood** - the coachee. His pest-control business, acquisition history, revenue, EBITDA, organic growth, and labor constraint are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- For repeated roles, retention can improve when the company creates more career advancement, not only bigger jumps.
- A three-step career path can be expanded into nine or eighteen rungs.
- Rungs can alternate title changes, pay bumps, schedule perks, better equipment, better routes, parking spots, or other visible rewards.
- The company should show the ladder during orientation so new hires can see where they are and what objective behavior unlocks the next step.
- The first win should be very easy, especially during the first 30-45 days when churn is often highest.
- After the first small win, milestones can expand gradually, lengthening the reinforcement cycle while keeping the next reward in sight.
- For this case, Alex recommends giving average employees more ways to win rather than only celebrating fast-rising A players.
