# How to Know When You've Actually Won

## Summary

Alex reflects on people who decide "enough" at much lower financial levels and may have
finished their race before him. He tells a story about his first gym manager choosing to
return from manager to trainer and accept less money because that made him happier; Alex
says he respected it but did not understand it then, and now sees that person as having
won.

## Key claims

- Alex says he has to fight the impulse to view people who stop at a few million dollars
  or less as "out of the game."
- He says the output of ambition comes from squeezing the person pursuing it.
- He says someone who reaches "enough" and stops may have won by finishing their own
  race earlier.
- He self-reports that his first gym manager asked to stop managing and return to being
  a trainer, cutting pay from about $45K to roughly half.
- Alex says he told the manager he respected the choice but would never understand it.
- After seeing the former manager years later still happy, Alex says the manager had won
  and Alex was still trying to catch up.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I respect it but I will never understand it."

> "Dude, you won."

> "I'm still trying to catch up to you."
