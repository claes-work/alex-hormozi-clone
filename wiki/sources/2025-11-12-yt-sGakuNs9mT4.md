# How to Speak So Well People Give You Money

## Summary

Solo Hormozi video presenting his framework for sales *tone* (delivery), distinct
from scripting (the words). He argues most tone advice ("curiosity tone," "aggressive
tone") is uselessly vague, and replaces it with five concrete voice levers: three
constants (volume, speed, articulation) and two variables (pauses and pitch/frequency),
all notated in a script with just three punctuation marks (period, ellipsis, question
mark). He then extends into memorization ("breathing the script"), the primacy of the
script, universal closes, objection handling, and a detailed discovery ("pulling teeth"
+ "chunking up") walkthrough. Framed against his August 2025 book launch that did $106M
in 72 hours and set a Guinness record.

## Key claims (his speaking / persuasion framework)

- **Two failure modes in sales**: you make less money than you want because you either
  say the *wrong words* (scripting) or say them the *wrong way* (tone).
- **Tone changes meaning**: same sentence, different emphasis = different meaning
  (his "I didn't say he hit his wife" demonstration — stress on each word shifts meaning).
- **Prerequisite: you must have a script.** References his CLOSER framework: Clarify why
  they're there, Label the problem, Overview past experience, Sell the vacation (three-step
  pitch), then Explain away concerns, Reinforce the decision.
- **Five voice levers.** Three constants + two variables:
  - *Constant 1 — Volume*: loud enough to be heard (matters more in person than on phone,
    where the listener controls volume).
  - *Constant 2 — Speed*: the sweet spot is roughly **135–185 words per minute**. Too fast
    → trust drops; too slow → they think you're an idiot.
  - *Constant 3 — Articulation*: enunciate/round out every word, don't mumble. These three
    together maximize comprehension.
  - *Variable 1 — Pauses*: length of pause signals intent — short pause draws attention to
    a point; long pause expects a response. Notated as `...` (short/soft stop) vs `.` (full stop).
  - *Variable 2 — Frequency/pitch*: voice up = question (soliciting a response); notated as `?`.
- **The buy-ask = long pause + rising pitch.** "Ask them to buy and you shut the [expletive]
  up." Combines the two variables to signal it's the customer's turn.
- **Only three punctuation marks** are needed to notate tone on a script: period, ellipsis,
  question mark.
- **"Breathing the script"**: memorize via a blackout method — read the full script aloud,
  then cross out one word and reread, repeat until the whole page is blacked out. You repeat
  the script as many times as it has words. Goal: not think about what *you* say, so you can
  listen to the prospect.
- **"The person who speaks the most in the sale loses."** Sales is in your ears, not your mouth.
- **Script is gold / paramount** — "no one is above the script." Consistent words are the
  foundation (building on sand vs. pouring concrete); 95% of sales teams lack this and so
  aren't world-class.
- **8-second pause after the ask closes ~30% more sales** — attributed to three meta-analyses
  (one he recalls as Columbia); he doesn't remember exact sources. Give people time to process;
  don't fill the silence.
- **People believe what they say, not what you say** — selling is asking questions that get
  the prospect to sell themselves.
- **Objection handling is only ~20–30% of sales.** The vast majority of selling happens before
  the ask (everything prior to the conversation, then discovery). "You'll typically lose them
  in discovery."
- **Salespeople who close the most are the ones who ask the most times** — the skill is asking
  as many times as possible without annoying the prospect. Every overcome is really just an
  excuse to ask again. "You're never going to sell someone by being right." "People don't buy
  from people they hate."
- **Seven universal closes** (memorizable, work for any non-logistical objection). Example given
  — the "very reason" reframe: whatever reason they give not to buy, reframe it as the reason
  they need it most ("the problem you're solving by not buying is the problem we solve through
  you buying").
- **Discovery = matching their problems to your products.** Ask questions until they state a
  problem. For uncommunicative prospects, use **"pulling teeth"**: alternate "Can you give me
  an example?" / "Can you be more specific?" until you extract a "meaty" concrete problem.
- **"Chunking up"**: recap the concrete problem into a category you sell against ("sounds like
  you have a marketing issue... is that fair?"), collect 2–3 chunked problems, then transition
  by getting them to agree that solving those would grow the business — "then they've already
  accepted the sale."
- **Coaching translation**: vague feedback → concrete lever. "Have more confidence" = speak
  louder. "You sound nervous" = you're speaking too fast. Enunciating fully auto-slows a nervous
  fast talker.
- **The close needs the most tone management** — highest emotion/adrenaline; too loud+fast reads
  aggressive, too slow reads slow.
