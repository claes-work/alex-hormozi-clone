# Helping a Service Business Scale

## Summary

Alex helps a fire-safety and cleaning company sequence several simultaneous problems.
He recommends learning one repeatable sales motion with restaurants through the existing
cleaning foothold, then repairing the compliance department, and only afterward cross-
selling that service or pursuing harder property-management accounts.

## Key claims

- The owner describes an acquired fire-safety, cleaning, and compliance business but
  gives unclear revenue in the caption; the compliance unit is self-reported at about
  $20,000 monthly with 20 clients, and all figures are unverified.
- As of April 2026, Alex says customer selection should compare lifetime-value-to-
  acquisition-cost ratios, not simply absolute account value; smaller restaurant accounts
  can outperform larger property managers if they are much easier to acquire.
- The existing cleaning offer creates a literal foot in the door with restaurants and
  should provide faster sales repetitions while cash flow is constrained.
- He advises the owner to remain in the business long enough to learn and document the
  sales motion rather than trying to remove himself before one exists.
- The compliance cross-sell should wait until the department can absorb more demand;
  replacing one former key person with three people while reporting overload signals an
  unresolved productivity or process problem.
- His sequence is restaurant acquisition first, compliance-department repair second,
  and compliance cross-sell third, with harder property-management sales deferred.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I want to solve for the greatest discrepancy between LTV to CAC on customers that are easiest to acquire."

> "You just have to get your hands dirty and learn the actual sales process."

> "We just have to take one fire at a time."
