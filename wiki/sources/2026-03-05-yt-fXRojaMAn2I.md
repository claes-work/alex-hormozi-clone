# You're right... (99% right, 100% wrong: the victim frame guarantees the long-term loss)

## Summary
Short clip on the trap of being "right." Alex recounts a woman at a conference standing up to list "six reasons why this isn't going to work for my business." After she finished, he replied, "You're right. You'll never be able to make this work for your business" — which snapped her out of the victim frame and made her defend that she *could* do it. His thesis, crediting a line he attributes to "Dr. Cash": you can be **"99% right, but 100% wrong."** Taking the powerless/victim frame to its natural extreme, you get to be right over and over in the short term (every business that "won't work," every partner "who isn't the one," every reason not to invest), and that short-term rightness feels good — but on a long enough time horizon you are guaranteed wrong, because eventually one business works, one partner is the one, and the market grows. By clinging to being right, you "lose everything." He extends it to investing (the same structure as never investing: a small risk of loss today buys a near-guaranteed macro win; refusing the small risk guarantees the long-term loss) and to ownership/time (trading pleasure today for pain tomorrow — but today is yesterday's tomorrow, so you are living the pain you cast; the shift is owning "this is my fault"). An off-camera interviewer briefly adds social reinforcement (complaining about the weather). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, his examples: parents rejecting every partner ("this isn't the one") are right every time except the one you marry; a doubter saying each successive business "won't work" is right until one does.
- As of March 2026, people get "deceived by their short-term rightness" and miss the long-term wrong; being right repeatedly feels good short term but guarantees the loss long term.
- As of March 2026, he applies the same structure to investing: never investing feels safe but guarantees you never get rich; taking a small risk of loss today buys a near-guaranteed macro win over the long haul.
- As of March 2026, he reframes it as ownership over time: many people flip pleasure-today for pain-tomorrow and forget the second half — and since today is yesterday's tomorrow, you are living through pain you chose; change starts with "this is my fault."
