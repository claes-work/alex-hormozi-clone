# The Cost of Constant Criticism

## Summary
Podcast clip (Alex as guest; an unnamed British host and possibly a third voice interject). The theme: cynicism as a losing long-term strategy. Alex argues saying "no" makes you right in the short term but wrong in the one way that matters — you never get the return. Family and friends are statistically right almost every time they doubt a new business or relationship, but wrong on the one that changes your life; cynics measure in days, winners measure in decades. He admits his first nine businesses "didn't really amount to anything" and that he's failed and been wrong more often than he's succeeded, but wins big when it counts. He ties this to the Skool bet (publicly documented risk: "if it goes wrong, I'll document what I learned… state the facts and tell the truth"). The host supplies supporting references (the "cynical genius illusion"; "16 out of the last three recessions"; a viral COVID WhatsApp rumor) and Shaan Puri's line "the cynics get to be right and the optimists get to be rich."

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says at acquisition.com they run a deliberate "no bias" on investments (easy to find reasons not to invest, needed for risk management) but warns that never investing is "wrong in the one way that matters — you get no return."
- As of March 2026, he frames cynicism as a habit built from short-term positive reinforcement: you get repeatedly rewarded for saying no and being right, but "you have to make big bets to win big," which means being wrong often and willing to "look stupid."
- As of March 2026, he says doubters "might have been right literally 19 out of 20 times," but "you only need one yes… to change your entire life."
- As of March 2026, he restates his contrarian time-horizon argument: doubters "are measuring in days, and you're measuring in decades" — every day you haven't hit it big, they feel right, "but they're wrong."
- As of March 2026 (biographical, self-reported): "my first nine businesses didn't really amount to anything." (⚠️ see contradiction note.)
- As of March 2026, on the Skool bet he says he's "been public about it," has pre-played the downside, and would respond to failure by documenting lessons and telling the truth — "do what I've always done."
- As of March 2026, he restates the optimist thesis: "the world belongs to optimists" because doing anything big requires believing it can happen; percentage-wise he's "been wrong more times than… right" and "failed more times than… succeeded," yet wins bigger in the ways that matter.
