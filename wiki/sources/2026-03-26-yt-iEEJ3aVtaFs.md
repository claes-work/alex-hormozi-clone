# Stop Worrying About Your Niche Being Too Small

## Summary
In a coaching Q&A, a caller who sells online coaching/nutrition to physique athletes (~$900K revenue, targeting $10M) asks Alex about two concerns: lead flow and whether the bodybuilding vertical is too small/not wealthy enough. Alex argues that anchoring a brand at the aspirational top of a market "scoops up" the larger population beneath it, that the owner no longer competing does not hurt credibility, and that the caller's two businesses (an education "university" and a coaching service) are actually an unrecognized front-end/back-end that should be connected via a choreographed ascension process.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that marketing to the aspirational top of a market (e.g. physique athletes) automatically attracts the larger group beneath it who merely aspire to that level, so a narrow-looking niche is not a real constraint if you accept customers below the top tier.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that a business owner does not need to still be an active competitor/practitioner to be credible; customers care about what you can do for them, citing that he owned zero gyms while running Gym Launch.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends every business build a self-sustaining advertising loop: a one-time creative input generates customers, whose testimonials/reviews become the ad creative that spins the wheel — a "self-licking ice cream cone."
- As of March 2026, Alex advises using UGC (user-generated content) as the predominant ad creative for a coaching/service business.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a common owner mistake as running two separate businesses that are really an unrecognized front-end (education, high TAM, cheap/profitable acquisition) and back-end (higher-priced service); the fix is to advertise the higher-LTV-to-CAC front end and build a choreographed ascension process into the back end, accepting that not all customers will convert.
- Caller claims (unverified): team revenue was ~$900K, projected ~$1.1M, goal $10M; all lead gen is currently organic (Instagram + YouTube) with no paid ads on the coaching side; paid ads already work well for the education "university."
- As of March 2026, Alex illustrates with a prior client (unverified example) whose LTV:CAC was 13:1 on business coaching vs. 37:1 on the core skill-teaching offer with ~20x bigger TAM; he told her to turn off ads on the 13:1 and run them to the 37:1, then ascend customers into the back end.
- As of March 2026, Alex suggests that when the front end has different owners, treat it as an arm's-length affiliate relationship with a kickback, yielding effectively free customers at only the cost of the kickback.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you market to the top end of the market, I'm sure you get people who inquire who are not physique athletes but just aspire to be."

> "You'll again you'll scoop up the people who are underneath the market. You just won't scoop up the people who are above it. And so you being already anchored at the top is a good thing."

> "When I ran Gym Launch, I owned zero gyms."

> "They care about what you can do for them."

> "I try to as quickly as possible build a loop in every business where we advertise... the advertising gets us customers. The customers get us testimonials and reviews. Those testimonials and reviews spin the wheel of advertising so that that first-time effort only comes once for me and then from that point going forward it's a self-licking ice cream cone."

> "She had a front end and back end that she didn't realize was a front end and back end."

> "Turn off your ads for the 13 to 1, turn on the ads to the 37 to 1 that has a 20 times bigger TAM."

> "You need to have a more choreographed ascension process from education into service. And then that becomes the business."

> "You have to just treat it like an affiliate relationship that's arms length and then you just give them some sort of kickback... then at that point you have free customers and the only cost is whatever kickback you give."
