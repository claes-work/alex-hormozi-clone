# Have a Conversation With Your Older Self

## Summary
Interview clip. An interviewer reads back one of Alex's tweets — that he had "a coaching call with myself" by typing a chat conversation with his problems while pretending to be his 85-year-old self — and asks what prompts or questions make for the best such conversation. Alex explains the practice, which he later learned is grounded in the **Solomon paradox**: the psychological finding (named for King Solomon, who gave brilliant advice to everyone while his own life was in ruins) that people reliably give better advice than they follow themselves. He pairs that with a second premise — that no one has more context on your life than you do — to argue the most powerful possible advisor is your own older self. Alex says he's a poor fit for therapists and performance coaches (maybe 5–10 hours total, mostly spent giving them enough context to advise him at all) because an outside coach knows none of his skill sets, background, or deal specifics, and — bills to pay, recurring revenue — may not even have incentives aligned with actually solving his problem. His future self, by contrast, has the two things no therapist has: **complete context** and **completely aligned incentives**. He recounts starting the experiment while genuinely stressed about a decision, formalizing his long-running mental habit into a written Google Doc dialogue where he coaches himself with the same questions he'd ask a portfolio-company CEO ("what's the objective?"), and hearing his older self laugh at him ("what did you expect? You're trying to build a billion-dollar thing in a year?"). His core reframe: most people don't have a knowledge gap, they have an accountability gap — illustrated by his weight-loss sales days with "Sandy," who already knew she should work out more and eat better; her problem wasn't knowing, it was doing. He now keeps a recurring one-hour Monday calendar block — the first thing he does — for a back-and-forth chat with 85-year-old him in a Google Doc, clicking enter between turns. He frames it as the ultimate gift of self-accountability, a razor for focusing on the few things that matter, and an emotionally powerful experience (old me has grace for young me; old me thanks young me for the sacrifice). He credits it with pumping the brakes on reactivity in relationships and business decisions, since "most of wisdom is just thinking over a longer time horizon." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
