# The SIMPLE FORMULA that has DESTROYED BILLION DOLLAR BUSINESSES

## Summary
In this short solo video Hormozi introduces a whiteboard mental model built on three lines that exist inside everything a business sells: price (what you charge), value (what the customer gets), and cost of goods (what it costs you to fulfill). He argues that the gap between value and price is "customer surplus," and that maximizing that surplus is what makes a business spread by word of mouth and grow quadratically rather than linearly. He walks through several configurations of the three lines — a high-value, thin-margin, operationally hard service business; a middling mom-and-pop business; and his own ideal of something that costs almost nothing to make yet delivers tremendous value — using Netflix as the archetype of low price, near-zero incremental cost, and high perceived value. His prescription is a three-step diagnostic: maximize value, then slash cost of goods to a crazy degree, then price so that customer surplus is enormous. His central claim is that simply understanding the interplay of these three lines is the difference between "making money and building a fortune." Recorded while unwell (a self-described "Rona sponsored episode"), so he keeps it short.

## Key claims
- Every business has three lines inside everything it sells: price (how much you charge), value (how much the customer gets), and cost of goods (how much it costs you to fulfill).
- Customer surplus is the excess between what a customer pays and what they get; the greater the distance between value and price, the more value customers perceive.
- The bigger the surplus / value-price discrepancy, the more viral the business becomes, because a large surplus drives word of mouth.
- Word-of-mouth growth is "quadratic" rather than "linear," and a business great enough that people can't help telling friends drives cost of acquisition toward zero.
- Growing on word of mouth is framed as "the antithesis of what everyone in the marketing world will tell you," yet he says it should always be the goal.
- Netflix is his example: priced so low it beat renting a new release, yet incremental cost of adding a customer was basically nothing, so gross margins stayed very high.
- A high-value, high-surplus but thin-margin business tends to be service-based, operationally difficult, needs little marketing, and grows steadily ~10-15% per year with little cash to reinvest ("old school" businesses).
- Most small businesses are middling mom-and-pop operations: decent margins, decent value, run-of-the-mill.
- His stated goal is always to find something excessively valuable that costs almost nothing to make.
- His diagnostic sequence: step one, provide the most value; step two, lower cost of goods sold to a crazy degree; then price it (he would rather have something that costs a penny, sells for 10 cents, and has $10 of value).
- Understanding the interplay of the three lines is "the difference between making money and building a fortune."
- He states acquisition.com does "about $85 million a year" (self-reported, dated 2021).
- He credits "unique, expensive, sticky air" to another channel/video as the origin of the make-something-that-costs-almost-nothing idea (attribution to that source is his; he does not name the creator).
