# You're Overpaying the People You Kept

## Summary
In this coaching exchange, a salon owner from Long Island explains that COVID crushed her industry and left her unable to replace the stylists she lost. She feels she is over-compensating the long-tenured stylists (10–15 years) she kept, and with inflation eroding margins, commissions almost negate the business's profitability. She is considering pivoting into a retail product line to capture more margin. Alex first reframes the framing itself, arguing that calling it a "tough situation" only makes it tougher, and that she is in a changeable condition because many salons are doing fine — so the useful question is what those salons do that she does not.

Alex diagnoses the real problem narrowly: she lacks a recruiting mechanism for stylists, and that acquisition function (outbound, content, or ads for stylists) is what is missing. He tests her motive by asking whether she would still pursue retail if the salon were simply fixed; she says no, so he advises against the retail pivot, calling retail much harder than the salon business she already understands. He notes that if she had a steady pipeline of good stylists asking to start, she would be thrilled.

Alex then shares a pattern from the many small service businesses he has evaluated for acquisition. The ones that consistently outperform share two features: a profit share with a manager who genuinely runs the business as an owner (relevant once there are multiple locations), and career pathing for low-skilled labor — showing staff how to upskill with a concurrent pay increase, which aids retention. He references a six-location nail-salon chain he nearly bought and admired for its career-path model, but declines to share its specifics on air.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) Calling a problem a "tough situation" is counterproductive because it makes the situation tougher for no reason; the owner is in a condition that can be changed through activity.
- (2026-07-19) Because many salons are doing fine, the productive question is "what are they doing that you're not doing?" — which makes the path forward much simpler.
- (2026-07-19) As an analogy to scale: acquisition.com gets about 500 real estate agents a month signing up, so finding one stylist a week for this salon is a small ask by comparison.
- (2026-07-19) The retail/product business she is considering is "super hard" — harder than running the salon she already knows.
- (2026-07-19) If fixing the salon would satisfy her, she should not pivot into retail at all; the pivot is only worth it if she wants that other business for its own sake.
- (2026-07-19) The precise problem is that she lacks a recruiting mechanism for getting stylists — that is the missing acquisition system.
- (2026-07-19) The fix is a recruiting function: outbound, posting content, or running ads for stylists.
- (2026-07-19) Small service-based businesses that consistently outperform tend to share two features.
- (2026-07-19) Feature one: if privately owned, a profit share with a manager who acts as a true owner and runs it so the owner is not involved at all — not merely "there less"; this becomes relevant once there are multiple locations (the "leave-behind" of who runs each site).
- (2026-07-19) Feature two: with low-skilled labor, staff strongly value career pathing — showing how they can upskill, that pay rises concurrently with skill, and that the owner will invest in them; this supports retention.
- (2026-07-19) He nearly bought a six-location nail-salon chain whose career-path model he admired, but declined to disclose its specifics to avoid giving away trade secrets.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ Post-COVID it has been very difficult to recruit stylists to replace those lost when the industry was hit.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The stylists she kept have been with her 10–15 years, and after paying commissions plus other rising costs, the margin almost negates why some stylists are in the business.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ She is pivoting into a retail product line to capture more margin, and does not want to get rid of her people; the salon is on Long Island, New York.
