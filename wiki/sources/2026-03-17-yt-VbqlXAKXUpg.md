# How I Deal with Stress and Missed Expectations

## Summary
Near-solo clip (an interviewer interjects one line — "How you see it. And that makes all the difference.") in which Alex lays out his mental toolkit for handling stress and unmet expectations. His governing thesis: nearly all upset comes from an **unmet expectation** — a thing you "demanded of the universe" that didn't happen — and the fastest lever is not to change reality but to change the expectation/perspective. He walks through several named reframes. (1) **The frame of the veteran** (credited to "Dr. Cashy"): when something bad happens, imagine it happening a thousand times in a row and ask how you'd feel on the thousandth time — by then you'd have habituated and treated it as just a fact of life, so you might as well feel that way now. His first use: a maid shrank a favorite flannel shirt; he reasoned that if every wash always shrank clothes, he wouldn't be upset because that's just how dryers work — "and then it was over." (2) The **lifespan / time reframe**: when his cat Bill died at 18 months, he was sad until he realized the sadness was an expectation that Bill *should* live longer; reframing to "what if cats only live six months?" turned grief into appreciation for the 18 months he got. He extends this to human life — within the scope of all time nobody lives long, so if you got to live the way you wanted, the only real loss from dying at 75 vs 85 "is the present." (3) **Zooming all the way out** (a favorite): picture a loss (e.g. "I lose $15 million and I'm really upset") from far enough out in space that you can't even see Earth, conclude "this just doesn't matter," then zoom back in. He describes the common mechanic across all of them — **playing with time and space** to shift the expectation, asking "what is the expectation I have, how is it unmet, how can I shift it, what if it were completely different?" (e.g. what if every drive always had 20 minutes of traffic?). The payoff: these tools make him less emotional/reactive to bad news, and the small amount of mental space, compounded over time, lets him make better decisions.
