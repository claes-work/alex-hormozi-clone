# What To Do If Your Employee Is Stealing

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a designer-handbag resale / pawn business that is under LOI for sale after discovering a key employee was skimming. Alex says the ethical and long-term-reputation answer is to remove the employee, disclose the facts to the buyer, and tell the whole truth rather than a half-truth. He frames the decision as a values and reputation question more than a narrow business question, especially because representations and warranties in the transaction could create liability if the seller knowingly hides the issue.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - designer-handbag resale / pawn-shop owner. His revenue, profit, sale process, employee-theft facts, and inherited-business context are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- When looking back from age 85, the owner will want to have acted honestly.
- If the seller knows about undisclosed liability, representations and warranties in the sale can create legal and reputational risk.
- The owner should be upfront with the buyer, state the facts, and tell the truth.
- The framing can include that the business may be slightly more profitable than shown because skimming has been removed.
- Alex would remove an employee who stole, even if the dollar amount is small.
- The issue is not the first time the person stole; it is the first time they were caught.
- Keeping a known thief sends the wrong signal to the team and to the owner.
- Reputation is the thing to defend with one's life.
- Future acquirers may call past acquirers, so honesty in one deal affects future deal flow.
- Hard conversations require whole facts and whole truth; half-truths create the danger.
- The decision is an ethical / values call more than a pure business optimization.
