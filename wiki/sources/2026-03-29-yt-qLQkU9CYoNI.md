# How to Know If Someone Will Take Advantage of You

## Summary
In a conversation with an interviewer, Alex Hormozi lays out his strategy for relationships, giving, and asking. He frames all relationships as reinforcement histories, endorses a disproportionately-giving posture (citing a game-theory "8 out of 10 give-to-take" ratio), and explains how content let him scale one-on-one generosity to a mass audience. He also covers the shift from saying yes to everything early to pathologically saying no at scale, treating time as a fixed-supply resource-allocation problem. The interviewer contributes several of his own framings (a definition of "friend," and a "how they treat you day one" heuristic); only Alex-attributed material is captured below.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says one of his strongest skill sets is knowing when to invest in a relationship and when to pull back, and he believes everyone is transactional — some people are just better at defining it.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes loyalty as a "history of reinforcement" with a person plus a belief that the reinforcement will return over a long-enough time horizon, which justifies incurring short-term cost (longest for a spouse).
- As of March 2026, Alex cites a game-theory book on the prisoner's dilemma claiming the optimal give-to-take strategy was roughly 8 out of 10 giving — 10/10 gets you walked over, 5/10 (tit-for-tat) opens far fewer doors, and never-askers never benefit; the model factored in forgiveness and learning. (Unverified: Alex could not recall the source.)
- As of March 2026, Alex describes a "fallacy of the third marshmallow" — a pathology among high achievers of always deferring gratification; he argues there is a point where one should start spending more than one earns rather than dying atop "a big pile of crumbs as an ant."
- As of March 2026, Alex says organic content let him scale his intrinsic give-first energy: the same effort he once spent helping one person he now spends making content, so many people benefit at once.
- As of March 2026, Alex says his strategy never changed — give to as many people as possible — but media and distribution put it "on absolute steroids," so he now does far fewer one-on-one favors.
- As of March 2026, Alex says at scale, with fixed time supply and rising demand, "no" must become the default, and you do not actually owe someone a response — especially once your content has already given them a lot.
- As of March 2026, Alex recounts manually DMing everyone who tagged his (Offers) book to ask for a review, doing it for four days, and reaching ~600 five-star reviews; he found the inbox grew faster than he could clear it ("paint the bridge").
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the arc as "yes to everything" in the beginning and "no to everything" at the end, with resource allocation as the core entrepreneurial skill throughout.
- As of March 2026, Alex offers a tactic: never decline or cancel a first meeting (second meetings are hard to get after a cancellation), so he gives new first meetings high priority and stays very flexible for them — one meeting converts an introduction into a real signal he can act on years later.
- Context: Alex credits Leila as the counterweight ("yin/yang") who historically pulled him back when he was about to be taken advantage of, which he says made their partnership strong.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I just believe everyone's transactional and some people are just better at defining it."

> "I have a history of reinforcement with this person and I have a belief that that reinforcement will return on a long enough time horizon. And so I'm willing to incur some cost in the short term."

> "You have to give first most of the time because that's what opens up doors of opportunity."

> "Me making content is the same energy as what I used to do for one person. All that effort and for like helping someone solve a problem. I now just do that same thing and then just many people benefit."

> "You have to almost pathologically say no to everything. No is the default."

> "You don't actually owe someone a response."

> "In the beginning you have to say yes to everything and in the end you have to say no to everything. And so being able to manage that apparent dichotomy comes down to resource allocation, which is still the core entrepreneurial skill set."

> "Never decline or cancel the first meeting."
