# What Makes The Perfect Business (5 Things)

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out the five structural "advantages" he would look for if starting the perfect business today — framed as an S-tier ranking for opportunity vehicles. The five are: Sticky (revenue/logo retention), Expensive (high gross margins), Expansion (a growing market/industry), Air (operational scale — low operational complexity and low capex), and Unique (a competitive moat). He argues each is a continuum, not a binary, that very few businesses have all five, and that retention is the single most important one — advising founders with none of these to build retention first and backfill the rest. He repeatedly uses School (Skool), Coke, and Nvidia as illustrations and ties the material to his Offers and Money Models books.

## Key claims (the 5 things)

- **1. Sticky (revenue retention).** "If you do not have what's called revenue retention, you have nothing." Distinguishes logo retention (how many customers remain) from net revenue retention (which can exceed 100% if remaining customers expand spend). Cites two churn types: involuntary/structural churn (moves, death, business dies, employee fired) vs. voluntary churn (they think you suck) — voluntary is the one to avoid. Skool membership data across categories: churn is highest in month one (over 20%), a ~10% drop-off around month three, another at month six, after which churn falls to ~2%/month. Takeaway: make the first 30 days awesome, get people past month three, and walk them to month six. If forced to pick only one of the five, he picks this.
- **2. Expensive (high gross margins).** Ideal: something that costs a penny and sells for a buck. High gross margins let you pay people better, speed the cash conversion cycle, reinvest in growth, and yield higher EBITDA/net margins. Low-margin examples: grocery stores, farming, restaurants (food is highly elastic), commodities. High-margin examples: media, information, education, community access, data, software, pharmaceuticals, supplements ("lotions and potions"). Topic of his Money Models book; the first Offers chapter is about decommoditizing yourself.
- **3. Expansion (growing market).** Easiest way to grow is to enter something already growing so a normal effort still grows by default. He cares about the *industry* growing, not just the business, since business growth (marketing/distribution) is a skill he already has — you just want to avoid a headwind. Shrinking: newspapers, formal education (down ~6%/yr), tobacco, alcohol, brick-and-mortar retail, administrative/clerical/data-entry roles. Growing: energy, AI, healthcare, cybersecurity, e-commerce, alternative education (CAGR over 20% annually — the basis for his School/Skool bet).
- **4. Air (operational scale = low operational complexity + low capex).** Low operational complexity = few variables needed to expand production (podcast ad read vs. running 100 restaurants with employees, suppliers, inventory, leases, permitting). Low capex = less capital needed to keep growing, so you dilute less and can expand faster without outside money (cites Warren Buffett preferring cash-generating businesses over cash-reinvesting ones). Caveat: raising capital to grow faster or capture real network-effect market share can be a valid advanced strategy (Skool has strong network effects, so reinvesting yields high ROIC).
- **5. Unique (a competitive moat).** Low barriers to entry breed competition that drives price down (e.g., social media marketing agencies). Moats include: capex itself (a shovel beats bare hands; a power plant is costly to build), specialized proprietary skills (Nvidia chips, nuclear energy), trade secrets, recipes, processes, and patents (a patent must be new, non-obvious, and useful). His favorite moat is brand — a brand makes any commodity unique (Revlon vs. white-label CVS makeup off the same manufacturing belt, priced higher and stickier). Coke as a multi-advantage example: capital + patents/recipe + brand.
