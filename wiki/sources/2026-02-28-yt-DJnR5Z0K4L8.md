# How to Scale Outbound Sales

## Summary
Short clip (panel/interview format; an unnamed interviewer interjects with `>>` a few times, but all the substantive content is Alex's). Despite the title, the clip is less a step-by-step outbound-ops playbook and more Alex's **buying-window / long-nurture thesis** applied to outbound. His frame: outbound is "kind of like a snowball" — early on you plant a lot of seeds; a few prospects are "hot and ready" and buy immediately, but most convert only via **long-term nurture** and staying "top of mind" until they get hot and ready. There are two buying windows: the **immediate** ("I'm in pain, I need this solved, and you happen to catch me") and the **long term** ("I'm not ready, I'm not ready… but now that I am, you're the last one I think about because you're still present"). He runs a live audience demonstration (who heard of him in the last 90 days / 6 months / 12 / 24 / 24+ months) to show buyers are spread across years, not weeks. Core number: **97% of the market in almost any market is not ready to buy today, but will be ready in the next 24–36 months**, so most businesses are capped because they compete only for the same impatient **3%** everyone else fights over. Big brands are big because "they sell to everyone… they just sell to them when they're ready." Practical implication: treat outbound not only as a direct-response channel (reply → book a call) but as a feeder into **every other part of the funnel** — email nurture, retargeting, ads — and judge it by what the motion produces over 6–12 months, not what a given day yields. He closes on decision-making under uncertainty: long sales cycles (some six months) mean you can't know at onset whether the motion worked; unlike McKinsey-style consultants who "need data to make decisions," entrepreneurs must bet on first principles ("if we get in front of more of the type of people who buy our stuff, we'll make more money — I can't promise it, but it feels pretty likely") and commit for months even with no immediate return.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, Alex frames outbound as a "snowball": most early activity plants seeds; only a few "hot and ready" prospects buy now, and most conversion comes from long-term nurture and staying top of mind.
- As of February 2026, there are two buying windows — an immediate one (prospect is in acute pain and you happen to catch them) and a long-term one (prospect isn't ready for a long time, then buys from whoever stayed present).
- As of February 2026, ~97% of the market in almost any market is not ready to buy today but will be ready within the next ~24–36 months; only ~3% are ready now.
- As of February 2026, most businesses are capped because they compete only for that same impatient 3% that everyone else also chases; big brands are big because they sell to everyone, just at the time each buyer is ready.
- As of February 2026, outbound should feed the whole funnel — not just direct-response call-booking, but email nurture and retargeting/ads too — so a non-buyer today keeps getting touched.
- As of February 2026, outbound should be judged by what the motion produces over 6–12 months, not by leads or sales attributable to a single day.
- As of February 2026, Alex positions his own content as an effective long-term nurture strategy (staying present until prospects are ready).
- As of February 2026, entrepreneurs must make many decisions with incomplete data (unlike data-demanding consultants); with long sales cycles you can't know at onset if a motion worked, so you bet on first principles and commit for months (e.g., six straight months) even with nothing coming back yet.
