# Why AI won't make you rich in 2026

## Summary

A solo, self-described "very impromptu" monologue in which Alex makes "a case against
AI" — more precisely, against the misconception that AI **canceled every other form of
leverage**. His observation: lots of people are token-maxing, vibe coding, and shipping
AI apps, and their token bills are going up, but they are **not making more money**.

Two proof points open the argument. First, his own business just had another step
increase in revenue, "but it's not because of AI" — the increase would have happened
without it. Second, in conversations over the prior week or two, his wealthiest, oldest
friends admitted that while they publicly advocate AI (and their teams genuinely use it
and are being trained up), **they personally are not using AI day to day**.

The core frame: leverage (output per unit of input) comes from many multiplicative
sources — capital, media, people/teams — and AI erased none of them. He notes the irony
that frontier AI labs themselves still employ thousands of people. The failure mode he
sees everywhere: because AI expands working capacity, owners now execute things they
previously (correctly) skipped — low-priority work, done faster and automated, which
"doesn't matter." Scarcity of capacity used to force prioritization; AI removes that
forcing function. Most AI use cases he has seen are not making businesses much more
money; the exceptions he grants are volume ad creative and his own AI sales rep — and
even the AI sales rep is "more efficient" rather than constraint-breaking, because sales
capacity typically is not the limiter. Most real business constraints are "not still as
perfectly solvable with AI" — otherwise everyone would already be richer.

He lists leverage moves that require zero tech expertise: one-on-one → one-to-many
(instant 10x), scheduled → asynchronous client appointments, and a sales motion that
educates prospects earlier so 2 reps close what 10 used to. Then the crescendo: the
highest-leverage activity — higher than AI — is **making good decisions**. Deciding that
a body of work is not a priority extracts more from a team than automating non-priority
output. Current AI, he offers as a rough analogy, is like access to a lot of cheap
virtual assistants — a form of leverage that has *always* existed (VAs are sometimes
still cheaper than tokens) without changing who wins; a terrible offer is not saved by
AI on the back end. His closing diagnostic: ask "Has implementing AI in my business made
me more money?" If not, you used it in the wrong place — return to identifying the
**true constraint** and focus your "tiny speck" of resources on the one needle-moving
thing. "AI is just a tool. It's not the answer."

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed._

- **AI did not cancel other forms of leverage.** Leverage = the difference between what
  you put in and what you get out; AI adds high leverage but capital, media, and people
  remain tremendous — and multiplicative — sources of it.
- **His own revenue step-up is not AI-driven.** The business had another step increase
  in revenue that would have happened even if AI didn't exist — "it's not like a direct
  line there."
- **Wealthy insiders' private behavior diverges from their public AI advocacy.** His
  wealthiest, oldest friends maintain public pro-AI appearances ("you have to do that")
  but admit they don't personally use AI daily; their *teams* use it and are being
  trained up. _(Self-reported private conversations, anonymized.)_
- **Frontier labs still employ thousands** — an ironic counterpoint that people remain
  needed and useful despite in-house AI.
- **AI-maxing automates the unimportant.** Because AI expands capacity, owners now do
  things they previously would have skipped — which were skipped *because* they were
  lower priority. Doing less-important things faster and automated "doesn't matter."
- **Scarcity forces prioritization.** Not having AI forces you to focus on what moves
  the needle; abundance of capacity removes that discipline.
- **Most AI use cases he's seen aren't making businesses much more money.** Exceptions
  he grants: high-volume ad creative (clearly helpful for advertising) and his AI sales
  rep — which is efficient and scales but is "typically not the limiter of the
  business."
- **Most business constraints are not yet cleanly solvable with AI** — "if they were,
  then everybody would be making a ton more money."
- **Non-AI leverage moves needing zero tech expertise:** one-on-one → one-to-ten (10x
  leverage in one decision); scheduled → asynchronous client appointments; a sales
  motion that educates prospects more fully upstream so conversations get fewer and
  shorter and 2 reps close what previously took 10.
- **The highest-leverage activity — above AI — is making good decisions.** Determining
  that something is *not* a priority extracts more from a team than automating a
  non-priority into irrelevant output.
- **Current AI ≈ access to lots of cheap virtual assistants** (his own explicitly
  imperfect analogy): that form of leverage has always existed — VAs are sometimes still
  cheaper than token usage — and the marketplace still sorted on quality, brand, and
  offers. A terrible offer is not rescued by AI on the back end; whether AI can improve
  the offer itself: "Maybe. Maybe not. I don't know."
- **The diagnostic question:** "Has implementing AI in my business made me more money?"
  If not, you were likely using it in the wrong place — return to identifying the true
  constraint.
- **Focus beats tooling.** Every business commands only "a speck" of the world's
  resources; skill at aiming that speck at the one needle-moving thing makes you more
  money "even more than AI."
- **What actually grows his business is what always has:** generate more demand, convert
  a higher percentage of it, deliver very high-quality stuff — AI can help, but as a
  tool inside those, not as the answer.
