# Helping a Dentist Get to $50M/Year

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A Florida dental-services owner runs ~$25M/year in revenue across 10 offices (going to 12) at ~21% EBITDA and wants to reach $50M/year. Growth is entirely **de novo / organic** — no M&A — building out "second-gen" spaces (old dental offices, sign a lease, drop in equipment), which cost ~$250–300k each. He names his two constraints as **talent acquisition** and **access to capital**. Alex quickly dismantles the capital constraint: at ~$5M in annual profit and a per-office cost of only $250–350k (including recruiting the team), he can more than cash-flow the planned five-to-six openings a year out of 20–30% of cash reserves — so the "limiter" isn't real, it's just that the owner would have to cut distributions ("I won't be able to get the yacht"). The owner admits it "feels less risky to use someone else's money," and Alex rejects that: debt isn't less risky ("they can take your [stuff]"). Alex reframes the whole thing as a **return-on-invested-capital (ROIC)** problem: with a repeatable model — ~$350k in (build + team), an office doing ~$1.5M its first year and eventually ~$500k profit — the payback is roughly 12–18 months, which is clearly good, so the trade of taking profit out today is trading upside (and growth) for security. On **talent**, Alex sizes it: six offices/year ≈ six dentists (one every ~2 months) plus staff and leadership hires, which functionally means **one hire every two weeks** — enough volume to justify a dedicated in-house recruiter. His rule on build-vs-outsource recruiting: use **headhunters for specialized one-time roles** (they have networks you don't want to rebuild), but for **repeated roles core to the economic engine** (for the dentist: dentists, plus front-desk / treatment coordinators / patient-care coordinators) own the recruiting/hiring/onboarding/training process internally. His favorite sourcing tactic: find a recruiting firm that does it, then go one level deeper on LinkedIn to the person actually doing the work and hire them to do it just for you ("I'll pay you better and treat you better"). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues the dentist's stated capital constraint is not the real limiter: at ~$5M annual profit and ~$250–350k per de novo office, five-to-six openings a year can be cash-flowed out of ~20–30% of cash reserves without outside funding.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that using debt/other people's money is **not** less risky than using your own — it only *feels* less risky — because lenders "can take your [stuff]" if it goes wrong.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames repeatable multi-unit expansion as a **ROIC** decision: ~$350k invested per unit (build-out ~$250–300k plus ~$100k to recruit the team), a unit doing ~$1.5M topline in year one and eventually ~$500k profit, yields roughly a 12–18 month payback — clearly worth doing.
- As of March 2026, taking profit out today (distributions) instead of reinvesting is "trading risk for security" and also **trading away upside** and growth speed; the owner's real choice is faster growth vs. personal cash-out ("the yacht").
- As of March 2026, Alex confirms it is genuinely harder to open one office per quarter than one per year — scaling cadence compounds the difficulty of finding dentists, salespeople, and leadership.
- As of March 2026, Alex sizes the hiring need: ~6 offices/year ≈ 6 doctors (one every ~2 months) plus ~4–5 staff each plus growing leadership (e.g. regional managers) ≈ roughly **one hire every two weeks**, which justifies a dedicated talent-acquisition person.
- As of March 2026, Alex's headhunter rule: use headhunters for **specialized one-time roles** (rent their existing network), but keep recruiting/hiring/onboarding/training **in-house for repeated roles that are core to the economic engine** (for the dentist: dentists and front-desk / treatment / patient-care coordinators — not roles that aren't causing problems).
- As of March 2026, Alex's favorite sourcing tactic: find a firm that does the recruiting, then go "one level lower" on LinkedIn to the individual actually doing it and hire them to do it exclusively for you, paying and treating them better.
