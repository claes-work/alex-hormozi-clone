# The Minimum Standard That Changed My Career

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says his later career outcomes grew because his minimum standard for how
much work he can put into something multiplied. He contrasts old presentations that once
felt complete after a day with his current process of hundreds of hours, repeated
run-throughs, recording, review, and edits until the work is polished.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex says the only voice that matters before a major presentation is his own judgment
  about whether he did everything under his control.
- A lucky good outcome after inadequate preparation still feels tarnished because the
  person knows the input was weak.
- Defining success by controllable preparation makes success feel more controllable, but
  also reveals how few things can be done well in one lifetime.
- A public-CEO friend who posted once per day reset his standard after hearing Alex's team
  puts out about 300 pieces of content per week.
- Alex says his later career returns grew because his minimum work standard multiplied
  roughly 100-fold.
- He frames serious work in "hundreds of hours" rather than single-day effort.
- His presentation process is game tape: make it, rehearse mentally, rehearse aloud,
  watch the recording, edit, and repeat.
- He listened back to every podcast he recorded for roughly his first two and a half
  years.
- More repetitions make the next level feel like the new minimum standard.
