# Sleeping on the Floor While Starting My First Gym

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks the questions on the `>>` turns; all substantive speech is Alex's and is the only voice-training material). Alex recounts the hardest stretch of his early gym days: after quitting his corporate job, driving across the country, and mentoring under a gym owner for three months, he opened his own gym at 20 with rent of $5,000/month — more than his old salary — and couldn't afford both the gym's rent and a place to live. So he moved out of the spare bedroom he'd been renting and **slept at the gym**, a dark warehouse underneath a parking garage. He punctures the romantic Instagram "chase your dreams" version of suffering: cars rattling the metal cracks in the concrete ceiling six or seven times a night sounded like gunshots, kids his age partied illegally on the roof, and he was trying to wake at 4:00 a.m. for the first training sessions. His refrain: "The Rocky cut scene lasts 30 seconds in the movie and five years in your life." He frames how he endured it — not by knowing he'd win, but by committing to the one thing under his control: never stopping. The wall at his back was "going home a failure," which to him "might as well have been death" (tied explicitly to his father and to imagined critics, real and fabricated). He'd already "swallowed the pill" of looking foolish by dropping from white-collar management consultant to personal trainer — a step down the status ladder — and refused to "go down a rung and then fail." His stated Plan B: drive Uber by day and strip at night, live on ~$1,000/month, and restart the cycle as many times as it took to win. He invokes a deliberately extreme analogy — enslaved people worked because the choice was "death or work" — to explain how a person keeps going when quitting feels impossible. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
