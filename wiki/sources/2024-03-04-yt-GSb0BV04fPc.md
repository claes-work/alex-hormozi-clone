# Alex Hormozi Describes The Moment He Sold His Company for $46 Million

## Summary

In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi reflects on the emotional and financial reality of
his ~$46M business exit. His central, counterintuitive claim: the money did not make him
feel better — he felt *poorer* afterward because he lost the monthly cash flow he'd
measured himself by, and because the lump sum roughly equaled what he'd already
accumulated through dividends. He describes the two-year sell process, a "very
depressing" year of having no purpose, the pain of losing his team, and the mistake of
making the private-equity buyer his "customer." He frames pivoting to Acquisition.com as
the ultimately higher-impact choice.

## Key claims

- Alex states the exit was for **$46 million** (self-reported figure).
- He **"did not feel the money"** but **did feel the loss of cash flow**, because he had
  measured himself by cash flow — after the sale there's a lump sum but "no money in
  every month."
- Because he had **taken dividends his whole life**, the cash from the sale was "pretty
  much about what we already had anyways" — so it was **not life-changing** and did not
  change how he lived "at all."
- **Losing the team was hard**: he says selling a company means selling the people, the
  organism, the system, the processes.
- Selling started because he and his wife were **"beat down"** and tired after nearly **10
  years in gyms**.
- The whole thing took **two years**: roughly **one year to fix everything wrong with the
  business** to make it sellable, then **one year to sell it**.
- Before the sale he spent **~12 straight months "not doing anything"** because the
  business ran without him and he couldn't start new initiatives mid-sale — which he found
  **"very depressing."**
- He calls it **"one of the most emotionally tiring years of my life"** — the deal
  repeatedly on/off the table.
- A **mistake he names**: once he decided to sell, everything became about satisfying a
  "fictitious overlord" — he **made the private-equity buyer his customer** instead of his
  actual customer.
- He weighed **keeping the business as a cash-flowing asset** ("Uncle Warren never sells
  anything") but concluded selling would ultimately yield **more impact for more people**
  via Acquisition.com and media.
- He expects **Acquisition.com to become "significantly bigger"** than the companies he
  sold; says the sold companies are now "killing it" and he feels **"renewed."**
