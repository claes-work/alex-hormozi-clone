# 10 KEYS to a TERRIBLE Business Partnership [GUARANTEED!]

## Summary

Solo Alex video using Charlie Munger's inversion technique: instead of asking how to build a great partnership, he lists ten things that would *guarantee* a terrible one, then flips each into its positive. His core model is that a good partner must bring **knowledge, money, or time** you don't already have (otherwise one partner is redundant), plus **documented, equitable, well-understood expectations**, plus **aligned mission, values, and lifestyle/interests**. He frames a business partnership as a marriage — the partner's personal life inevitably gets dragged into the business.

## Key claims (inverted to what makes a GOOD partnership)

- **Complementary, not redundant.** A partner must have knowledge, money, or time you *don't* have; if you're matched on all of experience/knowledge/time/money, one of you isn't required and both give away half their equity for no incremental benefit. (The "big three" of partnerships: knowledge, money, time.)
- **A need ≠ a partnership.** Just because you need a capability (e.g., an accountant) doesn't mean you must give equity for it — you can pay for services. Equity is the most expensive thing you give up, so reserve it for people who will really drive growth.
- **Aligned expectations.** Explicitly define what each partner expects to do; misaligned or unspoken expectations breed resentment.
- **Written agreements.** Document expectations rather than doing it on a handshake. The contract exists to create clear shared expectations and to facilitate dialogue around hard topics — not to get lawyers involved. Write contracts intending never to look at them again, but use them to force the hard conversations up front. If someone refuses to do agreements, walk away.
- **Equitable split / think ahead.** Don't try to "squeeze" the other party or take on a disproportionate load resentfully; either creates resentment and later renegotiation. Give more, think several steps ahead, and build a relationship rather than a purely negotiated contract.
- **Aligned mission (where you're going).** Both partners must want to take the business to the same place; if they want to go different directions, one shouldn't be there.
- **Aligned values (how you get there).** Shared values about how you deal with other humans; different values create endless strife even when the goal is shared.
- **Similar interests / lifestyle.** A saver partnered with someone who lives above their means and takes on debt is trouble — a business partnership is a marriage, and the partner's "extracurricular" personal life gets dragged into the business. An unreliable partner creates an unreliable business.
- **Long-term view.** You have to see the partnership 10 years out; almost no one thinks that far ahead, which is "why everyone's small."
- **Pride in association.** You should be *proud* — not merely okay — to introduce your partner to others (the "meet the parents" test).
- **Litmus test:** if you present both partners with the same data, would they make the same decision? If yes, far less long-term conflict.
