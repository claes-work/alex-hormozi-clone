# Eliminate "Shoulds" from Your Life

## Summary
Solo monologue. Alex argues that the word **"should"** ought not to exist in one's worldview, because it "creates expectations of an uncaring universe" — you demand something be a certain way (fair, easy, cheaper) and the universe wins every time. Complaining that life *should* be fair doesn't make you stronger; it only delays action and slows your rate of learning, which by his definition (intelligence = rate of learning) literally makes you dumber. He reframes life's ideals as **trade-offs with a price**: "privacy is the price of fame, loneliness is the price of ambition, pleasure is the price of discipline, novelty is the price of loyalty, discretion is the price of trust." People want the benefit but won't pay the cost — and the ideals aren't binary, they're a matter of *how much* (how lonely, how long, how hard). Most people believe they're doing 10/10 work but are really doing 1/10 of what the goal requires (10/10 for a $100k goal is 1/10 for a $1M goal). He introduces a **pipeline/valve** model: outcomes need *every* condition met, like water passing through a series of valves — three of four open still yields nothing, so "there's a condition I haven't met" replaces "the universe isn't fair." He then lays out **four levels of trade-off**: (0) *should* — expect it for nothing; (1) *too much* — you'll pay $30 for $300 Nikes and complain; (2) *bidding* — you keep resetting expectations and raising your bid ($30→$50→$100→$300) until you get through ("it takes what it takes / it costs what it costs," a Leila-and-Alex internal phrase); and (3) *source* — you set the price and hold the standard yourself, no longer using others' approval as your litmus test (Steve Jobs, Henry Ford, J.R.R. Tolkien on The Hobbit). At source level the only person you negotiate with is yourself, and the person with the *highest standards* — not the most senior or most shares — should make each decision. He closes on iteration realism ("19 drafts not two, 500 minutes of editing not 50, a thousand calls a week for two years not two months") and warns that believing it "should" be different chains you to mediocrity. Ends with the $100M scaling roadmap promo (acquirethis.com/roadmap).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues "should" should not exist in your worldview because it "creates expectations of an uncaring universe" — you demand reality conform, and the universe always wins.
- As of March 2026, demanding that life be fair, or noting that someone started ahead of you, provides no benefit; it only delays your speed of taking action and slows your rate of learning — and since he defines intelligence as rate of learning, it "makes you stupid."
- As of March 2026, life's ideals are trade-offs, each with a price: privacy is the price of fame, loneliness the price of ambition, pleasure the price of discipline, novelty the price of loyalty, discretion the price of trust. People want the benefit but won't pay the cost.
- As of March 2026, these ideals are not binary but a matter of degree — how much discretion, how much novelty, how long the loneliness, how hard and how long the work.
- As of March 2026, most people believe they are doing 10/10 work but are actually doing ~1/10 of what the goal requires; 10/10 work for a $100k goal is 1/10 work for a $1M goal — the fix is accurately assessing reality and finding your actual discrepancies.
- As of March 2026, outcomes behave like a pipeline of valves: every condition must be met before anything comes through, so meeting three of four still yields nothing. Reframing failure as "a condition I haven't met yet" makes life less emotional than "the universe isn't fair."
- As of March 2026, if you have to meet more conditions than someone else, that's not unfair — you'll develop more skills to hit the same goal; "you can't wish for strong character and an easy life, because the price of each is the other."
- As of March 2026, Alex maps four levels of trade-off: (0) *should* — expect it for free (the "thief" who takes the Nikes is the lowest, worst level); (1) *too much* — offer $30 for $300 shoes and complain it "shouldn't be so expensive"; (2) *bidding* — treat it like an auction, keep resetting expectations and raising the bid until you get through; (3) *source* — you determine the price and create your reality.
- As of March 2026, "it takes what it takes / it costs what it costs" is a phrase he and Leila use internally; getting what you want is like an auction where you must keep bidding again and again.
- As of March 2026, "source" means holding a standard higher than everyone else's and no longer accepting others' approval as your litmus test — the market tells you if you're good early on, but meeting only the market's standard actually lowers your own (Jobs/Ford: people would have asked for faster horses, not cars).
- As of March 2026, the person who should make any organizational decision is the one with the highest standard for that thing — not the most senior, not the largest shareholder — "whether it's their first day or their thousandth day."
- As of March 2026, excellence takes so long that you can realistically only do two-to-four things well in a life; believing you're special enough to succeed overnight (e.g. a first book) "chains you to mediocrity."
- As of March 2026, lazy people distract themselves because distraction sells the lie that you can try a hundred things and find the one that works; in reality any of them can work, but none will unless you pick one and make it work.
- As of March 2026, people underestimate iterations required: "it's 19 drafts, not two; 500 minutes of editing, not 50; a thousand phone calls every week for two years, not two months."
