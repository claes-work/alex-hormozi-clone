# "I have a newsletter, but it costs too much to get new subscribers."

## Summary

Live coaching Q&A in which a newsletter owner (audience member, **not** Alex) describes a health-and-fitness newsletter economics problem and Alex Hormozi prescribes a monetization fix. The questioner reports (self-reported figures) ~110,000 subscribers, ~£40,000/month in ad revenue, subscriber acquisition via Facebook at a CPA of ~£1.45, and only ~£0.30/month earned per subscriber — a ~5-month payback period. Their attempts at a self-liquidating offer (SLO) on ~$10,000/month of ad spend convert almost nobody (2–3 buyers). Alex diagnoses it as **an offer problem, not a market problem**, and lays out a stack of monetization mechanics: a thank-you-page SLO priced around $97 (benchmark ~2% cold-traffic upgrade rate) to self-liquidate CAC or halve the payback period; a quiz funnel routing to one of several offers with sales messaging woven between questions (VSL-style); an aggressive welcome sequence to promote the owner's own product in the first four days; and a GPT-assisted customer-call-mining workflow to write the offer copy in the avatar's own language. The avatar is 40+ women, mainly US.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only; 2026-02-18, paraphrased unless quoted)

- ★ **Self-liquidating offer benchmark: on a free front end you can expect ~2% of people to upgrade to a paid offer on cold traffic.** Put it on the thank-you page after opt-in.
- ★ **The SLO math to neutralize CAC.** If cost to acquire a subscriber is ~£1.4, price the offer around $100 (a $97 offer). At 2% conversion you get ~$2 back per acquired lead → you're self-liquidated. Even at 0.5% (a quarter of that) you'd still roughly halve the payback period.
- **Diagnose failed SLOs as an offer problem, not a market problem.** When the owner said maybe SLOs "don't work in my market," Alex rejected that — "It's an offer thing" — the market is fine; the offer needs to be right.
- **Concrete SLO product idea:** package "the top 30 most read, most shared newsletters from the last year" as a $97 self-liquidating offer. Works even without a strong brand because you're charging ~$100, not a fortune.
- **Stack additional value into the offer** — for a 40+ women / US avatar: a recipe ebook like "55 meals you can make in under five minutes with no prep" that can feed a family; suggested title "What's for Dinner?" ("a question we all hate but you could answer just by reading the book on one page"). Validate the idea against someone who fits the avatar.
- ★ **Quiz funnel routing to multiple offers.** Build a quiz that leads to one of five or six offers based on the respondent's answers; either way you capture their information. Great for qualifying cold traffic.
- ★ **Quiz-funnel drop-off pattern:** expect ~50% drop-off between question 1 and question 2, another ~50% between Q2 and Q3, then "almost nothing" between Q3 and ~Q20. If you can get them past the first three questions you have a "straight shot" to the end and can ask nearly as many questions as you need.
- ★ **Weave sales messaging, belief-breaking and testimonials between the quiz questions** (VSL-style): answer → interject value/agitate a pain they didn't know about → back to the next question. This increases the likelihood they buy. Frame the intent up front: no one wakes up wanting to join another newsletter — they're there to solve a problem, so help them solve it.
- Quiz build is not native to Meta — it's an integration; "there's a zillion tools" to build it.
- ★ **Welcome-sequence blast to promote your own product.** Promote the owner's product (e.g. their AI fitness app) hard in the first four days — the biggest conversion window. Cadence: two emails per day for four days plus a text each = ~12 communications over four days. Seems like overkill, but there's a finite window where conversion is maximized, so hit it hard.
- **Recommended full setup:** add a welcome sequence promoting your own owned product, then the self-liquidating offer, plus the quiz — the quiz also yields rich avatar information you can retrofit an offer from, and advertisers value that subscriber information.
- ★ **Customer-call-mining workflow for offer copy.** Email subscribers offering free "coaching calls" (framed as wanting to understand how to serve them better, with an expert on staff); get them on the phone, record all the calls, and just ask them about their pain / all the problems they're dealing with. Do 10–20 of those calls, transcribe them, put the transcripts into GPT with a prompt to "order these into the most prevalent and use only the language the avatars used," then use that copy as the front end of the VSL. (Owner already uses this technique on subject lines to maximize open rate.)
- **Pair benefits to pain points.** Weave the harvested testimonials and language into the front of the VSL and make sure the benefits are matched to the specific pain points surfaced.
- **Don't give up on building a self-liquidating offer** — persistence on getting the offer right, not abandoning the SLO concept.
