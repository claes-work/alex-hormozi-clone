# Stop Caring About Your Competition (Alex Hormozi and Lewis Howes)

Interview clip. Alex Hormozi in conversation with **Lewis Howes** (host/interviewer). Only Alex-attributed statements below are persona data; Howes' questions are framing only.

## Summary

Alex argues that obsessing over competitors makes you emotionally reactive and adds no value — competitors don't put you out of business, but fixating on them does. The alternative he prescribes is total customer and product obsession: relentlessly improving the product, measuring product-quality metrics (not just marketing metrics), and engineering fast early wins so customers get an emotional "proof of concept" that builds trust. He closes with the strategic payoff: problem-driven (product-obsessed) businesses have far greater Enterprise Value than marketing-driven ones.

## Key claims

- Obsessing over competition makes you emotionally reactive to a rival's activities, so your focus goes to things that add no value. Competitors don't put you out of business — obsessing over them does.
- The alternative is to focus entirely on customers and product value.
- Going from "good to great" in a product or service takes far more effort than people estimate — like the gap between a four- and five-star restaurant (looks like 20% better, but 10x the effort).
- Alex rewrote his book five times end-to-end, each pass aimed at making it shorter and easier to consume. Same iterative approach was applied to a product with Jim Lawrence.
- Most operators claim their product is "amazing" but can't cite a single product metric (CRSA, CHS, NPS, TTV) — while they can rattle off marketing metrics (CPM, CPL, cost per call, CAC). If you don't measure product, your claim is hollow.
- Small product-quality levers compound: if each customer brings one more customer, return on ad spend doubles, and because there's no acquisition cost on that extra customer, most of the contribution margin drops to the bottom line.
- Time to Value (TTV): engineer an early win in the first ~7 days (e.g., getting a publishing client their first $1 via an MVP "mini book") to create an identity shift and emotional buy-in.
- Same principle from his gym days: an aggressive six-week cut up front (even though slow loss sticks better) delivers a fast, visible win that earns trust and keeps people committed for the sustainable phase.
- If people obsessed over product the way they obsess over promotion, the profit gain would be huge — and product-driven businesses are more sellable.
- Problem-driven businesses have far more Enterprise Value than marketing-driven businesses.
