# How to Win With AI in 2026

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi argues that learning to use AI should be a
person's top priority because "AI will never be worse than it is right now," and that
the biggest coming shift will hit Main Street businesses, not just tech. He reframes
business away from role-based org charts toward workflow-based automation (treating a
company like a manufacturing line of task-workflows), urges people to automate their
own jobs before someone else does, and lays out a "barbell strategy" for the future:
aggressively AI-first bets on one end, and durable "won't-change" industries (housing,
food/consumables, health/fitness, entertainment) on the other. He closes with a
tactical prompt to list your daily tasks at the most granular level and feed the first
one to AI to automate.

## Key claims (his AI views)

- AI will never be worse than it is right now; assuming any reasonable rate of
  improvement, learning to use AI should be priorities #1, #2, #3, and #10.
- The biggest shift is coming to Main Street businesses, not just tech businesses. He
  is "not here to fear monger" but to prepare people to take action.
- Now is the best time ever to start an AI-first business to disrupt existing markets,
  because incumbents are too busy running their business to actually be AI-first (vs.
  just using the words "AI first").
- His own newer, less-public companies were built AI-first from day one and hit
  revenue-per-employee "in the millions per year per head."
- Being genuinely AI-first is hard for large orgs: it's hard to get people to do new,
  uncomfortable things, and leaders avoid the hard conversation of "we automated away
  this role." His stance: raise the bar for the whole company; those who meet it stay,
  those who don't, don't — he calls this ugly/harsh but "reality."
- Cites a Jerome Powell clip (embedded, not Alex) on "effectively zero net job creation
  in the private sector"; Alex attributes this to people automating jobs away.
- Business Darwinism / game theory: the most adaptable survive, not the strongest or
  smartest. Adapt by learning the tools.
- You often risk more by NOT adopting new tech than by fearing rare downside cases;
  AI-safety fears ("AI will spend my credit card") are edge cases from over-permissioned
  agents without guardrails — like refusing to use the internet because you got hacked.
- The real barrier to adoption is complacency and short-term thinking, not danger.
  Learning AI is like training an employee: short-term cost, long-term payoff.
- Repeats his tweet: it takes ~20 hours to become proficient at any new skill, but
  people delay the first hour for decades. Spend a weekend hands-on with an agent.
- Stop role-based thinking; adopt workflow-based thinking. For every hire, list the
  4-10 concrete things that person does and ask whether each could be a workflow instead
  of head count ("what are the five things an editor does?").
- Every business takes raw inputs, adds "special sauce," and produces a more valuable
  output — like a manufacturing business. Org charts organize humans/communication;
  ideally you organize inputs and outputs (linear task-workflows) instead.
- Automate one task at a time, one layer beneath the role. If you don't automate your
  own job, you're "missing the boat" — the question is whether you or someone else
  controls that automation.
- Medium-term future = BYOS / BYOA: "bring your own software" / "bring your own
  agent(s)." An individual can walk into a business and be its "entire marketing
  department." Cites Anthropic reportedly having one person in marketing (aided by agents).
- This can be monetized as a contractor/agency, embedded for equity, or for more cash.
  "Erase the titleism" in the private market — he doesn't think titles survive.
- Where people fail: they don't train AI the way they'd train a new employee. First bad
  output ≠ fire; it means "train more." AI is the worst it will ever be.
- Humans learn through reinforcement (do → outcome → more/less). "Taste" = pattern
  recognition, communicated and rewarded. Computers are even better at recognizing
  patterns, so you train a computer the way you should train a human.
- His operations mindset (observable behaviors, stripping out emotional/intangible
  words, defining what "good" looks like) translates directly into being a good AI
  trainer. Most people never define what they actually want.
- Copy example: generic prompts produce "AI slop" (trained on the internet); giving 12
  rules + 16 writing samples yields ~5x better output, and 100 feedback loops that would
  take 1.5 years with a person take ~100 minutes with AI (no complaints, ~1/100th cost).
- AI laggards ("no one will replace humans") are like people using fax machines or
  counting on fingers — winning despite a disadvantage; easier to beat.
- Throughout history, humans + superior technology beat humans + inferior technology
  (Stone→Bronze→Iron age). As long as it's humans-with-tools vs. humans-with-tools you're
  still competing against humans and should feel confident. The day you try to beat the
  machine itself (chess, go), you lose — machines always eventually win.
- In a world of near-infinite AI labor and intelligence (cost of intelligence/labor → ~0,
  bounded by energy cost), the last thing a human gets paid to do is take risk. Money
  will still exist; labor will lose value. A robot with infinite intelligence is
  stronger, faster, and "works for $200" — the price of the electricity that runs it.
- Every company becomes a technology company (like adopting social media, internet,
  email, phone). Expects a big GDP explosion — output is driven by education/skills and
  technology — with more companies and businesses "blooming" than ever, even as many
  roles get automated away.
- Barbell strategy (borrows Bezos's frame of betting on what won't change): one end =
  high-risk/high-reward, fully AI-first/AI-native; other end = durable bets. Durable
  bets: humans still have bodies (health, fitness), consumables (food, supplements),
  housing, and entertainment (leisure/downtime grows → entertainment booms, and it's cheap).
- You can now make a full motion picture with AI, get viral PR, and make $100-200M "all
  margin"; people don't because they're afraid to take action.
- To see the bleeding edge of tech-in-business, "look at porn" — what porn adopts first
  eventually trickles down (cites AI avatar performers and chatbots trained on thousands
  of conversations; framed as pure economics, "not good or bad").
- "Hell in a handbasket" frame: if everything collapses to anarchy/permanent underclass,
  none of it matters anyway, so plan for the good/normal scenarios. "Hope for the best,
  prepare for the worst"; be an all-weather person.
- Cites a Brian Johnson (Blueprint) "swimming" post: you've trained your whole life to
  swim in ever-harder water, but the coming change is a phase shift where the water boils
  to gas — swimming skill no longer matters because the environment's physics changed.
- Opportunity thesis: humans are slow to adapt, so early movers are ahead. Over-50s who
  have "all the money" but won't adapt will watch that money move to those who do.
- Price sensitivity is also slow to adapt: embedded in a $2,000/month price is the
  typical cost of labor. If you keep charging $2,000 but cost drops from $500 to $50 or
  $5, margin and operational leverage explode — one person can drive millions in revenue,
  because coordination between humans is one of the biggest costs of scaling.
- Closing tactic: write down what you do every day at the most granular task level (don't
  say "I run ads" — break it into campaigns, budgets, analysis, creative, copy, landing
  pages, headlines), feed the first task to AI and ask "help me automate this / what
  steps would you take," then do the first item. Pro tip: screenshot your screen and ask
  "what do I do now?" Everyone has a free AI tutor at their fingertips and isn't using it.
