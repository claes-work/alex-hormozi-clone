# What Agencies Get Wrong About Pricing

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A Canadian performance-marketing / demand-generation agency owner (serving mid-size healthcare and med-tech companies across North America; ~40 clients; $2M/yr; retainers $3,500–$10,000/mo; annual churn 10–20%; ~25% margin; 60–70% close rate) wants to reach $10M but says his real blocker is fear — he lacks the courage to invest in sales/marketing hires. Alex's diagnosis is that the fear is downstream of a pricing error, not a structural one: the owner **moved up an avatar (went up-market) but forgot to change his price**, so he doesn't make enough per customer to feel safe reinvesting. At ~$3.5–10k on mid-market clients he's badly underpriced — Alex guesses the right average revenue per client is "closer to six-ish a month," which would push margins past 50% and make hiring/ad-spend decisions feel trivial. He rebuts the owner's worry that raising price lowers conversion: at the top of the market being *too cheap* signals you're **not legit** ("bordering on so cheap for a mid-market company that they don't believe that you're legit enough"), so a low price loses good buyers. He names the underlying limiting belief — **"selling out of your own wallet"** — pricing off what *you* would pay because you grew up modestly, and offers the mechanic-changing-his-own-oil analogy: value is set by the buyer who *can't* do it, not by you who can. He then gives his **close-rate → price-increase heuristic** (35% close = correctly priced; the higher above that, the bigger the latent price increase — a 60–70% close rate implies "at least a double"). Finally he prescribes a **usage-based scaling structure**: a flat floor (e.g. $6k for the first $20k of ad spend, required minimum), then extra percentage points layered on at ascending ad-spend "water marks," charged **non-retroactively** and as the **"greater of,"** with the top tier deliberately made tiny to exploit survivorship bias (buyers anchor on the cheap top rung and aspire to it). Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's core agency-pricing thesis: owners who go up-market often "move up an avatar" without raising price, so they're structurally underpaid on better clients — the fix is price, not a new avatar or a rebuild.
- As of March 2026, being *too cheap* for a mid-market buyer actively costs you deals: a low price makes a serious buyer doubt you're legitimate, so raising price can *increase* (not decrease) conversion among quality clients.
- As of March 2026, the root cause of underpricing is "selling out of your own wallet" — pricing off what *you* (given your background) would be willing to pay, rather than off the value to a buyer who can't do the work themselves (the mechanic-who-won't-pay-for-an-oil-change analogy).
- As of March 2026, his close-rate → price heuristic: ~35% close rate ≈ correctly priced; 40–50% ≈ a 25–50% price increase available; 50–60% ≈ ~1.5–1.75x; 60–70% ≈ at least a double; 70–80% ≈ a double or triple. A high close rate is evidence of undercharging.
- As of March 2026, for this agency the estimated correct average revenue per client is ~$6k/mo, which would lift margins past 50% and remove the fear that blocks reinvestment (hiring, ad spend); at 25% margin on $2M, ~20% on ~200 clients' spend, the economics feel too tight to gamble.
- As of March 2026, ad-spend-linked pricing should use **decreasing rates per tier** across ad-spend "water marks" (e.g. flat $6k for the first $20k, then +5% up to $100k, then +2.5% up to $200k), charged **non-retroactively** — the incremental percentage applies only within its band and stacks on top.
- As of March 2026, price on the **"greater of"** a flat floor or the usage formula, and gate clients with a minimum ad spend (~$20k/mo) because setup costs are front-loaded; the flat floor covers that fixed cost.
- As of March 2026, deliberately make the **top pricing tier tiny** to exploit survivorship bias — buyers fixate on the most expensive rung, see it as "almost free," and aspire upward — while the incremental margin to you is high because delivery is "just clicking buttons."
- As of March 2026, price (like ad spend) is one of the biggest emotional limiting beliefs in business — owners cap themselves ("I can't go past a thousand dollars a day") for emotional, not economic, reasons.
