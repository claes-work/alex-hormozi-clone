# My Full Body Workout (with Chris Williamson)

## Summary

Alex Hormozi takes podcaster Chris Williamson (guest, context only) through his
full-body gym workout while they talk training philosophy, gear obsessions, and
business. Alex explains his core training system: full body every day, same body
parts each session, high volume, worst-to-best body-part order, heavy use of
supersets, and a "stimulate to maintain" mindset at his current stage. The bulk of
the fitness-relevant content is Alex's self-reported routine; interspersed business
talk (Acquisition.com model, book writing, VC vs. their model) is secondary.

## Key claims (Alex's training approach)

- **Full body every day.** He trains the same body parts every session, every day —
  chosen for best body composition, no separate cardio (via supersets), and maximum
  muscle. Paraphrase of his stated rationale: "max muscle, least headache."
- **Trains in reverse order of weakness** — worst body part first, best body part
  last. He explicitly does *not* advise this for others.
- **Calves first, every workout, for 10+ years.** Calves are his weakest body part
  (proportions); he starts every session with them, doubled the volume, and trains
  them every workout rather than ~2x/week — that's when they started to grow. Also
  serves as a low-investment warm-up ("no one's ever going to say no to another set
  of calf raises") and raises core temperature.
- **Leg order: calves → hamstrings → quads**, using prefatigue — calves get involved
  in hamstrings, hamstrings in quads, so each move isolates the next muscle more.
- **Superset heavy** — e.g. seated hamstring curls supersetted with extensions; lets
  him skip dedicated cardio. "Rest time is camera time" when filming; otherwise he
  rests as little as possible.
- **Perfect reps then imperfect reps** — does as many full-range ("perfect") reps as
  he can, then continues with partial-range ("imperfect") reps to fatigue everything
  and get more volume per set. Acknowledges "this will bother all the fitness people."
- **Shoulders: trains only medial and rear delts**, not front delts ("not my
  deficiency, not my constraint, not my problem").
- **Does not train chest** — says his chest doesn't need to grow anymore (reported by
  Chris in the video).
- **Trains delt range only to where he feels the delt** — stops before it becomes trap
  (gets shooting neck pain); trains only that range, concedes others disagree.
- **Warm-up philosophy:** warming core temperature matters more than movement-specific
  warm-ups; cites weak evidence for stretching preventing injury; notes the Bulgarian
  Olympic team went straight to squats (but attributes that to athlete selection, not
  method).
- **Injury rehab method:** after tweaking his tricep (~2 months prior), he didn't take
  time off — dropped to the lowest weight on the same machine that injured it and adds
  ~10 lbs per week back. Analogy: gymnasts getting straight back on the pommel horse.
- **Cycling exercises:** doesn't formally cycle/periodize; "I just don't have a lot of
  rules." Same body parts daily; swaps a leg exercise by feel. Weaknesses (a clear
  visual weakness) get moved up in the workout to be prioritized.
- **Training partners:** ~50/50 whether he trains with people. Strongly prefers a
  partner — trains harder, longer, more sets, better form. Cites ~2:1 rest-to-lift
  ratio when social. Distinguishes "going in to die" solo sessions from "social"
  sessions.
- **Did his experimentation early** (in his 20s): tried CrossFit, 6 months all
  bodyweight, only swimming, only kettlebells, isometrics — driven by reading sales
  letters/ebooks — before settling on what matters. Zero temptation now to switch
  modalities.
- **"Greatest hack" — training with people ahead of you.** His biggest physique
  improvements came when better people gave him insight; "I'd rather get the lesson
  without the scar." (Applies same logic to business.)
