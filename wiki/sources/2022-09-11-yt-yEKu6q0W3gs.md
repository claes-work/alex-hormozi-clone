# How TO Make A TON Of Money (in just a few minutes) (2022-09-11)

## Summary

A tactical pricing lesson: Alex argues you should always offer something 10x/50x/100x more expensive than your current main product, because there's no strategic benefit to being second-cheapest but there is to being the most expensive. He explains price anchoring (his own 13,000 suit story), how to structure small/medium/large tiers to steer buyers, and reframes affordability as a value-perception problem (the "Ferrari for 10 grand" thought experiment).

## Key claims

- No matter what you sell, offer a 10x/50x/100x version of your main price; the only strategic reason to be lowest-cost is if you built the whole business around operational efficiency — otherwise competing on price runs you to breakeven ("you will just run a non-profit"). (paraphrase)
- Price anchors do two jobs: convert the rare high-end buyer, and make everything below look cheaper by comparison — so on a menu, always show the most expensive option first. (paraphrase)
- The middle price steers volume: with a 5 and 10 option, pricing the medium at ~6 (a dollar over the small) vs. ~9 (a dollar under the large) flips which product most people buy toward the "great deal." (paraphrase)
- "Price is what you pay, value is what you get; the deal is the discrepancy between the two" — people don't want cheap, they want great deals. (paraphrase)
- Affordability is a value problem, not a money problem: if they truly believed you'd hand them a new Ferrari for 10k, they'd find the money — non-buyers simply don't understand the value. (paraphrase)
- Price to the value delivered to a specific avatar, not to yourself (a CRO who lifts an e-commerce store 10% is worth far more to a 100M store than a 1M store); if a price doesn't get a gasp, you didn't go high enough — expect ~1 in 20 to buy the top anchor. (paraphrase)
