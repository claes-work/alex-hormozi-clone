# How to sell a product — The most PERSUASIVE tactic you can use (2021-09-20)

## Summary

In this early (2021) sales talk, Hormozi recounts a stage presentation he "completely bombed" — a fitness mastermind where he half-heartedly pitched a $2,000 product after two and a half hours of giving value, and got hostile Q&A instead of sales. From that humiliation he draws his core rule: **no half measures — either pitch or don't pitch, never in between.** He pairs it with a flip side: when you can, hold back the ask entirely and keep giving until people hand you business unprompted, because reciprocity does the selling for you.

## Key claims

- (2021) The central lesson: **no half measures — either pitch hard or don't pitch at all.** Half-assed asking is the worst option and fails.
- (2021) If you are going to make the ask, make it known you are asking, mean it, and ask hard — don't nickel-and-dime with a small ask here and there.
- (2021) Half-pitching means telling the audience they "don't need" the persuasion elements (scarcity, guarantees, bonuses) — but people still need those elements to make decisions, so removing them kills conversion.
- (2021) The flip side and the "most powerful" move: keep giving without asking, and reciprocity means people will start handing things to you — you may never need to pitch at all.
- (2021) He frames this as an upgrade on the "jab, jab, jab, right hook" (give, give, give, ask) model: extend the giving long enough and the "get" comes without a deliberate ask.
- (2021) The ask is a withdrawal from your "relational capital" / goodwill with an audience; it's usually better to keep investing (over-delivering) than to withdraw.
- (2021) Goodwill is, in his view, the biggest thing separating big business owners from small ones — small owners don't understand or apply it. (He notes he'll do a dedicated video on goodwill.)
- (2021) Context from the story: at the time his gym-licensing business was doing ~$4M/month with ~4,000 licensed locations; the $2,000 offer was the cheapest thing he'd ever sold (previously nothing below ~$16,000).
- (2021) On extreme-emotion moments (citing conversations with "Dr. Cashy"): heightened emotion puts the brain in "hyperlearning" mode, so he deliberately asks in the pain of a failure "what am I going to ingrain right now?"

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "No half measures... either sell or don't sell, uh, but don't be in between."

> "Either ask hard or do not ask at all. There is no halfass asking."

> "That is how you pitch. You're saying that they don't need all the things that persuade them... people still need it to make decisions."

> "If you get to a point of goodwill, if you keep giving and keep giving and keep giving, you don't need to ask ever. You will just get."

> "There's the commonly told... jab jab jab right hook — give give give ask. But I would postulate that there is another one that is even more powerful, which is give give give give give... get get get... people will just start handing things to you because humans are reciprocal in nature."

> "If you're going to make the withdraw, then ask and ask hard and mean it. It's not the time to make it a nickel here, a nickel there."

> "If you must pitch, then pitch like you mean it, right, and don't have facet."

## Topic notes

The **most persuasive tactic** the title refers to resolves to two linked principles: (1) **"no half measures" — commit fully to the pitch or don't pitch at all**, and (2) the deeper move of **giving relentlessly so reciprocity produces the "get" without an ask**. Both belong under sales/persuasion communication.

Relates to [[../topics/business/sales-communication]] — the mechanics of how a pitch is delivered (conviction, using rather than apologizing for scarcity/guarantees/bonuses), and to [[../topics/business/sales-frameworks]] — the "give/get" reciprocity model as an evolution of Gary Vaynerchuk's "jab, jab, jab, right hook."

Note the 2021 framing: this predates his more systematized offer/CTA teaching. The "relational capital / goodwill withdrawal" metaphor and the reciprocity-over-asking stance are worth cross-referencing when a later goodwill-specific source is ingested (he explicitly promises a dedicated goodwill video here).
