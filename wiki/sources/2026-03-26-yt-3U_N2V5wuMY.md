# What is Learning?

## Summary
In this short clip, Alex Hormozi restates his definition of learning as a change in behavior under the same conditions, and frames the absence of behavior change as proof that nothing was learned. He extends the idea into a definition of intelligence as rate of learning, and applies it to entrepreneurs who stay stuck at the same revenue level despite constant effort. The clip closes with a promotion for his free acquisition.com scaling roadmap.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex defines learning as "same condition, new behavior" — if conditions are unchanged and behavior is unchanged, no learning has occurred, regardless of exposure to information.
- As of March 2026, Alex defines intelligence as rate of learning, arguing that a person who never changes behavior in the same conditions is not actually smart.
- As of March 2026, Alex uses a video-game boss analogy: repeatedly losing to the same boss with the same behavior means the player, not the game, is the problem.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims that entrepreneurs who work all hours yet stay stuck at the same revenue for 6+ months are "doing the wrong shit" — same inputs, unchanged outputs, therefore no learning.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames rising in entrepreneurship or a career as evidence that behavior (and therefore learning) has changed.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "So, I define learning by same condition, new behavior."

> "If you listen to this podcast and then you're in the same exact conditions as you were before and then you do not change your behavior, you learned nothing."

> "Rate of learning, which I define as intelligence."

> "If you are trying to battle the same boss over and over again and you don't change what you're doing and the boss keeps beating you, then it's not the game's problem. It's your problem. You are the problem."

> "If you put all your inputs and the outputs haven't changed, then you have the same condition and the same behavior and so you have learned nothing."

> "Exposure to information isn't learning."
