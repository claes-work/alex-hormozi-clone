# Helping a $250K Sales Training Business Get to $1M by Fixing Its Price

## Summary

Alex tells a sales-training owner that a $2,000 phone-sold course is mispriced. Rather
than optimize only the VSL, he recommends adding delivery that makes the offer worth
$6,000-$8,000, tracking promised outcomes such as job placement and pay, and using those
results as truthful marketing evidence. He supports low initial pricing while evidence
is scarce, followed by price increases once outcome data is strong.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says phone-sold products generally begin around $3,000, while
  sub-$2,000 products more often sell through an automated checkout.
- For this offer, he recommends designing a $6,000-$8,000 product by adding the support
  customers need to succeed rather than merely charging more for the same course.
- He says the business should track the promised outcome—placement rate and resulting
  pay—not generic engagement inside the course.
- Outcome statistics allow marketers to state facts and let prospects judge the product
  using results from similar customers.
- Alex says lower prices and CAC are normal early in a launch because warm buyers cross
  the buying threshold more easily.
- He recommends rewarding early adopters with a large price-to-value gap, collecting
  reliable data, and raising price after a meaningful outcome period.
- The owner's revenue, CAC, and customer figures are self-reported and not independently
  verified in the excerpt.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I think you're mispriced."

> "You need to track the outcome that you promise."

> "State the facts and tell the truth that you actually have a good product."
