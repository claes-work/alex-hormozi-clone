# This is why I was Poor (2021-05-21)

## Summary

In this early (2021) video, Hormozi reconstructs a coaching conversation he had with a
member of his sales team whose family was pressuring them to leave sales for a "more
secure" job. He uses it to lay out his core wealth framework: distinguish volatility
from risk, treat wealth as a ratio of income-to-expenses (not a number), aggressively
save off a base salary, pay off high-interest debt first, dollar-cost-average the rest
into the S&P 500, and tie yourself to a customer-acquisition channel to grow income.
He frames a $200K/year salesperson as effectively owning a "million-dollar business"
with far less risk than actual ownership. The title notwithstanding, the video is a
mindset/wealth teaching piece rather than a personal poverty narrative.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-05-21)

- Don't take money advice from people who make less money than you (or the same at an
  older age); listen to people who make far more — echoing Grant Cardone's "listen to
  billionaires, not millionaires."
- Volatility and risk are not the same thing. Something that goes up and down a lot
  (e.g. an insurance business hit by a catastrophe every ~9 years, or a sales paycheck)
  can still be low-risk overall. Conflating the two is "a classic poor person thing."
- In a company, the people who generate revenue (salespeople) are the lowest-risk, most
  secure — not overhead roles. Salespeople have more paycheck-to-paycheck volatility but
  generally earn more.
- To cut volatility while keeping the upside: drive your expenses far below your base
  salary and save/invest the delta. Plan as if your base salary is all you'll make, so
  variable/commission income becomes "gravy."
- Worked example: someone on a ~$40K salary who lives on $25K can invest the delta in
  the S&P 500 and reach the top 1% within ~20 years; living with roommates accelerates
  it.
- Wealth is a ratio, not a number — inflow versus outflow (how much you spend vs. how
  much you make), not "I need to make X to be free." Freedom and peace of mind matter
  more than a lifestyle number.
- Example: a salesperson making ~$140K living on $25K saves ~$115K/year (~4.5 years of
  living expenses saved every year); at ~10% compounding he could hit ~$230K in ~2 years
  and technically "retire" on his current spending — a "24-month way of saving your way
  to wealth."
- Debt priority: pay off high-interest credit card debt first (he cites ~16% compounding
  as "a negative investment"); low-interest student loans are lower priority.
- He is "wildly against student loans," calling them unethical and a bank scheme —
  giving an 18-year-old with no skills access to ~$200K, non-dischargeable in
  bankruptcy, making them "a slave to the bank."
- He personally dislikes carrying any debt; paying off student loans early is an
  emotional, not mathematical, choice.
- Invest via the S&P 500 using dollar-cost averaging every month; don't try to time the
  market. Cites Warren Buffett's instruction to put his estate's money in the S&P 500.
- To grow income as an employee: pick a customer-acquisition channel the company isn't
  using, figure it out in your off time, get paid disproportionately for self-sourced
  clients, then build and lead a team doing it — becoming a "rainmaker," who are always
  paid most.
- A $200K/year salesperson effectively "owns a million-dollar business" (at ~20% net
  free cash flow) but with far less risk than actual ownership — and avoids HR, finance,
  fulfillment, legal, and lead-gen headaches.
- Cites that the average small business owner makes ~$78K/year; his salespeople making
  $150–200K can out-earn the average owner several times over.
- "Track don't slack": tracking your net worth (including debt as negative) daily or
  weekly is one of the highest-leverage wealth habits — it builds a "pulse" on money
  flow and surfaces waste (e.g. forgotten software subscriptions) to plug.
- Recap action plan: (1) track net worth; (2) pay off high-interest debt first;
  (3) dollar-cost average excess into the S&P; (4) aggressively cut expenses and save
  off your base salary.
- Luxury signals (private jets, exotic cars, penthouses, fancy restaurants/clothing)
  are "not what they're cracked up to be"; freedom and peace of mind are more
  fulfilling.
