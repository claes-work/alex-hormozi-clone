# $100M Offers — The Lost Chapter

**What it is.** A single ~7-page bonus chapter titled "Your First Avatar," a cut/companion chapter to
*$100M Offers* (2021) distributed by ACQUISITION.COM. It teaches a customer-selection method — survey
your existing customers, isolate the top-20% highest-value buyers, extract the few qualifiers they share,
then re-target advertising and re-engineer the sales/buyer journey around only those people (`PDF p. 2-8`).
Raw path: `raw/books/raw/books/2022-100m-offers-the-lost-chapter-alex-hormozi-compressed.md`.

## Key claims
- The method is adapted from Vista Equity Partners' playbook: score customers by who stays longest and
  pays most, buy companies with a "vein of underserved valuable customers," then cut the channels feeding
  low-value customers and double down on channels feeding high-value ones (`PDF p. 2`).
- It is "Pareto's principle (80/20) on steroids" — 20% of customers bring 80% of revenue; replacing the
  low-value 80% with high spenders can grow a business ~5x (`PDF p. 2`). It became a pillar of
  acquisition.com's "value acceleration method (VAM)" (`PDF p. 2`).
- Four-step install process: (1) survey customers on demographics, business stats, aspirations, and
  buying process; (2) sort for biggest/longest/most-liked spenders and focus on the top 20%, ignore the
  rest; (3) find the fewest common qualifiers (usually 3–5); (4) execute — speak the avatar in ads and
  re-engineer the sales process (`PDF p. 3-4`).
- Applied inside Gym Launch, the top-20% avatar was profiled as: right-leaning/conservative, married,
  25–45, male, US gym owner, signed lease, 1+ employee, $10k+/mo revenue, 30+ existing clients (`PDF p. 5`).
- 78% of their top customers had consumed at least two pieces of long-form content before buying; a
  prospect who hadn't was less likely to close (`PDF p. 5-6`).
- Fix: inject two long-form value-in-advance content pieces into every lead's buyer journey, increase
  content output, and arm the sales team with a "greatest hits" list to hand-pick pieces — forcing
  prospects through the same journey that produced the best customers (`PDF p. 6`).
- Comparison anecdote: a competitor serving the same fitness-owner vertical with the same total sales
  made 70x less profit because "they accepted anyone with a pulse and a credit card," yielding high churn
  and low retention (`PDF p. 6`).
- Removing qualification steps raised lead volume but lowered profit every time; Alex merged marketing and
  sales into one "acquisition department" to stop the volume-vs-quality infighting (`PDF p. 6-7`).
- Gym Launch LTV was north of $45,000 vs. competitors' $6,000–$8,000; he'd rather pay $5,000 to acquire
  $45,000 than $1,000 to acquire $5,000 (`PDF p. 7`).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> That’s it. More of the high profit customers. Fewer low profit customers. Rinse. Repeat. (PDF p. 2)

> Speak your new avatar. Be upfront about your customer requirements. Get all advertising to speak directly to them. You will repel the bad customers and attract the good ones. (PDF p. 3)

> The difference. They accepted anyone with a pulse and a credit card. As a result, they dealt with high customer churn, high costs of acquisition, low retention rates, and lower satisfaction scores. (PDF p. 6)

> Every time we removed qualification steps, our lead volume increased, but we made less money. (PDF p. 6)

> I would rather pay $5000 to acquire $45,000 than pay $1000 to acquire $5000 (even though it costs five times as much). (PDF p. 7)

> It feels like cheating because it's so obvious. But here’s the great part - no one does it. (PDF p. 8)
