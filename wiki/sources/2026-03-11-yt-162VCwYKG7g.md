# Why I Started ACQ Ventures

## Summary
Interview clip (interviewer asks; Alex answers) in which Alex explains why he launched **ACQ Ventures**, a new early-stage venture arm sitting alongside Acquisition.com's core private-equity operation. His stated reason is deal flow: so many founders were approaching Acquisition.com that a venture vehicle became the natural fit. Alex frames the opportunity as **largely tech** — venture economics require the possibility of very large exit outcomes, which lend themselves to tech companies. He describes two "buckets" of inbound. **Bucket one:** founders who have already raised a round, consume his content, and would "rather have you on our cap table than somebody else" — his ideal scenario, because these companies can have large exits and don't require operational work, letting Acquisition.com take *smaller equity slugs* and be "money only" (versus the core model, which required a significantly larger equity position to justify the return in exchange for hands-on growth work). **Bucket two:** bootstrapped founders with no cap table at all who aren't trying to exit or take on a growth partner — they just need capital and prefer his. These require "almost no work and expertise." Because check sizes are small, Alex says he doesn't even need to raise a fund — he deploys his **own money**, so there are no outside LPs ("your LP is yourself"). He reports having already done roughly six to eight deals in the prior two months and notes the venture is "heating up." He contrasts fund incentives (a fund structure incentivizes deploying capital / LP money) with deploying his own money, where a big deployment isn't cause for celebration ("I took a lot of swings. We'll see... I've spent all my money"). Structurally, the core group operates 100% like a private-equity firm while ACQ Ventures operates like a VC, run by a **general partner who came from the Y Combinator world** and sources his own deal flow outside the network. Alex closes on his recurring thesis: he wants businesses that already work so he can "put gasoline on" them (citing Skool, which compounded month-over-month with zero advertising before they "added gas"), rather than businesses that only work because of him — and he restates that he solves for **revenue retention**, because a company that dies when you go on vacation is "a higher-leverage income," not a sellable asset.
