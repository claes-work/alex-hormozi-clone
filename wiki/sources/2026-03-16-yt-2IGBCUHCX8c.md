# Helping an E-Commerce Brand Doing $2M/Year with Recruiting

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A custom cowboy-hat e-commerce brand (~$2M/yr, ~780k organic followers, 12–18x ROAS on paid, hats priced $800–$1,200, all custom and sold end-to-end virtually) wants to reach $5M but is **supply-constrained**: they cannot make hats fast enough, and mastering hat-making takes years. The owner frames it as a recruiting/training problem and asks how to hire skilled makers, especially given they're based in Kentucky where "no one wants to be." Alex reframes it as a **pricing-of-talent** problem: if you are supply-constrained and "making money hand over fist," you are *under-pricing your talent*. His recruiting playbook: (1) figure out how much more you'd need to pay to get someone to move to Kentucky and go through training, and check whether you still keep good margin; (2) if a raise (e.g. 30%) works, test whether passing that cost through to the customer changes acquisition at all — if not, "jack the price, jack the talent." On sourcing, he's blunt that recruiters *can* find hat makers: just Google "hat maker" and call the business owners, because hat-making is a **dying trade** full of skilled artists who "can barely make ends meet" and lack business sense — offering them the money they want while doing the craft they love is "a pretty good offer." He uses his own Acquisition.com team as the analogy: they earn ~$300k–$750k+ because top marketers command that elsewhere, and paying above-market for scarce talent is correct when you have the margin and pricing power. Sequencing: **supply first** — get the makers before you hit the "big red button" to double demand. On latency, pushing delivery from ~2 months-under-industry out further won't change conversion much but also doesn't solve the real recruiting constraint. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's diagnosis: if you are **supply-constrained and highly profitable, you are under-pricing your talent** — compensation can be over- or under-market, and scarcity that leaves you "making money hand over fist" is a signal you're paying scarce labor too little.
- As of March 2026, his recruiting/compensation test: calculate how much more you'd need to pay to get a skilled worker to (1) relocate and (2) go through training; if you still keep a good margin at that higher pay, do it.
- As of March 2026, on funding the raise: model what a pay increase (e.g. 30%) does if you change nothing about price, versus if you pass it through to the customer; if passing it through "doesn't change anything about our acquisition," then "jack the price, jack the talent."
- As of March 2026, on sourcing scarce skilled labor: recruiters *can* find them — literally Google the trade (e.g. "hat maker") and call the business owners directly.
- As of March 2026, a **dying trade is where the "alpha" is**: it's full of skilled artisans who "can barely make ends meet" and lack business sense, so offering them the income they want while doing the craft they love is a compelling, "dream life" offer for many (not all — some have ego barriers to working for someone else).
- As of March 2026, don't try to "pay as little as I can" and then be surprised no one wants to relocate; when you have margin and pricing power, use it to out-pay for scarce talent.
- As of March 2026, he uses his own team as proof: Acquisition.com staff earn roughly $300k on the low end up to $750k+, because anyone great at marketing can earn a lot elsewhere, so he pays well above what a comparable operator would net alone (e.g. "double" a ~$200–250k take-home) plus the appeal of working with high-level business owners.
- As of March 2026, **sequencing rule: supply first** — solve delivery capacity (hire the people) before hitting the demand lever ("the big red button") to double content/ads and sales; don't scale demand you can't fulfill.
- As of March 2026, on latency: he dislikes using delivery latency as a lever ("there's no difference between 6 and 8 months — it's just forever"), and around the 3–4 month mark you have to "slowly grab everyone off the ledge"; pushing delivery out can jam in cash but "doesn't solve the recruiting problem."
