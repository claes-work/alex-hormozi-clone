# My Evidence Based Guide to Making Money Online [TIER LIST]

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi (10M+ subscribers across platforms) ranks online
platforms by their money-making potential, using his own up-to-date CRM traffic and
conversion data. He evaluates each platform against five criteria — **who** (share of
$100k+ households), **how many** (US market share), **growth** (ease of gaining
audience), **depth** (time spent per unit of content), and **conversion** — then
assigns a tier grade and states where he is allocating resources for the next year.
Key reveal: raw follower counts mislead; per-follower conversion to his site varies
enormously, so he divests from TikTok and doubles down on YouTube, Instagram, Facebook,
email, and Amazon.

## Key claims

Final tier ranking (Alex's grades, for **his** B2B / business-owner avatar):

- **S tier — YouTube**: "highest praise," where he's doubling down. 89% of $100k+
  households present; 83% US market share; ~8.5 min average view duration; YouTube
  traffic converts at 3–4x every other platform; ~60% of his contacts (portfolio /
  business-owner leads) originate on YouTube; 91-second average on-site session length.
  Conversion on-platform is low-to-medium (must send people off-platform; community tab
  helps — his community posts got 2.5M impressions in 30 days vs. 18M from shorts).
- **S tier — Instagram**: 54% $100k+ households, 47% US market share; high growth via
  Reels; depth low (12–20 sec, skewed low by his tweet-reels); conversion second only to
  LinkedIn (stories, swipe-ups, ManyChat, IG shop). Sends **48% of all his site
  traffic** — in absolute terms more traffic than YouTube — though it converts about the
  same as other platforms (less than YouTube). 28-sec session length.
- **A tier — X / Twitter**: 29% $100k+ households, 22% US market share; most viral
  platform (share button); high conversion for niched accounts; ~6% of traffic (roughly
  1:1 with his follower share). Its real value is as his **content hub** — 100% of his
  tweets are him in the moment, and X feeds captions/long-form for every other platform.
  He'd pick YouTube over X if forced, but X is "the wellspring of everything else."
- **A tier — Facebook**: opinion he changed this video. 68% $100k+ households and 68% US
  market share (highest reach); high growth again since Reels; 3.8% of traffic from only
  ~150k followers — nearly double his TikTok output (900k followers) = ~9x the potency
  per follower. Second-highest on-site session length (53 sec). Now investing more.
- **A tier — Email**: "the conversion platform of conversion platforms"; ~36:1 average
  return; 40%+ open rates; least compliance risk (owned media); 133-sec on-site session
  (highest, but these are existing contacts responding to CTAs). Not S only because you
  can't grow a list on the list itself. Says you should make 30%+ of revenue from email.
- **B tier — LinkedIn**: opinion he changed (upgraded). 53% $100k+ households, 30% US
  market share, ~7 min/day usage; conversion extremely high per follower (Sales
  Navigator = outbound monster; 40% of B2B marketers call it primary channel); 9%+ of
  traffic, 40-sec session (2nd to YouTube). Biggest unspoken ROI = **recruiting** talent
  for his portfolio companies. Growth medium-high (connection-driven, share culture).
- **B tier — Podcast (Spotify)**: 54% of $75k+ listen; 64% of Americans use audio;
  deepest engagement (20-min average listen, blows everything away) and highest trust,
  but very hard to grow and hard to convert (listeners can't click). Long-term nurture.
- **F tier — TikTok**: 27% $100k+ households (lowest), 33% US market share; easy-ish
  growth that's gotten harder; shallow depth (5–20 sec); low conversion for services
  (78% of businesses sell services); only 2.5% of his traffic despite ~900k followers
  (~1/4 the effectiveness per unit); 8-sec on-site session (lowest). Divesting attention.

Evaluation criteria he uses: **who** (% of $100k+ households, via Pew), **how many**
(US market share), **growth/discoverability**, **depth** (avg view/listen duration),
**conversion** (ability to start a conversation + take people to an offer), then
**results** (actual CRM traffic share + on-site session length).

Honorable mentions (resources going here, no full breakdown):
- **Amazon** — his "big secret" #4 and a sleeper. Everyone (300M Prime members) is on
  it with a card on file; his book page converts at ~30% vs. Shopify's ~2% average
  (~15x). Books sell ~1,000/day = 1,000 customers/day; sold more copies than he had
  email contacts. Affiliate model (~10% commission) under-tapped for influencers because
  Amazon's innate trust means far higher conversion, and it feels less salesy. Books also
  act as a discovery/traffic source back to him via in-book CTAs/QR codes.
- **Pinterest** — highest average revenue per user (~$120k+); great for visual/female-
  skewed niches (art, wine, home decor); low creator competition. Had an art dealer with
  90% of business from Pinterest.
- **Snapchat** — one of the highest average revenue per viewer; strong commerce;
  Millennial-heavy, coming back with Gen Z. Watching it, not yet investing.
- **Twitch** — favors very long "day in the life" streams; big opportunity if your
  workstyle allows it (his portfolio meetings are confidential, so it's hard for him).

Growth/production tips he gives:
- Shorts are now **platform-specific** — what works on one platform's short-form won't
  necessarily work on another; the ecosystem is fragmenting.
- Two things to obsess over on YouTube: the **thumbnail** (he split-tested 9 for one
  video) and the **first 8 seconds** (be done saying what the video is about by then; he
  did 8 takes of this intro, working it from 30 sec down to 8).
- Consistency compounding: he committed to 3 videos/week with one guy and a webcam;
  build a "mondo checklist" by improving one thing weekly.
- LinkedIn growth hack: get large accounts to comment (a comment ≈ a collab); attract
  them by writing something significant about them.
- Instagram penalizes over-posting — his sweet spot is 2 curated sessions/day; TikTok and
  X don't penalize volume.
- "Give in public, sell in private" — convert via DM so you never have to make public
  asks; content should be about what you sell and your avatar's problems, not just views.
- Twitter DM etiquette: open with a specific compliment (name the exact thread and why),
  never lead with an ask.
