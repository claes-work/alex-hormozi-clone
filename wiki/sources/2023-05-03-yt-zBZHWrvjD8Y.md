# How I Set Goals That ACTUALLY MAKE MONEY

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out his four-step, science-derived goal-setting
process, which he claims to have adapted from a science book rather than any business
book. The core idea is to treat a goal not as a fixed target but as a testable
hypothesis linking an input (an activity you control) to an output (the result you
hope for), and to force every goal to tie back to one of three business levers. He
argues that goal-setting done right is deliberately hard because it makes you think
about *why* you do what you do, and that big businesses win through compounding 1%
improvements rather than reinvention.

## Key claims (his goal-setting method)

- **The four steps:** (1) What problem are we solving? (2) Create a hypothesis. (3)
  What are we going to do (the input/activity)? (4) What do we think is going to happen
  (the output)? Then review: did we do it, and did the desired result happen?
- **Every goal must chunk up to one of three things:** get more customers, make
  customers worth more, or increase the enterprise value of the company. If you can't
  articulate how a goal does one of these, you shouldn't do it.
- If you can't tie your department/function to one of those three levers, you don't
  understand your job or how you provide value to the business.
- **Frame goals as hypotheses, not goals**, because doing the activity never guarantees
  the outcome. A hypothesis defines two variables: the input (activity you do) and the
  output (result you expect).
- Example hypothesis: "If I decrease the time to call leads from 30 minutes to 5
  minutes, we will get a higher percentage of leads to book calls." Input baseline vs.
  target (e.g. 37 min → 5 min); output baseline vs. target (e.g. 50 scheduled → 70).
- **Review has a "before phase" and an "after phase."** After the quarter/year, check:
  did the input change happen (green/red), and separately did the output improve?
- You can hit the input and still fail the output — that's the whole point of a
  hypothesis. If a partial input change (e.g. down to 7 min) produced no directional
  improvement in the output, don't bother pushing further to 5 min.
- **Growth comes from a series of 1% improvements**, not reinventing the wheel. Stacking
  small gains across the funnel (opt-ins, schedules, shows, close rate) can multiply the
  business. This is especially relevant in the $5–20M top-line range.
- **Goals should cascade top-down:** company sets ~5 goals; department heads derive
  multiple activities/hypotheses under them. He wants everyone far down the org to do
  this so they connect their daily work to how the company grows.
- Employees lose motivation when they don't see how their work ties to the bigger
  picture; thinking this way makes a teammate far more valuable and lets them think like
  a business owner.
- In early-stage businesses, simply measuring/establishing metrics can itself be the
  right goal for a quarter, so you have something to improve later.
