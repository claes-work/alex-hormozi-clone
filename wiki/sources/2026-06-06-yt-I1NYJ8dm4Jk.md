# Helping a $1.5M Nail Salon Owner Stop Doing Too Much

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a premium nail salon owner in San Antonio doing about $1.5M from one location while building social media, education, products, and a future salon ecosystem. Alex distinguishes vision from plan: the long-term vision can include education and franchising, but the near-term plan is to make the core salon model stronger, less founder-dependent, and profitable without relying on the founder's social audience. Only then should she use media to sell franchises.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A founder should distinguish the long-term vision from the current plan; trying to defend too much terrain alone creates burnout.
- The best path may be franchise sales funded by the founder's media, but only after the unit model is truly nailed.
- A salon model should be tested as if it had to buy customers with paid acquisition, not rely on founder influence.
- A local service model with 25% margins may still be under-optimized if founder magic and free social media are masking acquisition cost.
- The founder should be pulled completely out of the salon before selling the model nationally.
- Franchising is fundamentally a capital play that lets other people's capital fund many locations.
- The hard problem is training quality control; solving that is what can make the model valuable.
