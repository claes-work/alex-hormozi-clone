# Start High Ticket, Then Go Mass Market

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A creator whose strength is organic Facebook/Instagram marketing (running a coaching/info business) asks Alex whether to combine an info product with a done-for-you service, and whether to sell to the masses. Alex's core answer is a **sequencing rule**: unless you have a genuinely scalable deliverable (software, information, media — e.g. Skool), a service business should **go up-market first** and sell to people who have more money, *then* build toward a business that retains customers at near-zero incremental cost, at which point you can descend to the masses. His reasoning: ~80%+ of businesses are service-based and incur real cost to deliver, so serving the masses requires capital and skill most under-capitalized founders don't have (he cites Amazon needing to raise huge capital to serve the masses). If you're going to do the same work anyway, you "might as well get paid 10 times as much," because there's an audience that would pay 10x more — "they just don't know you exist. So let's go find them." On combining info + service: don't; selling to a low-level avatar and bolting on high-touch service will be painful. The **bottom of the market is the worst to serve** because a small amount of money is a huge percentage of the buyer's income/savings, so they carry the expectations of someone handing over their life savings — nothing satisfies them, and they drain your team's morale and your reputation. Alex prefers to set expectations at zero and pleasantly surprise, rather than promise the world and under-deliver. A percentage of low-ticket buyers can ascend into a higher-touch, higher-priced offer — and that's the right path, rather than serving everyone at the bottom.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, Alex advises service businesses to **start up-market (high ticket) and descend to mass market later** — go to people with more money first, then build a retentive, near-zero-marginal-cost business, and only then sell to the masses.
- As of February 2026, mass-market monetization is straightforward only if you have a super-scalable deliverable — software (e.g. Skool), information, or media; ~80%+ of businesses are service-based and incur cost to deliver each customer, so this doesn't apply to most.
- As of February 2026, the failure mode is under-capitalized, under-skilled entrepreneurs trying to serve the masses without the capital or skill to scale it; he cites Amazon as an exceptional mass-market business that nonetheless required raising enormous capital to build.
- As of February 2026, if you're doing the same work either way, you should charge ~10x more, because an audience exists that would pay 10x — the only barrier is that they don't know you exist yet, so the job is to go find them.
- As of February 2026, he advises against combining a low-ticket info offer with a done-for-you service; selling to a low-level avatar and adding high-touch service will be "painful."
- As of February 2026, the bottom of the market is hardest to serve because the small sum they pay is a huge share of their income/savings, so they hold the expectations of someone who gave you their life savings — almost nothing satisfies them, and they drain team morale and reputation.
- As of February 2026, low-end buyers also tend to demand you "promise everything" for a tiny amount (his example: $10); Alex prefers to set expectations at zero and pleasantly surprise rather than over-promise and fail to fulfill.
- As of February 2026, the healthy path for low-ticket buyers is that a **percentage ascend** into a higher-touch, higher-priced offer — not serving everyone at the low end.
