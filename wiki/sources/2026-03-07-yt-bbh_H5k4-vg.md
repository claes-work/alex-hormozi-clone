# What Makes a Good Entrepreneur?

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks the questions; only Alex-attributed material is used as voice/belief data). Asked what makes a really good entrepreneur/leader, Alex names **four things**. First, the **power to influence** — the ability to move other people. He unifies sales, leadership, management, and marketing under one definition: "the ability to get people to comply with your requests" (marketing being that request made publicly / one-to-many). Second, **tremendous drive** — fuel that can run "towards" a big mission or "away" from a big fear; either polarity works. Third, **impulse control** — the ability to say no to things regularly for an extended period. Fourth, the ability to **boil the business down into inputs and outputs**: if you don't know the inputs that produce the output you want, "what are you doing?" An entrepreneur who has all four — can lead/sell, has a big motivator, can control themselves long enough to persist, and does the right things because they know their inputs and outputs — "becomes a very difficult person to beat." On the host's follow-up about door-to-door sales, Alex reframes it as **high-volume transactional sales** (door-to-door, cold calling, or 20 gym consults a day). Skill acquisition needs two ingredients: maximum **repeated exposure** to the action plus **quick feedback loops**; the ideal is a mentor-mentee cycle of "fix this, try again." Most people who weather the first three months of sales end up fine; he tells newcomers to shadow the best rep and do twice their volume, working all the hours, to compress the "you have to suck for X period" phase in calendar days. The payoff is learning to get rejected and keep going, plus interpersonal skills reusable with teammates and in marketing ("marketing is just sales one-to-many"), building a deep understanding of human psychology. He closes with a mindset point: people are oriented to lifestyle and what they can post on Instagram rather than to developing skills, which requires **patience** — a trait "absent in modern culture." Every trait we say we want (patient, humble, tough) "has a price tag attached to it," and the price is paid in time — "the seconds of life that they're trading for it." Reframing a hard period as that price, he says, has given him comfort in hard times: at 80 he'll want to have these traits, and he knows he has to go through these things to earn them. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
