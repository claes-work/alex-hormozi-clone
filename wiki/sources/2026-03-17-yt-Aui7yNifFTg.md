# Helping a Coaching Business Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client is a former air-medical helicopter pilot who now runs an aviation-safety training/coaching business — coaching, courses, and content across the whole pilot career path (through airline interview prep). He charges helicopter owners ~$10k for an initial engagement plus a mentorship program that cuts their insurance from ~$180k/year to ~$90k, and he sells his one-on-one training for ~$1,750; he's making ~$500k/year one-on-one (up from a flight instructor's ~$20k) but is maxed out and wants to move to a group/online model. His grand mission is to "close the knowledge gap for all pilots" and prevent crashes (he cites 363 air-medical crashes, ~$100M insurer cost per crash, 17,000 NTSB safety recommendations), and he keeps spiraling into tangents about the tech/AI product, the insurance carriers, the brokers, and the 600,000 pilots retiring over a 10-year window. Alex's whole method here is **forcing clarity**: he refuses to strategize until the client answers one question — "how much money do you want to make?" — and repeats it many times until the client lands on **$65M over 10 years = $5M/year = 10x current revenue**. Once the number is fixed, Alex's diagnosis is fast: the model isn't broken; the client is simply **supply-constrained** because he can only deliver one-on-one. The fix is two moves — **change the delivery ratio** (one-on-one → group/online, which can charge roughly the same price) and then **advertise more** to get 10x the leads. He notes that had he learned the supply constraint up front, "this would have been a 2-minute thing." On the "biggest impact / prevent all crashes" ambition, Alex says if the client truly wants $100M-scale impact he should go back to the insurance companies, do the first implementation **for free**, and use the resulting case study (saving ~$1B/year) as a "money printing machine" — but that's a separate path. He also does the arithmetic on the mission: 1% of 600,000 pilots × $10k = ~$60M, and argues "not dying and not killing people" is a strong enough motivator to sell on. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex refuses to give scaling advice until the client names a concrete revenue target — "how much money do you want to make?" — because the target dictates the strategy: staying at ~$2M means "keep doing this, do it better," while $100M means a fundamentally different approach (go back to the insurers).
- As of March 2026, Alex's diagnosis: the client's model isn't broken — he's **supply-constrained** because he can only deliver one-on-one, which caps a business making ~$500k/year; the real problem is the delivery mechanism, not the offer or the market.
- As of March 2026, the fix to scale 10x is two moves: **change the delivery ratio** (one-on-one → group/online) and then **advertise more**; everything else discussed (the tech product, the carriers, the brokers) can be ignored.
- As of March 2026, a group/online version can be priced at roughly the **same amount** as the one-on-one because "you sell the same product" — the vehicle doesn't matter much, and if delivery is more convenient, faster, and on-demand, that's "better, not worse."
- As of March 2026, the one question that actually matters for this business is "how do I get 10 times the leads I'm currently getting?" — and the client admitted he had not maxed out his current advertising method.
- As of March 2026, for maximum *impact* (preventing crashes at $100M scale), Alex's play is: beg/borrow/steal your way into one insurance company, do the first implementation **for free**, then use the case study (10 crashes/year × $100M = ~$1B saved) as proof — "you just need to demonstrate that once, and then you have a money printing machine."
- As of March 2026, Alex sizes the mission opportunity: selling to just **1% of the 600,000 pilots** entering the market over the next decade at $10k each is ~$60M, roughly the client's $65M goal.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues "not dying and not killing people" is a high-value motivator to sell on — stronger than the ~$90k/year insurance savings alone.
- As of March 2026, Alex reflects that diagnosing the supply constraint up front would have made the whole consult "a 2-minute thing" — the lesson being to find the binding constraint before exploring tangents.
