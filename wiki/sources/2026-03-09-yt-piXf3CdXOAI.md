# You Need More Skills

## Summary
Solo monologue built on a four-quadrant taxonomy of how people spend their two currencies (time and money): **"The rich buy time, the poor buy stuff, ambitious people buy skills, and lazy people buy distractions."** Alex pairs these — rich people use money to buy time; ambitious people use time to buy skills — and argues skill acquisition is the real engine of wealth. His justification for why it's *ambitious* people who buy skills: **generational wealth isn't transferred through assets or stuff but through education and skills.** He cites a Sanskrit proverb ("If you have a good son, you don't need to accumulate wealth; if you have a bad son, there's no point accumulating wealth") to argue that passing on money to an heir without skills is irrelevant — a skilled heir makes the inheritance redundant, and an unskilled one squanders it either way. He then turns to the "lazy → distractions" quadrant with a harsh psychological read: lazy people distract themselves (fluff videos, feel-good content) because confronting the mirror and admitting "I am inadequate" is too painful; the answers people seek "are not in your news feed." He detours into a **gratitude/entitlement** argument: entitlement kills gratitude (if you feel you deserved something, you're never grateful when you get it), gratitude raises subjective well-being, so you should expect nothing and treat yourself as worthy only of what you've already earned ("You have proven you can work minimum-wage jobs, so you are worthy of minimum-wage compensation"). He acknowledges the take will get clipped to paint him as "some evil rich guy" and says he'll take the hate for the few who change. Finally he warns you can be **ambitious AND distracted (which keeps you poor)**, illustrating with his own past: at his peak he ran **nine businesses** because he'd heard "seven income streams make you wealthy" — but that myth just "spread you seven ways." He had partners in each (a chiropractor agency, a dental agency, a gym-turnaround business, and six gym locations) yet was still the sole rainmaker required to sell into all nine. Real multiple income streams come from **owning assets** that generate cash flow, and buying those assets requires first accumulating cash through **time and focus**. He closes by operationalizing focus: **"Focus is measured by the amount of things you say no to"** — the most focused person says yes to only one thing and no to everything else. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
