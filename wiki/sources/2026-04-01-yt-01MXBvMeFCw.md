# The Bar for Excellence Is Embarrassingly Low

## Summary

Alex treats consistency, preparation, and measured time-on-task as unusually accessible
advantages. He recommends beginning difficult work immediately, timing only focused work,
and even stopping mid-task to create an open loop that makes restarting easier.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex cites Charlie Munger's inversion argument that inconsistency is
  a reliable ingredient of failure; the speech is paraphrased rather than sourced here.
- He distinguishes claimed consistency from exact adherence, illustrated by asking gym
  clients how many of 21 prescribed meals they followed precisely.
- Twenty minutes of meeting preparation can create a disproportionate impression because
  many people arrive unprepared; he self-reports once spending four hours preparing seven
  pages for a consulting day.
- He uses a kitchen timer for predicted task duration and stops it whenever attention
  leaves the task, exposing the gap between desk time and genuine focused work.
- His advice is to start rather than build elaborate conditions or routines around work;
  he says the working mood typically follows several minutes of actual engagement.
- Stopping halfway through a sentence can preserve an open loop and lower the friction
  of returning to the task.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Do the boring work because boring is what makes you rich."

> "The harder that you try, the easier it gets."

> "Instead of spending time getting in the mood to work, just start working."
