# You Need to Do More

## Summary

In this short solo clip, Hormozi argues that the primary lever most small businesses are missing is sheer volume of activity: they "do so little and think they're doing so much." He illustrates the point with a story from his first gym, where a mentor with 20 locations advertised via flyers at vastly larger test sizes than Hormozi's initial attempt. He extends the principle to content and personal branding — contrasting a typical creator's one-to-two pieces per week against his own team's output — and frames the whole idea as leverage: getting more out for what you put in, then reinvesting profit into either more of the same or a better/newer channel. The clip closes with a promotion for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

## Key claims

- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** The first thing to do to grow is simply to do more; most small businesses do very little while believing they are doing a lot, and this gap in understanding is huge.
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** In his first gym, Hormozi asked a mentor (who had 20 locations) how to advertise; the mentor said "flyers." Hormozi put out 300 flyers, it didn't work, and he complained. The mentor asked about his "test size."
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** The mentor's operation used a test size of 5,000 flyers; once they had a winner, they distributed 5,000 flyers every day (placed on cars) — roughly 150,000 flyers over a 30-day period, versus Hormozi's 300 in the same period (~500x the volume). The larger operator naturally got a better result.
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** A fundamental misconception of small businesses is that they mistake low volume for volatility: sporadic, unpredictable sales feel volatile, but there is a definite amount of advertising activity behind them. Competitors doing one-to-two sales a day are simply doing every day what the small business does in a month.
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** The same volume gap applies to personal brands. When someone says they want to build a personal brand, Hormozi asks how much content they put out; a typical answer is one or two pieces a week. For context, his team puts out 450 pieces a week, which is why their brand is far larger.
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** People struggle to fathom that someone can work 2x, 5x, 10x, or 1,000x the output — but that difference in output is usually the real reason for a corresponding difference in results.
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** This is fundamentally the concept of leverage: getting more for what you put in. You start by doing the flyers yourself; once they generate enough revenue, a time study lets you pay two people to do the flyers and reclaim eight hours — which is still double the original output and double the revenue.
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** At the next allocation decision, you choose between more of the same (hire more people), something better (change the flyer, change the offer on the flyer), or something new (e.g., start Facebook ads) — the new channel being the "10% thing."
- **(2026-02-25, paraphrase)** For a company with profit to deploy, part of reinvestment is "insurance for the future."
