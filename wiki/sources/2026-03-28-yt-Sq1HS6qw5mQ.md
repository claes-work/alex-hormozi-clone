# Start Buying Skills

## Summary
In a conversation with an interviewer, Alex Hormozi argues that investing in one's own earning capacity beats investing in financial markets, framing "ignorance debt" as the most expensive cost a person carries. He contends that most people who want to build wealth have an income problem, not an investing problem, and should spend money buying skills, mentorships, and certifications rather than dollar-cost-averaging into index funds. He illustrates the point with a phlebotomy certification example, a critique of overpriced college, and his own past "plan B" fallback baselines as his skillset grew.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames "ignorance debt" as the most expensive cost: not knowing how to make money costs him more than any market return, and paying that ignorance down fast unlocks rapid income growth.
- He argues you get a significantly higher return investing in your own ability to make money than in any market ("S&P 500 versus SME 500"), noting the very wealthy have tremendous earning capacity.
- He calls college overpriced (not inherently bad): a ~$200,000 US student loan could instead buy a rental property, or ~$2,000/month tuition could buy masterminds on ads, selling, or courses that yield a superior return on earning capacity.
- He argues dollar-cost-averaging into the S&P 500 ignores inflation (~4%/year historically); "$4 million" at retirement equals roughly "$1 million today," which he says is not a lot to live on for 30+ years.
- He uses a phlebotomist example: a ~$500 weekend certification could triple a minimum-wage worker's earning capacity from ~$7 to ~$25/hour, versus saving that $500 to earn ~$2.50/year in the S&P 500.
- He claims being poor is a useful head start because it proves you have learned to live on less — keep living on little while raising income.
- He recounts his own rising "baselines" as skills compounded: a stripping-plus-Uber fallback (~$250k/year), then car sales (~$400k/year once he could sell), then a solo turnkey ads/sales system (~$100k/month with zero employees, working 14 hours a day) between owning his gym chain and starting Gym Launch.
- He claims any "sub ten million dollar skill" is buyable for about $30,000 a year of education.
- Unverified caller/second-hand claim (relayed by Alex): a PhD-student friend used government student loans to buy 20 rental homes, graduating with ~$10,000/month of income on doors carrying no debt service.
