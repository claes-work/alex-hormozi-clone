# Maximizing vs. Optimizing

## Summary
In a clipped exchange, Alex Hormozi contrasts being a "maximizer" (doing as much of a high-return input as humanly possible) with being an "optimizer" (obsessing over marginal improvements). He argues that optimization tips into superstition and fragility, that stress over perfection costs more than imperfection, and that he treats optimizer-competitors as easy to break. An unnamed interlocutor briefly interjects (agreeing that "optimizations become superstition") but is not a co-source for persona material.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex describes himself as a lifelong maximizer rather than optimizer: given an input-output equation, his instinct is to scale the input as much as possible (10n, then 1,000n) rather than fine-tune it.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames competitors who depend on optimizations (eight hours of sleep, morning routines, supplements, coffee) as fragile and easy to exploit and beat.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims optimizations become superstition, and that the stress of chasing perfection harms you faster than your imperfections do.
- As of March 2026, Alex recounts that an Oura-style sleep-tracking ring made him chase "sleep PRs," which stressed him into sleeping worse; ditching it, he slept fine the next night. He still says he believes in tracking progress generally.
- As of March 2026, Alex recounts a roughly two-year period doing monthly gym turnarounds across the US, eating gas-station food and gaining weight — framed as acceptable because it was "for then and not forever," and he is in shape now.
- As of March 2026, Alex recycles a weight-loss sales line (aimed at a prospect "Susan"): short-term results that revert are pointless, so commit to lasting change — a pitch he says he used to sell longer memberships but also believes is true.
- As of March 2026, Alex's stated principle: whatever you are unwilling to give up for your dreams is exactly what the person who beats you will give up.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You lose more life trying to optimize everything than just living it. The stress of trying to be perfect is killing you more quickly than your imperfections."

> "I've always been a maximizer. So I've actually not really been an optimizer in my life. It's just like, as soon as I have an input-output equation where it's like n equals x... and if I want 10x I do 10n. I'm like, great. Then can we do a thousand n?"

> "I love to compete against people like that because they're so easy to break."

> "The optimizations become superstition."

> "I kept trying to like set sleep PRs. And you can only get so high anyways. And then I started stressing more about not hitting sleep PRs that I was sleeping worse than when I wasn't tracking it. And so then I was like, well, [screw] this thing. And I slept fine the next night."

> "It was for then and not forever, and I'm in shape now, and so whatever, who cares?"

> "List all the things that you aren't willing to give up for the dreams that you have, and that is what the person who will beat you is willing to give up."
