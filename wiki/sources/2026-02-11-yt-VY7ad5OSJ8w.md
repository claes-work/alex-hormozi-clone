# The Biggest Factor Limiting the Growth of Your Business

## Summary
In this short @MoreMozi clip, Alex Hormozi argues that — absent a zero-marginal-cost viral product — all business growth is ultimately constrained by irrelevance: if every person in the world knew about your business, you would have both the demand and the talent needed to grow. Whether a business is supply- or demand-constrained, the deeper limit is that it remains irrelevant within the larger universe, and the fix is reaching more people, making more for the people you serve, and advertising more. He reinforces the point with two motivational frames about the scale of effort required: a hypothetical he posed to a roofing-company owner (reframing a year-long hire of 40 sales reps as something you could do this month if your kids' lives depended on it), and a story about a "many-deca-billionaire" mutual friend who hired 300 people in seven days via ~2,000 interviews to staff a new $30B venture. Hormozi frames these perspective-shifting ideas as more important than the tactics he says he will cover elsewhere.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) Unless you have a viral product — some zero-cost software where adding another user has no marginal cost — all growth is constrained by irrelevance.
- (2026-02-11) If every single person in the world knew about your business, your business would be bigger than it is right now.
- (2026-02-11) If everyone knew about your business, you would also have the talent you need to grow it — so irrelevance limits supply (talent) as well as demand.
- (2026-02-11) Whether you are supply-constrained or demand-constrained, your growth is fundamentally being limited by your irrelevance within the larger universe.
- (2026-02-11) The remedy Alex reminds himself of: he can probably solve his growth problem by reaching out to more people, making more stuff for the people he wants to serve, and advertising more.
- (2026-02-11) Alex advised a roofing-company owner named Brent, who he says needed roughly 40 more sales reps to hit his goals, a hire that sounded like it would be a year-long project. [self-reported anecdote]
- (2026-02-11) Alex reframed the hire by asking Brent to imagine his kids would be abducted at the end of the month unless he had 40 sales guys — to show that the "year-long" timeline is a choice, and that it could be done far faster if the stakes were high enough. [self-reported anecdote]
- (2026-02-11) Alex and Leila have a mutual friend Alex describes as a "many-deca-billionaire." [self-reported; third party]
- (2026-02-11) That friend raised $30 billion for a new venture. [guest-attributed / third-party claim, self-reported by Alex]
- (2026-02-11) To build the venture's first level of team, the friend flew every candidate into a warehouse and hired 300 people in seven days, running about 2,000 interviews with a group of 15 people. [guest-attributed / third-party claim, self-reported by Alex]
- (2026-02-11) Hearing that story of "violent effort" reminds Alex how much more he himself needs to do to get where he wants to go.
- (2026-02-11) Alex says these perspective-shifting frames are more important than the tactics, which he will cover separately.
