# 5 Ways to Scale ANY Business

## Summary

In this solo talk, Alex Hormozi argues there are only five ways to scale *who* you can sell to for any business, plus a framework for choosing between them. He frames the whole market as a triangle you can move within — up, down, sideways (adjacent), broader, or narrower — and stresses that most businesses should pick only one move at a time, sequenced after first niching down. He closes with a bonus "door number six": simply keep getting better at what you do.

## Key claims

- **Scaling = expanding your addressable market (TAM).** There are only five ways to increase how many people you can sell to; new businesses should niche down first, then expand over a long time horizon (Apple/Netflix aren't niche — it's a question of sequence).
- **1. Go up-market** — sell a higher/more leveraged version of your core market (hair salons → multi-location chains/franchises; Salesforce SMB → Enterprise). Pros: bigger deals, far less churn, no non-payment issues. Cons: harder to sell, 6–18 month sales cycles. "If you increase the quality of your customer you increase the quality of your company." (Examples: Neil Patel, GaryVee selling to Fortune 500.)
- **2. Go down-market** — sell to the smaller/aspiring segment (hair salons → hair stylists; small biz owners → aspiring entrepreneurs). Pros: endless, emotional buyers, an ocean of them. Cons: hard to build recurring revenue, payment issues, unrealistic expectations, ~30% go out of business yearly. Best fit if you're better at marketing/sales than product/delivery — many iterations to improve on.
- **3. Go adjacent** — markets that sell the same promise but deliver differently (hair salons → nail salons; chiropractors → physical therapists). Extracts maximal value with little product change ("some lipstick and a different wrapper"); slower, vertical-by-vertical; requires bringing in someone with trench knowledge of the new vertical.
- **4. Go broader** — generalize the core promise across all verticals that solve the same problem (hair salons → beauty in general; gyms → fitness centers in general). Pros: 10x your TAM overnight, scale faster. Cons: harder to deliver the same value (templated, "fill in the blank"), and you compete against every entrenched vertical specialist. Only earn the right to go broad after you have champions/case studies in multiple verticals. "If you want to go fast go broad, but if you want to be good you're staying narrow for a little and scaling out." (Facebook started in colleges.)
- **5. Go narrower** — become more *specific* by adding qualifications to the same avatar (small business owner → small business with $30k/mo revenue and ≥2 employees). Based on a fractal/80-20 customer analysis (learned from the former head of packaging & pricing at Vista private equity): find the 20% of customers who bring the most money, find their common traits, and reverse-engineer the buying process so every prospect becomes that higher-quality buyer. Can 3–4x profit without much new infrastructure. From a chapter cut from *$100M Offers* ("Your First Avatar," free on his site).
- **Bonus (door #6):** just keep getting better at what you do and you'll ultimately make more money. Big businesses eventually do all five; most viewers should do only one.
