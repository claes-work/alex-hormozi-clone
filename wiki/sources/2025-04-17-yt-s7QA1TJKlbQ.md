# 4 Ways To Get Ahead of 99% of People

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out four "vectors" a business can compete and win on: **speed, risk, price (cheap), and ease**. His core thesis is that winning on any single vector is enough to beat 99% of competitors, and winning on multiple lets you dominate. He walks through sub-tactics for each vector and closes by revealing that the four vectors map directly onto the four elements of his [[topics/business/value-equation|value equation]] (dream outcome, perceived likelihood of achievement, time delay, effort & sacrifice), which is why the framework "feels true."

## Key claims

The four vectors (you only need to win one; more than one and you dominate):

1. **Speed** — do it faster than everyone else. Alex ranks this the most important vector because "humans learn behaviors with decreased latency" — compressed latency (like a social-media thumbs up) trains behavior more powerfully than delayed rewards (like a paycheck). Speed rarely costs more; it usually comes from "idea alpha" (thinking through the process better than competitors). Three ways to build speed:
   - **Templates** — turn everything into repeatable templates (ads, emails, landing pages, presentations, steps) so people don't have to decide; decision-making is usually the slowest part of an org.
   - **Pre-made** — prepare in advance of predictable demand (McDonald's pre-made burgers on the line; a Persian restaurant grilling chicken right before the lunch rush).
   - **Availability** — more/extended hours and staffing so customers get served or booked sooner. Alex calls this an underappreciated "hidden diamond" that can drive 20-40% more throughput; acquisition.com sells 12 hrs/day, 7 days/week and is investing to reach 20 hrs/day for its international market.

2. **Risk** — make your thing not risky, i.e. reliable and consistent. Recurring services especially need consistency month after month. Components: **consistency/reliability** (the lawn-care or cleaner example — people stay with "Rose" who never missed a day even at 20% more), and **reputation/brand** (word of mouth; negative word of mouth is "10 times as viral as positive"). Short-term tactic when you lack reputation: **guarantees**, of which the book covers four types — unconditional, conditional, implied (performance-based / skin in the game), and anti-guarantee (once you have reputation, like McDonald's not guaranteeing the burger). Guarantees only work if you have stakes to put on the table. Tactical consistency = studying and controlling as many variables as possible ("if many variables exist, many variables must be studied" — attributed to B.F. Skinner).

3. **Price (cheap)** — decide on day one that being the cheapest is your moat, and organize every part of the business (click to close to delivered) to pass cost savings to the customer. This is NOT the same as just lowering price. Three ways to build for cheapness: **AI** (gives your best employees 10x leverage), **automation** (still underused, separate from AI), and **offshoring/near-shoring**. Pro tip: you don't need to tell customers you use AI/offshoring/VAs — just be a normal design firm with an automated backend and keep the margin. Cheap businesses have easy sales/marketing; the hard part is staying profitable.

4. **Ease** — make it more convenient by *removing everything that's hard*, not by adding features. "Easy is not the outcome; it's removing all hard and then easy happens as a consequence." Like an iPhone / good design, ease should vanish. Customers will tell you what's hard; cross the hard things off one at a time. Applies to sales (don't make customers repeat info across calls — take notes and reference them) and delivery/onboarding (a massage business that logs each client's pain points so any therapist can reference them, making the experience superior AND stickier — clients don't leave with a departing masseuse).

Additional points:
- **To have all four vectors it almost always has to be tech**; a labor/service business can typically win on about three, but must nail at least one.
- **Pick ONE core vector for marketing** — customers can't absorb multiple benefit claims in marketing ("we're the fastest, least risky, and easiest" is too much). Choose the vector the customer values most. Start with the customer and reverse backwards; look at the competitive landscape.
- **"Fast beats free"** — Spotify beat free file-sharing (Napster, LimeWire, Kazaa) on speed and risk (no viruses, instant play). Alex would pay $20 to skip a huge Chipotle line even if the burrito were free.
- Reveal: the four vectors ARE the value equation — risk = perceived likelihood of achievement, ease = effort & sacrifice, speed = time delay, cheap = dream outcome.
- Positioning of his own businesses: acquisition.com wins primarily on one vector (not cheap); a teeth-whitening chain in the portfolio competes on speed, ease, and cheapness.
