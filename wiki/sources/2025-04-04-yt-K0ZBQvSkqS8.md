# How to Unlock Your True Potential - Alex Hormozi

## Summary

In this coaching-call Q&A, Alex argues that the best opportunity is always the work already in front of you — get so good that customers refer friends, and new opportunities become unnecessary. He reduces business to three sequential jobs (get attention, convert, retain) and four ways to make people aware you exist (partner, content, outreach, ads), championing his "rule of 100" and the principle that volume negates luck. The back half is a mental-health riff: he reframes resilience as speed of return to normal function, defines trauma as a permanent behavior change from an aversive experience, and prescribes usefulness and productive work over rumination.

## Key claims

- The best opportunity is not a new venture but confronting the work already on your desk — solving the problems you don't yet know how to solve (he attributes a version of this to Charlie Munger).
- If every customer tells their friends, you have no need for new opportunities; being that good generates opportunities by definition.
- Business is three sequential steps: get customers/traffic, convert them, then retain them. You cannot convert people before they know you exist, so awareness comes first.
- There are exactly four ways to make people aware you exist: partner with people, make content, do outreach, or run ads. If most of your day isn't spent on these, you're not doing the work that matters.
- The "rule of 100": Alex says he has never once seen someone complete day 100 of the rule of 100 and not materially change their life forever.
- Volume negates luck: reach out to 10,000 people and you will get a customer; make 100 pieces of content and your top piece will far outperform your median and generate leads.
- A high-leverage move for many people is finding one partner who already has an audience — you bring skill and time, they bring an asset/audience/traffic but want money and lack time, so you combine forces.
- Alex says he never works on mental well-being directly; working is itself a great way to work on mental well-being.
- A primary symptom of depression is rumination — a continuous loop of thinking and talking about your problems — and many people perpetuate their problems by wanting to keep discussing them.
- His definition of resilience: the time elapsed between disruption and return to regular function; the most resilient person doesn't get upset to begin with, so demonstrating resilience means acting as if the event didn't happen.
- His definition of trauma: a permanent change in behavior from an aversive experience — i.e., learning; whether it's good or bad depends on whether you learned the right behavior.
- Utility creates purpose: he has never found a person with incredible utility who didn't find purpose. Get in shape, develop skills, contribute value, and it becomes much harder to be depressed. Fill your day with productive things and you have less time to ruminate — "if you want to feel better, do more."
