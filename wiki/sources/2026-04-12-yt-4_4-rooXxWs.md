# How I Think About Work-Life Balance

## Summary

Alex rejects a universal work-life-balance prescription. He says workload can vary by
season, but exceptional outcomes generally require behavior that differs from the
average. He frames criticism of extreme work as an easier response than admitting that
another person is making trades one is unwilling to make.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says work-life balance depends on seasons and individual goals;
  he imposes no universal "should."
- Someone seeking an above-average outcome will likely need to work more than average.
- He argues that exceptional outcomes require becoming an exception in behavior and time
  allocation, not merely expecting an unusual result.
- Alex self-reports being criticized for working hard both before and after becoming
  wealthy.
- He says observers can either acknowledge another person's greater motivation and
  tradeoffs or criticize them; criticism is easier.
- The source cites approximate top-income thresholds as illustrations, not
  independently verified statistics.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You cannot expect an exceptional outcome and then not expect to be an exception."

> "I also have no shoulds. You can do whatever you want."

> "I think it's a much easier path to criticize."

## Notes

Reinforces the exceptional-outcome/exceptional-behavior frame and non-prescriptive balance stance.
