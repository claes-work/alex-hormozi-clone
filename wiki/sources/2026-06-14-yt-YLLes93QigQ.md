# "I'm Stuck Doing $10/Hour Tasks in My $3M Business"

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a direct-response e-commerce co-founder doing about a $3M/year run rate across several SKUs. The caller asks about scattered founder chores, but Alex says that is more of a complaint than the core constraint if the business can likely double by finishing current hires and spending more. He suggests contractors/VAs for sporadic specialized work, then pivots to the bigger warning: direct-response e-commerce portfolios often cap out as CAC rises, margins compress, dupes appear, and cash flow deteriorates. Alex recommends building one believable, defensible product/brand rather than cycling through SKUs.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - direct-response e-commerce co-founder. His SKU count, paid-media background, team, delegation pain, and portfolio-exit goal are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Some founder complaints are not true constraints if the company can still grow by executing the current plan.
- Sporadic specialized tasks can be handled with contractors, VAs, or a bench of project workers rather than full-time hires.
- The founder should list recurring chores and ship as many as possible to contractors.
- Direct-response e-commerce media arbitrage can work well up to a certain scale, but tends to get harder around $10M-$12M.
- As spend scales, CAC rises, gross margins shrink, supply-chain problems increase, and dupes/knockoffs appear.
- A portfolio of unrelated direct-response products is much harder to sell than founders expect.
- The stronger path is one product the team believes in, with brand and influencer/affiliate advocates who actually believe in it.
- Product completes the brand loop: the promise and associations must be reinforced by the customer experience.
- Private equity buys brands, not isolated products.
- Legal defensibility is weaker than brand for a small company unless the company can afford enforcement.
