# How to Get Good at Sales

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host prompts him and briefly asks for a role-play; Alex delivers all the substantive teaching). Alex lays out his mental model of what makes someone good at sales. He opens by noting that most elite sales trainers (Belfort, Bradley, Grant Cardone) share the same origin story — they were naturally the best rep on the team, then reverse-engineered what they were doing — so some people do have a higher innate proclivity for selling ("gift of gab and empathy"). Because of this, he runs a **very short "allowing for people to fail at sales" cycle**: he's never had a killer salesperson who didn't do pretty well in the first week, so he cuts fast, which leaves a team of killers (he cites Grant Cardone's line that his sales floor "is a dangerous place to work"). His central reframe: **you're not selling, you're helping someone make a decision that helps them.** The number-one predictor of good sales is **conviction**, and the two ingredients you need are **trust and conviction** — trust is the mechanism that transfers conviction from the person who believes to the person who doesn't yet. Conviction isn't binary but a matter of degree. The strongest way to "juice" a sales team isn't drills (blocking and tackling) but **hearing fresh testimonials** from last week's clients — he saw near-100% close rates when he stacked sales appointments during gym "weigh-out days" while transformed clients were crying with excitement. You can either **trick yourself into the right tone or train it**, and tricking yourself via genuine belief is easier because if you truly believe in the product you talk about it differently. He then gives his conversation structure (a nod to the [[../topics/business/sales-closer-framework|CLOSER]]-style flow): why are they here, what's the problem, what have they tried, why it failed, why your product differs, ask permission to explain, explain via **experiences and analogies not features**, then close (referencing a "no-based close"). On objections, people fear confrontation, so he sells with **"childlike curiosity"** — meeting resistance ("my husband won't approve") with genuine interested questions rather than argument, because "in arguments no one wins." He compares it to fighters learning to slow their breathing when adrenaline/fight-or-flight kicks in. Closes with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, most top sales trainers (Belfort, Bradley, Grant Cardone) share the same story: they were naturally the best rep on the team, then reverse-engineered their own method — so innate proclivity (upbringing, "gift of gab and empathy") is real.
- As of March 2026, Alex runs a much shorter "allowing for people to fail at sales" cycle than most, because he's never had a killer salesperson who didn't do well in the first week; cutting fast leaves a team of "killers."
- As of March 2026, the first reframe of selling is: you're not selling, you're helping someone make a decision that will help themselves.
- As of March 2026, the number-one predictor of good sales is conviction; the two things you fundamentally need are trust and conviction, and trust is what transfers conviction from a believer to a not-yet-believer.
- As of March 2026, conviction is not binary but a matter of degree ("to what extent do I believe it"); most salespeople lack 100% conviction.
- As of March 2026, the biggest lever for a sales team isn't drills but hearing fresh testimonials of clients sold last week and how their lives changed; stacking appointments during emotional gym "weigh-out days" produced near-100% close rates.
- As of March 2026, you can either trick yourself into the right tone or train it, and tricking yourself via genuine belief is easier — if you truly believe in the product you talk about it differently.
- As of March 2026, his sales conversation framework: establish why they're there, the problem, what they've tried and why it failed, why your product differs, ask permission to explain it, explain via the experiences and analogies (not features), then close (e.g. a "no-based close").
- As of March 2026, you can sell without confrontation using "childlike curiosity" — answering objections with genuine interested questions instead of arguing, because "in arguments no one wins."
- As of March 2026, sales is like fighting: adrenaline triggers fight-or-flight and fast breathing, so experienced reps learn to slow down and stay curious so the prospect feels helped, not combated.
- As of March 2026, you should only sell someone if it actually makes sense for them.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You're not selling, you're helping someone make a decision that's going to help themselves."

> "The number one predictor of good sales is conviction. And so, fundamentally, you have one person who should believe in something, another person who does not believe it yet, and trust is the thing that transfers that conviction."

> "The idea of conviction as a binary is false. So, it's not like I believe it or I don't believe it, it's to what extent do I believe it."

> "You can either trick yourself into having the right tone, or you can train yourself. And I think that it's much easier to trick yourself into it by just simply believing because if you truly believe in the product, you will talk about it differently."

> "We've had this conversation a hundred times. They have only had it once. We should be the one knowing how this conversation is supposed to go."

> "Explaining the product not in any way based on features, but only based on the experiences that they will have as a result of it and using analogies to explain those experiences."

> "I believe that you can sell without ever having confrontation. And you can do that with what I like to call childlike curiosity."

> "In arguments no one wins."

> "You should be selling them only if it makes sense."
