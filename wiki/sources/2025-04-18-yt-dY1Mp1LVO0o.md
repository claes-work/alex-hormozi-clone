# Starting From Zero: What You Should do to Make Your First Dollar

## Summary

A Skool community Q&A in which Alex Hormozi answers members' questions about getting a content-and-community business off the ground from scratch. His central themes: extend your time horizon (12 months near-guarantees success where most quit in 12 days), give away your best value for free to build goodwill and monetize the next problem, and "do epic shit and talk about it" — real reps generate the nuance and credible stories that make content believable. He also covers outbound vs. paid ads vs. content by time horizon, chunking a single-problem business into many sellable steps, and how authority works in vague categories like self-improvement.

## Key claims

- **Choice of channel depends on time horizon.** If you must make money in the next 30 days, do outreach/outbound or paid ads; if you lack paid-ads skill, outbound is the fastest, cheapest way to get exposure. This is why Hormozi teaches the "rule of 300" — post, then do heavy outbound off any engagement and off other people's content.
- **Extend your time horizon and success becomes near-guaranteed.** Most people quit within 12 days (cf. New Year's resolutions abandoned by the second week of January); if you can wait 12 months of consistent action, you can almost guarantee success. Impatience is why most people fail.
- When Hormozi started YouTube he told his vendor he'd "find out in 10 years" if he'd keep doing it — and grew bigger than all that vendor's paying clients combined.
- **Build a big brand around an aspirational outcome.** Do epic work, then tell the story — of either the epic outcome or the epic failure; both are equally valuable. Epic amounts of work create epic stories.
- **Reps create nuance, and nuance differentiates you.** Doing something repeatedly develops distinctions others lack (his analogy: a man who has only drunk Budweiser for 40 years can tell how fresh a bottle is and where it was bottled).
- **Give your best stuff away for free; monetize the next problem.** Solve one problem free so people pay you to solve the next. Solve two, give one away and upsell the second; solve ten, give nine away and upsell the most valuable. More problems solved = more you can give = more goodwill = more likely they buy.
- **Reject tit-for-tat.** You can never be repaid proportionally to the value you create for a large audience; help a lot of people, some will pay, some won't, and that's fine.
- **A single-problem business can be chunked.** Break the problem into smaller sub-problems that occur immediately before, during, and after (his example: selling a house entails fix-its, light aesthetics, cleaning, staging, landscaping, pricing, the sales process). Give away some pieces free; they'll pay you to finish.
- **Efficiency and "effortless" content come from volume, not tricks.** Don't solve for skill; solve for volume, and skill (leverage) follows from repetition. Create on the platform you're already native on. Hitting "post" permanently shifts you from consuming as a consumer to consuming as a creator.
- **Content = solving your customer's problems publicly.** If they believe you for free, they'll believe you for money. Look at your calendar, see what problems you solved, and solve them publicly in a consolidated format.
- **"Do epic shit and talk about it" beats how-to content.** "I did 100 TikToks over 30 days, here's what happened" outperforms "here's how to make good TikToks" ~20-to-1 because people believe you did the reps.
- On promoting within Skool communities: don't make content to attract owners — become a valuable member by talking about problems you solved, and remove CTAs. He cites member "Evelyn" as the best at this: she does something costly in time/money/effort, then posts it so others save days by reading for three minutes.
- **Authority in vague categories (self-improvement, philosophy) comes from specific actions, not the category.** Self-improvement is the aftermath of teaching a specific action (how to get up on time, resolve conflict, attract a mate). The personal-development space is "seeker heavy," heavy on rhetoric and light on action — "ripe for disruption," "tired," full of "old dogs."
- On evidence-based fitness content: people "want to feel scientistic" but don't actually care about science; what wins is the entertainment and credibility (proof, e.g. a PhD) you layer on top. He names Mike Israetel and "More Plates More Dates" (Derek) as the best executors, plus Alan Aragon, Greg Nuckols, and Brett Contreras.
