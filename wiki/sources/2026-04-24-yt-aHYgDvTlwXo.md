# I Didn't Know If I Would Succeed. I Knew I Wasn't Going to Stop.

## Summary

Alex says fear and anger were major early motivators. He did not know whether he would
succeed, though he thought success was probable; what he knew was that he would not stop.
He builds confidence from controllable input-output equations: do enough volume, shadow
the best people, collect enough "nos," and it becomes unreasonable that the skill will not
develop. He gives examples from sales, GMAT prep, and content volume.

## Key claims

- Alex says fear and anger were his major motivators early on.
- He says he did not know whether he would succeed, but he did know he would not stop.
- He likes making "unreasonable that it doesn't work out" statements from controllable
  inputs.
- For sales, he frames the input as volume, shadowing the best salesperson, and collecting
  5,000 rejections.
- He says enough input makes it unreasonable not to become at least mediocre and probable
  to become above average.
- He compares skill growth to replaying video-game levels to accumulate experience points.
- He self-reports studying for the GMAT by doing four hours of problems daily for three
  months after seeing a graph connecting practice problems to score.
- He applies the same input-output logic to content volume: post more often, on more
  platforms, to get more eyeballs.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I didn't know whether I would succeed... but I did know I wasn't going to stop."

> "What are the controllables? Me."

> "If I collect 5,000 nos I'll be a very good salesman."

> "I try and make statements that I believe are unreasonable that if I do it enough it will be true."
