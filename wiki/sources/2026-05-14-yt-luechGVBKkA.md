# Buying the Dip for My Own Negative Consequences

## Summary

Alex says prolonged suffering beyond normal variance has historically preceded large
improvements for him. He frames this as buying the dip on his own negative consequences:
if the situation is painful, he asks how to make it worth it. He also operationalizes
gratitude by imagining something terrible or imagining something loved disappearing,
then remembering that it has not happened or has returned.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex says his large post-dip improvements make him more willing to buy the dip on
  painful consequences.
- Painful periods can be shortened by asking what must be learned or improved to make
  the pain worth it.
- Alex says gratitude has been difficult for him, so he tries to operationalize it.
- One gratitude method is imagining something terrible and remembering it has not
  happened.
- Another is imagining something loved disappearing and then remembering it is still
  present.
- He says gratitude is largely a manufactured contrast or "playing pretend."

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Buy the dip for our own negative consequences."

> "I have to make this worth it."

> "All gratitude is is playing pretend."
