# How To Level Up In Business - Understanding Tradeoffs

## Summary

Hormozi recounts a walk-and-talk with his 18-year-old neighbor, weighing college against
going all-in on entrepreneurship, to illustrate a general decision-making process:
separate the logical question (which path gets you closer to your goal) from the
emotional driver that's actually blocking you. He surfaces the kid's real fear — using
college as a socially acceptable shield against being seen as a failure — and uses it to
argue that leveling up requires naming your fear, making real trade-offs, and accepting
unbalanced, unreasonable sacrifice for a season.

## Key claims

- (2020) The same decision-making process applies whether the choice is a multi-million-
  dollar business call or a personal one: confirm what you want, ask which path gets you
  closer, then identify the emotional driver preventing you from choosing it.
- (2020) People present themselves "socially acceptable solutions" (like college) as a
  foil to avoid confronting the possibility of not being good enough yet; naming that
  fear removes its power.
- (2020) Four years and $200,000 invested directly into starting a business gets an
  aspiring entrepreneur closer to their goal than the same time and money spent in
  college.
- (2020) His founding gym mindset: "I will not outwork everyone, but instead I will
  outsacrifice anyone" — willingness to put anything on the altar of success while
  competitors socialize, sleep in, or watch football.
- (2020) Failing does not make you a failure; failure and recovery are part of
  entrepreneurship, and the story you tell yourself about your choices creates your
  identity.
- (2020) He rejects "balance" as advice for achieving greatness: extraordinary outcomes
  require extreme, unreasonable sacrifice — but for a season, not necessarily your whole
  life.
- (2020) Nearly every hyper-successful person goes through a three-to-five-year "Rocky"
  period of getting hit in the face daily — cites Russell Brunson (five years pitching on
  stages), Billy Gene (2-3 years cold calling), Dan Kennedy (three years door-to-door),
  and himself (4,000 one-on-one fitness consults over four years, 15-20 appointments a
  day).
- (2020) Prefer a "maybe" over a "never": take the reasonable bet that full commitment
  puts you far further ahead than the person splitting focus.
