# Are You Wasting Your Life by Working So Much?

## Summary
Interview clip. An off-camera interviewer presses Alex on his highly repetitive daily routine: isn't he wasting his "golden years" doing the same thing over and over rather than staying open to new experiences, travel, or hobbies (like ping pong) that might turn out "10 times better" than what he has now — why not allocate a weekly percentage of time to trying new things? Alex rejects the premise on two grounds. First, novelty as a path to happiness: he wouldn't know if some alternative were actually better, and even if it were, he'd **hedonically adapt** to it and drift right back to his baseline — so "finding out more things" is not the path (he offers, as illustration, people who never leave their tribe and are happier than "we" are). Second, and central, he reframes the "you work too much" critique as **projection**: for him, work IS doing exactly what he wants, the highest possible percentage of his day, with the people he wants to do it with. The critics — including family who still say he works too much — carry a negative idea of what "work" means; if he said he played video games all day they'd say he wasn't working enough, but if playing video games were his job they'd say he worked too much. The complaint reduces to "I'd prefer you live your life the way I want you to live it," to which his answer is simply "no." He lives his life the way he wants, and "that is enough." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex is unbothered by the idea that some untried alternative might be better: he wouldn't know it was better, and he'd **hedonically adapt** to it and return to his baseline anyway, so seeking out more novel experiences is not "the path" to well-being.
- As of March 2026, he illustrates this with people who "never leave the tribe" yet are happier than people in wealthy modern society — implying happiness is not a function of range of experience.
- As of March 2026, he defines his work as doing exactly what he wants for the highest percentage of his day that he can, with the people he wants to do it with — so "working a lot" is, for him, the same as living how he wants.
- As of March 2026, he frames the "you work too much" criticism (including from family) as the critic's **projection** of their own negative associations with the word "work," not an objective judgment about his life.
- As of March 2026, he argues the criticism is arbitrary: if his all-day activity were labeled "video games" they'd say he isn't working enough; if video games were his job they'd say he works too much — the label, not the life, drives the complaint.
- As of March 2026, he reads such criticism as the demand "I would prefer you live your life the way I want you to live your life," and his stance is to refuse it and live as he chooses — "that is enough."
