# Going All In on One Thing Always Makes the Most Money

## Summary

Alex distinguishes working in a business, on a business, and above businesses, then
argues that going all in on one thing usually creates the most wealth. He contrasts
serial entrepreneurship with parallel entrepreneurship and warns that buying businesses
without operational skill can create debt and liability rather than freedom.

## Key claims

- Alex defines "in" the business as direct production such as sales calls, "on" the
  business as leadership and management, and "above" business as portfolio-level work.
- He says he currently sits above businesses rather than having a direct report inside
  each portfolio company.
- He argues that the richest operators tend to get there by going all in on one thing,
  even when that one thing is private equity or a portfolio strategy.
- He self-reports that when he had nine companies, he felt near failure daily because he
  lacked leadership cadence and was the rainmaker for all of them.
- He warns that no-money-down business buying is not free money: if the buyer cannot
  operate the business, seller financing becomes a liability.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Going all in on one thing always makes the most money."

> "If you cannot operate it, you're screwed."

> "I had nine companies."
