# I Ranked the Best Superbowl Ads 2026 [TIER LIST]

## Summary

Solo tier-list video in which Alex Hormozi grades twelve 2025/2026 Super Bowl ads
against a repeatable six-factor advertising rubric, using each ad to illustrate a
transferable principle (brand association, demonstration, avatar targeting, direct
response, crowdsourced creative). He repeatedly contrasts brand-building ads (Jeep,
Nike, Rocket Mortgage) with direct-response demonstration ads (Google Gemini, Ramp,
Instacart) and stresses that ad quality is ultimately judged by sales data, not
opinion. The video doubles as a soft plug for his Leads book, his brand keynote, and
Skool communities.

## Key claims

Alex's ad-evaluation rubric — the six factors he ranks every ad on:

1. **Did it grab attention?**
2. **Did it make you care?**
3. **Did it sell the dream** (show the desired/dream outcome)?
4. **Did it create urgency?**
5. **Did it show social proof / proof?**
6. **Did it tell you what to do next** (a clear CTA)?

What makes a GOOD ad, in his framing:

- **Demonstration beats description.** The strongest pattern he praises: open with a
  pain-based hook of what life is like *without* the product, then immediately solve it
  by demonstrating the product in use. He notes most business owners fail to demonstrate.
- **Weave product usage throughout**, not just at the end — this is what he says
  separates the top-tier ad (Google Gemini) from an otherwise equal emotional ad
  (Rocket Mortgage), where you couldn't tell whose ad it was.
- **A brand ad should build clear associations.** He reduces brand ads to an equation:
  "Brand = Freedom; Freedom = things our demo likes; therefore you like the brand."
  Curate association after association that the target avatar already values.
- **Match the hook to the audience context** (e.g., a football-player hook for a
  football audience); question-based hooks around pain "fundamentals" work well.
- **Cut to many human experiences** so multiple audience segments each see themselves
  ("you've been the kid, the adult, the one for the job").
- **The end screen / CTA must make the advertiser and offer unmistakable.** A one-second
  logo flash is a knock — many viewers won't know who ran the ad or what it sells.
- **Add text/subtitles**, don't rely on audio alone — many people watch TV from afar
  with no sound and no subtitles (he admits he personally over-indexes on text).
- **Crowdsourcing creative is powerful and cheap.** Letting your audience make the ad
  (e.g., a fan-contest ad) gives you a pre-validated winner, costs far less than an
  agency, and customers who use the product know it more intimately than agency staff.

What makes a BAD ad:

- **Making the ad about you instead of the customer.** "Rule number one is that no one
  cares about you, they just care about themselves." An ad that only talks about the
  company (e.g., spelling out the brand name) misses.
- **Fast cuts that aren't explanatory** — the viewer doesn't know where the ad is going
  and it feels "mid."
- **Requiring a rewatch to be understood** is fatal: most people will never rewatch and
  the moment is gone.
- **Pandering / incongruent messaging** (e.g., attacking an industry for being
  profit-driven, then selling your own version).
- **No dream outcome shown**, and failing to show the product working for the target
  avatar.

Judgment caveats he insists on:

- **You may not be the demo.** Judge an ad by whether *its* avatar would respond, not
  whether *you* personally like it.
- **Ads have different objectives** — pure branding / positive-sentiment, reinforcing an
  existing position, or direct response — so judge against the intended objective.
- **You can't beat data.** Opinions don't decide the best ad; the ad that drove the most
  sales wins. He ties this to running headline split-tests for his own book rather than
  polling five people.
- **Persuasion ≠ awareness.** Advertising lets people know about your stuff; persuasion
  changes behavior. He questions whether a "fun" ad that leaves you feeling good actually
  changes behavior (e.g., getting screened).
- **A winning brand needs losers.** "If everybody's a winner, no one is" — a brand built
  on winning can't say everyone wins. He names reach, influence, and directionality as
  the three elements of brand.
