# Alex Hormozi Explains the Infinite Game of Business

## Summary

In this clip Alex Hormozi reframes success through the lens of finite vs. infinite
games: worthwhile endeavors (business, marriage, health) can't be won, only played, so
the goal is simply to stay in the game. He argues that redefining success as "being in
the arena" makes failure impossible and lets you enjoy the process rather than chase a
finite outcome you'll only move past. He then shares three gratitude/presence frames —
"it's always the good old days," appreciating "lasts," and the "grandfather frame"
(experiencing the present as your 85-year-old self reliving it).

## Key claims

- A finite game has known players, agreed-upon rules, and an agreed-upon way of winning;
  an infinite game has known and unknown players, no rules, and the only point is to keep
  the game going. (Alex credits Simon Sinek with popularizing this.)
- People fail because they apply a finite construct to an infinite game (his example: the
  US "lost" in Vietnam by seeking a finite outcome to an infinite game).
- Most games worth playing — marriage, health, business — can't be won, only played; the
  point is to stay in the game.
- Redefining success as "being in the arena" means that by definition you are already
  winning simply by continuing to play.
- People achieve a goal and immediately move the goalpost; success is really a game you
  play with yourself.
- If you have a fear of failure, making your games infinite guarantees victory because you
  can't lose.
- Alex spent his first 18 years in business not realizing he could have enjoyed the entire
  process, because he lacked this perspective.
- "It's always the good old days" — you don't realize in the moment that the present will
  later be looked back on fondly (he cites sleeping on the gym floor / having six gyms
  before losing it all as moments that were "forging" him).
- Practicing presence frames (good-old-days, appreciating "lasts," the grandfather frame)
  is like training a muscle and makes even painful losses sweeter.
- Alex self-reports that he struggles with gratitude and has to deliberately work at it
  using these frames.
