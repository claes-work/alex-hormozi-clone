# I Helped Business Owners Overcome Their Fear of Failure

## Summary

A live Q&A/coaching session (not a solo monologue): Hormozi takes questions from ~7 audience members — a self-publishing writer, a founder grieving deaths in his family, a service-business owner, a fitness coach on/offline, an aspiring "big vision" founder, a lonely online coach, and an accountant who went back to a 9-to-5. Across all of them he pushes one core reframe: **judge yourself by inputs you control (the actions), not outcomes you don't**, and treat entrepreneurship's uncertainty and pain as required parts of the story.

## Key claims

- Set goals as **actions you fully control**, not outcomes. If you commit to a promotion volume (e.g. four hours/day) and do it, that's the win — "you can judge how successful you were by how much you adhered to the actions that you committed to." Waiting for a guarantee is "asking the universe for a guarantee that it will never give you"; talk to the marketplace instead.
- On hard times/grief: "don't judge yourself on how you feel, judge yourself by what you do despite how you feel." The struggles become the story — "no one likes epic heroes without epic monsters" (cites *Shoe Dog* as 30 years of getting kicked, then Nike).
- **Strategy is prioritization** — "allocating limited resources against unlimited options." Pursuing multiple businesses at once "will virtually guarantee that you don't succeed at any of them"; pick one problem, solve it completely (Amazon started with books, Facebook with colleges), then go adjacent. Stack knowledge in one vertical — the richest people he knows have been in the same space 40 years.
- Give-in-public, sell-in-private: keep publishing free value, but put light-touch CTAs in comments/DMs/stories so cash flow comes in without burning goodwill.
- The lonely-growth phase is universal — "you're too big for the old friend group but not quite big enough for the group you want to get into." Fix it by moving toward and joining communities of people doing the same thing; make friends by giving first ("givers recognize givers").
