# From $1.5M to $9.4M in One Year With One Piece of Advice

## Summary

A live coaching testimonial + Q&A in which a UK dental-services business owner tells Alex Hormozi that a single piece of advice given in Vegas the prior year — "do more of the same" (more ads, more time on ads, more creative) — took his business from ~$1.5M to $9.4M top line (~$3M profit) in one year out of a single four-wall location. Alex congratulates him on the ~6X, then coaches him on the next level: shift from acquisition to retention (churn reduction) as the compounding engine, then open a second location across town using the same model. This is a coaching case; only Alex's advice and frameworks are persona-relevant.

## Key claims

- **The "one piece of advice" that drove the 6X was volume: do more of the same.** The prior year's prescription was to spend more on ads, spend more time each day doing ads, and get more creative. "You need to do more ads, spend more time in the day doing ads, get more creative." The owner did exactly that and still needs to keep that volume going.
- **Truly large businesses get big because of a compounding vehicle.** Alex claims that "really tremendously large businesses get really really big... 100% of the time" because they have one thing: a compounding vehicle — something that compounds onto itself (virality, brand, media — because people tell other people).
- **A high-ticket B2B service won't compound through virality; retention is its compounding vehicle.** Given the nature of a high-ticket B2B service, it won't go viral. So the mission shifts to "how do we make sure that dentists never leave?" — reduce churn so the base compounds.
- **Retention compounds revenue with disproportionate margin growth.** If you keep every year's customers and add another ~$10M/yr, you stack: $10M → $20M → $30M → $40M, and margin grows disproportionately because OpEx doesn't scale at the same rate. "That's the game we need to get to now."
- **The answer is no longer "spend more."** "The answer was spend more when you came. That was the answer. Right now, the answer is not spend more." Now: keep the people you're selling, keep delivering, keep them happy, reduce churn.
- **Green-light for a second location — because the $9.4M was out of one four-wall location.** Once Alex learns the whole $9M came from a single location, he says the owner is ready to open a second using the same model. Go across town to minimize ad-audience crossover; the key risk is backfilling the talent needed to staff the new location.
- **Decrease sales complexity so ordinary reps can succeed.** The "midwit meme" of sales: it comes down to just asking more times and not bothering people. Build a very straightforward script anyone can learn — five questions, asked in a set way and order. Removing variables makes reps easier to onboard, train, and grade, and makes a better business model than one requiring exceptional salespeople.
- **Recruit salespeople exactly like you acquire customers.** Same concept: advertise, have an offer, work the leads, interview like a sale. Sales-team headcount is now the constraint on opening the next location.
- **Follow the lead-nurture playbook to control no-show rates.** "Follow the lead nurture playbook that's coming in the mail... literally follow that to a T. Number one before you do anything."
- **On incentivizing prospects (bribing):** demonstrate personalization for the prospect so they want the value — it's less about "bribing" and more about what that person really wants (getting them out of pain, or guaranteeing the solution).
- **Context (owner, not persona):** UK dental business, ~$1.5M last year → $9.4M this year, ~$3M profit, from a single four-wall location (one room still available); got the original advice at a live event in Vegas; sales team feeling pressure from increased ad spend.
