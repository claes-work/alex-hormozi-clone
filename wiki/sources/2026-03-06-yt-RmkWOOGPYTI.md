# Make Sacrifices Wisely

## Summary
Interview clip (Alex + an unnamed host; the host frames questions and contributes his own material, so only Alex-attributed statements are voice data). The host opens by quoting a line he credits to Alex — that we "sacrifice the thing we want for the thing that's supposed to get it" (e.g. sacrificing freedom for money, hoping money buys freedom back; sacrificing happiness for success in the pursuit of happiness) — and asks how to avoid over-complicating life. Alex reframes the whole question through **game theory: finite vs. infinite games** (crediting Simon Sinek). Finite games have known players, agreed rules, and a winning outcome (baseball); infinite games have known and unknown players, no fixed rules, and the point is simply to keep the game going (his Vietnam War example — the side trying to *stay alive and keep fighting* beats the side trying to *win and end it*). His thesis: most games worth playing are infinite — you don't "win" fitness, marriage, or business; you stay in shape, stay married, stay in business. So the point of success is to keep doing the things that make you successful, and if you're doing them you are "by definition winning." His personal win condition: redefine the perfect day and live as many in a row as possible. He adds a contrarian, low-stakes worldview on death (nothing happens; nothing he does matters in 500 million years) that strips pressure off external outcomes. On why success looks so unappealing up close, Alex reports the **three most common traits of hyper-successful people** — a superiority complex (they think they deserve more), massive insecurity (they'll never be enough), and impulse control — mapped to a "big toward, big away, stay on the path" motivation structure (echoing the host's starving-rats-with-a-cat-behind-them study). He quotes Dr. Cashy: "champions are broken" — they lack an off button. Conclusion: if you optimize purely for outcomes, the most broken person wins, so the real question — his single most-asked question to portfolio companies — is **"what problem are we solving?"** Optimizing to be *content* takes a very different (and far cheaper) path than optimizing to be the richest man in the world. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
