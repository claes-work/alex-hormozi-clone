# Building a $46M Company

## Summary
Interview clip (Alex Hormozi with an interviewer, marked `>>` below; only Alex-attributed
statements are used as voice data). Alex narrates the **full origin story of Gym Launch** —
how a six-location brick-and-mortar gym operator became a B2B licensing business doing
~$26M then ~$37M top line. The arc: at 26, with six cash-flowed gyms (having slept on a gym
floor for nine months), a mentor told him to stop owning gyms and **license the model** instead
(B2C → B2B). For two years he and Leila personally ran **32 gym turnarounds** as pay-on-performance
consultants — flying in, fixing pricing, layout, and sales process, and charging **100% of the
upfront cash collected (~$100k in 21 days per gym)**. That "Gym Rescue" model had a fatal flaw:
**he didn't control fulfillment**, so struggling gyms would poach the members he sold and undercut
him (~$150k in refunds across three facilities in months). The pivot came when a gym owner he was
about to cancel begged for help; Alex named "$6,000" hoping for a no and got an instant "Done" —
the moment he discovered he could **license the acquisition system** (ads, funnels, scripts, sales
training) instead of doing it himself. The catalyst insight came earlier, at a Russell Brunson
mastermind, where after seeing Alex's **30:1 LTV:CAC** numbers Brunson told him: *"you're in a level
two opportunity with a level 10 skill set"* — introducing Alex to the concept of an **opportunity
vehicle**. Alex also details the gym offer mechanics (free 6-week challenge, money-back on 20 lb
lost, EFT conversion, supplement upsells), the licensing packaging (front-end "Launch" → continuity
"Legacy," "Gym Lords"), the **Prestige Labs** drop-ship supplement play (retail kits + Amazon-Fire
kiosks, 40% commission, $1.7M first month), and two self-described mistakes in year three: starting
a second active company too early, and **cutting price 25% to reduce churn — which changed nothing
and just lost 25% of revenue.**

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex recounts he had **six working gym locations at age 26**, built off cash
  flow (no investors), and slept on a gym floor for **nine months**; a mentor advised him to stop
  owning gyms and license the model, moving him from B2C to B2B.
- As of March 2026, before licensing he and Leila personally did **32 gym turnarounds in ~2 years**,
  operating as pay-for-performance consultants (fix pricing, layout, sales process), averaging
  **~$100,000 in cash collected per gym in 21 days**, of which he took 100%.
- As of March 2026, the "Gym Rescue" model's fatal flaw was **not controlling fulfillment**:
  struggling gyms poached the members he sold (offering the same for less), producing **~$150,000 in
  refunds across three facilities within months** while he'd already sunk marketing, flights, sales
  staff, hotel, and car costs.
- As of March 2026, the licensing pivot began when a desperate gym owner insisted on help; Alex named
  a deliberately high "$6,000" to get a no and got an instant yes, then repeated it at "$8,000" —
  revealing he could **license the acquisition system** rather than fly out and run it.
- As of March 2026, what he licensed was the **front-end acquisition machine**: the exact ads (with
  him in them), landing pages, targeting, text/reminder sequences, lobby setup, sales scripts, plus
  his **internal 2-hour sales training** — the "how to run ads" piece was the only new part, built
  over a weekend. To stay legal he sold **"system and fee" but not name** (a franchise = system + fee
  + name); otherwise he'd be an illegal franchise.
- As of March 2026, the average licensee collected **~$30,000 in additional cash in the first 30
  days** (vs. Alex's own ~$100k); the system price was **$16,000**, then **3-year licensing
  agreements at $42,000/year** (~$800/week) for the continuity.
- As of March 2026, the catalyst was **Russell Brunson's mastermind** (~$30k, aimed at internet
  marketers; Alex was the only brick-and-mortar owner). After Alex walked through his **30:1
  LTV:CAC in the first 30 days**, Brunson said he "shouldn't be running gyms" and was in a
  **"level two opportunity with a level 10 skill set."**
- As of March 2026, Alex defines an **"opportunity vehicle"** as three multipliers: (1) number of
  potential units sellable, (2) gross margin per unit, (3) supply–demand dynamics of the space
  (his telecom counter-example: great TAM and margin, terrible supply-demand for him to enter).
- As of March 2026, the gym front-end offer was a **free 6-week challenge** with a full money-back
  stick if the client lost 20 lb — driving ~70–80% success rates; monetization stacked $500 upfront
  → ~$200 supplements 24h later → converting the $500 into ongoing EFT billing → upsell to smaller
  group coaching.
- As of March 2026, Gym Launch revenue: **year 1 $6.8M (≈$3M EBITDA)** as a Gym Rescue→licensing
  hybrid; **year 2 $25.9M (≈$17M EBITDA)**; **year 3 $37M (≈$13.4M EBITDA)** as licensing softened
  to ~$20M and supplements added ~$17M.
- As of March 2026, **Prestige Labs** (beta Dec 2018, launch Jan 2019) used a **drop-ship model**:
  gyms bought ~$100 retail kits (130 empty display bottles) plus an Amazon-Fire kiosk preloaded with
  their affiliate link; gyms earned **40% commission** as recurring near-pure-profit; first month did
  **$1.7M**.
- As of March 2026, Alex names two year-three mistakes: **(1)** starting a second active company
  (Prestige Labs) while already running one, given his skills at the time; **(2)** cutting the
  top-line price **25%** (from $800/week to $600/week) hoping lower price would reduce churn to please
  an acquirer — result was **no change in churn and a straight 25% revenue loss.**
- As of March 2026, Alex's pricing philosophy: charge big **up front** because "burn rate was Alex's
  bank account" and because a buyer is **most excited on day one** (before the first workout / the
  work sets in); then **decrease cost over time** because he believes **information decreases in value
  over time**, sustainable because gross margins were ~100%.
- As of March 2026, on learning to sell: **do first, then read** — you can't bucket sales concepts
  (e.g. rapport) into anything actionable until you've confronted the reality; the one idea that made
  it click was **"make people offers so good they'd feel stupid saying no."**
- As of March 2026, an offer-fix example (agency/software space): move clients from a **retainer to a
  pay-per-performance / pay-per-show** model. An internal pricing survey showed customers would pay
  ~$300–400/month flat, but **~4x as much** for the identical result priced as pay-per-show — so he
  priced it that way (also easier to sell and self-financing).
