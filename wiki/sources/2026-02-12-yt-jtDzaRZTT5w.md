# Does Pricing Affect Churn?

## Summary
A caller asks Alex Hormozi whether there is a correlation between the price of a community offer and its churn rate — for example, whether a $47/month tier churns harder than a $3,000/month tier. Hormozi says he does not believe churn is purely tied to price itself. He introduces a price-to-value discrepancy: at very low prices, value can be effectively zero if the customer never uses the product, while at very high prices a well-qualified customer who does use it can get many multiples of that price back. In his own observation across price points, higher prices do tend to correlate with higher churn, but he attributes this to two other variables — mismatched value delivery and poor qualification of who is allowed to buy — rather than to price as a direct cause.

He then reframes the optimization target: churn should not be treated as the thing to minimize in isolation, because LTV is the real objective and churn is only one multiplier on it. Every pricing decision ultimately rolls up into earnings per click (EPC), since anyone running paid or organic traffic is optimizing the whole box a click falls into, not any single metric inside it — a price cut that increases churn could still be correct if it also increases conversion enough. Drawing on his own pricing experiments across service tiers, he says the revenue curve in response to price changes is far less extreme than most people assume, and illustrates this with his own launch pricing (9K vs. 6K) and with a hypothetical of a $500K/month operator moving price from $1,250 to $1,350.

His closing point is that incremental price tuning is not what produces order-of-magnitude growth (e.g., going from $100K/month to $5M/month); offer quality — "how good is the food" — combined with pricing appropriately for the qualification level of the buyer produces outsized results, while marginal price optimization mostly does not. The clip ends with a short promotional pitch for a free "10-stage roadmap from zero to $100 million+" resource, framed as a diagnostic broken down by eight business functions with constraint symptoms and graduation steps per stage, available at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-12. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-12) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The caller asks whether there is a correlation between price and churn — e.g., whether $47/month tiers churn more than $3,000/month tiers.
- (2026-02-12) Churn is not purely tied to price; across the pricing range there is a price-to-value discrepancy — at very low prices, unused value is effectively zero, while at very high prices (e.g., $10,000/month) a well-qualified, engaged prospect can get many multiples of that back.
- (2026-02-12) In general, he has observed that higher price correlates with higher churn, but attributes this not to price itself but to (a) failure to appropriately match value to price and (b) insufficient qualification of who is allowed to buy.
- (2026-02-12) If you lower price and hold everything else constant, churn will almost certainly go down — but that is not the right question, because the real question is whether you make more money overall; a 50% price cut that only reduces churn by 20% would be a bad trade.
- (2026-02-12) Churn should not be treated as the thing to optimize in isolation ("make churn king"); LTV is the real objective, and churn is one multiplier feeding into it.
- (2026-02-12) All pricing/churn/conversion variables roll up into earnings per click (EPC), since everyone driving traffic is ultimately optimizing the box that traffic feeds into; a price cut that raises churn could still be net-positive if it also raises page conversion (e.g., cutting price from $100 to $50 while churn worsens 20% could still win if conversion doubles).
- (2026-02-12) From his own experimentation with pricing across different levels of service, the response curve to price changes is way less extreme than people tend to assume.
- (2026-02-12) On his own launch pricing decision (9K vs. 6K): he does not know whether pricing at 9K would have made more net money, because higher price points bring other costs — shifted buyer expectations, more card declines — so it is not simply true that everyone who would buy at the lower price would also buy at the higher one (e.g., a 50% price increase could come with a ~40% conversion drop).
- (2026-02-12) Illustrative math: an operator doing $500,000/month who raises price from $1,250 to $1,350 (roughly +8%) likely will not capture the full proportional gain — maybe +2% revenue rather than +7-8% — and that kind of marginal price tweak is not what produces order-of-magnitude growth (e.g., going from $100,000/month to $5,000,000/month).
- (2026-02-12) The dominant lever is offer/product quality ("how good is the food") combined with pricing appropriately for the qualification level of your prospect pool — that combination is what drives large gains, not fine-tuning price alone.
