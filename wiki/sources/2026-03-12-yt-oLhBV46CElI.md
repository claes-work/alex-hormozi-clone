# Why Do Businesses Die?

## Summary
Interview clip (Alex speaking with a host who interjects via ">>" turns; the argument is entirely Alex's). Alex opens with a line he attributes to Churchill: businesses "die of indigestion, not starvation" — they overeat rather than starve. The killer isn't lack of opportunity but too much of it, driven by a behavioral trap: entrepreneurs get **reinforced for changing direction** ("nothing works, nothing works… you change direction, something clicks"), so they learn the wrong lesson — that switching is what pays — instead of the right one. He stresses that a story's facts are not the same as the lesson one should take from it (his example: "I hired a sales guy, he did a bad job, all sales guys suck" is the wrong lesson). The central theme is **saying no to increasingly attractive opportunities as you scale**. He illustrates with his "woman in the red dress" analogy from The Matrix: the better you get, the more attractive the distraction — you can say no to a six, but what about a seven, a twelve, a hypothetical thousand? Since there's no cap on the upside, the cost of each shiny thing is "the one thing that matters most." He credits Leila with helping him focus, recalling that when they met he was running a chiropractor agency, a dental agency, five gym locations, and a gym-turnaround business simultaneously — CEO of all of them, all breaking even, "nine spinning plates," classic new-entrepreneur yes-to-everything. He cites Warren Buffett (the most successful people "say no to almost everything"), a Churchill line on doing "the obvious thing for an extraordinary period of time without convincing yourself you're smarter than you are," John Maxwell ("you cannot overestimate the unimportance of practically everything"), and Greg McKeown's *Essentialism* (a top-five book for him, an antidote to the type-A "I can do it all" fallacy). He distinguishes working hard from being spread thin, floats **periodizing work** like a weightlifting coach cycles training blocks, and resolves a tension with the host into a crisp formulation: as you scale you should be **specialized in projects, generalized in skills** — because the higher you sit, the more magnified the downstream damage of getting distracted. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues (attributing the line to Churchill) that businesses "die of indigestion, not starvation" — failure comes from overextension and too many opportunities, not from a lack of them.
- As of March 2026, entrepreneurs get behaviorally reinforced for changing direction (persistence fails, a pivot finally clicks), so they wrongly learn that switching is what pays rather than extracting the correct lesson from the episode.
- As of March 2026, he warns that the facts of what happened are not the same as the lesson you take from it, and that drawing the wrong lesson is pervasive even in the online-business community.
- As of March 2026, the higher you climb, the more attractive the opportunities you must learn to decline; each level requires saying no to bigger and bigger ones ($10k, then $100k, and up).
- As of March 2026, his "woman in the red dress" analogy (from The Matrix): distractions grow more seductive as you improve, and because the upside has no limit the temptation is effectively unbounded.
- As of March 2026, the true cost of pursuing any one of a hundred possible things is "the one thing that matters most" — a vision statement exists partly to make that opportunity cost visible.
- As of March 2026, he recounts that when he met Leila he simultaneously ran a chiropractor agency, a dental agency, five gym locations, and a gym-turnaround business, was the only CEO across all of them, and they merely broke even — "nine spinning plates," which he calls classic new-entrepreneur behavior.
- As of March 2026, paraphrasing Warren Buffett: the difference between very successful and the most successful people is that the most successful say no to almost everything.
- As of March 2026 (crediting a Churchill quote he wasn't fully sure of the attribution for): success comes down to "doing the obvious thing for an extraordinary period of time without convincing yourself you're smarter than you are."
- As of March 2026, he cites John Maxwell ("you cannot overestimate the unimportance of practically everything") and names Greg McKeown's *Essentialism* a top-five book — an antidote to the type-A belief that one can and should do it all.
- As of March 2026, working hard and being spread thin are two different dynamics; he is experimenting with **periodizing work** the way a strength coach cycles submax blocks, comp prep, and mobility.
- As of March 2026, his resolution on scaling: you should become **specialized in projects but generalized in skills** as you get bigger, because the downstream ramifications of a leader getting distracted are magnified the higher up you are.
