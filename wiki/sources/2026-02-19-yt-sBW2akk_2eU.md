# How I Decide What Content to Consume

> Interview clip (two speakers: an unnamed interviewer + Alex Hormozi). Only Alex-attributed
> statements below feed the persona. The clip's title frames it as a media-diet piece, but the
> substance is Alex's **decision filter for information** — how he evaluates inputs by the action
> they enable, and why he stays silent on things he hasn't personally done.

## Summary

Asked whether he takes a "control what I can control" approach to news and politics, Alex lays out
the filter he applies to consuming information: every input gets judged against the question *"what
am I going to do?"* He argues the current media environment makes genuinely informed opinions almost
impossible ("banana foam times 100 across all media"), so being an informed voter would cost him
weeks, not the "10 minutes" people claim. Weighed against the near-zero impact of a single vote,
that time fails his opportunity-cost test — so he has voted only once in his life. He's explicit
this is not anti-voting advocacy but pro-being-informed, and that because he *is* uninformed on
politics he refuses to give political opinions (they'd just be made-up answers, same as inventing
business advice). This generalizes into a broader rule: he only speaks on what he has personally
"walked." He'll teach getting to eight figures/month because he's done it, but won't teach getting
to nine figures/month until he's been there — even though he has a roadmap. He frames avoiding
politics as declining a "game I don't know the rules to" where he has no advantage, and says he has
no interest in that arena "on any time horizon that I associate with the current identity I have"
because he genuinely likes the life and work he's on now. Ends with a promotional ad read for
acquisition.com/roadmap (not persona-relevant).

## Key claims (2026-02-19, paraphrased unless quoted)

- The governing question for any information input is **"what am I going to do?"** — media/education is
  only worth consuming to the extent it changes an action he'll actually take. (Alex)
- The modern media environment makes informed opinion nearly impossible: sources conflict, "no one
  can actually touch the situation anymore," so knowing what an *informed* decision even is has become
  extremely hard for any voter. (Alex)
- Being a genuinely informed voter costs **weeks**, not the "10 minutes" people claim — 10 minutes is
  only enough to click the buttons, not to understand which buttons to press. (Alex)
- He has **voted once in his life**. His reasoning: the cost (in time) of becoming informed is large
  relative to the impact one vote makes on his life, so the opportunity cost fails — he could improve
  his life more with that same time. He frames this as **pro-informed, not anti-voting**. (Alex)
- Because he is uninformed on politics, he **refuses to give political opinions** — they'd be pure
  opinion / made-up answers, the same as inventing business answers he doesn't know. (Alex)
- ★ **He only speaks on what he has personally "walked."** He'll teach reaching **eight figures/month**
  because he's been there and knows what it takes; he **won't teach nine figures/month until he's done
  it**, despite already having a roadmap. ("I won't talk about it until I've walked it.") (Alex)
- He only plays "games I know I can win" — he avoids arenas (like politics) where he'd be "trounced by
  anybody more educated," because entering a game without an advantage and without knowing the rules is
  a losing move. (Alex)
- He rules out entering politics **"on any time horizon that I associate with the current identity
  I have"** — grounded in genuinely liking his current path, work, and impact, not in disdain. (Alex)
- Aside anecdote reinforcing "walk it first": the DM critic telling him he *doesn't* know how to reach
  nine figures/month is, by definition, not someone doing nine figures/month. (Alex)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you're an idiot, it only takes 10 minutes to literally click the buttons. It takes weeks to be
> informed about what buttons you're pressing."

> "This isn't me saying I'm an advocate for not voting. I'm an advocate for being informed. But the
> cost of being informed relative to the amount of change I can make with my vote is relatively small."

> "I try to talk about only the few things that I feel relatively confident that I know about... [it's]
> the same as me making up answers because I don't know."

> "Not that I don't have a map or a road map of how I plan to get there, but I won't talk about it
> until I've walked it."

> "This is a game that I don't know the rules to... I don't want to play, and so I play games that I
> know that I can win."

> "I don't [see politics] on any time horizon that I associate with the current identity that I have."
