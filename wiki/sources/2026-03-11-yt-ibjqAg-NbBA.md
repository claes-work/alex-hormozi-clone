# Who Is Alex Hormozi?

## Summary
Long-form interview clip (MoreMozi) in which Alex tells his full life-and-company story in the first person — the single richest biographical account in the corpus so far. He opens with the mission of Acquisition.com's content: **"to make business accessible for everyone"** — put everything out free (no paywalls; books at 99 cents; free courses on the site), share learnings from the portfolio to stay cutting-edge, and thereby attract companies who already got value before ever speaking to the firm ("pay for ourselves in advance"). Audience is entrepreneurs at every stage; a survey found ~25% already have a business and ~75% want to start one, ranging up to $100M+ companies, so he designs content to go "wide and deep."

He then goes deep on childhood and origin: both parents immigrants (mother born in France, father in Iran; met in medical school in Europe), split young; his mother "had a lot of demons," so he was raised almost entirely by his father, an only child, until his father remarried when Alex was ~15 and he left home soon after. He worked hard at school and took a management-consulting job at a defense contractor (space, cyber, and ISR) primarily to gain his father's approval — his father, a doctor, wanted him to be one too (Alex enrolled premed, switched to a business degree). At 22, looking out from a condo he'd bought with that job, he had a "rock-top moment": he had done everything his father wanted and still "always hoped I wouldn't wake up the next day" — persistent suicidal ideation without a plan. He decided to leave Baltimore, quit the corporate path, and drive across the country to open a gym in California — the hardest decision of his life because it meant "letting my dad's dream die for mine to live" and "being comfortable dying in his eyes," which he says he did; they barely spoke for years.

He then narrates the business arc: multiple gyms (~six locations), sold them; a gym-turnaround business for two years; then **Gym Launch** (licensing), which took off. He recounts the near-collapse in December 2016 — a dishonest business partner (already indicted for fraud) drained his account, a payment processor held his launch revenue for six months, and gym owners reneged on the model, forcing $150,000 in refunds — leaving him with $1,000 in the bank at Leila's parents' house. Leila (then newly with him, formerly a personal trainer he'd pitched to quit and work for him) refused to leave ("I would sleep with you under a bridge if it came to that"), and the pivot to selling the licensing system to gym owners for a flat fee (starting at $6,000, escalating) turned it around — $60,000 in a day, $300,000 that month, then $26M topline / $17M EBITDA in the first full 12 months. He closes the timeline: added a supplement company (Prestige Labs) ~two years later, then a software company; sold all three in 2021 (supplement + licensing to American Pacific Group for $46.2M for two-thirds of the company; software as an all-stock deal to a strategic buyer); and, from dividends plus first investments begun in 2020, launched **Acquisition.com** as a family office taking minority stakes (usually 25–49%) as "growth partners." He reflects on how his interpersonal style softened (from "purely fear driven" to significantly friendlier, credited largely to Leila) and defines his own zone of genius as **defining the vague terms everyone uses ("value," "grow my business") so problems become solvable** — how he stays "relatively competent in a world that feels confusing." Ends with the $100M scaling roadmap promo (acquisition.com/roadmap).
