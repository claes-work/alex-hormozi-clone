# Helping an E-Com Brand Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner runs a skincare e-commerce brand selling to women aged 55–65 via direct-response funnels with a back-end phone sales team; revenue is $4.5M/month and the goal is $25M/month. They already solved an earlier bottleneck (couldn't call leads fast enough after cranking ad spend → margins dropped) by building an in-house recruitment team on the sales side, and now want to apply that same in-house-recruiter solution to the **leadership layer** (head of growth, COO-level, channel owners) so the owner and COO Kyle can move to higher-value work. Alex reframes the situation: "You built the bottom layer, you're missing the top layer, which means you're getting pulled into the day-to-day fires and can't be strategic." His diagnosis splits the missing roles by whether the owner has a **network and pattern-recognition** for them. Sales/marketing leaders — spin your own network, you already know what "game" looks like. Finance, tech/AI/head-of-analytics, and people/HR — you lack the expertise to judge, so **pay a headhunter** (blue-chip firms will run the technical screens for you), then you do culture/fit. On the fear of pulling himself out to recruit full-time, Alex gives the **"six-week sprint"** move he and Leila use for big hiring constraints (only for C-suite; not directors/managers), plus the **CEO cold-outreach** tactic (response rates jump when the CEO reaches out) with a low-pressure "do you know anyone like you?" intro script. He tells the owner not to replace all six reports at once but to sequence: fix the role "most on fire" first, then the next. Underlying principle: speed in business comes from **pattern recognition with people and talent** — you rebuild fast where you know what a role looks like, and slow down at levels you've never hired; there he pulls in outside experts (e.g. his attorneys) just to vet candidates on human judgment. On the cost objection (recruiters charge 20–25% of a $100–200k package; several roles at once = a big cash hit vs. ~$10k/month in-house), Alex both validates in-house recruiting to smooth the cash impact and reframes it as an **investment**: "Would I pay 40 to make 400? Yes" — it's a risk-reward trade, "which is why we get compensated when it works." Closes with the observation that scaling is just an endless sequence of solved-problem-then-more-problems, then the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses the client as having "built the bottom layer" but "missing the top layer" — the absent leadership tier is what pulls the owner into daily fires and prevents strategic work.
- As of March 2026, Alex splits missing hires by whether the owner has a network for them: sales/marketing leaders can be sourced through the owner's own network (strong sales/marketing "chops"), while finance, tech/analytics, and people/HR are areas the owner lacks expertise to judge and should hire an outside headhunter for.
- As of March 2026, Alex says finance is "incredibly important" as you scale, and that data/tech "gets really compromised" without a tech lead — head of analytics being a mess is a common failure point.
- As of March 2026, for roles you lack expertise in, Alex advises just paying a headhunter; good "blue-chip" firms will run the technical interviews, leaving you to assess culture and company fit.
- As of March 2026, when he and Leila hit a big hiring constraint they run a "six-week sprint," pulling themselves out of the business to do nothing but interviews — but only for C-suite hires, not directors or managers.
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends CEO-led cold outreach for headhunting: it's "an outreach game," and response rates are "significantly higher" when the CEO reaches out personally.
- As of March 2026, his low-pressure outreach script is to flatter the target's experience and ask if they "know anybody exactly like you" for a great role paying X — which lets the person opt in ("fuck my friends, I'm interested") without pressure.
- As of March 2026, Alex says you don't have to replace all reports at once: rank them, recruit for the role "most on fire" first, then attack "the second shittiest" once the first is fixed, working across — "this is growing a business."
- As of March 2026, Alex claims speed in business is dictated by pattern recognition with people/talent: you can rebuild businesses fast where you already know "what a salesperson/sales manager looks like," and slow down at levels you've never hired for.
- As of March 2026, for domains outside his expertise (e.g. legal) Alex pulls in outside experts he already pays — such as asking his attorneys to vet a candidate — relying on human judgment where he lacks technical context.
- As of March 2026, on cost: recruiters charge 20–25% of a $100–200k annual package; doing several roles at once is a large cash hit, so bringing recruiting in-house (~$10k/month) smooths the impact if roles are staggered month over month.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes hiring cost as investment and risk-reward: "Would I pay 40 to make 400? Yes" — you trade money today to make more tomorrow, "which is why we get compensated when it works."
- As of March 2026, Alex characterizes scaling as endless: you solve one problem, "look up," and there are more problems — "it just always sucks / it's just always problems."
