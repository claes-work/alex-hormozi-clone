# If I Had To Start a Business From Scratch, I'd Do This

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi retells the origin story of his first online
business — the free personal-training project that grew into "Skinny to Swole" —
and extracts the lessons he'd apply if starting over today. He narrates the four
stages that got him his first ~20 paying customers (homies → referrals → the
charity-donation free project → paid "Skinny to Swole"), reads his actual first
advertising post aloud, and updates the playbook with his current "Rule of 300"
(a three-step breakdown of the Rule of 100 from *$100M Leads*). The back half is
a 13-point list of mindset directives he originally wrote as a Slack message to
a group of Skool community starters.

## Key claims

- His first online money came from fitness: he was a powerlifter (VP of the
  college powerlifting team) and a Baltimore consultant who wouldn't stop talking
  about fitness; colleagues offered to front him money to start.
- The "light bulb moment": a woman at his gym (Sandy Smith) asked for nutrition
  help over an hour-and-a-half meeting at a pizza shop, then wrote him a $100
  check — proving he could make money doing this.
- Four stages to his first customers: (1) **Homies** — reached out through his
  phone contacts, got ~6 friends to train free in exchange for before/after
  photos; (2) **Referrals** — asked those first 6 for intros, got ~6–10 more;
  (3) **Free Training Project** — asked people to donate $500–$1,000 to a charity
  of their choice instead of paying him (skin in the game while still "free");
  (4) **Skinny to Swole** — a year later, charged new people $200/month for 12
  weeks; got 20 clients = $4,000/month, his first $12,000.
- Getting the first six homie clients took ~6 months; the whole arc to paid took
  over a year of only doing referral-style outreach because he "didn't even know
  what the word advertising really meant."
- **Get proof before anything.** He claims 100 pieces of proof + a terrible offer
  beats an amazing offer + no proof. Proof only needs to be "similar enough" to
  the prospect that they believe it applies to them.
- Referrals are the most valuable marketing: free, higher conversion at higher
  prices, and referred people are more likely to refer. If you get no referrals,
  it means you're not good yet — so let as few people as possible know you suck.
- **Rule of 100** (from *$100M Leads*): 100 daily reps — reach out to 100 warm
  contacts, 100 cold prospects, 100 minutes making + posting content, or $100/day
  on ads.
- **Rule of 300** (current Skool experiment): (1) post one piece of content on
  one platform; (2) leave 100 thoughtful comments, ideally inside a relevant
  group/community (you only compete against the other commenters, so a thoughtful
  comment stands out and piggybacks on bigger posts' reach — "like running mini
  ads"); (3) apply the Rule of 100 to the resulting "lukewarm" list — people who
  liked the post, commented, liked your comment, or replied to your comment — and
  do one-on-one outreach starting with a question, not just "thanks."
- The purpose of advertising (per the leads book): move a lead from *unengaged*
  (someone you can contact) to *engaged* (someone who has shown interest).
- Rule of 300 outreach takes ~2–4 hours a day; objective #1 when starting is to
  let people know you exist.
- **What worked, in retrospect:** walk the walk / be the biggest walking
  billboard for what you sell ("proof over promise"); live cheap (lived on
  ~$1,200/month, saved ~$50K over two years of consulting — runway to go on
  offense and take bigger risks); start free (non-monetary payment — testimonials,
  feedback, referrals — is often worth more than what a client would pay);
  template a one-to-many solution (a ~200-hour Excel macro sheet that produced
  custom macros + grocery lists for 100 meals in ~10 minutes); niche without
  forcing it (he niched into "skinny to swole" because that's who responded and
  what he liked); recurring revenue model.
- **What didn't work / mistakes:** didn't ascend anyone (no second offer — should
  have sold more of the same thing); "stopped for new" — abandoned a business
  making $4K/month working ~4 hours/week to chase a brick-and-mortar gym because
  he didn't think an online business was "legit"; no real offer; no sales
  call/script (just sent payment links, so couldn't charge more); made huge life
  decisions based on imagined judgment from classmates and his dad; did it all
  solo with no YouTube-style help, which slowed him down.
- Pricing philosophy: "full price or free" (Chick-fil-A philosophy); never
  discount to close because it tells the buyer your prices aren't real.
- Business is "shockingly simple but surprisingly hard" — the hard part is
  consistency, not complexity. Businesses are machines for consistency: consistent
  inputs → consistent outputs; volatility means inconsistent or insufficient input.
- Context: this happened ~2013, before online coaching was normalized and when
  Instagram was barely a platform; he argues it's never been easier to start.
- Promotes Skool (school.com/games): he's a co-owner/investor; a paid, monetized
  Skool community makes (an unstated check amount) in recurring revenue per month
  on average; he takes a call every Monday.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "if you have 100 pieces of proof and a terrible offer versus an amazing offer
  and no proof the guy with 100 pieces of proof is going to win"
- "the big picture reason that referrals are so important when you're starting is
  that if you're not getting any it means you're not that good and so you don't
  want to let more people know you suck so instead you want to let as few people
  know that you suck until eventually people find out about how good you are."
- "saving money won't make you money but it'll give you the security to think
  further out and take bigger risks."
- "the biggest hack of all for saving money is to work more because working more
  makes you money in two ways one is you get paid for the work you do second you
  don't have time to spend the money you make."
- "I follow Chick-fil-A's philosophy with pricing it's either full price or it's
  free I never discount... the biggest one being I don't negotiate with terrorists."
- "if you're fat it's going to be hard for you to sell weight loss if you have
  terrible teeth it's going to be hard for you to sell dentist stuff... you should
  be the biggest walking billboard of whatever it is that you sell."
- "work starts when excitement ends not while you're excited I don't even count it
  as work while you're excited."
- "everything is unscalable in the beginning that's the point it's how you learn
  every single piece of it and when you learn every single piece of how something
  works predictably that's called Mastery."
- "if you exchange goods and services for money you have a business period."
- "business is shockingly simple but surprisingly hard the hard comes in the form
  of consistency."
- "it is better to be envied than pitied... I didn't get in the game to get pitied."
- "the pain that you are going through right now will fade but the result that you
  get will stay with you."
