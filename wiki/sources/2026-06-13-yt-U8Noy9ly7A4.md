# How to Scale a Tutoring Business From $250K to $1M

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a high school English tutoring business (~$250K/year, $15K/month profit, six tutors) who wants to reach $1M in revenue and is stuck on offer and sales process. Her problem: a short summer program converted well through 2024 but died in early 2026, and a switch to advertising ongoing tutoring cut cost-per-lead 75% but brought poor traffic that didn't convert. Alex diagnoses a demand problem and prescribes returning to the defined, short front-end offer, refreshing the ad creative, and upselling that front-end into a bundled one-year continuity package anchored by an outcome guarantee.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- The core issue is a **demand problem**, not that customers genuinely only want the short program.
- People buy toward a **defined end thing**, not open-ended continuity — the gym analogy: nobody buys a membership saying "I want a lifestyle"; they buy a six-week challenge, 21-day detox, or 28-day fix.
- The rule: **give people what they want in order to give them what they need** (the "ham and garlic" parable — wrap the thing they need inside the thing they want).
- Selling a **defined end thing on the front end** turns the relationship into a sales motion: the longer time window lets you educate the customer on what actually matters, and once they see momentum they choose to stay long-term.
- Practical prescription: (1) if the 3-week program was selling, **go back to it**; (2) the failed campaign likely suffered from **weak/insufficient ad creative variation** — make more variations of the offer that worked; (3) **upsell** front-end buyers into continuity by bundling the 3-week program against a **1-year package** (he recommends a year over a single term).
- Build the offer around a concrete **outcome** (e.g., a score of 40+/50, top 10% of state) backed by a **guarantee**: keep tutoring for free until they hit the score.
- The predictable objection — students who don't hit the score will give up — is overstated: **people don't study for years and get no results**, so the guarantee is safe and compelling.
- Competitor validation: a large local tutoring company doing ~$1M/month sells a **1–2 week free trial** before the school year, not straight-to-tutoring — because **continuity is rarely sold directly**; you give the "ham" (a defined trial they want), let them taste it, then sell them into the long-term thing.
