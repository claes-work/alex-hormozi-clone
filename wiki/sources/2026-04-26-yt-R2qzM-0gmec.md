# Helping a Medical Service Business Scale

## Summary

Alex coaches a medical-support service attached to surgery recovery. The owner wants 80%
gross margins but fears patient-care coordinators will not sell the higher-priced kit.
Alex says the higher price can fund higher coordinator incentives, because commission is
acquisition cost, not delivery cost. He also recommends simplifying the offer: if delivery
is essentially the same across rhinoplasty, weight-loss surgery, and semaglutide patients,
make the delivery and price the same.

## Key claims

- Alex repeats his service-business rule of thumb: he wants 80%+ gross margins, preferably
  90-95% if possible.
- He says low service gross margins make it very hard to build a profitable business after
  all other costs.
- He separates delivery gross margin from acquisition cost: coordinator bonuses belong in
  the acquisition bucket.
- He suggests doubling price can create room to 4x the coordinator incentive, which may
  overcome the uncomfortable moment of selling the add-on.
- If hospitals refer patients directly to the company, he says the owner can simply state
  the higher price.
- He likes temporarily high incentives when the constraint is teaching referral/sales
  behavior.
- He advises simplifying price and delivery if the core recovery-support service is the
  same across patient types.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I have a rule of thumb for services which is I if I'm doing service, I want 80% or higher gross margins."

> "The whole point of having a higher price is so that you can spend more in acquisition."

> "If you make the delivery the same, then you can make the price the same."
