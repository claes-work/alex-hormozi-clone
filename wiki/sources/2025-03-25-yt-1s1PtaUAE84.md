# How to build a successful Skool Community with Alex Hormozi

## Summary

A solo Alex Hormozi walkthrough of how to design and launch a paid (or free-to-paid) online community, framed around the Skool platform (which he co-runs and promotes). He lays out a sequence of design questions — who is it for, free vs. paid, and what to put inside — and maps community value onto the Value Equation from *$100M Offers*. He argues you do not need to be the expert (you can crowdsource answers through the community), that a community is a leverage asset for reaching high-status people, and that starting free to build momentum sets up a stronger paid launch later. He closes with a "make it fun / find a friend / partner on complementary skills" principle and a promotional invite to the free 14-day Skool challenge. The framing is promotional, but the content contains a full, reusable community-building playbook.

## Community-building playbook

**1. Start with "Who is it for?"**
- Do not overcomplicate the niche. Target people who share your interest and have the same goal or the same problem to solve.
- Easiest lens: "scratch your own itch" — build around a problem you personally want solved (day trading, house flipping, painting, ping-pong, fixing cars, etc.).
- You do NOT have to be the expert. Two paths:
  - **Expert path** — if you already know the skill, teach it directly.
  - **Crowdsourced path** — (what he says most people will pick) gather people interested in the same problem and let the community find answers, bring them back, and create value for everyone.

**2. Community as a leverage/access asset ("skeleton key of life")**
- Once you assemble a group (100 / 500 / 1,000 / 5,000 people), you can approach people far above your status/reach and offer them distribution of their message in exchange for a 30-minute video or a Q&A call with your community.
- Win-win-win: the high-status guest gets distribution, you get a favor and are seen delivering value to your members, and members gain access to people they could never reach individually (a lone member emailing a master mechanic gets ignored; "2,000 mechanics" gets a yes).

**3. Free vs. Paid**
- If just starting: **start free.** Get people in, learn what they like, learn to actually run a community, then flip to paid once confident.
- The decision is reversible — you can switch to paid in month 2, 3, or 6.
- If already confident you can deliver value, you can launch paid from the start.
- Rationale for free-first: you are building an audience, which is the most valuable long-term asset; a paid community launched on top of an already-lively free community of contributors "skyrockets."
- **Nightclub analogy:** clubs let people in free early, then start charging once the party is full — same idea done digitally. Launching a party that's "already rolling" beats showing up to an empty room.

**4. What to put inside — three buckets**
- **Access** — access to you. If 100 people want your time, your time becomes scarce and valuable. Includes Q&A calls with you AND access to outside people/guests the community couldn't otherwise reach.
- **Content** — because the community is a closed/exclusive area, you can post content you don't make public, created by you or by the community.
- **Events** — digital or in person, large or small — from a group hike to a big digital conference.
- Create a **schedule/cadence** around when you deliver these things.
- The lists are not exhaustive — do anything you can imagine that provides value.

**5. Sourcing what to build**
- Ask yourself (scratch-your-itch): "What would be epic?"
- Ask the members directly: "What do you think would be cool?" You now hold the collective asking power of the whole community and act as their champion to go ask on their behalf.

**6. What actually makes a community valuable — the Value Equation (from *$100M Offers*)**
- The "who" work ensures you're solving the same aligned problem/outcome for everyone.
- The other three variables determine value:
  - **Lower risk / perceived likelihood of achievement** — e.g., teach how to NOT blow up your car or break your tool.
  - **Faster (time delay)** — compress a result from a year to 3 months.
  - **Easier (effort & sacrifice)** — don't make them stop doing what they love or start doing what they hate.
- For every piece of content/access/event, ask: How can I make it less risky, faster, and easier for my audience?

**7. Retention / engagement principle — "make it fun"**
- Rule #1 of the Skool Games: have fun (not hokey). Cites Tony Robbins' *Money Master the Game* — billionaires he interviewed all treated money/business as a game and genuinely loved doing it; success "can't suck."
- His own buy-in to Skool came from seeing high engagement and many people (especially first-time earners) making money.
- Two ways to make it more fun beyond participating:
  - **Find a friend** — doing it with a friend beats doing it alone, even when the work is bad.
  - **Partner on complementary skills** — cites his own Skool partnership: Sam (Skool founder) loves building product but not making content; Alex loves content/teaching/marketing but doesn't want to build product. Each fills the other's gap.
- Generalized: if you don't want to build an audience, find someone who has one; if you lack expertise, find someone who has it or source it from your community.

**8. Promotional CTA**
- Invites viewers to join the free 14-day Skool challenge; says he takes calls in that community to help people get their first business online, and that once a month he drops a full-day mastermind training for people starting communities.

## Key claims

- You don't need to be the expert to build a valuable community; you can crowdsource the expertise from members. (self-attributed method)
- A community of 100–5,000 people is a leverage asset that lets you "punch above your weight class" to reach high-status people by offering distribution.
- Free-to-paid is not an irreversible decision; starting free builds a lively, contributor-rich base that makes a later paid launch far stronger.
- Community value is governed by the Value Equation from *$100M Offers* (dream outcome, perceived likelihood/risk, time delay, effort & sacrifice).
- The three things you can put inside a paid community are access, content, and events, delivered on a schedule/cadence.
- Success requires the work to be enjoyable; billionaires (per Tony Robbins) treat business as a game they love. (secondhand from *Money Master the Game*)
- Skool was built to facilitate the crowdsourced/community model "so you no longer have a reason to not get started." (promotional)
- His Skool involvement is a complementary-skills partnership with founder Sam — he handles marketing/content, Sam builds product. (self-reported)
