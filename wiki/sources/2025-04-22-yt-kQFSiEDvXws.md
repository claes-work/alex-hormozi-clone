# The Biggest MYTH You Probably Still Believe

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi attacks the belief "my business isn't scalable"
as one of the most business-ending myths he sees among the thousands of owners who come
through acquisition.com. His core argument: every business is scalable, it's just that
some scale harder and slower; difficulty is a feature, not a bug; and the real killer is
that founders, unable to tolerate the pain of waiting for solutions to kick in, break the
things that already work. He reframes the complaint "not scalable" as "it's hard and I
want it to be easy," and argues the true problem is the timeline of expectations, not the
business itself.

## Key claims

- "My business isn't scalable" almost always really means "it's hard and I want it to be
  easy" — every business has limits to scalability, with different problems at different
  stages. (paraphrase)
- Business categories scale differently: service businesses (humans serving humans) are
  easy to start, profitable, but harder to scale while maintaining quality; e-commerce is
  in the middle (scales faster but hits supply-chain/logistics problems); SaaS/software
  costs the most time and money up front but becomes significantly easier once scaled.
  (paraphrase)
- Claim: 78% of U.S. businesses are service-based, so switching from one service business
  to another won't change your growth trajectory — you're still constrained by humans.
  (paraphrase; self-reported statistic)
- Businesses are either demand-constrained (e.g. brick-and-mortar gym: hard to find
  customers) or supply-constrained (e.g. accounting firm: hard to find good accountants),
  and can alternate between the two as they grow; both are solved fundamentally through
  promotion (front end for customers, back end for talent). (paraphrase)
- Difficulty is a feature, not a bug, of scaling — some business problems you manage
  rather than solve; they are permanent "overhead," not fixable. (paraphrase)
- The danger zone is the waiting period: once you start solving a constraint (e.g. hiring
  and training people takes 3-6 months), nothing changes immediately, and that's when most
  entrepreneurs break their model, change their price, or degrade their deliverable,
  creating a structural problem (churn up, margins down) on top of the original one.
  (paraphrase)
- Cites Sam Altman (founder of OpenAI): you can't delegate execution because the idea /
  product is only ~10% of the business (the fun part); the other 90% is execution, the
  "doingness." (paraphrase, attributed to Altman)
- The "brick in a building" analogy: when you move a brick (change something that works),
  the odds it lands in a better position than where it already worked are very low.
  (paraphrase)
- Attributes to his CFO Suzanne: "All businesses have features and bugs" — founders'
  big mistake is believing a feature (e.g. "people are hard") is a bug. (paraphrase/quote,
  attributed to Suzanne)
- The real problem is the delta between expectations and time: you can have the business
  you want, just not by the time you want it — change your timelines, not your dreams.
  (paraphrase)
- His realistic timeline: he works "in decades"; you can go from new idea to massive global
  company in ~10 years (cites Microsoft IPO at ~7 years, OpenAI working since ~2017). The
  rush is imaginary and self-imposed. (paraphrase)
- You have more ideas than time — you must say no to nearly all opportunities and stick
  with one thing; gains come from compounding and sticking with the plan, and the hard part
  of a plan is sticking with it. (paraphrase)
- Entrepreneurship is trading problems, not eliminating them — the longer you deal with
  your problems the better you get at them (analogy to a long relationship). (paraphrase)
