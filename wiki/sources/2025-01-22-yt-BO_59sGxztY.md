# How to Become Ultra Wealthy (4 Methods)

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi lays out what he claims are the only four paths to
becoming ultra wealthy, defined by a 2x2 of *whose money* and *whose business*: (1)
other people's money in your business (raising funding / venture-backed), (2) your money
in your business (bootstrapping), (3) your money in other people's businesses (investing),
and (4) other people's money in other people's businesses (fund management). He frames all
four around return on invested capital and risk, and closes by explaining fund economics
(the "2 and 20" structure, leverage, and carried interest). He notes he studied how to go
from $100M to $1B after starting acquisition.com.

## Key claims (the 4 methods)

- **Framing**: There are only four paths to ultra wealth, defined by two axes — whose
  money and whose business. The common thread across all four is return on invested
  capital and the risk taken to justify that return.
- **Path 1 — Other people's money in your business (raising funding / venture-backed)**:
  You sell a percentage of your company, diluting yourself but gaining capital to grow.
  Makes sense only when the business needs huge capital to start (e.g. pharma) or is in a
  winner-take-all / network-effect market where speed captures a monopoly. Examples: Tesla
  (Elon ~17%), Amazon (Bezos ~10%), Nvidia (Jensen Huang ~4%) — founders own small slices
  of enormous pies yet are centibillionaires. Bezos sold 20% of Amazon for $1M to ~50
  investors in the first round. Risks: aggressive VC-forced growth, board seats, dilution,
  and getting ousted (Steve Jobs at Apple). Exits come via IPO or private-equity buyout
  (e.g. Kajabi / Tiger). "Every single business incurs debt the moment you start it" —
  bootstrappers take on talent debt and technological debt instead of financial debt.
- **Path 2 — Your money in your business (bootstrapping)**: The most common path and the
  one Alex favors. No dilution, complete control. "Rush is imaginary" — 99% of businesses
  don't need aggressive growth timelines. Chick-fil-A (S. Truett Cathy, prudent, no debt,
  2,600 locations) vs. Boston Market (raised heavily, grew fast, went bankrupt). Key lever
  is **return on invested capital (ROIC)** — reinvest profits where your own returns are
  highest, which compounds. Build a "compounding vehicle": either (a) sell things people
  never stop buying (recurring like Netflix, or reoccurring like Coca-Cola / Starbucks) or
  (b) build a distribution base of people who never stop selling for you. Company A vs.
  Company B example: the business that retains customers compounds and always grows.
- **Path 3 — Your money in other people's businesses (investing)**: The path of the
  retail/traditional investor (Shark Tank analogy). You're compensated for shouldering
  risk with capital rather than time. You can diversify across many bets, but investors
  tend not to be the wealthiest people because returns are diluted across a portfolio — a
  win is only ~1% of your book vs. the founder's concentrated slice. "Real estate has
  created more millionaires than any other vehicle, but private equity has created more
  billionaires than any other vehicle." Alex shares his own active PE thesis (see notes).
- **Path 4 — Other people's money in other people's businesses (fund management)**: Makes
  you a fund manager — the highest-leverage path and one that reliably produces the
  wealthiest people. Fund economics: GP puts in ~5% ($5M of a $100M fund), raises 95% from
  LPs, then levers $100M into $300M of businesses (with $200M debt). "2 and 20" structure:
  2% annual management fee + 20% carried interest on profit (carry taxed as capital gains).
  Worked example turns a $5M GP contribution into $100M+. American Pacific Group (APG),
  which bought Gym Launch + Prestige Labs for $46.2M in 2021, ran exactly this model using
  endowment money (Harvard, Yale, teachers'/police/firemen funds). Value comes from two
  edges: **proprietary deal flow** (better prices/terms, off-market, no auction) and
  **insider industry knowledge** (specialized PE firms).

  age 31, and then I started acquisition.com, our investment firm."

  debt you're going to incur."

- "Sometimes the way to build something for the long term is to not build it for the short
  term... because sometimes you grow bigger by giving yourself the time to grow longer."

- "Real estate has created more millionaires than any other vehicle, but private equity has
  created more billionaires than any other vehicle."
- "For them you are 1% bet. For you, you're 100% bet."
- "Betting on the jockey is the highest leverage bet you can make because you're betting on
  someone's ability to solve problems in a dynamic environment."
- "The two strongest forces in business are leverage and supply and demand."
