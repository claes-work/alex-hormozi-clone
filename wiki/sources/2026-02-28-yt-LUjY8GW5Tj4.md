# Why Offers Eventually Stop Working

## Summary
Short live Q&A clip. An audience member asks what to focus on first for a "comeback" when the market changes, an offer "dies out," and new things like AI arrive. Alex **rejects the traditional direct-response framing** that offers naturally die out. In his view, an offer "dying out" usually just means the product was bad: "I sold everyone I could dupe into buying, and now I have to dupe them again with something new" — which he calls "kind of shitty." His alternative: make something people genuinely want, so that a purchase signals real desire for the thing, and then simply **deliver the thing you originally promised**. The hard part isn't inventing a new offer; it's actually doing a good job — "easier to say than it is to do." On the AI follow-up, Alex says applying AI to an existing business (plumbing, coaching) to make it more money is a real opportunity, but building a business that *is* entirely AI is riskier: "AI will put more AI businesses out of business than AI will put normal businesses out of business" — because a thin AI wrapper may be replicated by OpenAI within months, whereas a plumbing business "has a little more time." Attribution is clear: all substantive claims are Alex's; the audience member only asks the question.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, Alex breaks with the traditional direct-response idea that offers inevitably "die out"; he argues an offer dying usually means the product was bad and the seller had merely exhausted everyone they could "dupe into buying," forcing them to dupe again with something new.
- As of February 2026, his fix for offer decay is product quality and delivery, not novelty: make something people actually want, so buying signals real desire, then deliver exactly what was promised.
- As of February 2026, the durable path is "much harder" than launching a new offer — "you actually make sure that you do a good job," which is easier to say than to do.
- As of February 2026, on AI: applying AI to an existing business (e.g. a plumbing or coaching business) to increase its revenue is a valid opportunity worth pursuing.
- As of February 2026, building a business whose entire value is AI only makes sense if you are "really good at product" with "a really amazing engineering team"; otherwise it's fragile.
- As of February 2026, AI will put more pure-AI businesses out of business than it puts normal businesses out of business, because a thin AI product risks being replicated by a foundation-model provider (e.g. OpenAI) within months, whereas traditional businesses like plumbing have more time.
