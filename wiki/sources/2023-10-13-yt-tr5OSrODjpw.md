# Alex Hormozi: "Stop Doing This To Yourself"

## Summary
An interview clip in which Alex Hormozi answers how someone moves from consuming
motivational ideas ("mental masturbation") to actually taking action. His answer is a
two-part model: (1) know the input-output equation — define, down to the most basic
first action, the input that produces the output you want; and (2) know your "fuel."
He argues most people stall because they wait for passion, motivation, or a "big
carrot" they don't have, and instead prescribes "use what you have" — pain, anger, and
shame — as starter gas until you can escape the loop and find genuine passion. He
illustrates with his own story: the "wantrepreneur vs. entrepreneur" wake-up, taking
six months to quit his job, and the hatred of his existence and of living his father's
dreams that finally pushed him over his "action threshold." He closes on pain being a
stronger motivator than pleasure, and on his preference for negative visualization
(future-casting the cost of not changing) to manufacture the "figurative gun to your
head" that triggers the first move.

## Key claims
- Getting from ideas to action requires two things: (1) knowing the input-output
  equation, and (2) knowing what your fuel is going to be. If you can't define the
  inputs and outputs that get you what you want, there's no way to start.
- You must define the required action down to the most basic first input — post the
  content, make the call/DM, run the ad, put your shoes on and lift the weight — because
  a defined first input is what you actually execute.
- Once the input-output is defined, the next question is simply "why aren't you doing
  it?" People misattribute their inaction to lacking passion or motivation.
- People look for one big thing (a big vision, big passion) that is hard to find; in
  reality what moves you is often "a big bag of whys" that add up to push you over your
  action threshold.
- First rule of entrepreneurship he says he learned: "use what you have." Far more
  people have pain, anger, and shame than have passion — use that as gas at the start,
  and later you get out of the loop and find real passion.
- Self-reported biography: watched all the TED talks in college and read self-help books
  every night after college with no life change; encountered the term "mental
  masturbation," then the "wantrepreneur vs. entrepreneur" distinction, which stung him
  into recognizing he was a wannabe. It then took him six months to quit his job.
- He was motivated by hatred of his current existence — being a "wantrepreneur," a
  talker who didn't act — and by resentment at living his father's dreams rather than
  his own; references his tweet "sometimes your parents' dreams have to die in order for
  yours to live."
- His father repeatedly "talked him off the ledge" over dinners when he tried to quit;
  Alex frames this as authoritarian compliance ("you don't need to persuade when you
  have compliance"). Everyone has such a person, or a voice in their head.
- He was so avoidant of the confrontation that he drove across the country before
  calling his father to tell him he had left the job. (self-reported)
- Attributes to Tony Robbins the principle that change happens "when the pain of staying
  the same is greater than the pain of change." Pain moves people far more effectively
  than pleasure.
- Prefers negative visualization over positive visualization: asking "what if I keep
  doing what I'm currently doing for the next 10 years — what will my life look like?"
  magnifies current pain and raises the action threshold enough to take the first move.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)
- "Two things: one is knowing the input-output equation, the second is knowing what your
  fuel is going to be."
- "You have to define what the input is that's going to get you the output you want."
- "A lot of people are looking for something that is very hard to find, and then they
  attribute their lack of success or lack of action because they don't have passion or
  motivation."
- "The first rule of entrepreneurship that I have learned, which is: use what you have.
  And a lot more people have pain, a lot more people have anger, a lot more people have
  shame — and if you can use that as your gas in the beginning, you'll eventually get to
  a point where you can get out of that loop and then find something that you are really
  passionate about."
- "Sometimes your parents' dreams have to die in order for yours to live."
- "The idea that my father had of me as his son, that image had to die in order for the
  image of myself that I wanted to be to live."
- "You don't need to persuade when you have compliance."
- "I think pain moves people far, far more effectively than pleasure does."
- "Death is the great motivator, and the gun just reminds you."
- "People are talking positive visualization; I prefer negative visualization, which is:
  what if I keep doing what I'm currently doing for the next 10 years, what will my life
  look like?"
- "Not the one you wish you had, but the one you've got — and you use that to do the
  first input, you've crossed the line, you're in the game."
