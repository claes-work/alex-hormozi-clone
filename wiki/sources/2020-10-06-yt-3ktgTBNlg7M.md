# What I Would Do If I Had My Gyms (Do This in 2020)

## Summary

Prompted by a question from Leila ("what would you actually do right now if you had
your six gyms during COVID?"), Hormozi walks through a step-by-step playbook for
flipping a six-location gym business to a 100% remote model overnight: break/renegotiate
leases, convert recurring members to remote nutrition-and-accountability coaching, keep
only the best trainers, and redirect his own time to sales. He repeatedly stresses that
workouts don't matter — accountability, relationship, and (during COVID) hope are what
clients pay for.

## Key claims

- (2020) His guiding philosophy: "I would rather have a solution that I can get 90% of
  the marketplace to get an 80% win rather than have 5% of the marketplace get a 100%
  win." He designs offers and sales processes anyone can execute, not what he
  personally could do (he'd sell high-ticket over the phone all day). He cites Elon
  Musk making a similar "good for a ton of people vs. a lot for a few" point.
- (2020) Step 1: Get out of the leases now. Break them or negotiate 3-6 months of free
  rent. He argues COVID gives "permission" to exit with zero repercussions — government
  and clients are both encouraging the flip to remote.
- (2020) Step 2: Flip all recurring members to remote (nutrition + accountability).
  Worked example: six gyms at $30k/month recurring EFT = $180k/month; assume ~25%
  churn from the flip (staff, not owner-operated, "not rubbing bellies and kissing
  babies") leaving ~$135k/month recurring.
- (2020) Step 3: Keep only the best 1-2 trainers per location (the most caring,
  engaged, upbeat) — ~12 people; the rest are "non-essential" because they can't add
  value in the new model. Pay each ~$3,000/month to work from home (a raise for some),
  costing ~$36k/month overhead — leaving roughly $100k/month in profit "overnight."
- (2020) Trainer daily job = three things: (1) first two hours doing 100 cold reach-outs
  to book sales calls, (2) reach out to existing customers so they feel someone is
  paying attention, (3) run/record one workout a day (which they'd do for themselves
  anyway).
- (2020) Gym Launch is centralizing fulfillment — running 16 remote workouts per day
  (roughly one per hour during the workday) so member gyms can cut overhead and not
  even staff their own workouts.
- (2020) "No one cares about the workouts." Workout apps (Beachbody, Kayla Itsines'
  Sweat) already exist at $10-20/month with better content; you win on accountability
  and service, not workouts. "They pay for you to pay attention."
- (2020) His own time in the crisis = sales. Trainers book appointments on his
  calendar; he closes. He'd turn on cold paid traffic because COVID lead costs are
  "50% to 75% off" — "I've never seen anything like this." The owner's job is to "make
  it rain and feed the machine."
- (2020) A soft cold-outreach script: "Hey [name], do you know anybody who's trying to
  stay in shape during the quarantine?" — framed as asking for a referral, not
  soliciting, which works because there is strong goodwill toward small businesses in
  the moment.
- (2020) Selling during COVID: "everyone right now is at the airport" — already at ~6/10
  stress before you start. Fundamentals haven't changed but there's more resistance.
  What people (prospects, team, customers) want in one word is "hope." Spend the first
  15 minutes of a call making them feel good; the weight-loss sale is almost secondary
  to how you make them feel. He notes therapy and life coaching were "blowing up."
- (2020) Reframe on the pivot: just because it changed doesn't mean it's bad. He'd
  walked through the math ~eight months earlier on why remote fulfillment has better
  margins; people resisted because it "felt" bad, but now they're being given
  permission — "It actually makes more money."
