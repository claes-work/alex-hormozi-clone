# How Do You Show Proof When It's All Under NDA?

## Summary
In this short coaching exchange, a caller who sells marketing services to precision machine shops and aerospace suppliers tells Alex Hormozi his best wins are real but locked behind NDAs and an industry culture of not tipping off competitors. He asks for Alex's hierarchy of proof when the strongest evidence can't be named, and whether he'd ever walk away from a great client because the win can't be used.

Alex answers the walk-away question first: no, he wouldn't walk away from a great client over an unusable case study unless proof rights were part of the original deal terms (e.g., a discount given in exchange for the right to use the case study) — in that scenario the terms would simply need to be renegotiated. On the proof hierarchy, his main move is anonymization: tell the story as "Acme Corp," stay specific about what was done, and change the company/person names for anonymity rather than dropping the story altogether — this can actually unlock more testimonials, not fewer. He then broadens the frame beyond past-tense case studies to proof that happens in the present: approximating what being a customer looks like before someone buys, such as playing real (anonymized) call recordings for prospects, or the ultimate version — a free trial where the prospect doesn't have to trust him at all because they pay only after seeing results.

The clip closes with a scripted pitch for Acquisition.com's free "$100 Million Scaling Roadmap" quiz tool, distinct from the coaching content itself.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-20. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-20) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The caller sells marketing services to precision machine shops and aerospace suppliers; the wins are real but live behind NDAs in an industry culture where nobody tells competitors what's working.
- (2026-07-20) Alex would not walk away from a great client just because a win can't be used as proof, unless usage rights were already part of the deal's terms (e.g., a discount granted in exchange for the right to use the resulting case study) — in that case the terms themselves would need to change.
- (2026-07-20) When the strongest evidence can't be named, stay specific about what was done but anonymize it (e.g., call it "Acme Corp," change company/person names) rather than omitting the story; this can unlock more testimonials/proof, not fewer.
- (2026-07-20) Proof isn't limited to past-tense case studies — it can also be proof-in-the-present: approximating what being a customer would look like before the prospect buys.
- (2026-07-20) Example given: for a lead-gen client, obtain (anonymized) call recordings of the leads coming in and play those recordings for a new prospect, then ask directly whether they could handle that volume (e.g., "can you handle 10 to 15 of these a week?").
- (2026-07-20) General principle: proof comes in many forms, not just case studies — the goal is finding as many ways as possible to approximate the post-purchase experience before the sale and strip out risk for the buyer.
- (2026-07-20) The "perfect world" version of proof/risk reversal is a free trial: let the prospect experience the result first and pay afterward, rather than asking them to trust claims upfront.
