# In Your Darkest Days, How Did You Keep Going?

## Summary
Answering a gym-owner's question at a workshop/roundtable, Hormozi explains how he keeps going through dark periods in business. His frame is that life is suffering as a constant — through growth, plateau, shrinkage, or doing nothing — so he chases usefulness rather than happiness, and treats setbacks by asking what actually changed about his life and what he'll do next. He shares candid self-reported hardship detail, including calling this his hardest year in eight and a recent $17M cash loss.

## Key claims
- (as of 2026-03-22, self-reported) Hormozi says this is the hardest year he has had in the last eight, and — having ranked his last 13 years — the fifth-worst year overall.
- (as of 2026-03-22, self-reported) He recently made a bad business decision that lost him $17 million in cash.
- (self-reported) His two worst years were: the first year he started his business (worst), and the year he "lost everything" twice (second worst).
- (self-reported) He took a year off during "the sale" and was miserable; he claims misery is a constant whether growing, plateauing, shrinking, or idle.
- (paraphrase) At 19 he abandoned happiness as a goal (was chronically dissatisfied) and shifted to "be useful" — an inherently service-oriented, learning-driven goal that has served him well.
- (paraphrase) His overarching frame for reality is behavior: boiling everything down to "what will I do and how does this affect what I do."
- (paraphrase) Most suffering is optional/voluntary and happens in the mind; after a loss he asks what materially changed (food, wife, job, team, building, city) — usually nothing but a number.
- (paraphrase) The less you change your lifestyle as the business succeeds, the more resilient you are — like anchoring 100 feet below the surface so wave choppiness doesn't reach you.
- (paraphrase) Invoking a Buddhist idea: the opposite of joy is apathy, not suffering; capacity for suffering exists because there's capacity for joy.
- (paraphrase) Hedonic adaptation runs in reverse too — people adapt to suffering; the second time you get sued or screwed by an employee it hurts less. Business is "good at changing the intensity and variety of punishment," so staying in one vein hurts less but forgoes the novelty-pleasure of growth.
- (self-reported) He says the thing he struggles with most is nihilism / "why bother," not unhappiness; he does the work because usefulness gives him meaning.
- (paraphrase) He frames his content as documentary, not sermon — "this is what worked for me," not prescriptive.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "The premise of life is that life is suffering. And so suffering is a constant."
> "I was 19 years old when I decided that [expletive] happiness was kind of going to be my mantra... And so then I was like, I shifted to like, well, I'll just be useful. And that has served me really well in my life."
> "When you're useful it's inherently a service-oriented goal, cuz you have to be useful to someone. But it also gives you something to do, because in order to be useful to someone, you have to learn stuff."
> "This is the hardest year I've had in the last 8 years... I actually ranked my last 13 years and this is the fifth worst year I've had."
> "Let's say I make a bad business decision and I lose 17 million dollars in cash, which I just did... I eat the same food. I'm married to the same girl. I work the same job. I have the same team. I come to the same building. I live in the same city. Nothing has changed about my life besides the idea that there was a number that no longer is there. And so that means that all of the suffering is optional."
> "The less you change your lifestyle in accordance with your business success, the more resilient you are, because it means that fewer things ever change anything about your life."
> "It's almost like making your anchor 100 feet below the surface of the water. And so the choppiness of the waves doesn't matter."
> "The opposite of joy is apathy, not suffering."
> "If you want to punish someone effectively, then you need to change the intensity and variety of punishment... And so what's nasty about business is that it's very good at changing the intensity and variety of punishment."
> "My content is never a sermon. It's always just a documentary. This is what worked for me."
> "I've never strove — striven — to win the award for happiest guy... I think if anything I've struggled a lot more on nihilism of why bother."
> "It's just a game."
