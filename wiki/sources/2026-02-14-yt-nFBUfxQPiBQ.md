## Summary

Short interview clip (unnamed @MoreMozi host questions, Alex answers) in which Alex Hormozi
reflects on how he has changed his identity over time. He reframes identity not as a fixed trait
but as something built from repeated **behavior**: "you describe someone by what they do," so
changing who you are means changing what you do. He says his emotional attachment to outcomes has
lessened over time — his "emotional regulation" has improved rather than disappeared — while
admitting he still cares about some outcomes (a good book launch, a YouTube video performing). He
names the behaviors he has had to build: **patience** and "figuring out what to do in the
meantime," including the discipline to do *nothing* when nothing is the correct action ("let the
turkey cook"). He stresses these changes are not permanent — you "fight it every day like
addiction." He then gives a behavior-change model: rather than personifying the habits you fight,
recognize you were *rewarded* in the past for a behavior that no longer serves you, and note that
**"punishment fades, but reward sticks"** (hangovers, exes) — so the only way to beat a
once-rewarding behavior is to find something to do instead that rewards you better. The host
supplies one prior identity Alex "killed": attachment to his dad's approval. Clip ends with an
acquisition.com/roadmap ad. All content self-reported.

## Key claims (2026-02-14, paraphrased)

- His emotional attachment to outcomes has decreased "less and less over time"; he frames this as
  improved emotional regulation, not the absence of emotion — "it's not like I'm a robot."
- Outcomes he is still emotionally tied to: whether his next book launch goes well, and (on a
  micro level) whether a YouTube video performs — with the caveat that he recalibrates expectations
  by intended audience (a beginner-targeted video gets far more views than a business-owner one).
- Identity, he argues, is best defined by **actions**: you describe a person (carpenter, selfish)
  by what they do, so identity "ladders up" from behavior; you can't really be something without
  doing something about it.
- Behaviors he has had to build into his identity: becoming **more patient**, and learning to
  "figure out what to do in the meantime" — including the harder lesson that sometimes the correct
  task is to do nothing ("let the turkey cook").
- These changes were "tremendously hard" and are **not permanent** — you have to fight the old
  behavior "every day like addiction."
- Reframe over anthropomorphizing the habit: instead of humanizing what you fight, recognize "I
  was rewarded in the past for behaving this way… but that behavior no longer serves me," while the
  memory of the reward persists.
- Core principle: **"punishment fades, but reward sticks."** You remember the reward of a behavior
  (drinking, an ex) and forget the punishment (the hangover, the bad times), which is why the
  hardest behaviors to change are the ones that once rewarded you.
- The only reliable way to beat a once-rewarding behavior is to **find something to do instead that
  rewards you better** (replacement, not just suppression).
- Prior identity he has killed (raised by the host, not disputed): attachment to the approval of
  his dad.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "It really depends on how we define identity… but if we define that by the actions that we take —
> like if you were to describe somebody, 'he is this way,' you really describe it by the actions
> that person does. It's very hard to say someone is something without doing something about it."

> "The changes in behavior which then ladder up to identity — I've had to become more patient. I've
> had to figure out, which I define as figuring out what to do in the meantime."

> "I just need to let the turkey cook. I just got to let it cook. The right task for me to do is
> nothing… I've had to learn that."

> "I don't think it's permanent. I think you have to fight it every day, like addiction."

> "What's really tough about human behavior is that punishment fades, but reward sticks… when you
> drink too much, the next morning you're hungover and you're like, 'I'm never going to drink
> again.' But a week later you remember the reward, but you don't remember the punishment. It's the
> same reason people go back to ex-boyfriends and ex-girlfriends."

> "The hardest behaviors to change are ones that have rewarded you in the past… And so the only way
> to really beat those is to find something to do instead that rewards you better."
