# How to avoid tons of problems in life

## Summary

Alex looks for single behaviors with many positive downstream effects. His first example
is going to bed on time, especially for people in their 20s and early 30s: it reduces the
odds of drinking problems, poor sleep, missed work, accidents, and other avoidable errors.
He suggests setting an alarm for bedtime rather than wake time. He rejects getting a dog as
a universal high-leverage behavior because for his money-making game it creates time,
travel, interruption, and focus costs. He also names exercise and eating enough protein as
high-leverage health behaviors.

## Key claims

- Alex defines high leverage as one behavior or skill producing many large positive
  outcomes.
- He says going to bed on time is a high-leverage behavior for 20-to-35-year-olds because
  many bad outcomes cluster late at night and after poor sleep.
- His action step is to set an alarm for bedtime, not just wake time.
- He rejects dog ownership for his own goals because it interrupts deep work, travel,
  events, and time control; he says Leila bought a dog and he persuaded her to give it to a
  good home.
- He says exercise has multiple physical and psychological benefits.
- He says eating roughly body weight in grams of protein per day is a high-leverage health
  behavior because protein, resistance training, and hormones are the anabolic levers.
- He recommends operationalizing nutrition by optimizing the three to five meals someone
  already eats most often rather than treating "eat healthy" as an amorphous project.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I want to find single behaviors that have many positive outcomes."

> "You set your alarm for when you go to bed, not for when you wake up."

> "Dogs cost a tremendous amount."

> "There's five meals, 10 meals max that you eat 90% of the time. You just need to shift the dynamic of those meals."
