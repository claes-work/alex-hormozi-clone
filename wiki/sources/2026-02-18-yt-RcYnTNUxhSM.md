# Just do more...

## Summary

A multi-person coaching roundtable in which several creators/operators ask Alex Hormozi how to grow their organic reach faster. His baseline answer is the familiar "do more, better" — most people wildly underestimate how much front-end work reach actually takes. But the clip adds two less-common framings worth keeping: (1) the real constraint on a content business is usually **proof/credibility** ("why should I believe you?"), which he ranks above tech and people as the biggest source of leverage; and (2) the most efficient way to generate volume is to **start from the longest content and derive everything downward**, and to deliberately design that source format to be natively clippable. He debunks the "optimal posting frequency" mythology and uses concrete anecdotes (posting 450x/week vs a billionaire's 7x, a Bollywood account, Family Feud, Power Slap) to make the points.

## Key claims (2026-02-18, paraphrased)

- People wildly underestimate how much work the front end (content/reach) actually takes — the back of the business is comparatively straightforward.
- Anecdote: at a dinner, a billionaire "business guy" new to social said he posts ~7x/week and wanted faster growth; Hormozi says his own operation posts ~450x/week, which shocked the man — the gap is a comprehension gap about how much skill/volume reach requires.
- The "optimal posting frequency" advice (e.g. "twice a day is perfect") is mythology; he cites Instagram head Adam Mosseri saying you are not penalized for posting more, and a Bollywood account posting ~100x/day getting ~1.8 billion views/month.
- You cannot exhaust the platform — the audience is enormous relative to any one creator. Skool has ~22–23M users out of ~9 billion people; all the content from all those users only grows Skool by ~1M/month, so there is far more room than people assume ("you probably just aren't doing enough").
- More posting only underperforms when the extra ideas are worse — you had one good idea for the day; if you can make *more good stuff*, put it all out.
- ★ The biggest lever in a content operation is **proof**, not volume: leverage = tech + people + proof, and proof is "the biggest one of all." The core question the audience is really asking is "why should I believe you?" — that credibility problem ("the big Vegas lights") matters more than making more content. (Hypothetical: if Jimi Hendrix came back and taught guitar, it would blow up — because of proof, not volume.)
- Collaborations produce big spikes in reach and are a legitimate growth lever alongside volume and talent/proof.
- The efficient way to create volume is "many things from one thing": make one long piece (stream/podcast/long-form) and have a team derive shorts, clips, the YouTube cut, email, etc. — "quadruple/quintuple/sextuple dip" — so you get more output without generating more ideas.
- ★ It is a two-way optimization: deliberately engineer the long-form source so it is natively clippable (plan the prompts/questions that will make good clips), rather than just chopping it afterward.
- Content formats can be built to be born-for-social: Family Feud "re-emerged" because its structure (question → open loop → payoff → audience reaction, with built-in social cues and suspense) happens to be a perfect short format; Dana White reportedly designed Power Slap from the outset as a sport built for clipping/social.
- Direction of derivation matters: you start from the long piece and go short, never the reverse — "you can't make a long from a short." He likens it to reverse-engineering ads; the most efficient creators (podcasters, streamers) start long, then it gets short ("hero content").
- His own practice: he spends ~2 days/week on content and has for years, and runs ads on top; he refuses to personally post 450 things ("I would just quit") — the volume comes from leverage (team/tech), not from him doing it all.
- Livestream authenticity is an asset, not a liability: audiences are fine with behind-the-scenes moments (recording an intro mid-stream, background noise) — the realness gets positive reactions.
