# Clear Messaging Always Beats Clever

## Summary
Solo clip on messaging discipline. Alex's thesis: **clear beats clever in all things**, and the practical consequence is that you must repeat yourself relentlessly because "no one cares, no one is listening." He grounds it in his book launch: people asked for "the secret" and his answer was that he made ~5,000 ads that "pretty much said the same thing" — and even then most of his audience never found out about the launch, most who registered didn't show, and less than 2% of people who saw an ad even clicked. Because you are working with such tiny conversion percentages, "the idea that you want to be clever in any way is ridiculous." The longer he's been in business, the more he emphasizes **clarity over cleverness** and **simplicity over complexity**: continually learning to *compress* what you're saying and get it "simpler and simpler" is where messaging becomes "elegant" and "effective." He illustrates with a friend — a phenomenal, long-successful marketer — who spent a day with him and had a very complex marketing structure selling 10 things. Alex's fix: instead of **10 things you're selling, have one thing and give 10 reasons for it**. The friend simplified to one big benefit with 10 reasons and ran the highest-converting campaign in his company's history. (The friend also noted his team had heard him say the same thing many times but needed an outsider to be "the bearer of bad news.") The takeaway marker for copy, sales pitches, emails, and content: **one big idea with 10 reasons, rather than 10 big ideas with one reason each.** Ends with the $100M scaling-roadmap promo (acquisition.com/roadmap).
