# I Just Learned This After 14 Years of Business

## Summary

Solo talk in which Hormozi lays out five business lessons he says he learned crossing
$250M in revenue in 2024. The lessons cover the hidden "cost of change" in a business,
why virality is conditional but revenue retention is universal, why LTV:CAC targets must
scale with how manual the business is, why the $1–3M revenue range ("the swamp") is the
hardest, and why "rush is imaginary" and focus on one business beats chasing many.

## Key claims

- **Cost of change.** Changing any people-dependent function (onboarding, sales,
  outbound) causes an immediate ~20% drop in that function's performance — a "guaranteed
  cost of change." This is his own estimate, not science. (Split-testing a headline is
  different and excluded.)
- Because of that guaranteed ~20% loss, only pursue changes with large upside (20/30/50%+),
  not the many small ~5% tweaks. Constantly changing things keeps the business permanently
  ~20% below where it could be; chasing perfection makes the current business worse.
- **If you change nothing, you get ~5% guaranteed improvement** — people get more efficient
  at what they already do just by being left to do it. He likens it loosely to GDP growth.
- **ICE framework** (borrowed from investing): Impact, Confidence, Ease — score every big
  idea and rank them. He keeps a master sheet titled "GROWTH" (all caps) where he dumps
  every idea so the team isn't overwhelmed, then prioritizes when the team has bandwidth.
- **Virality is an "it depends" answer; revenue retention is the gold standard, full stop.**
  Word of mouth is dampened by (1) disincentive to share (common in B2B where a customer
  won't tell a competitor) and (2) low frequency of communication between customers and
  potential customers. Virality skews consumer-side.
- Key retention metric: of customers you had at the start of the year, what % are still
  buying 12 months later. Distinguishes **revenue retention** (dollars retained) vs **logo
  retention** (customer count) — he optimizes for revenue retention.
- The $100M Leads book has a referrals chapter: ~6 product/stick tactics plus 7 referral
  tactics (one-sided, two-sided, timing, referral events, ongoing programs, unlockable
  bonuses).
- **LTV:CAC (he prefers LTGP — lifetime gross profit, not revenue).** The software rule of
  thumb is 3:1, but that only holds when all three functions — attraction, conversion,
  delivery — are fully automated/high-leverage. Fully manual businesses (outbound + 1:1
  sales + concierge delivery) should target ~20:1; he shoots for 30:1+ and says most of his
  material wealth was made at 30:1 or higher. Absolute floor: 15:1.
- The extra padding manual businesses need is due to **lumpiness**: new SDRs and
  salespeople who underperform or wash out, plus recruiting costs (a high-level hire can
  cost 25–30% of first-year pay; ~$80K on a $300K role). Efficiency "undulates" like a wave
  as you scale; the faster you scale, the more inefficient it is.
- **The "swamp" ($1–3M revenue) is the hardest stage.** A $2M business at 20% margin has
  ~$400K profit; a real "stud" second-in-command costs ~$250K all-in — over half the
  profit bet on one person. You need help but can't afford it. His recommendation: do both
  — go into overdrive AND hire, using unscalable higher-profit activity to fund 2–3 hiring
  attempts so you "win either way." He notes scaling stage tracks **headcount, not revenue**,
  and points to acquisition.com/roadmap.
- **Rush is imaginary.** FOMO comes from an arbitrary self-imposed timeline. 2024 was the
  first year he had no FOMO. Counter to rush: look at people 20 years ahead of you — almost
  all have one massive business, not several. Exception: winner-take-all / network-effect
  tech platforms.
- Focus and saying no is the real hard part of business ("business is built on the amount
  of nos you make"). "Deciding" from Latin *decidere* = to cut off. Starting four things at
  once means the odds of taking any of them to the extreme are ~zero.
- **Hard morphs.** Suffering in business is constant; only its nature changes (unknown →
  no one buys → complaints). People get stuck because they conquer one kind of hard but not
  the next. Growth means confronting why your $3M business isn't a $10M business — not
  starting more $3M businesses.
