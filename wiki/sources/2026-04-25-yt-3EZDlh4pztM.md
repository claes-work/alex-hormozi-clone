# How to Know Which of Your Beliefs Are Actually Yours

## Summary

Alex expands on belief ownership. He says people often defend beliefs they cannot explain,
which means the belief is effectively someone else's. A belief becomes yours when you can
explain why you hold it and change it when the evidence changes. He gives examples from a
high-school affirmative-action debate and changing positions in gym training, and ends with
a lighter pleasure frame: do not overanalyze what you genuinely enjoy.

## Key claims

- Alex credits the first line, "There are two types of people..." to Morgan Housel; it
  should not be treated as Alex's coinage.
- He argues that concern about AI/bot content misses that many humans already repeat ideas
  they did not generate and cannot explain.
- He says a belief is not really yours if you cannot explain why you believe it.
- He says people often defend someone else's belief while rejecting someone else's newer
  belief.
- He uses the "what if he's right?" check as a way to avoid identity threat when someone
  challenges a belief.
- He says arguing the opposite side in a high-school affirmative-action debate changed his
  mind.
- He describes repeatedly changing his views on training technique as he learned more, then
  becoming humbler about asserting certainty.
- He likes the phrase "don't analyze your pleasures," meaning do not turn a harmless thing
  that feels good into a second round of judgment.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you can't explain why you believe what you believe then it isn't your belief."

> "People are willing to defend someone else's belief, but they're not willing to accept someone else's belief."

> "Maybe I should back off a little bit."

> "Don't analyze your pleasures."
