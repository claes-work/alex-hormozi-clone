# The REAL Reason Your Business Isn't Growing

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video diagnosing why businesses plateau: entrepreneurs wrongly
believe their market is saturated and respond by adding new product lines or new
businesses, when in reality they have not exhausted their ability to get customers in
their current niche. He argues "your market isn't capped, your mindset is capped" and
lays out three diagnostic frameworks — the marketing hierarchy (method → platform →
media → volume), the four-variable market-selection test from *$100M Offers*, and the
"Pyramid of Markets" with five directions to expand once a niche is genuinely saturated.

## Key claims (growth-constraint diagnosis)

- The core mistake: an entrepreneur at a first plateau (he cites a ~$3M/year top-line
  business) assumes the market is saturated and adds "massive complexity" via new
  product lines/offers, instead of confronting that they don't yet know how to get more
  customers in their current niche.
- **Framework 1 — the marketing hierarchy (four levers).** Four nested levels you can
  pull *immediately* before ever concluding you're capped:
  1. **Method** — the biggest thing you do to get customers: paid ads, content,
     outreach, referrals, affiliates.
  2. **Platform** — where you run the method: Facebook, phones, email, YouTube, Google.
  3. **Media** — the formats within a platform (e.g. Instagram DMs, stories, posts,
     reels).
  4. **Volume** — how much you do within a given media (one story a day vs. multiple
     posts a day).
- Most entrepreneurs haven't maxed even one of these four levers; the fix is to do one,
  then another, then eventually all of them.
- **Market-selection test (from *$100M Offers*)** — four questions before picking a
  market: (1) Are they in pain (am I solving a real problem)? (2) Are they easy to find?
  (3) Do they have money to spend? (4) Is the market growing (avoid dying markets like
  newspapers; prefer growing ones like retirees)? A market passing all four is a good
  opportunity.
- To size a niche, add up the total ad spend of all competitors serving it on a given
  platform — that estimates how many new clients per month are available there.
- **Framework 3 — the Pyramid of Markets: five directions to expand** once a niche is
  truly saturated:
  1. **Up market** — fewer buyers, higher cost/higher quality, they spend more
     (Enterprise; e.g. franchisors, multi-location/chain owners).
  2. **Down market** — ~10x more people, lower on the pyramid (e.g. from salon owners to
     individual hair stylists working chairs).
  3. **Adjacent** — a similar-but-different vertical with similar owner/customer
     characteristics in the same larger industry (e.g. chiropractor → physical therapy;
     salon → lash-and-nails), attacked the same way as the first.
  4. **Broader** — encompass the adjacent pyramids into one bigger market serving the
     same psychographic (e.g. salons → all "Beauty": medspas, lash, massage studios).
  5. **Narrower** — the only direction that shrinks the market but doesn't necessarily
     shrink income; niching down (e.g. salons that only do Brazilian blowouts) lets you
     get more specific with messaging/offer/value. He claims niching has doubled,
     tripled, or 5x'd revenue while cutting the addressable audience by ~85%.
- **Personal anecdote / lesson.** At a meetup of 8 entrepreneurs doing a combined ~$500M/yr
  (he was doing ~$30M/yr, one of the smallest), he concluded they weren't smarter or
  harder-working; two factors made the difference: (1) almost all had been in business
  longer than him — "big [stuff] takes time"; (2) they went after bigger markets, because
  at some point you do saturate a niche.
- **Gym Launch example.** A licensing company that initially served only micro-gyms
  (only ~50,000 in the US; they'd already spoken with 20,000 and maxed every channel).
  They expanded up-market into health clubs after asking "who do we provide the most
  value to by dollars made?" — health-club owners, where they could restructure
  onboarding/sales/retention/upsells to double or triple the owner's bottom line.
- Investor reframe: look at the marketplace like an investor — "this is a $60 billion
  industry, I don't even have 1% of it" — rather than assuming saturation.
- Closing point: entrepreneurs already have a mental checklist of the "shoulds" (call
  leads faster, follow up better, make more content, run ads on more platforms) but
  aren't doing them; the reason growth feels slow is that building big things takes time.
