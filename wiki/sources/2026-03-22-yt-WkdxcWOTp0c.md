# Why Should You Be Happy?

## Summary
Alex argues that subjective well-being is largely a stable, partly inherited trait that does not shift dramatically over a lifetime, drawing an analogy to how breeding nicer wolves eventually produces gentler dogs. He claims the human brain evolved to find problems and threats to keep us alive, which keeps us chronically stressed and prevents any single achievement from delivering lasting happiness — though that same restlessness drives civilization forward. He closes by reframing risk: the downside of one's bets is not as bad as feared and the upside is not as good as hoped, so the real question is how one wants to spend one's time.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that a person's level of subjective well-being does not change dramatically throughout life and is substantially inherited (demeanor, disposition).
- As of March 2026, he claims happiness has a genetic ceiling analogous to muscle: you operate within your genetic potential and should maximize it, but genetics set the range (someone with muscle genetics who never trains won't reach the same level).
- As of March 2026, he asserts the "I'll do this thing and then I'll be happy" model is false — the brain evolved to find problems and threats to keep us alive, keeping us stressed and unhappy; solving a problem does not make us happier.
- As of March 2026, he frames that same core unhappiness/anxiety as the engine that moves humans and civilization forward, because we keep innovating and solving problems believing it will make us happier (it won't, but progress happens).
- As of March 2026, he reframes risk-taking: the downside of your bets is not as bad as you think, and the upside of it all working is not as good as you think — so the operative question is what you want to do with your time.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Your level of subjective well-being doesn't change a dramatic amount throughout your life. A lot of it is inherited."

> "Our brains are only meant and evolved to keep us alive, and most of that is about finding problems and potential threats within our environment, which tend to keep us stressed out and unhappy."

> "That core unhappiness, that core anxiety, tends to move humans forward because we continue to innovate, we continue to try to solve problems because we all think that when we solve that problem, we're going to be happier. And we're not, but we do move civilization forward."

> "The downside of your bets are not as bad as you think they are. And the upside of you making it all work is also not as good as you think it is. And so it's really: what do we want to do with our time?"
