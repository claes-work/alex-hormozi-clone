# You Need More Pain

## Summary
In this clip Alex Hormozi argues, in conversation with an interviewer, that pain is a far stronger and faster motivator than passion or pleasure, and that people who lack the drive to pursue a goal simply aren't in enough pain to change. He recounts that quitting his white-collar consulting job to become a gym owner was the hardest decision of his life — harder than any later struggle — because it meant defying the opinions of his father and former classmates. He also offers a counterpoint: some people should stop dreaming, accept their current life, and find honor and joy in ordinary work rather than chase an idealized sense of purpose. The interviewer frames the audience as people contemplating a "leap."

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that pain motivates significantly faster and stronger than pleasure; he tells people who ask how to get motivated that they won't get motivated and instead must "hate something."
- As of March 2026, Alex argues most people are not naturally passion-driven prodigies; people only start to like something after they get good at it, and only get good by doing it many times while still bad at it.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises people to deliberately "agitate" or "stoke" their own pain to force themselves out of a comfortable-but-miserable situation.
- As of March 2026, Alex states quitting his job was the hardest decision of his life, bar none — harder than any later hardship — because what caught him up was the opinions of others (his father, his classmates) who he expected to judge him for leaving a "fancy" job to likely become a failed gym owner.
- As of March 2026, Alex says money does not really motivate him; his driving motivation for the leap was "beating my dad" — not wanting his father to be right about him.
- As of March 2026, Alex recounts running his early gym essentially alone, teaching classes from 4 a.m. onward plus marketing, sales consults, and billing until past midnight for about six months, to the point of nearly losing his mind from lack of sleep. (self-reported)
- As of March 2026, Alex offers a counterpoint: if a dream doesn't cause you enough pain to act, maybe you're not caged and should simply enjoy the life you have; there is honor in work itself, and joy can be chosen by deciding to do any work well.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims modern culture (Instagram) inflates idealized versions of purpose, and that 500 years ago people simply did the work their fathers did to eat, without expecting passion.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You're not going to get motivated. You have to hate something."

> "Pain motivates significantly faster and stronger than pleasure does. Like people are like, no, passion is the right way. It's like, point a gun at a family member, all of a sudden 10 out of 10 motivation."

> "You only start really liking stuff when you get good at it, in my opinion, and you only get good at it by doing it a lot of times before you're good at it."

> "Sometimes you have to let other people's dreams for your life die, for yours to live."

> "All the stuff that I described that was really tough that I went through was not as hard as me quitting my job. By far the hardest decision of my entire life, bar none."

> "Money doesn't really motivate me. I love the game for sure, but I love playing the game and the tokens are there. But for me, it was beating my dad. I didn't want him to be right."

> "I knew that from that moment on he would own me, and I just couldn't do it. I was like, I will do anything but go back to that."

> "I think you can find joy in work if you decide to do it well."

> "Maybe some people just need to stop dreaming. Maybe they need to accept their current reality and actually enjoy it."

> "I think there's a lot of honor in work, period. And I think a lot of people fool themselves by thinking that what they do for some reason is not honorable."
