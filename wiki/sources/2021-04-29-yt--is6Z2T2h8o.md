# All Entrepreneurs Have Only 1 Problem... (2021-04-29)

## Summary

Reflecting on a strategic meeting where newly hired talent was driving growth,
Hormozi claims every entrepreneur really has only one problem — a people problem —
spanning finding, hiring, managing, and growing good people. He argues that past
~$10–30M/year the business becomes about talent rather than the founder, and
illustrates the gap between A players and everyone else with a story about a brand
manager who took ownership of an apparel launch.

## Key claims

- (2021) Every entrepreneur thinks they have many problems but really has one: a
  people problem — broken into (1) finding good people, (2) hiring/recruiting them,
  (3) managing them, and (4) growing/cultivating that talent.
- (2021) Somewhere around $10–30M/year, operations and talent take over; ~$30M is
  "all about talent." Understanding that it was about talent — "it's actually not about
  you" — was his biggest bottleneck crossing $30M to $100M.
- (2021) Founders cling to being needed (an interpersonal/childhood need) but the
  business shouldn't require the founder; it should run on its own. The founder can
  keep strategic vision while stepping out of the doing over time; the company becomes
  a "company of companies" / conglomerate.
- (2021) Entrepreneurs are "eternal optimists" who overrate their team ("rose-colored
  glasses"); if you can't identify who on your team is weak, you're not being honest.
- (2021) A-player vs. B-player is the difference between companies that thrive and
  companies that die. The distinguishing trait is ownership and intolerance for
  anything but excellence — not raw genius. His example: a brand manager who sampled
  ~20 apparel manufacturers, rejected the bad ones ("I'm not sending that to my
  customers"), and returned with a vetted solution, raising customer LTV.
- (2021) A B player does the job and goes home (nothing wrong with them, and there are
  many); an A player takes initiative, raises the bar, and asks questions you hadn't
  thought of.
- (2021) The person highest in a hierarchy should have the *lowest* tolerance for
  anything but excellence; that standard dilutes as it trickles down. If someone below
  cares more about the customers/business than you do, the roles should reverse — they
  should run it (cites founders stepping down: Bezos, Phil Knight/Nike).
- (2021) Most people hire people worse than themselves, so standards decay down the
  org; the founder must "carry the torch" and set the bar so A players are attracted.
- (2021) Culture (the "winning team" vibe, speed of response, mutual expectations)
  comes only from having the right people on the bus and removing the wrong ones.
- Attribution note: Alex cites a Chick-fil-A head of people (paraphrased) — "a lot of
  companies want to compete against us in the championship but the reality is they lost
  in the draft" — and a "Harvard study" claim that A players are worth ~5x B players
  (cited by Alex; not independently verified here). Core value "Bur[eau] and boss" =
  "act like an owner."
