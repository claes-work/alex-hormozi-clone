# Helping a Wellness Clinic Doing $6.7M/Year Get to $25M

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A husband-and-wife med spa / "transformation optimization" clinic (Botox, filler, hormones, peptides, weight management) doing $6.7M/yr across three Austin-area locations wants to reach $20-25M. The owner frames his blocker as "which lever to pull next" and admits to "shiny object syndrome" — his wife/CEO Brooke is the "woman in the red dress" (distraction), and they'd nearly built a 10,000-sq-ft flagship before pivoting to smaller 1,200-1,500-sq-ft high-ROI units. Alex reframes the whole diagnosis: the real problem isn't picking a lever, it's that **they don't want to do more of the thing that's working**. Profit is only ~15% ("a little low"), so his prescription is **fix the current three locations to 35-40% margins before opening a fourth**. He explains his canonical **over-expansion pattern**: location 1 has founder magic and great margins; location 2 splits attention and its gains come "almost all from the bottom line," so you end up with twice the liability for nearly the same profit; each subsequent location is chosen/sized better but under-jammed. His verdict: "you over-expanded, which really just means that you under-talented" — they need to backfill talent to drive margins back up. On demand, Alex flags that their organic/SEO lead flow only works "because you're in a market that the supply demand curve is stupid," not because it's good practice — learning **paid acquisition (PPC + paid social)** would let them crush competitors who all rely on reputation/word-of-mouth. Core operational fixes, in order: (1) build a **membership/sales motion** so nearly every customer leaves with a membership → revenue retention, margin expansion, and better schedule utilization (sessions-per-hour-per-technician up → revenue per square foot "through the roof"); (2) a **talent retention model** (career path + structure so clients are loyal to the brand, not the injector, since one-on-one providers "take a big swath with them" when they leave) — which requires paying providers more even on thin margins. The owner shares that Brooke (a top-100 injector) went from 60% of revenue down to 23%, and they use a **phantom stock program** vesting per-location for provider/GM buy-in. Alex endorses that but insists the sales motion and membership model are what to focus on first.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes "which lever do I pull" as avoidance: the thing holding the owner back is that he doesn't want to do more of what's already working.
- As of March 2026, with profit at ~15% (which Alex calls low for the space), the right move is to fix the existing three locations to 35-40% margins before opening a fourth, rather than spreading thin.
- As of March 2026, Alex's over-expansion pattern: location 1 has founder magic and high margins; opening location 2 splits founder attention, so its contribution comes "almost all from the bottom line" — you get twice the liability for roughly the same profit; later locations are sited/sized better but under-utilized on top line.
- As of March 2026, his diagnosis maxim: "you over-expanded, which really just means that you under-talented" — the fix is to backfill talent across existing locations to drive margins back up.
- As of March 2026, med spas are an attractive market because people keep aging, want to look younger, and have the money — a favorable supply/demand curve.
- As of March 2026, relying on SEO/organic/reputation for leads works only because the med spa supply-demand curve is lopsided, not because it's good practice; learning paid acquisition (PPC and paid social) is the edge because nearly every competitor just relies on word-of-mouth and local reputation.
- As of March 2026, the first fix is a defined sales motion + membership bundle so (near) every customer leaves with a membership, which raises revenue retention, expands margin, and lets you optimize scheduling (higher sessions per hour per technician → higher revenue per square foot).
- As of March 2026, one-on-one providers (injectors) build rapport and "walk with your customers" when they leave, so a med spa needs a talent-retention model: a career path plus a model structured so clients are loyal to the brand rather than the individual injector.
- As of March 2026, retaining top providers usually requires paying them more even when margins feel thin; the owner's phantom-stock program (vesting per location, given to providers/GMs rather than a single overseeing manager) is Alex-endorsed as a loyalty mechanism.
- As of March 2026, the sequence to a replicable model: fix sales motion → membership model (maximize revenue per day per technician) → spend more on a better career path so talent sticks and quality rises → then the business makes money when the founders step away → that becomes the replicable unit to expand.
