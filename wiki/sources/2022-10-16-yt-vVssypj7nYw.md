# Why old friends keep you poor...

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Alex Hormozi lays out his philosophy of friendship, reference
groups, and environment curation. He argues that shedding friends every few years is a
natural and necessary part of growth: most people statistically are not "bound for
greatness," old friends anchor you to an outdated identity, and greatness requires
surrounding yourself with rare people who share your goals. He frames all relationships
as transactional exchanges (reinforced or punished behaviors) and gives a filter of
"which friends am I willing to sacrifice the most to keep?" He closes with a prescription:
weed people out, join communities of aligned people, rise via volume, and leapfrog into
higher-status circles.

## Key claims

- He has shed friends every season of his life; his close circle has always been very
  small. He thinks in three-to-five-year "seasons" (high-school womanizer/angry phase →
  cleaned-up college fraternity phase → post-college), and largely dropped contact with
  friends from each prior season when it ended.
- "Most people suck at most things and aren't bound for greatness" because of who they
  are and the choices they're willing to make. Statistically, the people you grow up with
  are unlikely to be in the top 1% or 0.1%, so it makes sense to change friends over time.
- The "friend who left the hometown and forgot where he came from" story is told by
  society because society is the majority — and the majority get left behind.
- Winners and losers share the same goals; the goal doesn't make you unique — the
  willingness to pay the price does. (Analogy: you don't buy everything you see in the
  mall because you won't pay the price.)
- He cuts people out sooner than most because he dislikes the labels they speak over him.
  People from your past know you by an old identity and "beat that label into you"
  because you no longer match their pattern. They call it "change"; he calls it growth.
- Best response to "you've changed" is "yeah, you have."
- Love is measured by what you're willing to give up to maintain a relationship. His
  useful filter is not "do I love my friends?" but "which friends am I willing to
  sacrifice the most to keep?"
- All relationships are transactional/utility-based — an exchange where behavior is either
  reinforced or punished. Relationship strength = how long you'll wait for the next
  reinforcement; a long history of positive experiences buys patience, but with no
  reinforcement even a 10-year friendship gets cut off.
- Leaving a friend can be an act of kindness to the next friend who fits better — someone
  who does want to drink on Sunday and do fantasy football will move into that slot.
- You want people around you who don't believe the old lies you told about yourself; ones
  who encourage the character traits (patience, kindness, loyalty, honesty) you're growing
  into, not who retell stories of who you used to be.
- Shedding identities is core to entrepreneurship: "I am no longer this way; I don't
  accept that reality as truth."
- Kindness = honesty. If you'd say it behind their back, say it to their face. Slowing
  communication cadence lets relationships fizzle naturally; but if it's obvious, have the
  direct conversation.
- Prescription after weeding people out: join communities of people with the same goals,
  crush it (volume is the answer, time is the ingredient), rise in status, and leapfrog
  into other communities. "It's just lunch tables on steroids."
- He wants to live a rare life, so he must be surrounded by rare people. He's fine having
  many friends across different seasons; things don't need to be forever to be good — the
  "friends for life" ideal is just a romantic story. Dying with one or two good friends
  means a good life.
- Confessional example (self-described as painting him in a bad light): a high-school
  teacher stayed after school for a year working out with him two hours a day — a huge
  catalyst for his fitness and confidence. Years later the teacher asked for a two-hour
  call every week; Alex declined, saying he doesn't even talk to his mother or anyone that
  much, only his wife. He wasn't willing to give ~100 hours a year for life to pay a past
  debt, because time/energy/focus ("juju") is finite and scattering it slows you down.
