# Why People Actually Buy (Not Logical vs Emotional)

## Summary

In this short talk (apparently a Q&A/workshop clip), Hormozi rejects the common "logical buyers vs. emotional buyers" dichotomy as unmeasurable and unhelpful. He reframes buyers instead along a single axis of **high-information vs. low-information**: people who already have enough information to decide versus those who don't yet. He argues the entire job of advertising (and outbound, which he treats as a form of advertising) is to move a buyer along that axis by giving them enough information to make a decision. He then explains why outbound is hard — you must have multiple sequential conversations to build up a cold prospect's information level — and how a strong brand shortcuts this by doing the early informing at scale, so an outbound team only needs one or two calls to close.

## Key claims

_All claims below are paraphrased from Alex Hormozi unless quoted; dated 2026-02-26._

- He disagrees with the popular "logical buyers vs. emotional buyers" framing: it sounds good and people nod along, but no one can actually identify or measure who is a logical vs. an emotional buyer. (paraphrase)
- He replaces it with a **high-information vs. low-information buyer** axis. People start at different points on this axis based on who they naturally are and their history of reinforcement around decision-making. (paraphrase)
- People who were rewarded for making fast decisions start further along; people who got "burned" early for fast decisions take longer to decide. (paraphrase)
- No matter where someone starts, there is only **one direction they can move** — toward higher information — and the purpose of advertising is to give them enough information to make a decision. That's it. (paraphrase)
- He explicitly treats **outbound as a form of advertising**. (paraphrase)
- At marketplace scale, only a tiny percentage of people are already educated enough to be "tipped in" and ready to purchase; the rest are what brand and good advertising exist to solve. (paraphrase — connects to what he calls a "reverse awareness funnel.") (paraphrase)
- Outbound is difficult because you start a cold prospect at zero information and must run conversation one, two, three, four before they have enough information to decide. (paraphrase)
- A **big brand** shortcuts this: the brand (e.g., "Alex") effectively holds conversations one through three with thousands of people at once, so the outbound team only needs one or two calls to close ("tip everyone in"), changing the proportions of the funnel dramatically. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "So, I don't think that it's logic because first of all, if I'd be like, 'Show me a logical buyer. Show me an emotional buyer. How do you know that?' It sounds nice. Everyone nods their head, but like no one has any idea."

> "So I take the position that you've got high information and low information buyers."

> "Some people got burned early on for making fast decisions and it takes them longer to make decisions. Fine. But no matter where someone starts on here, there's only one direction that they can go in, which is this way."

> "The point of advertising in general — and I see outbound as a form of advertising — is to give them enough information to make a decision and that's it."

> "The reason that it's so difficult to do outbound is that you start someone here and you have to have conversation one, conversation two, conversation three, conversation four. I have enough information to make a decision."

> "If you have a big brand, Alex is having conversation one with a thousand people, two with a thousand people, three with a thousand people, and then my outbound team only does one or two calls and they tip everyone in."
