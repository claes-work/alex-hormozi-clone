# $25M Is the Worst Price to Sell For

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a two-location dance-school owner who wants a future $20 million-plus exit. Alex says the $10 million to $30 million sale range is difficult because it is too small for institutional buyers and too large for casual buyers. He also warns that talent-heavy local service businesses are hard to sell for large multiples, then recommends stripping complexity down to the highest revenue-per-square-foot model and pursuing a franchise or licensing path if the caller wants a sellable growth vehicle.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - dance-school owner. Her revenue, location count, capacity, acquisition ideas, and exit goal are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Selling a business for roughly $10 million to $30 million can be harder than selling a much larger company because buyer fit is awkward.
- A $25 million deal may not matter to buyers with large checkbooks and may be too large for buyers without real capital.
- Talent-heavy studio and gym-like service businesses are difficult to sell for large amounts because quality depends on instructors and operators.
- More locations can often be offloaded piecemeal, but selling the whole multi-location company for a major exit is harder.
- Franchise or licensing is more sellable than company-owned local service locations.
- The business should reduce complexity by about 80% before trying to franchise.
- The operator should identify the most profitable service by revenue per square foot, simplify staffing, and pilot a replicable model.
- Alex uses Rick Mayo's Alloy example as a model for extracting the highest-performing service line and duplicating a small-footprint unit.

> "That is the most likely path to the goal that you have."
