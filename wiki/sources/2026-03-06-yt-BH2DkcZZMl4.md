# You're Not Supposed to Know What You're Doing

## Summary
Short solo clip. Alex reframes not-knowing as the natural state of growth: **"It's normal to not know what you're doing. If you did, it wouldn't be called growth."** Knowing exactly what you're doing means you're doing nothing new, which means stagnation — so wanting to grow *and* wanting to already know everything are contradictory beliefs. He advises to "high five your fear, uncertainty, and doubt, and carry on." He then folds this into his broader "price of the thing" thesis: much discontent comes from wanting two opposite things at once — strong character *and* an easy life, growth *and* no hardship. Hardship creates growth and character; the two are priced against each other. His metaphor: wanting the outcome without paying its price is "wanting the Jordans without paying the price" — the price isn't bad, it's just what it is, so either pay it happily or don't buy. In the second half he pivots to a hard credibility argument: beginners copy the *surface behavior* of successful people ("they look like they know what they're doing, so I should look like I know what I'm doing") while ignoring that they're a white belt imitating a third-degree black belt. Worse, many broadcast advice that their own results disprove — the 900-follower account teaching how to grow a following, the train-wreck friend giving relationship advice, the out-of-shape person giving fitness advice. **"The evidence that encapsulates your message disproves literally everything you say before you open your mouth."** People fixate on small levers (algorithm, font, vertical vs. horizontal format) and miss "the big obvious thing" — the one domino. He recounts telling a friend who wanted to grow a fitness business that he wasn't in shape and "you don't look like someone that anyone wants to look like"; conversely, if you look like a shredded god you could tell people to eat fried chicken daily and they'd still buy. Find the one big domino that makes everything else take care of itself, do that, and treat every ounce of effort spent elsewhere as waste. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
