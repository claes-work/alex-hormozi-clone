# I Own an E-Com Brand but Make Zero Profit. Should I Sell It?

## Summary
In a live Q&A on @MoreMozi, a founder describes a 9-year-old wellness e-commerce brand scaled to $5–10M annual revenue on Meta ads but now making essentially zero profit — to the point they stopped paying themselves to service company debt — while simultaneously helping their brother scale a much stronger e-commerce 3PL (high-volume, high-ticket, sticky, profitable). They ask whether to try to save/exit the ecom brand. Alex reframes the ecom business as a media-arbitrage business that has lost its arbitrage: the only thing sellable in e-commerce is a brand, and they don't have one (no spokesperson, no named collaborations — just ads on self-made products). He gives a "full green light" to wind it down rather than waste time chasing an acquisition that won't come, redeploy the marketing/sales skills learned into the thriving 3PL, and carry the two or three genuinely great employees over. He tells them to first get their 3PL equity written down with a lawyer. On the 3PL, he counsels against pulling new monetization levers now because the business is already at the brink of breaking from growth — levers that persist a year out (which most monetization levers do) should wait, ideally to be "juiced" ~6 months before a future sale to show a skyrocketing trajectory to acquirers.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) The founder's ecom brand is really a media-arbitrage business, and they no longer have the arbitrage. [guest reports: 9 years old, $5–10M annual revenue, scaled mainly on Meta ads, currently ~zero profit — self-reported]
- (2026-02-11) The only thing buyers acquire in e-commerce is a brand; no one buys media arbitrage.
- (2026-02-11) This founder does not have a brand: no big brand spokesperson, no collaborations with any named brand — they just ran ads on products they picked/created and people bought.
- (2026-02-11) Trying to save the ecom company by switching to a monthly subscription model with upsells does not fix the core problem, because they still have no brand. [guest-proposed model; Alex rejects]
- (2026-02-11) Alex gives a "full green light" to jump ship: wind the ecom business down rather than keep trying to revive it.
- (2026-02-11) Spending more time trying to revive and fix the brand to get a better acquisition would waste enormous time and effort, and they still won't get an acquisition.
- (2026-02-11) The most valuable thing the founder got from the failing business is not just marketing/sales skills but understanding all the pain of building — that lived pain is what makes content and future work resonate.
- (2026-02-11) A silver lining of shutting a business down is that there are usually two or three genuinely great people; move those (and any key employees who want to come) into the new company to carry over "the entire soul of what created value" and propel the next venture.
- (2026-02-11) The founder should make writing down their equity in the brother's 3PL the first priority — get it on paper, using a lawyer to mediate — because that is the asset with real value.
- (2026-02-11) Alex says he doesn't normally say a business "doesn't have value," but states the ecom brand doesn't and the 3PL does. [guest reports 3PL is high-volume, high-ticket, sticky, high-profit, bootstrapped, "very profitable" and scaling — self-reported]
- (2026-02-11) Don't pull a new monetization lever just because you can; only add urgency to a lever if the opportunity is going to disappear.
- (2026-02-11) The vast majority of monetization levers available in most businesses only get better with time, not worse — so if a lever will still exist in a year, don't rush it.
- (2026-02-11) When you're already at the brink of breaking a business from growth (ops can't keep up with sales), fix the constraints one by one rather than adding new profit levers on top.
- (2026-02-11) The best time to activate saved monetization levers is during a sales process: juice the business ~6 months before the sale so acquirers see it skyrocketing and want to catch it before it gets too big.
- (2026-02-11) To fix the 3PL ops constraint, the founder can either jump into the warehouse themselves for a bit or hire someone who has already done it — but should get back to "rainmaking" (sales/scaling) as quickly as possible.
