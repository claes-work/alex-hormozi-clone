# $100M Leads: How to Get Strangers To Want To Buy Your Stuff (2023)

**What it is.** The second book in Hormozi's "$100M Series" (Acquisition.com Volume II),
copyright 2023, Acquisition.com LLC (Austin, TX). It is the "how to find people" volume,
sitting between *$100M Offers* (2021, what to sell) and *$100M Money Models* (2025, how to
get them to buy) — it assumes you already have a Grand Slam Offer and answers the next
problem: "Who do I sell it to? Leads. Lots of leads" (PDF pp. 8–9). 385 PDF pages. Raw
file: [raw/books/2023-100m-leads.md](../../raw/books/2023-100m-leads.md) (verbatim text
extracted from PDF; cite by `PDF p. N` markers).

**Reliability note.** Effectively all biographical and revenue claims are self-reported.
The book's headline track record (Acquisition.com portfolio $250M+ annual revenue,
20,000+ leads/day, a lifetime 36:1 return on advertising, $100M+ net worth at age 32 in
2022) is presented without third-party audit (PDF pp. 10–11). Portfolio-uplift figures
(avg company 1.8x revenue / 3.01x profit in 12 months; 2.3x / 4.7x by 24 months) are
Acquisition.com's own internal numbers (PDF p. 35). The externally-verifiable datapoints
are second-hand case studies used to illustrate referrals — Dropbox "39x'd their business
in fifteen months" and PayPal reaching 100M users via a $10/$10 referral credit (PDF
p. 276) — and the American Pacific Group acquisition of 66% of the supplement/gym-licensing
business at a $46.2M valuation (PDF p. 28, also cited in *$100M Money Models*).

## Thesis

Businesses stall because they don't get enough leads, and they don't get enough leads
because they don't advertise enough — full stop (PDF p. 31). Advertising is "the process
of making known," and its true output is not raw leads but **engaged leads** — people who
*show* interest in what you sell (PDF pp. 9, 42). You grow a business only two ways: get
more customers or make them worth more; this book is about the first, via more, better,
cheaper, and reliable engaged leads (PDF pp. 31–32). There are only **four ways** any one
person can let others know about their stuff (the Core Four), and only **four kinds of
"lead getters"** who can do it for you — master those and "it's hard to be poor with leads
bangin' down your door" (PDF pp. 8, 76, 252).

## Key claims

- **A lead is "a person you can contact"; the goal is engaged leads.** Leads alone aren't
  enough — you want people who *show* interest, which are "the true output of advertising"
  (PDF pp. 41–42, 2023).
- **You grow only two ways** — get more customers or make them worth more — and this book
  attacks the first by getting more, better, cheaper, reliable leads; "when you double your
  leads, you double your business" (PDF pp. 31–32).
- **Lead magnets engage leads.** A lead magnet is "a complete solution to a narrow problem,"
  usually free/low-cost, that reveals a bigger problem your core offer solves — like salty
  bar pretzels that sell drinks (PDF pp. 48–49). Three types (Reveal Problems, Samples/
  Trials, One Step of a Multi-Step Process) × four delivery methods (software, information,
  services, physical) = up to twelve magnets for one problem (PDF pp. 53–58). Rule:
  "Give away the secrets, sell the implementation" (PDF p. 65).
- **The Core Four.** Combining warm/cold audience with one-to-one/one-to-many yields the
  only four ways to advertise: 1-to-1 warm = **Warm Outreach**, 1-to-many warm = **Posting
  Content**, 1-to-1 cold = **Cold Outreach**, 1-to-many cold = **Paid Ads** (PDF p. 76).
  "If you aren't getting as many leads as you want, you're not doing the core four with
  enough skill or with enough volume" (PDF p. 77).
- **Warm outreach benchmarks/math.** Expect ~1 in 5 contacts to engage, ~1 in 5 of those
  to take a free offer, and ~1 of those to convert — about one customer per 100 reach
  outs; at 1% of a list buying a $400 offer, 500 reach outs/week = 5 customers = $2,000/wk
  = $104,000/yr (PDF pp. 104–105). Make "the first five free" when launching anything new
  (PDF p. 95).
- **Content is Hook–Retain–Reward.** The smallest unit of audience-growing content hooks
  attention, retains it, and rewards it; longer content just links units together. Hook
  with topics (Far Past, Recent Past, Present, Trending, Manufactured), headlines (the 7
  news components: Recency, Relevancy, Celebrity, Proximity, Conflict, Unusual, Ongoing),
  and platform-matched format; retain with lists, steps, and stories (PDF pp. 114–128).
  The audience — not the content — is the compounding asset (PDF p. 111).
- **Give:Ask ratio and "give until they ask."** TV runs ~3.5:1 give-to-ask and Facebook
  ~4:1; growing platforms over-give massively, so to grow you should too. His refinement:
  "give until they ask" — over-give in public, let leads self-select and ask to buy in
  private (PDF pp. 131–132).
- **Cold outreach = list + personalization + big fast value + volume.** Strangers create
  three problems (no way to contact them; they ignore you; they're not interested), solved
  by building a targeted list (software → brokers → elbow grease), personalizing to "act
  like a warm reach out," and leading with "big fast value" — give away stuff people
  actually pay for (PDF pp. 161–171). It's slow to build (took him ~a year, not the 12
  weeks he expected) but "brutally effective," with 7 structural benefits incl. secrecy
  from competitors and a more sellable business (PDF pp. 159, 182–184).
- **Paid-ads scaling has three phases** — Track Money, Lose Money, Print Money; he has lost
  money more often than he's made it on ads but "know[s] when to shut it down" and when to
  "hit the gas," 10x-ing winners (PDF pp. 216–219).
- **LTGP:CAC and Client Financed Acquisition.** Measure lifetime *gross profit* against
  cost to acquire; every struggling company he invests in has an LTGP:CAC below 3:1, and
  fixing it (usually by raising LTGP, not cutting ads) makes them take off (PDF pp. 219–221).
  If a customer pays back the cost to get and fulfill them within 30 days (fundable on a
  credit card), cash stops constraining scale — "the key to limitless scale" (PDF pp. 222–225).
- **More, Better, New + the Rule of 100.** To get more leads on your own: do more, do it
  better, or do it somewhere new. "More" runs on the **Rule of 100** — 100 primary actions
  a day for 100 days straight (PDF pp. 232–235). "Better" means testing the **constraint**
  (the step where most leads drop off) one change per week (PDF pp. 237–239). "New" follows
  New placements → New platforms → New Core Four, defeating the "Size of the Pie Fallacy"
  that the tiny slice you advertise to is the whole market (PDF pp. 242–243).
- **The Four Lead Getters give leverage:** Customers (referrals), Employees, Agencies,
  Affiliates. They aren't things you "do" — you use the Core Four to *get* them, and then
  they run the Core Four for you; ideally every lead becomes a lead getter (PDF pp. 247–253).
- **Referrals are a way of doing business, not a hack.** Growth requires referrals > churn;
  goodwill (value minus price) drives word of mouth. Six ways to give more value (mapped to
  the parts of an ad) plus seven ways to *ask* — e.g. Dropbox's two-sided referral 39x'd it
  in 15 months and PayPal's $10/$10 credit reached 100M users (PDF pp. 262–281).
- **Use agencies to learn, not to depend.** Because agencies rotate their best rep off your
  account, hire one for ~6 months to teach you (and your team) a new method/platform, then
  drop to consulting once your team beats them — an $6,000 / 8-hour Facebook-ads tutorial
  early on "made me millions" (PDF pp. 305–313).
- **Affiliates are the highest-leverage lead getter.** An affiliate is an independent
  business that tells its warm audience to buy your stuff for commissions; ALAN's three-tier
  affiliate stack scaled it to $1.7M/mo within six months, and ALAN + Prestige Labs did
  $75M+ revenue from 5,000+ affiliates (PDF pp. 319–321, 351).
- **Open To Goal beats the Rule of 100.** Elite operators commit to an *outcome* daily
  ("sign 5 members no matter what"), not a fixed number of actions; paired with his habit
  stack (wake 4–5am, get right to work, no meetings until noon) (PDF pp. 362–364). Volume is
  the usual missing ingredient — he once ran only 300 flyers when 5,000/day was the real
  test size, doing "1/1500th the level of effort required" (PDF pp. 360–362).
