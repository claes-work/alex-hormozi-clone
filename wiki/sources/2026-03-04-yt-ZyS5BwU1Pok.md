# Why I Work Even Though It Doesn't Make Me Happy...

## Summary
Short interview clip in which Alex pushes back on the premise that hard work should be justified by happiness. His argument: the assumption that "the goal of life is to be happy" is itself the trap. His stated goal is instead "to do epic [stuff]" and "to be useful." He recounts an early-life **obsession with happiness** (studied positive psychology in college, "consumed all that stuff") that made him *more* sad and depressed, until he "cracked one day" and gave up on happiness as a target entirely — deciding to "just do stuff." Doing so paradoxically relieved his multi-year funk: he looked up later and realized he was "not miserable." He reframes the mechanics of chasing happiness: wanting to "be happy" really means wanting to be *happier* than you currently are, which manufactures a permanent gap between where you are and where you want to be — "the recipe for being unhappy." By relinquishing the *requirement* that activities make him happy, enjoyment became "a pleasant surprise" rather than a prerequisite. He argues the joys he values most in retrospect came from things he was **willing to sacrifice for over an extended period**, which "pay memory dividends far greater than the momentary cost" — a long-term/short-term tradeoff. His deeper personal goal: "squeeze every ounce of potential out of whatever I have," warning that unused potential "will eat you alive until you do something about it." Notes he had achieved by conventional measures (finished school in three years, did well, good job) yet found it "empty." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
