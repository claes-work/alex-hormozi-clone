# If You Hate Them, You Don't Understand Them

## Summary
In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi recounts a formative intro-to-psychology assignment from age 19 in which he had to write the story of a pathology from the sufferer's perspective. He chose his mother — who has been public about depression and ADD — and by inhabiting her life story (immigrant who arrived not speaking English, beaten up as a foreigner, first female class at Hopkins as a doctor) he came to understand the external pressures that shaped her. The exercise dissolved what he describes as roughly a decade of anger toward her within a few weeks. He builds this into a general principle, anchored to a Blaise Pascal quote ("to understand is to forgive"): you cannot simultaneously hate and understand someone. Anger, for Hormozi, has become a signal that he is missing understanding — a person to attack becomes a question to solve. He notes the self-centered childhood tendency to assume everything happens because of us, and argues that writing empathetic narratives about people who wronged him (usually around loyalty or betrayal) has repeatedly defused his anger throughout life. The clip closes with a promo for his free acquisition.com 10-stage scaling roadmap.
