# How to Use Affiliates to Grow Your Business

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-camera host asks the questions, marked `>>`; all substantive answers are Alex's). Alex lays out his affiliate/referral playbook, opening with a concrete recruiting mechanic he'd use to build a UGC "army" for the books: start a Skool group, invite interesting micro-creators, announce a fixed monthly ad-spend budget sourced only from that community's ads, then convert a slice of that spend (e.g. a $10,000 pot) into a **prize split proportional to each creator's ad spend/performance** — a way to run affiliate-style creator competition even outside TikTok Shop. Pressed on how to actually get affiliates to *activate* (since referral asks can make people defensive), Alex's first and heaviest point is that **it comes down to product, not affiliate strategy**: people freely tell everyone about products they love, and it's "very rare" that an affiliate strategy is the *reason* something grows — the product is good first, then a clear-incentive wrapper is added (Dropbox, PayPal as examples). He then gives his four-part model — **right product, right incentive, right time, right way (low friction)** — ranking product #1, incentive #2, time and friction tied for #3. The core risk to the referrer is **social capital**: recommending something mediocre costs them standing (the "why did you send me this restaurant" problem), so the product must be genuinely great before any of the machinery matters. He argues TikTok Shop works precisely because product, incentive, and friction collapse into one: creators are already posting, the incentive is baked in, there's zero friction (it's physical products only — no digital). Alex then maps affiliates onto his **value equation** — make it easy, make it fast (value), make it low risk — and adds the **timing** dimension: ask for the referral at the point of greatest satisfaction, but *only* when the customer is satisfied-but-not-deprived; if they're deprived of the next thing, ask for the upsell instead (an upsell beats a referral at the same moment). His canonical illustration is the steak: a diner who loved the steak doesn't want a *second* steak (satisfied, not deprived) — that's the moment to ask "do you have friends who are also deprived of this thing?" He cites Skool, Allan(?), and Prestige Labs as businesses he's built primarily off affiliate/referral. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
