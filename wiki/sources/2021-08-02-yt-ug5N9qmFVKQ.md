# How I'm building a BILLION DOLLAR empire working 2-3 days per week

## Summary
In this solo talk aimed at entrepreneurs already doing one to five million dollars a year, Hormozi argues that the entrepreneurial itch for "novelty" and "challenge" is usually an insecurity projected onto the business, and that acting on it (starting a new company, entering an unfamiliar arena) breaks a machine that is already working. He opens with the parable of a neighbor who sold a boring trash-collection business for a fortune and planned to simply build and sell another one rather than learn something new. His prescription is to redefine growth as developing the character to do the same thing repeatedly, to channel the innovation urge into new front-ends, hooks, creatives and product lines rather than new businesses, and to invent challenge by adding constraints to the existing game. He introduces "multiple metrics for success" — pursuing net worth alongside a good marriage, physical shape, and limited working time — and frames his own current constraint as building a billion-dollar-a-year revenue portfolio while working two to three days a week and training one to two hours a day. He self-reports the portfolio is at about $85 million a year in revenue, short of the billion-dollar goal, and reasons through compounding math (matching a ~10% market growth rate) to argue that patience beats out-hustling. He closes with a personal origin story: learning to sacrifice everything during a demanding fraternity pledge in college, which trained the discipline he later transferred to business, and a philosophical coda that you must achieve mastery over a thing (money) before you can transcend the need for it.

## Key claims
- Entrepreneurs crave novelty, challenge and growth, but often conflate "growth" with "new"; growth can instead be redefined as developing the character traits to persist at the same thing over and over.
- A business does not exist to satisfy the owner's internal deficiencies (insecurity, need for connection, status) — it exists only to provide value to the customer; projecting personal needs onto it leads to wrong decisions that break a working business.
- The urge for innovation should be channeled into new front-ends, hooks, creatives, ad ideas and product lines that attract more avatars and grow the business — not into starting new businesses ("you have lots of businesses and you don't have any money").
- Leaving a field you have mastered to enter a new arena means relearning everything from zero against established "kingpins" who have a decade of head start.
- If a $100k/year business grew at the market rate (~10% a year) for 40 years without ever quitting, it would compound into enormous wealth; most people try to beat the market when simply matching it would make them "extraordinarily crazy wealthy."
- The marginal utility of additional money is very low once basic wealth is reached — an extra $10M or $50M buys nothing the first ~$50M did not — which forces you to create new ways to win by adding constraints.
- Multiple metrics for success: instead of only net-worth increase, ask whether you can grow net worth AND have a good marriage AND stay in shape AND work limited hours.
- His current stated portfolio goal is $1 billion/year in revenue; self-reported current run-rate is ~$85 million/year across the portfolio (2021).
- A billion cannot be reached by out-working; it requires good decisions and letting compounding work — at ~10%/year, doublings every ~7 years put $85M near a billion roughly 28 years out.
- The first level of achieving any big goal is learning to sacrifice everything for it; he says he does not know ultra-successful people who have not put everything "on the altar of success."
- The willingness to sacrifice everything comes from a great deficiency/pain in the soul — the pain of not having the thing outweighs everything else.
- His personal framing was never "I'll outwork everyone" (he didn't think that realistic) but "I will out-sacrifice everyone" — getting ahead while competitors went to dinner or spent time with family. (self-reported)
- The mature challenge is adding constraints to the existing game (billion-dollar revenue while working 2-3 days a week and staying in competitive physical shape) rather than breaking what works to chase ten new things.
- You must achieve and master a thing before you can genuinely give it up or transcend the desire for it (invokes Buddha's "middle path" after having been a wealthy prince).
- Big-picture prescription: use multiple metrics of success; don't change your business just to challenge yourself; channel the entrepreneurial "ADD" into front-ends and product lines; otherwise use constraints to create challenges that improve your life in other ways.
