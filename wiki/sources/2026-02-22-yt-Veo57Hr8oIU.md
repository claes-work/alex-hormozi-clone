# Helping a Cotton Candy Manufacturer Scale | Hormozi Hotline

## Summary

A "Hormozi Hotline" call-in Q&A. The caller (a business owner — **not Alex**, treat as
audience) runs a company selling robotic cotton candy machines. Their model has two
buyer types: entrepreneurs who buy a machine (~$15k) as an investment and place it in a
location, and businesses/venues where they either place a machine for free in exchange
for a percent of sales, or sell it outright. Their stated constraint: they have more
entrepreneurs wanting to buy machines than they have locations to place them, so they
want to build an outbound funnel to source venue locations.

Alex diagnoses that the caller already has a **working process that simply hasn't been
scaled**. Cold email works for them (~100 emails → 2–3 replies → most close), while paid
Meta/LinkedIn ads "didn't work." Alex reframes the failed-ads point: an ad failing means
*that* ad + conversion process failed, not that the channel doesn't work. His core
prescription: stop treating lead-gen as the bottleneck and instead **scale the proven
outbound channel** — go from 100 to ~5,000 emails/day via automation (warmed domains,
multiple sending names, AI personalization on the front end), and feed inbound replies
straight to the existing closers on a round-robin, first-come-first-serve basis while
those closers keep doing ~2 hours of their own outbound daily. He notes the caller's team
is closer-heavy (four closers, one setter) and that SDR capacity — human or increasingly
AI SDR/dialers — is the lever to fill the pipeline. Alex gives a single "immediate next
action": focus entirely on scaling email volume (~5,000/day), automated, not done by the
sales reps.

**Caller metrics (venue economics, machine cost, funnel conversion) are self-reported and
unverified.** Only Alex's advice below is treated as persona/belief data.

## Key claims (paraphrase unless quoted; dated 2026-02-22)

- A business with a proven-but-small outbound process doesn't have a lead-gen problem —
  it has a **scaling problem**; the fix is to multiply volume on the channel that already
  converts. (Alex, paraphrase)
- When a paid-ad channel "doesn't work," it usually means *that specific ad and its
  conversion process* failed — not that the channel (e.g. Meta ads) is broken for the
  business. (Alex, paraphrase)
- If you can profitably close ~1 in 100 cold emails, the constraint is email throughput —
  so the priority action is to raise volume (here, 100 → ~5,000 emails/day), not to
  redesign the offer. (Alex, paraphrase)
- Scale cold email with automation, not rep labor: **warm up domains, run multiple
  sending domains/names (one per rep, plus backups), and add AI personalization on the
  front end**; emails can send from a rep's account without the rep doing the sending.
  (Alex, paraphrase)
- **The owner should build the email-automation system, not delegate it to sales reps** —
  "you can automate most of this stuff; you don't need them to do it." (Alex, paraphrase
  + quote)
- Hand email-inbound leads directly to closers via **round-robin, first-come-first-serve**
  (whoever grabs it first). (Alex, paraphrase)
- Even with a high close rate and handed leads, **closers should still do ~2 hours of
  their own outbound daily** — it keeps them sharp and keeps them appreciating the leads
  you send. (Alex, paraphrase + quote)
- A closer-heavy team (four closers, one setter) means the closers must "hunt their own
  food" — work their own leads — because there aren't enough setters to feed them. (Alex,
  paraphrase)
- Pipeline can be filled by installing a **human SDR team OR an AI SDR team**; AI dialers
  are now good enough to be near-indistinguishable from humans for the first few minutes
  of this type of call. (Alex, paraphrase)
- Owning a location outright vs. splitting with an entrepreneur is a service-logistics
  tradeoff: entrepreneurs are worth splitting with because they front the capital and do
  the local servicing, so you avoid hiring service staff in every distant market. (Alex,
  paraphrase, reasoning through the caller's model)
