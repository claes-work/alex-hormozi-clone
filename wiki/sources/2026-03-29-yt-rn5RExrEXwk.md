# Helping a $2.5M Real Estate Company Scale

## Summary
In this coaching exchange, Alex Hormozi advises Cameron, a Missouri residential real-estate flipper doing ~$15M in home-sale volume ($3.7M project gross, ~$2.5M EBITDA), who feels he has saturated the market within a 4-hour driving radius sourcing 95% of deals from foreclosures with no advertising. Hormozi frames the choice as "push vs. pivot" and steers strongly toward pushing: rather than replicating the model in a new city, he tells Cameron to add advertising (PPC, mailers, billboards) to his existing market and to study the national leader (e.g., We Buy Ugly Houses) as the "ultimate version" of the business to replicate and beat. The caller's business metrics and foreclosure-sourcing details are self-reported.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames scaling decisions as "push or pivot" and argues that expanding what already works (a "push") beats changing the entire business (a "pivot"), especially for an operator who has already paid down the "ignorance debt" of 8 years in the trade.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises studying the "ultimate version" / biggest player in your category as a blueprint: the person who built it is likely gone, leaving "suits who don't know how this business works," making it ripe to replicate and beat.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes competition as a positive signal: more competitors means more buyers and a bigger market, so long as you can become number one.
- As of March 2026, Alex tells the operator his single biggest immediate lever is advertising (PPC, mailers, billboards, social) — adding it to the current market could roughly double the business without changing anything else.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes national leaders likely out-earn per deal through vertical integration and back-end monetization, letting them spend more to acquire each deal and win across markets.
- As of March 2026, Alex counsels against "jumping back down the curve" — starting over in a new city forfeits accumulated skill and reliability; expanding a proven model is the "normal way of things."
- Caller-reported (unverified): Cameron's firm did ~$15M in 2025 home-sale volume, $3.7M project gross, ~$2.5M EBITDA; sources ~95% of deals from foreclosures with essentially no advertising; and made $2.7M during COVID when foreclosures were legally halted in Missouri.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Whenever people are like, 'Oh, there's a ton of competition in my market.' I'm like, 'That's awesome. It means there's tons of buyers.'"

> "You've already paid down like all the ignorance debt. So at this point you're like right here on your curve. So don't jump back down to like here again."

> "Whenever there's an ultimate version of the business that I'm currently in, it's like they already did it. And so now all I have to do is do it better than them. And whoever built that business is already gone, probably. And so it's just a bunch of suits who don't know how this business works. And so it's just ripe for us to just murder them."

> "Step one would be start advertising more, cuz then that'll just immediately make more within your current market. Like that might double your business if you just did that. And then number two is I would analyze the living hell out of them."

> "They probably vertically integrated as much as they possibly could within [the] business, that they actually make more on every deal than you do. So they can spend more on every deal."
