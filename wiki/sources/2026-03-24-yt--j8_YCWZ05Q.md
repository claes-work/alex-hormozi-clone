# How to Get Your Customers to Stay FOREVER

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out a 13-point retention playbook for any
recurring-revenue business, built largely from data and insights the Skool platform (and
Sam Ovens, whom Alex credits repeatedly) learned from running communities. Skool is cited
at 22,265,736 total users. The through-line: churn is a leaky bucket, most improvement
comes from getting members past day-90 and month-six thresholds, and the highest-leverage
fixes are usually subtraction (less overwhelm) plus daily owner presence — not adding
features.

## Key claims (his retention playbook)

1. **Treat churn like a leaky bucket; diagnose first.** MRR grows when new members > churned members. Track joins vs. cancels monthly and force-label the business growing / flat / declining.
2. **Use retention benchmarks** (for consumer/online/remote services, not home services or insurance): Skool platform average is ~80% monthly retention (20% churn). Best groups: <10% churn (90%+ retention). Weak groups: <70% retention (30%+ churn). If fewer than 7 of 10 members stay monthly, retention is the highest-ROI lever.
3. **Small churn numbers have outsized effects.** Cutting churn from 20% to 10% (80%→90% retention) doesn't add 10-12% — it doubles LTV per customer (they stay twice as long). The smaller the number, the bigger the percentage change from small tweaks.
4. **Churn drops dramatically with tenure — target thresholds, not the average.** Months 1-3 are highest churn (20%+). Past day 90 it roughly halves to ~10%. By month six it drops to ~2% (a 5x LTV effect). So reframe the goal from "make monthly churn go down" to "get people past day 90 / past month six." Don't overhaul the business over month-one churn — it's normally high (unless nearly everyone leaves). Businesses <90 days (or <6 months) old have inflated churn because no cohort has yet crossed the drop-off points.
5. **Three tactical levers for the thresholds:** Past month one = activation, onboarding, expectation-setting, selling the right front-end promises, showing up daily, providing content. Day 90 = deliver an outcome / behavior change. Month six = connect them with other people in the community.
6. **Ask people why they canceled and look for patterns.** Don't dismiss "it costs too much" as them not getting the value; don't knee-jerk on one response. Use a simple DM script, tag reasons in a spreadsheet (price, overwhelm, not using, missing feature, not what I expected), and for every ~20 cancels fix the top 1-2. (Sam seats the head of customer support next to him for a tight feedback loop between cancel reasons and what gets built.)
7. **Ask your best (longest-tenured, most-engaged) members why they stay**; whatever repeats becomes your retention engine — everything else is optional. Fix through subtraction/amelioration, not addition: fulfilling the original promise and removing friction usually retains people.
8. **Handle price objections with evidence-based tiering.** Skool ate its own dog food: added an 11x-cheaper $9 tier (vs. $99) for hobbyists/beginners while keeping the higher tier (extra admins, unique URLs, no competitor sidebars) for high-level users. Rule: if your *ideal* customers say price is the issue, add a lower/standard tier; if *non-ideal* people say it's too expensive, ignore it — price is working as a filter.
9. **Overwhelm is the #1 churn driver outside price** ("I don't have time / can't keep up / can't utilize it" = too much stuff). Doing less retains more. Planet Fitness example: surveys showed people used only ~10-15% of a full health club, so PF stripped it to cardio + light weights and charged far less for a superior value proposition. When you pay for 5 things but use 1, you feel you get 20% of the value; offer just that 1 thing and it feels like 100%. One Skool community deleted content and cut churn from 30% to 5% month-over-month (6x LTV).
10. **Find the vital features by survey:** ask "if I deleted everything, what one thing would you fight for?" and "if I kept everything but removed one feature, which would you not mind losing?" Chart the answers to reveal the top 1-2; usually do just those. Think **value per second, not seconds of value** — one post, one call recording, one valuable weekly action can be enough.
11. **Design for the extremes.** Give busy "dabblers" an explicit start-here / weekly-cadence flow so they get value from a distance and don't fall behind; leave optional side quests and depth for power users.
12. **Move the ladder closer to the front (snakes & ladders).** Remove snakes (friction, confusion, overwhelm); front-load the sticky "ladder" (best call, key implementation, pin post) into onboarding. Pin a fast win so new members hit value ideally within the first 24 hours.
13. **Show up daily and care.** Owners active daily had significantly higher retention across communities — "basically never seen this fail." Minimum: set an alarm, make it routine (clear notifications/DMs, handle reported content, read complaints every morning). Reinforce good behavior (praise reviews/testimonials); "delete bad stuff and delete bad people" — deleting cancer from a community is powerful.
14. **Run a weekly call + recording.** Live voice/face deepens relationships; recordings let busy people keep up. Default cadence: one big thing per week plus a "best of" post (e.g., weekly call + weekly post). Weekly (TV-show style) is enough for people to keep up.
15. **Add an annual plan with a discount.** Annual churns less: one decision instead of 12, can't-cancel effect, more committed and more qualified (paid more up front). Common discount is ~16-17% (buy 10 get 2). Implement immediately; it stabilizes retention.
16. **Build belonging via one-on-one relationships and ~10 true regulars.** Leila's shared stat: an employee with one friend outside work stays 5x longer — transfers to communities, so help members make one real friend. Anchor the community with 10 regulars/model citizens: DM them, learn their goals, spotlight/publicly recognize them, invite them to contribute, and matchmake new members to them (like introducing a newcomer at a party).
17. **Don't implement everything at once** — take one item, make it consistent, then add the next. Retention = doing 100 small things consistently over a long time. Growth isn't a hockey stick; it's a flat retention curve that then compounds upward.
