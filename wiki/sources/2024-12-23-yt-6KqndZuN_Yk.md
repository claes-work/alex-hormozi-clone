# If You Want 2026 to Be the Best Year of Your Life, Please Watch This Video

## Summary

A long-form solo year-end motivational monologue (published 2024-12-23, framed around
2025) in which Alex Hormozi lays out a four-step self-improvement framework: **eliminate
distractions, get started, get better, never stop.** He argues that priorities are
revealed by where you spend time and money (not what you say), that ambition requires
cutting bad friends and their opinions, that skills bought with time and money are the
highest-return investment, and that persistence through failure — not talent — is what
compounds into success. Heavily autobiographical: his father's immigrant doctor story,
his own gym/Gym Launch arc, losing everything twice, and his "spend all your savings on
education" philosophy anchor the argument.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed)

- **Four steps to win**: eliminate distractions → get started → get better → never stop.
- Where you invest your money and calendar is what you get more of; **you vote with your
  dollars and time** about the person you want to become. Priorities are dictated by what
  you spend, not what you say.
- His father's framing: **"Rich people buy time. Poor people buy stuff. Ambitious people
  buy skills. Lazy people buy distraction."**
- **Commitment = the elimination of alternatives.** To get someone to commit, ask them to
  eliminate everything that isn't the main thing.
- **Bad friends** are the first thing to cut. "Better friends are busy friends." Offers a
  5-question litmus test for keeping/cutting a friend, plus his two filters (same *scale*
  of goals; same level of dedication).
- To be **exceptional is to be the exception** — it's normal for normal people to find you
  abnormal. Ambitious people go through lonely seasons transitioning between friend groups.
- **Delayed gratification is a function of intelligence**; expanding your reinforcement
  window increases your rate of learning.
- Your reference group isn't the 5 people you spend time with — it's the **5 people you
  compare yourself to / want to impress**, whose voices you hear on big decisions. Listen
  to people closest to your *goals*, not people closest to *you*. These can be heroes you
  only follow on the internet.
- **"Winners focus on winning. Losers focus on winners."** You never receive hate from
  above, only below.
- How far you go is predicated on **your standard**. Borrow a better measuring stick — ask
  what Elon / Bezos / Jobs would accept. Standard is what rich kids inherit from rich
  parents.
- **Environment drives efficiency**; you must be able to sit still, ignore notifications,
  and focus for 8 hours or you'll never build anything great. Focus is created by
  *removing everything else* (Jerry Seinfeld's empty-room writing method).
- Phone tactics: **grayscale (~30% less consumption, research-backed), turn off all
  notifications, block all callers** (real emergencies call 911), and eliminate whole
  channels (he doesn't use email, citing Bill Clinton).
- **"It's always harder, takes longer, and costs more than you think"** — that's a
  reflection of poor expectations, not bad reality. Difficulty is proportional to reward
  because *you are the asset you build*.
- **Entitlement** (expecting reward without earning it) is the worst expectation. "Hard
  times create strong men..." cycle. Beat weak competitors just by showing up on time,
  preparing, following up, and trying like you mean it.
- Success is like **running a marathon, not watching one** — you only see people at the
  finish line; you must run alone not knowing where the finish is. Pacing matters (micro
  speed, macro patience).
- **Identity is a lie / "isms" (I'm not good at math, I'm not techy") don't serve you.**
  His razor for every decision: does this make it more or less likely I hit my goals? He
  personally overcame "I'm bad at math" in his 20s. "Doing is being."
- **Getting started is 100% under your control.** "It's not about the resources you have,
  it's about how resourceful you're willing to be." Every self-made person — billionaire,
  millionaire, or in poverty — is self-made.
- **"If you want to feel better, blame others. If you want to get better, blame yourself."**
- **Failure raises your probability of success**, not lowers it — failure requires action,
  action yields feedback, feedback makes you better. "Everyone falls the first time."
  Second attempts take twice the emotional effort but half the actual effort (unknown
  optimism → known pessimism).
- Cites the **Jeff Bezos "swing for the fences" quote** that opens *$100M Offers* —
  business has a long-tail (up to 1,000 runs) outcome distribution, so be bold; big winners
  pay for experiments in failures.
- **Bet on yourself** ("SM500" not S&P 500) — you'll always beat a 10% market return, and
  every skill compounds and stacks (Steve Jobs / calligraphy → Mac fonts).
- **Education is the highest-return investment.** The "$950,000 whiteboard" close: what you
  don't earn is your ignorance tax; buy skills to "pull the future forward at a discount."
  Spent $10K (of $50K life savings, age 23) to join a gym-owner mastermind *before owning a
  gym*, reaching profitability by month 9 vs. peers' 5 years.
- **Move geographically** to get near people better than you (moved to SoCal for fitness).
- **"Spend all your money on education for as long as you can, until you can't even spend
  it because you're making too much."** Learn to make money while awake before while asleep;
  passive income is premature when you're starting.
- Become an expert by doing **more repetitions in a narrow field than anyone** — raw effort
  *plus* an improvement loop ("post one, see what worked, do more of that"). Try 10x harder
  to be sure you win. Work = output.
- **Skills are the one thing no one can take from you** — no divorce, no government (his
  family's assets were seized in the revolution; his father came with $1,000).
- **Staying focused is like beating addiction** — a daily fight, because the distraction
  morphs. Measure focus by the quality/quantity of what you say no to; opportunities to
  reject only grow as your skills grow.
- Persistence: hard never lasts forever — **you quit, it gets easier, or you get better**;
  it always ends, and you only lose by quitting. Declarative vs. procedural knowledge —
  watching 10 videos ≠ knowing how; you learn from your first 100 cold calls.
- **"When it's easy, do more. When it's hard, do different."** Story of an 8-figure
  entrepreneur telling him to double down when Gym Launch started working (someone bigger
  will copy you).
- **Work volume**: challenges viewers to work 30 days straight (10–12 hrs); references
  China's **996**; took ~10–11 days off in 2024; dismisses "hoodoo mental health" pushback;
  he gets more fulfillment from a hard day's work ("earning my shower") than from pleasure.
  Left a luxury vacation after one of two days — "this is beautiful and I don't care."
- **"If you don't know why you believe what you believe, it's not your belief, it's
  someone else's."** Goal is net output over the longest period — rest is allowed, sleep as
  much as you can.
- **Obsession with speed** ("Pac-Man of skills"): reject arbitrary timelines, question
  timelines aggressively, use real deadlines based on how long things should take, then ask
  how to make them take less.
- **"Goals aren't finish lines, they're mile markers"** (Haitian proverb: "behind mountains
  are more mountains"). You did it for the climb, not the mountaintop.
- **"The lazy don't know how to start, the losers don't know how to finish, and the legends
  don't know how to stop."** Says he doesn't believe in legacy or an afterlife.
- Closes with **13 rules of wisdom** he gave a 90-day Skool-community sprint group,
  including the **Rule of 100** (100 reachouts / 100-min post / $100+ ads / 100 min on ads
  daily), "your work works on you more than you work on it," "if you complain you are dead
  to me," and "business is shockingly simple but surprisingly hard."
