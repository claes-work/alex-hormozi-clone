# Helping a Concrete Business Owner Fix His Margins

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a founder split between a family concrete business and a faster-growing concrete-contractor marketing agency. The concrete company has about $4M revenue but only about 10% margin, making it hard to hire an operator and free the founder. Alex diagnoses the company as stuck in the "swamp": enough profit to survive, not enough to easily hire leadership. He recommends fixing the sales motion first, then raising price, especially by ending emailed quotes and closing in person at the site visit.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - concrete business / concrete marketing agency owner. His revenue split, family ownership, margins, bidding process, and close rate are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A low-margin $1M-$4M service business can get stuck in a "swamp" where the next leadership hire consumes a huge share of profit.
- Industry average should be erased as an acceptable benchmark.
- Residential concrete likely has more pricing power than the owner assumes.
- The company should move from cost-plus thinking toward value-based pricing.
- The sweet spot in pricing is not where close rate is highest; it is where profit is highest.
- A 10% price increase can double profit in a low-margin business.
- A 10% close rate reveals a sales-motion issue before a pricing issue.
- The company should stop emailing quotes.
- The set call should qualify, give a wide range, position against phone quotes, and set the expectation that the site visit ends with quote and deposit.
- In-person closing should dramatically outperform emailed quotes.
- After fixing the motion, the owner can add about 20% to the normal pricing math and consider incentives for sales reps.
