# The 4 Stages of Entrepreneurship (2021)

## Summary

In this 2021 talk, Alex Hormozi lays out a four-stage model of business growth,
segmenting a company's journey by annual revenue and describing the dominant bottleneck
at each stage. He presents it as a hard-won map — explicitly noting he had "painstakingly"
figured it out — and candidly admits that at the time of recording his own company was
stuck at the last transition, having done roughly $28M / $37M / $32M over the prior three
years. The framework moves from mastering acquisition, to expanding lifetime value, to
professionalizing operations, and finally to distributing entrepreneurial drive across
"intrapreneurs" running chartered profit centers.

## Key claims

- (2021) Business growth breaks into four revenue-defined stages, each with a distinct
  bottleneck; ranges are rough/approximate.
- (2021) Stage 1 ($0–$1M) is simply "one product on one channel" — building a reliable,
  repeatable system for acquiring customers. Most people who stall here either haven't
  nailed a reliable acquisition system or sabotage themselves with interpersonal /
  personal problems.
- (2021) A channel = a specific acquisition source (cold email, Facebook ads, Instagram
  ads, YouTube ads, referral partners, organic/earned media).
- (2021) Stage 2 ($1M–$10M) is multi-products via cross-sells and upsells — building a
  back end / value ladder — NOT starting multiple businesses. You sell the same number of
  units but multiply LTV (e.g. 5x a $1K LTV to $5K), which then funds expansion to new
  channels.
- (2021) Stage 3 ($10M–$30M) is professionalizing the business: HR, legal, accounting,
  and IT systems — strengthening operational infrastructure. At this range you "become a
  target" for lawsuits, so structure and compliance become necessary. Risk: the corporate
  hires who professionalize often stifle innovation and drain the creative energy.
- (2021) Stage 4 ($30M–$100M+) requires distributing entrepreneurial drive — the founder's
  vision gets "diluted" across a large org, so you need more "souls" who can drive growth.
- (2021) Do NOT hire entrepreneurs (who want to own their own thing); hire INTRApreneurs —
  people who love driving growth/change/innovation but lack the appetite for risk and
  prefer a safe place with a (variable) paycheck. Hormozi says he learned "don't hire
  entrepreneurs" the hard way multiple times.
- (2021) At scale the structure becomes a central operations core (HR, legal, IT, finance)
  with profit-center spokes; each profit center contains its own product + acquisition
  (marketing/sales). He likens the sub-leaders to Bezos's "CEOs" of businesses within
  Amazon.
- (2021) New product lines should reuse existing resources/skills/infrastructure to make
  "similar but different" offerings. Example: his gym-coaching business added a "boiler
  room" sales-training upsell using the team it already had — high margin, sticky, clear ROI.
- (2021) He cites starting the supplement company as a probable mistake in ordering — a huge
  undertaking he "probably shouldn't have done in that order."
- (2021) Business growth is slow because it's all about people; a new product division needs
  a true leader "as smart as you are." Belief he uses: a perfect leader/manager could turn
  everyone into an A-player, but being imperfect, he must hire people with "a little bit of
  battery included."

## The 4 stages

Exactly as Hormozi names them (by revenue band and dominant task):

1. **$0 to $1M/year** — "one product on one channel" (a reliable system for acquiring customers)
2. **$1M to $10M/year** — "multi-products" via cross-sells and upsells (building the back end / value ladder to expand LTV)
3. **$10M to $30M/year** — "professionalizing the business" (HR, legal, accounting, then IT systems — operational infrastructure)
4. **$30M to $100M+/year** — distributing entrepreneurial drive through "intrapreneurs" running chartered profit centers around a central ops core

## Later status / supersession

> ⚠️ NOTE (2021 framing): This revenue-banded "4 stages" model is early Hormozi. In his
> later Acquisition.com era he foregrounds different scaffolding — the "$100M" trilogy
> (Offers / Leads / Money Models) and constraint-based diagnostics rather than fixed
> revenue-band stages. The underlying mechanics here nonetheless persist in his later work:
> "one product, one channel" until it's repeatable, LTV expansion via back-end/value ladder,
> and the entrepreneur-vs-intrapreneur hiring distinction. Whether this named four-stage
> model is explicitly retained, revised, or dropped in later sources is not yet confirmed
> in this wiki — flag for a later synthesis pass.
