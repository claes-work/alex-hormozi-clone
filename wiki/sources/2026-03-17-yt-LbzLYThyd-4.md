# my thoughts on meditation...

## Summary
A candid two-person conversation (Alex plus an unnamed friend/interlocutor) in which Alex thinks out loud about seriously taking up meditation as a disciplined practice — "a snapshot of where I'm currently at right now." His trigger is the research literature: he cites Buddhist monks with 40,000+ hours of meditation scoring roughly **three to four standard deviations above the norm on subjective well-being** (he glosses one SD as moving you from ~50th to ~84th percentile, two SD to ~99th), a state described as "a constant state of bliss." He reasons that ~a year at an hour a day yields the first standard deviation, and that replicating the monks' outcomes takes on the order of four hours a day — so, being a maximizer, he's contemplating going hard (he floated four hours a day). What "troubles" him is that when he asks whether devoting his available time to it would be worth it, the answer is **yes**. The appeal is partly that it fits his worldview: **that level of happiness takes work**, just like everything else he's built. He notes he has spent his life changing his *external* conditions — "I literally live in a fortress of my own making" (the gym, his physique, marrying who he wanted, the business) — "and yet this guy who has a chair in the middle of Tibet" is beating him on the one metric that matters. He also frames himself as a likely "hyperresponder" / newbie-gains case because he's so bad at it. The clip carries a strong biographical thread: at ~19–20 (16 years earlier), during the existential-crisis window when people form their worldview, he seriously debated leaving everything to pursue the meditation-monk path — and instead swore off the happiness question ("I'm not going to even think about this anymore") and made his life goal to **just be useful**. He judges the monk who sits happy in a hut for eight hours a day as, ultimately, "a waste," because he believes we have a *duty* to be useful to one another (flagged explicitly as a "should"). His summary of his own life so far: **his desire to be useful has been greater than his desire to be happy** — but if there's now a world where he can have both, he's open to it. The friend reframes the whole thing as the classic trap of sacrificing happiness to chase success, and asks whether it still makes sense to fill an internal void with external goals. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
