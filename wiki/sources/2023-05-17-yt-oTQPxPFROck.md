# How to Build a LEGIT Online Course (Works in 2026)

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video contrasting a "scam" education business (e.g. a get-rich-quick course promising fast income to anyone with a credit card) against a legitimate one, using Harvard as the model of a 100+ year old education business that isn't called a scam. He distills the difference into four pillars — screen customers, set data-based expectations, give away content and sell implementation, and don't graduate/endorse everyone — and works them through a hypothetical sales school example. The framing is that "legitimacy" in education is a set of design choices, and those same choices build a durable brand and pricing power.

## Key claims (the course-design method)

- There are multiple "levels of legitimacy" for education; both Harvard and a five-second get-rich ATM course "sell education," yet one scores zero on a scam index and the other maxes out. The difference is only four things.
- **Pillar 1 — Screen customers / sell to qualified leads only.** Harvard turns people down (he claims he applied 10 times and was rejected). If the only requirement to become your customer is "a credit card and a pulse," that is a red flag. Define a qualified lead as someone with the highest likelihood of success, and set screening metrics for who is *not* a good fit — unqualified buyers are the ones who get upset, leave bad reviews, and call you a scam.
- **Pillar 2 — Set expectations from data, not income promises.** Harvard gives no income expectations and no "make money tomorrow" claim; there is a delay between starting and graduating. Track customer success metrics: of people who buy, what percent succeed at 30/60/90 days and 12 months, and the averages/medians. If you track the data, you can report it without sensationalism. Publish medians, top-20% outcomes, and where graduates go — real data doesn't set false expectations. Harvard's highlighted value is the experience, network, relationships, and character development, not income.
- **Pillar 3 — Give away the secrets, sell the implementation.** Monetizing education is fine, but the smart move is to give away for free all the content you would normally sell as a course, and sell implementation instead. Example: if you sell the ability to sell, give the whole "course" away as content across platforms, and charge for listening to the buyer's live sales calls, giving real-time feedback, and monitoring their performance until they become a salesperson.
- **Pillar 4 — Don't graduate/endorse everyone.** Harvard doesn't graduate everyone; some fail, which protects the brand. Build in the possibility that someone pays and does *not* get your endorsement. That is how you maintain the brand long term. In the sales-school example: reject ~90% of applicants because you can tell in an interview who lacks a proclivity for sales; select people naturally good at sales, cut those who aren't, and rank-order graduates on who succeeded best.
- **Demand > supply keeps prices high.** Generate more (and better) free content than everyone else's paid content; this maintains thought leadership. The market reasons: if your free stuff beats their free stuff, your paid stuff must beat their paid stuff.
- **It takes time / small cohorts compound.** Building a "big ship" takes time — even a year of content might produce only ~25 graduates, but if they're all "killers," the companies that hire them create more demand on both sides, and those graduates' friends want in. A graduate earning ~$300k (vs. an industry-average sales rep at ~$60k) makes a one-year education highly valuable; since Harvard charges ~$250k for four years, a data-backed one-year program can charge a lot.
