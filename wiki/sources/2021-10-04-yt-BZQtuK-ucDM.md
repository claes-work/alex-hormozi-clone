# NEVER lower your prices (2021-10-04)

## Summary

Hormozi contrasts two of his own pricing decisions to argue that lowering prices almost always makes you less money. In one, he tripled his gym boot-camp price from $99 to $299/month and doubled revenue while serving fewer clients; in the other, he added services and *cut* a $42,000/year program's price to ~$31,000 to reduce churn — churn stayed flat and he lost ~$5M/year in profit. His conclusion: raise price and value to serve fewer people at higher prices rather than more people at lower prices.

## Key claims

- (2021) Rule: 99 times out of 100, lowering your price makes you *less* money — the price cut rarely produces the volume increase needed to compensate.
- (2021) The never-lower-prices argument, in his math: at a $100 price and $40 profit, cutting to $80 halves your profit, so you'd need to *2x* unit sales just to break even — which a price cut almost never delivers. Conversely, raising to $150 ($90 profit) more than 2x's per-unit profit, and you might only lose a quarter of your customers, netting more money overall.
- (2021, self-reported) Boot-camp story: he tripled price from $99 to $299/month (adding a better semi-private program, two free weeks up front). One-third of customers left; two-thirds stayed at 3x the price — so revenue 2x'd while fulfillment cost *fell* (serving fewer clients), more than doubling profit.
- (2021, self-reported) Price-cut story: to lower churn on a $42,000/year (~$800/week) program, he added ~$100K/month of overhead and cut price ~25% to ~$31,000 (~$600/week). Churn stayed unchanged; he raised cost ~$1M and cut topline ~$400K/month, losing ~$5M/year in profit. On a ~70% margin business the 25% topline cut wiped out roughly a third of income.
- (2021) The trade he *hoped* for (cut price 25%, halve churn from 10% to 5% to 2x LTV) would have been worth taking — but the churn improvement never materialized.
- (2021) LTV can be increased six ways: (1) raise price, (2) cut costs, (3) resells — buy more times, (4) upsells — buy a bigger version, (5) cross-sells — buy different things, (6) downsells — get non-buyers to buy something. His mistake was chasing volume via a price cut instead of these levers.
- (2021) Selling something you genuinely believe in makes you more money; his conviction that semi-private beat boot camp for real body transformation drove the price increase.
- (2021, self-reported) At time of recording his acquisition.com portfolio did $85M/year.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I hate lowering prices. I don't like lowering prices. It just generally doesn't mean you make more money."

> "One decision, $5 million a year I lost as a result of this... most times when you lower your prices, you make a lot less money."

> "Two-thirds of them stayed at three times the price... I 2x'd the revenue of my business and I serviced only 2/3 of my customers, which means my cost of fulfillment went down and my revenue went up."

> "In most cases, providing more value and charging a higher price... will make you more money because you will service fewer people at higher prices."

> "99 times out of 100 when you lower the price you make less money. Sometimes you make more, but the vast majority of the time you make less."

> "Small tweaks, big changes, lots of impact."
