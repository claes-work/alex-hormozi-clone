# My Framework for Regret Minimization

## Summary
In a live coaching exchange, a caller who runs profitable sports facilities (~$3.5M/year in Summerlin, ~$1M in California) asks whether he should relaunch a professional Las Vegas soccer team that once failed after four years and nearly cost him his marriage. Alex reframes it as a life question rather than a business question, and lays out his approach to deciding how much risk is right — arguing the true risk is not the money but splitting focus and losing the good business already working. He recommends a regret-minimization lens and a commit-or-don't posture rather than a half-hearted attempt.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames certain choices as "eternal questions" for entrepreneurs that have no universally right answer because they are 100% personal: how much risk is right, how much to invest versus consume today, and how long to push before pivoting.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues the real risk in the caller's relaunch is not using own-money vs. other-people's-money, but splitting focus and thereby likely losing the existing, comfortable, working business.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises that if you pursue a high-risk venture you should go "all in" and accept forgoing a comfortable lifestyle, because a split, half-committed attempt is likely to fail at the new venture and drag down the existing one.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes his regret-minimization framework: imagine 85-year-old you looking back and asking whether you'd be okay having torpedoed the working business for a shot — and decide by whether you can accept the worst-case scenario, not the fantasy best case.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes that swinging for a low-odds bet can look dumb at the time yet prove right (citing Elon Musk reinvesting after his ~$300M+ exit into multiple ~5%-odds businesses), while others doing the same lose everything — reinforcing that the "right" risk level is personal.
- Caller-reported (unverified): he came from Israel ~25 years ago, is a former soccer player with no prior business background, ran the Las Vegas Legends (first pro team in Vegas before the Raiders/Golden Knights), failed after four years, and his wife gave a "team or divorce" ultimatum; he has been married 28 years.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "So, you don't have a business question, you have a life question."

> "There are a couple of eternal questions for humans or entrepreneurs that there is no right answer for. Like how much risk is the right amount of risk? How much should I invest versus how much should I consume today? How long should I push to make something work before I pivot?"

> "I like the regret minimization framework of looking at 85-year-old me coming back to the present and saying like, 'Would I be okay with failing at this business and torpedoing the one that was working so that I could say I had a shot?'"

> "We have to look at what's the worst-case scenario and am I okay with that worst-case scenario? If I'm okay with the worst-case scenario, then I'm going to take the swing. If I'm not okay, then I just say that I'm not going to take it."

> "The risk is that you already have something that's good and you're going to probably lose it. Just cuz you're going to be split."

> "If you were to do it, I would say go all in if you really want to make it work, and just accept the fact that like I'm forgoing what I consider to be a really comfortable lifestyle because I really want to make this work."
