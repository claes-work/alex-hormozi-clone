# How to Raise Prices Based on Close Rate

## Summary
Solo talk. Alex lays out a "tier ladder" rule-of-thumb mapping a salesperson's **close rate** to how far a business is **underpriced** — aimed specifically at businesses that sell with a human closer (in person, phone, or Zoom). The logic rests on the supply/demand curve: lower price → higher demand → higher close rate, so an unusually high close rate is a signal the price is too low. His bands: closing **80%+** ("four out of five buy") means you're underpriced by **3–4x**; **60–80%** means **2–3x** underpriced; **50–60%** means **1.5–2x**; **40–50%** means **1.25–1.5x**; and **30–40%** (his "I'm at 35%" example) is **appropriately priced** — but only under the assumption you have a proper sales motion where the prospect has already consumed educational material *before* the call, so the close call is about personalization and helping them decide rather than a pitch or spiel. If someone hits ~35% *without* that machinery in place, they still likely have a 2–3x price increase available once a proper sales motion is built. Below **30%** (fewer than one in three buy) he does **not** cut price — he diagnoses an **avatar problem** (wrong buyer) or a **sales-motion problem** first, since dropping price is the reflexive move of a fearful entrepreneur or a bad sales culture. He frames raising prices as almost always the correct direction, with **one exception: infinitely scalable businesses like SaaS** (~5% of his audience), where price trades off gross margin against growth rate and both must be optimized. For the ~80% of businesses that are **unscalable/service-based**, the only direction is **up**: as you get good, demand exceeds capacity, you can't serve everyone, so you raise price — a virtuous cycle of rising margins, reputation, and talent, versus the vicious cycle of serving more people for less. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues close rate and price are linked via the supply/demand curve: lowering price raises demand (and thus close rate), so a high close rate signals underpricing — a rule of thumb applies specifically to businesses that sell via a human salesperson (in person, phone, or Zoom).
- As of March 2026, closing **80% or more** (four of five buy) typically means you are underpriced by **3–4x**.
- As of March 2026, closing **60–80%** typically means you are underpriced by **2–3x** (e.g. charging $100 could go to $200, possibly $250–$300).
- As of March 2026, closing **50–60%** typically means you are underpriced by **1.5–2x** ($100 → ~$150–$200) — the multiplier jumps compress as close rate falls.
- As of March 2026, closing **40–50%** typically means you are underpriced by **1.25–1.5x** ($100 → ~$125–$150).
- As of March 2026, closing **30–40%** (his "35%" example) is **appropriately priced** — but only if a proper sales motion exists where the buyer consumed educational content before the call, making the close about personalization, not a pitch.
- As of March 2026, if you close ~35% *without* that pre-education sales motion, you still likely have a **2–3x** price increase available once you build a proper sales motion.
- As of March 2026, closing **below 30%** (fewer than one in three) indicates an avatar problem (wrong buyer) or a sales-motion problem — Alex fixes those before ever considering lowering price, which he calls the reflex of a fearful entrepreneur or a bad sales-team culture.
- As of March 2026, raising prices is almost always the correct direction, with one exception: infinitely scalable businesses (e.g. SaaS, ~5% of his audience), where price balances gross margin against growth rate and both must be maximized.
- As of March 2026, ~80% of businesses are unscalable/service-based, and for them the only price direction is up — as quality drives demand past capacity, raising price creates a virtuous cycle of higher margins, better reputation, and better talent, versus a vicious cycle of serving more for less.
