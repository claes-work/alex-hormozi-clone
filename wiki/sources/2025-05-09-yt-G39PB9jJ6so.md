# Why It Is Lonely At the Top - Alex Hormozi

## Summary

A clip (reposted by @TheSkoolSchool) from a podcast conversation between Alex Hormozi and a host who references his "Modern Wisdom" audience and listener DMs (i.e. Chris Williamson). The two riff on "fame's seesaw": on the way up people root for you because you remind them of their dreams; at the top they tear you down because you remind them of the dreams they gave up on. Alex grounds the idea in his own gym-owner years and a coach's line that hate is a requisite for success, and both frame the pushback you get when self-improving as a *leading* indicator that you are on the right path.

## Key claims

- "Fame's seesaw": on the way up everyone roots for you (you remind them of their dreams); at the top everyone tears you down (you remind them they gave up on theirs).
- The challenge of being at the top attracts almost no sympathy — "the total addressable market for sympathy is basically zero" because almost everyone is on their way up and being at the top reads as privilege that "legitimates" others twisting the knife.
- Online, public figures get dehumanized because attackers see a "representation" / "menagerie of ideas," not a real person on the other side.
- Alex's lesson from his gym days: "people want you to do well, but not better than them." The moment you cross from underdog to "the man," the same supporters flip — and how fast depends on the person (a lower-status employee flipped quickly; more successful people took longer; only members doing far better than him kept rooting).
- A performance coach's framing Alex adopted: hatred isn't something to avoid, it's a *requisite* for success — "if you get no hate you are not successful" — so hate becomes a leading indicator you're on the right path.
- "No one hates you from above" — the distaste comes from people whose (smaller) mountain you've now topped; it's projection of what they know they should be doing, and has nothing to do with you.
- Nothing worthwhile comes without associated discomfort; the pushback from old friends ("too cool for us now," "not drinking tonight") is the *lead* (not lagging) indicator you're doing the right thing.
- The "lonely chapter": you no longer fit your old friends but don't yet have outcomes to fit a new group — a universal stage for anyone who does something with their life.
- Once you posit an ideal, you begin comparing yourself to it, and the potential-gap becomes stark; knowledge removes the excuse of ignorance ("every single night of bad sleep is on you").
- Every worthwhile goal is worthwhile *because* it has a cost; if it were immediately available it would be available to everyone and therefore not worthwhile — "the very fact that it's difficult is why it is worth doing."

## Biographical facts (dated) (self-reported)

- **College:** Alex says he got a writing scholarship in college, was vice president of the newspaper, and editor of the creative writing magazine — "I've been writing for a long time. I enjoy writing." (self-reported)
- **Gym-owner years:** He wrote an essay he recalls titling roughly "everyone believes in the American dream until it comes true." (self-reported)
- Recounts sleeping on the gym floor as the underdog — clients could see his blanket and pillow in the corner and knew he lived there; he had no shower and went to the YMCA to shower. (self-reported; consistent with other wiki sources on his early gym period)
- Within about **nine months** he had hired people and a manager; when he'd walk into the lobby the same clients who had rooted for him flipped to "boss man's here — you're not too good for us now." (self-reported)
- Frames this "underdog to the man" shift as happening when he was **23** — "the first time I went from underdog to the person that someone would want to attack." (self-reported)
- **Middle school:** says he had bullies but also advantages he couldn't recognize at the time — he had advanced a grade in multiple subjects, had straight A's, worked hard, and was in shape. (self-reported)
- A **performance coach** told him hatred is a requisite for success — a framing that stuck with him. (self-reported)
