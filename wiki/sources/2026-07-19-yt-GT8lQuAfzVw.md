# 'How Much Should I Actually Charge?'

## Summary

Alex Hormozi answers a coaching question on how much a business should charge, especially when currently stuck at commodity pricing. He frames pricing as partly speculative — like a house with no comparables, worth whatever a buyer will pay — and lays out the wrong ways to price (average the competition; undercut the average; cost-plus markup) before defining the "perfect price" as the maximum a customer is willing and able to spend. He stresses that willingness to pay changes three ways: within a single customer over time (diminishing marginal value, illustrated by beers after skiing), across the changing nature of a deliverable (people buy for one reason but stay for another), and between customers (rich vs. poor buyer facing the same offer).

From these dynamics he derives operational tactics: front-load pricing with onboarding fees and upfront charges because early pain (and deprivation) is highest — the breakthrough that reshaped the large-group gym-training space when he flipped from selling free trials to charging $500–$5,000 on the first transaction. He argues the profit objective is units sold times gross margin, which forms a curve where two different price points yield the same total profit, and makes the case for being a premium price leader in human-delivered service businesses (same money from 10 customers as 100, but the 10-customer business is far easier to scale).

He then connects pricing to wealth distribution via his "51-1 rule" video on the fractal nature of customers: of $100 of total wealth spread across America, the bottom 50% holds roughly $2.70 while the top 1% holds ~30x that — so tiered offers should reach up and down the wealth spectrum to maximize revenue from an existing audience. His central pricing mistake diagnosis: people don't go high enough or low enough, and they raise tiers from their own wallet (e.g. $129 → $149/month) rather than pricing to a genuinely different buyer. His rule of thumb: design each tier to roughly double core revenue, which — given typical upsell uptake of 20–30% — means pricing the next tier around 5x the base and then building something worth that price. Closes with a promotional offer for the free $100M scaling roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) Pricing is partly speculative: with no comparables, an item is worth exactly what someone else is willing to pay, which can vary enormously (e.g. a remote house a billionaire fixates on).
- (2026-07-19) A hard-cost baseline for such an item is what it would cost the buyer to reproduce it themselves (buy the adjacent land, build the house); the premium on top is the value of the time saved by not doing it.
- (2026-07-19) Alex's most-used method is the "back of the napkin" way rather than formal pricing theory, though entire books on pricing exist.
- (2026-07-19) Wrong way #1: look at what everyone else charges and take the average — this guarantees you become a commodity in a commoditized marketplace.
- (2026-07-19) Wrong way #2: charge slightly less for slightly more than the average — even worse.
- (2026-07-19) Wrong way #3: cost-plus pricing (e.g. $10 cost, fixed 50% markup, charge $15) — better because you've at least considered margin, but flawed because it's about you, not the customer's willingness to pay.
- (2026-07-19) The perfect price is one a customer will buy that is the maximum they are able to spend.
- (2026-07-19) Willingness to pay changes three ways: within a customer's purchasing history in the moment, across the changing nature of the deliverable, and between customers.
- (2026-07-19) Value diminishes within a single customer's consumption (the first post-skiing beer is worth far more than the second or third; eventually they won't take another at any price).
- (2026-07-19) Pricing math example: if the first beer is worth $7, second $3, third $1, pricing at $1 sells 3 beers for $3 total; pricing at $3 sells to the $3-and-up buyers for ~$6 total; pricing at $7 sells only 1 beer for $7 — so the total-revenue-maximizing price sits between the extremes, not at the top willingness-to-pay.
- (2026-07-19) Because value is highest early, Alex advocates non-linear pricing where affordable: onboarding fees and upfront charges, since early on the customer has the most pain and is the most deprived of the promised outcome.
- (2026-07-19) Service businesses often must front-load the work, so onboarding charges make sense — they offset customer-acquisition cost while matching the customer's level of deprivation.
- (2026-07-19) Gym-industry breakthrough: instead of selling a free trial ("a taste") to a hard-won lead, Alex flipped the model to charge $500 / $2,000 / $5,000 on the first transaction, then a cheaper ongoing price — because the day someone joins is the maximum-pain, maximum-motivation moment where he can charge most.
- (2026-07-19) The reason people buy is not always the reason they stay; the deliverable's nature changes, so price to the relative value of each specific moment (e.g. joined to lose weight, stayed for friends).
- (2026-07-19) By willingness to pay, losing weight is worth more than making friends — stated purely on what people pay for, not on life values — implying separate "weight-loss price" and "community price" that reconcile.
- (2026-07-19) Between customers, the same $2,000 offer is trivial (even too cheap to care about) for a rich buyer and impossibly expensive for a poor buyer, even though the core service is identical — so the poor buyer simply isn't the right fit.
- (2026-07-19) The pricing objective / formula: number of units sold × gross margin gives the highest absolute money made in a marketplace.
- (2026-07-19) That relationship is a curve: two points on either side yield the same total profit — many units at low price, or fewer units at high price — so it's a zone, not a single correct answer, and it changes as the market changes.
- (2026-07-19) For most human-delivered service businesses, Alex argues strongly for being a premium price leader: if 10 customers make the same money as 100, the 10-customer business is far easier to scale, and once you can service 100 you're already 10x bigger in absolute profit with more ad spend capacity.
- (2026-07-19) You'll never have a perfect price unless you have a pure real-time marketplace function like Uber's surge pricing; otherwise pricing is always somewhat inefficient.
- (2026-07-19) The solve for inefficient pricing is tiers of service that signal to a specific customer type that "this is for them."
- (2026-07-19) Alex's favorite video of the year, the "51-1 rule" (on his channel), covers the fractal nature of customers.
- (2026-07-19) Wealth-distribution claim: of $100 of total US wealth across 100 circles, the bottom 50% holds ~$2.70; the 50–90% band holds ~$30; the 90–99% band holds ~mid-$30s; the top 1% holds ~$30-something — meaning the top 1% holds roughly 11–13x the wealth of the bottom 50%.
- (2026-07-19) If you're poor and only know poor people, you'll wrongly assume no one has money — it's just that no one you know has money.
- (2026-07-19) Go where the money is and service those customers, not out of disdain for poor people but because that's where the money is and those customers can give some of it to you.
- (2026-07-19) Tiers appealing to different customer fractals maximize revenue from an existing audience.
- (2026-07-19) The big pricing mistake: people do not go high enough and do not go low enough.
- (2026-07-19) Raising a tier by a small increment (e.g. $129/month A tier → $149/month B tier) targets the exact same buyer and reflects the owner selling from their own wallet / cost basis rather than the customer's value.
- (2026-07-19) Design offers by starting with the price and working backwards, asking how valuable solving this problem is for that specific person.
- (2026-07-19) Distinct buyers exist at very different price points — e.g. a $9/month buyer and a $99/month buyer (11x more expensive), as with School.
- (2026-07-19) Tier math: if 20% of customers buy something 5x as expensive, adding that 5x price point above the core offer doubles overall revenue.
- (2026-07-19) Alex's target: make every offer tier double the core's overall revenue, which requires basic assumptions about uptake rates for a given population.
- (2026-07-19) Most customer upsells land in the 20–30% uptake range, so to make a tier worth it he prices it ~5x the base and then designs a deliverable worth 5x the price.
- (2026-07-19) _(Outro, promotional)_ Alex offers a free "$100M scaling roadmap" — ~200 hours distilling his portfolio companies' 10 growth stages, where they got stuck and how they got past it — as a quiz at acquisition.com/roadmap that diagnoses your stage and next steps across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, and finance; the thank-you page offers a call with his team and a possible in-person invite to Vegas.
