# He's Copying the Business Model That Makes No Money

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a motocross-training business that is shifting from many single-day tour dates to more profitable five-day camps. Alex says not to worry if a competitor copies the lower-profit single-day model, because the competitor is copying the model that does not make money. He tells the founder to compare event formats by return on human and financial capital, raise prices until willingness to pay stops, and avoid lowering prices in response to competition. He illustrates the warning with a self-reported Gym Launch mistake: he cut recurring prices and added value to respond to a competitor, lost major profit, and saw no churn improvement.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - motocross-training founder. His single-day tours, five-day camps, prices, pro-rider background, and competitor concern are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A competitor copying the low-profit model can be good because they are copying the wrong thing.
- Free content can serve people who cannot afford in-person work, while paid in-person offers should be priced for those who can afford them.
- Cash flow enables broader impact by funding growth and media.
- The business should compare single-day and five-day events by unit economics, not founder preference.
- The correct metric is return on human capital and financial capital.
- Pricing should continue rising until willingness to pay stops.
- The founder should focus on the customer rather than reacting to competitors.
- Alex says one of his most costly business mistakes was lowering Gym Launch recurring pricing and adding value in response to a competitor.
- He says that move reduced top-line revenue by about $500,000/month, increased costs, did not reduce churn, and may have cost roughly $50M after sale multiple effects.
