# Why I Like Decentralization

## Summary
Operational monologue for advanced business owners. Alex says he's a big advocate of decentralization — a "feather" taken from Warren Buffett — because shared/centralized resources split focus. His goal is to make each function self-sustaining as fast as possible, with independent leaders owning their own P&Ls. He confesses his own past mistake: getting "distracted" was really *him distracting his own team* by pulling resources to chase a new opportunity without adding infrastructure. He frames the big unlock for going "from a few hundred million to a billion a year" as thinking "a business of businesses" / "a team of teams" — like Amazon, one brand with many independent revenue lines (AWS, logistics, private label), each with its own CEO, shared culture but separate operations. He explains the talent math: a $100M business is the "aggregate of the added-up potential of all the skill of all the players," e.g., a couple of $25M-caliber entrepreneurs, several $10M ones, many seven-figure ones. His talent test: "if none of the people that you have could go do it on their own, then you probably don't have good enough people." The job of the founder is to design opportunities big enough (uncapped earning capacity) that A-players stay because the return on their effort is higher inside your infrastructure. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex advocates decentralization (credited to Warren Buffett) because "shared resources split resources"; the goal is functions "self-sustaining and ideally from day one," with any Holdco investment paid back as fast as possible so units become independent.
- As of March 2026, he wants "independent leaders who own their own P&Ls so that they can stay completely focused on making that thing win."
- As of March 2026 (self-critique): "the times in my career where I have… gotten distracted has actually been I distracted my own team from winning" by reallocating resources to a new opportunity without increasing infrastructure.
- As of March 2026, he names this the big unlock for scaling "from a few hundred million to a billion a year": think "a business of businesses… a team of teams," citing Amazon (AWS / logistics / private label as effectively separate businesses, each with its own CEO, shared culture and ideals).
- As of March 2026, he notes acquisition.com now runs several revenue lines outside the portfolio, each with its own presidents and leadership team, because "humans can only manage so many humans" and a Holdco CFO is "too far away" to judge frontline spending at a specific company.
- As of March 2026, exception to decentralization: software, where shared services scale better; there, revenue lines are imagined as feature sets, each with a "CEO of this feature" even when revenue is consolidated under one membership. Service businesses instead split into distinct service lines with their own sales/marketing/pricing under one brand.
- As of March 2026, on leaders: you need people "incentivized on bottom line," and "if they need you to drive the revenue, then they are not the driver — you're still the driver."
- As of March 2026, his talent math: aggregate business revenue ≈ "the added up potential of all the skill of all the players," e.g., a $100M business = ~two $25M entrepreneurs + ~five $10M entrepreneurs + many seven-figure entrepreneurs beneath them.
- As of March 2026, his talent test: "if your head of sales could [not] go do sales [on their own]… if none of the people that you have could go do it on their own, then you probably don't have good enough people."
- As of March 2026, he reframes the "they'll steal my stuff" fear: "if you don't bring them in, you will literally do it for the rest of your life"; the founder's job is designing opportunities with uncapped earnings so it makes more sense for A-players to stay (higher return on effort inside your brand/infrastructure).
- As of March 2026 (self-reported): the missing piece for him was "pattern recognition on what high-quality talent looks like."
