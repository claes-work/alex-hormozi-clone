# The MOST important rule in Business

## Summary

In this interview clip on The Skool School, Alex Hormozi argues that the single biggest mistake entrepreneurs make is a lack of focus — chasing the "new opportunity" (his "woman in the red dress" metaphor) instead of fixing the business they already have. He contends every business can reach $100M/year, that all business types have difficulty (just at different stages), and that the real limiter is almost always the operator, not the vehicle, market, or niche. His core rule: you can own as many businesses as you want, but you can only *run* one, so you must commit and stick with it.

## Key claims

- The biggest mistake people make is a lack of focus — specifically dithering between starting a new business and fixing the current one.
- The "woman in the red dress": the new opportunity always looks more interesting, which is exactly why it works as a sales pitch — it convinces the most people.
- All businesses have difficulty; the only question is *which* difficulty you want. Different business types are hard at different stages:
  - Information businesses: easiest to start, hardest to scale.
  - Software businesses: hardest to start, easiest to scale.
  - E-commerce: in the middle (needs capital, plus logistics/supply-chain issues on the back end).
  - Service businesses: easy to start, harder/slower to scale because people are the business (recruiting and training).
- When you switch opportunities you restart at zero, quickly clear the levels you already know, then hit the same "boss" (the same underlying problem) again — often ending up running two businesses because you can't shut the first one down.
- You can own as many businesses as you want, but you can only run one — this is what everyone messes up, especially small operators who lack resources.
- Every business — even a solopreneur's — can become a $100M business. ("Solopreneur" is an organizational structure, not a business.) Whatever you sell, you could sell $100M worth of it.
- The right question is: why isn't your business a $100M business? Whatever the answer is, that is the problem you need to solve. Entrepreneurs who can't solve it blame the business, industry, market, or vehicle instead of themselves.
- The opportunity-vehicle idea is over-attractive: it's true only when the model is the genuine limiter, which is roughly 1 in 10 cases; the rest of the time the operator is the limiter.
- Doing one thing for 50 years and getting better at it will probably make you a lot of money.
- Caring about the customer and the product is what matters most long term — if that's your real motivation, you stick with it, keep improving it, and it compounds.
- The consolation against "picking wrong": all businesses end in the same place — a competent team of ~5-7 (heads of marketing, sales, IT, legal, ops, finance) running it — so the day-to-day is the same regardless of what you sell, as long as you stick with it.
