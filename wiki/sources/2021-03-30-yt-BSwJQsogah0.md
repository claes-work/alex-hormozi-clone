# Investor Boils Down Business Growth to Simple Math

## Summary

Alex Hormozi walks through the arithmetic of building a million-dollar business,
reverse-engineering a $1M/year revenue goal into weekly, daily, and per-unit
targets and then into the activity (deals, calls, appointments, outreach, clicks)
required to hit them. He argues that most people never do this math, and that
because customer acquisition is uniformly hard, the practical path — especially
early — is to raise lifetime value and sell premium-priced offers rather than
grind high volumes of cheap units.

This is a **proto-version of Hormozi's later business-math frameworks** — an
early public statement of the reverse-engineering / activity-math logic that
matures into the LTV-and-conversion "growth math" of *$100M Leads*.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- (2021) The surest sign someone won't hit their goal is that they don't know the
  math behind achieving it.
- (2021) Reverse the goal into clean, non-fuzzy targets: $1M/year ≈ $83K/month
  ≈ $21K/week ≈ ~$3K+/day. He prefers weekly cycles because daily numbers are too
  volatile until roughly $10M+/year. He credits Jason Fladlien for "fuzzy targets
  don't get hit."
- (2021) Distinguish $1M in *revenue* from $1M in *take-home income*; at a strong
  40% net margin, $1M revenue yields ~$400K owner pay — roughly the US top-1%
  income threshold (~$400K/yr).
- (2021) **Growth math:** units needed = revenue goal ÷ LTV. At a $1,000 LTV, a
  $21K week requires ~21 units/week (~5 deals/day over a 5-day week). LTV need not
  equal ticket price — it is total customer lifetime value (e.g. $100/mo gym
  membership × ~10 months = $1,000 LTV; Starbucks ~$8,000 LTV from repeat
  purchases).
- (2021) Cascade the unit target down the funnel by conversion rates: e.g. close
  1-in-3 calls → 15 calls/day; ~66% show rate → ~22 set appointments/day;
  ~50 outreach efforts per appointment → ~1,000 outreach efforts/day. For
  e-commerce, use site conversion (1% conversion → 100 clicks per sale →
  ~500 clicks/day for 5 sales).
- (2021) The model is scale-invariant: "you can add as many zeros as you want" to
  price or goal and the same logic holds.
- (2021) Deal size is the key lever: 9–10 ten-thousand-dollar deals, or one $100K
  deal, or ~83 one-thousand-dollar deals, or ~830 hundred-dollar widgets all
  produce the same monthly revenue — and low prices are far harder because of the
  operational volume they demand.
- (2021) Hand-to-hand (outreach-and-call) selling only makes sense at higher deal
  sizes (~$10K); a $1,000 deal doesn't justify that effort.
- (2021) Starting out, it's easier to over-deliver for a few premium clients
  (~$5K–$15K sweet spot) than to build scalable operations for cheap, high-volume
  products; marketing/selling and operating high volume are different skills.
- (2021) Salespeople should run the same math on commission per unit: a $1M income
  goal at $1,000 commission/unit ≈ 83 units/month; every role has a time-based cap.
- (2021) He states that having a high-paying job is underrated and that, knowing
  the risk he took on, he isn't sure he'd start a business again — he wasn't an
  entrepreneur personality type, he just hated his job enough to do anything else.
