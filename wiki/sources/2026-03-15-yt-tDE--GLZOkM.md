# I Run a Social Media Marketing Agency. How Do I Get Better Clients?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A 27-year-old founder runs a social-media-marketing and "financial influencer management" agency for publicly traded ETFs and stocks (on pace for ~$2.7M revenue, keeping ~$700k; ~100-influencer network; average client pays ~$20k/month piecemeal, gated by each client's compliance department). He wants to move up-market to an "S-tier" client (a Vanguard or BlackRock), fix a talent drop-off below his top operators, and prove ROI in an industry where sales can't be cleanly attributed. Alex's core answer is a **positioning + pricing** reframe: stop selling as a marketing/services agency and instead sell as a **media company that owns audiences**. He tells the founder to price on **revenue-per-earned-media** — retail-investor CPMs run ~$30–40, and reaching ~3 million people is worth a lot if you charge for "cheaper impressions to the correct audience." The pitch becomes: *instead of paying Meta $100k/month for ads, pay me $100k/month and get trusted people vouching for your product* — third-party endorsement being "five times more valuable" than a brand praising itself. He judges the founder badly **underpriced** ($20k/month is "not even a rounding error" to a $60-billion company), notes the founder has already tripled from $8.5k → $15k → $20k → $25k by raising $5k each meeting, and says he could "just skip to $100" (i.e., a million a year) since he'll get there eventually — and a bigger number makes prospects take him seriously. On the **age worry**, Alex dismisses it flatly: he sold to 40-year-old gym owners at 26; the only person who believes age matters is the founder. On **breaking into S-tier accounts**, poach a connected insider from one of the target firms (a ~$300k hire) — fund it by charging more. On **hiring**: top-level talent (director/VP) comes almost entirely through outreach, headhunters, or industry-specific job boards (his general counsel came via two general-counsel-specific posting sites); mid-level and below is "just ads" — and hiring ads obey the same rules as marketing (offer, funnel, headline, copy). Ends with the standard $100M scaling-roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says the founder is underpriced for his avatar: $20k/month is "not even a rounding error" to a client company worth tens of billions, and he should reposition toward $100k/month (≈$1M/year).
- As of March 2026, Alex advises pricing on **revenue per earned media**: reaching a retail investor costs ~$30–40 CPM, so reaching ~3 million people has a large paid-media equivalent value the agency should charge against.
- As of March 2026, Alex's central reframe: sell as a **media company that owns audiences** ("we own these audiences, we will give you access to these audiences for this amount of money"), not as a marketing/services agency — a services deck showing "campaign start → AUM movement" is marketing-agency positioning and should be dropped.
- As of March 2026, the pitch he recommends: *instead of paying Meta $100k/month for ads, pay me $100k/month* and get trusted third parties talking about the product — third-party endorsement is "five times more valuable" than a company praising itself, provided the audiences are the correct retail audiences.
- As of March 2026, Alex says charging more is the enabler for everything else — higher prices fund the top-tier talent needed to serve bigger accounts.
- As of March 2026, on age: it "doesn't matter at all"; the only person who believes it matters is the founder — Alex sold to ~40-year-old gym owners when he was 26. What matters is demonstrated competence.
- As of March 2026, a higher price is itself a credibility signal: prospects "might not take you seriously" at $20k/month, whereas framing it as "a million bucks a year" makes them take it seriously.
- As of March 2026, to land an S-tier legacy client, poach a connected insider from one of the target firms (a ~$300k/year hire who brings relationships) — "one of the better acquisition strategies," used by a friend who owns a big marketing agency.
- As of March 2026, hiring channels split by seniority: manager-into-director/VP and above is "almost all through outreach" (headhunters, or role-specific job boards — his general counsel came via two general-counsel-specific posting sites); mid-level and below is "just ads."
- As of March 2026, recruiting ads follow the same rules as marketing campaigns — if there are no bites, "the offer isn't good, the funnel sucks, the headline isn't good, the copy needs to be improved."
