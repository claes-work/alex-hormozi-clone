# The Mathematics of Business, Explained

## Summary

In this solo talk (recorded before a live audience), Alex Hormozi lays out 12 "rules of thumb" — paired-metric ratios he uses to diagnose whether a business is healthy or has a problem to fix. He argues almost all core business terms are relationships between two numbers, and that most of them are actionable diagnostics: close rate vs. price, LTV:CAC by operational drag, the rule of 100, lead response time, calendar utilization, payback period, gross margins, 30-day cash collected, retention/churn, prepay rates, and a closing rant that "industry averages are dumb." Heavily promotional in places (plugs the $100M scaling roadmap, Money Models, $100M Offers), but the frameworks are Alex-attributed and dense with specific numeric benchmarks.

## Key claims (business-math frameworks)

- **1. Close rate ↔ price (pricing ladder).** Close rates map to how underpriced you are (in-person / phone / Zoom sales): ≥80% close → underpriced ~3–4x; 60–80% → ~2–3x; 50–60% → ~1.5–2x; 40–50% → ~1.25–1.5x; **30–40% (~35%) = appropriately priced** *if* you have a full selling mechanism educating the buyer before the close (so the call is personalization, not a pitch). Below 30% → avatar problem or sales-motion problem — fix those before ever lowering price.
- **Services go only one way in price: up.** ~80% of businesses are unscalable/service-based; the virtuous cycle is raise price → higher gross margins → better talent → better customers → raise price again. The exception is SaaS (~5% of businesses), where price balances gross margin vs. growth rate and you optimize both.
- **2. LTV:CAC scaled by "operational drag" (humans in the loop).** LTV = lifetime gross profit per customer; CAC = cost to acquire. Model three steps — attraction, conversion, delivery — as binary 0 (scalable/no human) or 1 (human). All zeros (e.g. SaaS) → **3:1** is fine. One human → **6:1**; two humans → **9:1**; three humans (fully human service) → **12:1**. Reason: every human added creates "lumpiness"/padding you need to absorb the cost of ramping and under-performing new hires.
- **Only two long-term winning strategies:** drive CAC toward zero (massive brand, or a viral product — e.g. Facebook) OR extremely high LTV (e.g. Salesforce enterprise contracts). To get extraordinary LTV:CAC, one side has to approach zero or infinity.
- **His own peak: gym launch year 1 LTV:CAC was 100:1** — spent $100k, made $10M. He's had ~4 arbitrage windows in his life, each above 30:1; ratio later normalized to ~30:1.
- **3. Rule of 100 (and 100 days).** Do 100 actions/day for 100 days on one acquisition channel = 10,000 deliberate actions; most people get their first customer by ~week 3, but must commit to the full 100 days. "Feast or famine" is not inconsistent lead flow — it's **insufficient volume** measured on too short a timeline. Companies doing 30x your sales are usually doing ~30x your advertising. Acquisition.com makes ~450 pieces of content/week (~25–30k/year) vs. a $1M business doing ~one/day (365/year).
- **Diminishing returns are still returns.** People wrongly stop optimizing when per-action return drops; if you do 1000x more and get 100x the outcome, that's still worth it. "You can get the most for the least, but you're not going to get the most."
- **4. Lead response time: call leads within 60 seconds.** Slow follow-up means paying ~4x more per customer, lower close rates, lower gross margins. If LTV:CAC is the number that shows your soul, lead response time is underneath it — "how you do one thing is how you do everything."
- **5. 70% calendar utilization (sales team).** Sweet spot ~70–75%; don't exceed 85% or drop below 60%. Fully booked → appointments pushed further out and to inconvenient times (show rates drop), and reps stop doing outbound/follow-up. Too empty → morale drops, "commission breath." Blank space lets reps squeeze their pipeline. "I have yet to make less money by hiring more sales."
- **6. Payback period: recover CAC within 30 days.** Rationale: ~30 days of interest-free credit-card float lets you acquire a customer, get paid back by month-end, owe nothing, and repeat — enabling limitless bootstrapped growth. Even at 90-day payback, look for a money-model/pricing configuration to pull cash forward without adding operational drag. (~1 in 25 in the room had outside investors; the rest are "Bank of Alex" bootstrapped.)
- **7. Gross margins — target ≥80% for services.** Gross margin = price − cost to deliver. Net margin can never exceed gross margin, so gross margin dictates everything downstream. At 80% GM on a $100 delivery cost, price = $500; at 70% → ~$333; at 90% → $1,000 — small GM% changes are huge in dollars (going 70%→90% roughly doubles net). Undercharging comes from "selling out of your own wallet." He won't enter a business with <80% gross margins. Decommoditize the offer to earn it (plug: $100M Offers).
- **8. 30-day cash collected.** Specifically, cash collected in the first 30 days should exceed **COGS + CAC** combined — so the customer pays back both the cost to deliver and the cost to acquire, freeing cash to go get the next customer.
- **10 (aka 9). Churn & retention — acquire a customer only once.** Most businesses stay small because they fill a leaky bucket. B2B benchmark: **>80% annual retention**. Measure retention as: of the original cohort, how many remain a year later (don't confuse growth from new customers with retention). Reverse-engineer LTV from retention: 50% retention → LTV ≈ annual spend ÷ 0.5; 80% retention → ~4–5 turns. "You don't want to be in the sales business, you want to be in the reselling business" (attributed to John Paul DeJoria). Since CAC is roughly commoditized within an industry, retention is where one business becomes 5/10/100x more valuable.
- **11. Prepay / pulling cash forward.** Give at least a 10% discount for paid-in-full (money today is worth ~10%+ more than money in a year). A "buy 10 get 2" deal → ~15–20% take rate; add ancillary bonuses (faster delivery, less risk, easier — skip the line, dedicated concierge, extended guarantee) → 30–40% prepay. Third-party financing (cited from "a firm"/Affirm contact) → ~35% increase in overall sales, but "financing will not save your business." Payment structures: sell durations then split half now / half in 30 days; ask when a customer gets paid and align payments; **layaway** — start paying now, get the thing when fully paid — de-risks delivery, eases collections, and creates buyer anticipation.
- **9 (closing). Industry averages are dumb.** Don't benchmark against a mediocre industry average ("the average American is in debt, divorced twice, overweight"). Play to win; only physics limits the outcome. Example: a friend's complex-machinery business runs **net 70% margins** — sells $400k machines that cost $17k to build, on know-how. A business is "a black box that transforms raw materials into an output where the value is higher than the inputs."
- **Bonus conversion benchmarks:** Meta leads → in-person service: close ~10% of leads. Cold webinar leads → sale: ~2–3% (broad market), up to ~5% (niched). In-person salesperson (proper process): ~35%. Web/landing pages: ~1–2% (Skool "about" pages ~4% due to platform trust; his Amazon book page converts ~25% of clicks but at ~1/3 the gross profit).
