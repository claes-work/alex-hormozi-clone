# Helping a $400K Event Business Get to $1M

## Summary

Alex coaches an owner of a $400,000 balloon-and-flower event-decoration business who
wants to reach $1 million. Because large and small events consume similar time while
large events have better economics, he diagnoses the constraint as acquiring more
right-fit large customers, not broadening the business model. With most customers
already coming from paid ads and a reported 58:1 LTV-to-CAC ratio, his recommendation is
simply to spend more.

## Key claims

- As of April 2026, Alex says a business should narrow its avatar when serving different
  customer sizes creates materially different operations.
- If small and large jobs use similar time but small jobs earn worse margins, he prefers
  the larger jobs.
- He says a $400,000 business is unlikely to have saturated the large-event market; its
  constraint is lead generation for the desired customer, not market exhaustion.
- He reserves expansion into a lower-end segment for much larger businesses that have
  already captured a material share of their primary market and can build a lean,
  efficient delivery process.
- When paid ads produce most customers and unit economics remain strong, he recommends
  increasing spend rather than changing the model.
- The owner reports a 58:1 LTV-to-CAC ratio; this figure is self-reported and not
  independently verified in the excerpt.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You have a lead gen issue."

> "We just need to get more customers that are the right fit."

> "If that's still good, well, spend more."

## Notes

Reinforces avatar focus and scaling paid acquisition under strong unit economics.
