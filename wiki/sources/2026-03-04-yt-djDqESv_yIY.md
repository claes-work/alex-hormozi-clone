# How an 18 Year Old Got a Job at Acquisition.com

## Summary
Conversation clip (Alex plus a co-host, marked `>>`) built around a single hiring anecdote and the culture lessons Alex draws from it. The story: an 18-year-old wanted onto Alex's sales team, so **he emailed every single person in the company with a personalized cover letter for each one** explaining why he'd be an excellent add — and **the day he turned 18 he officially applied**. Once hired, he out-works everyone: beats Alex to the office, "here at 6:00, leaves at 6:00, seven days a week," to the point Alex tells him to take a day off. Alex's read: "he's here to win," and that energy pressures the rest of the team to step up.

From there Alex generalizes to his hiring/culture doctrine. **The first three to five sales hires are decisive because they solidify culture** — which he defines as "the series of rules, spoken or unspoken, in an organization." A strong team polices itself: high performers "black-sheep" slackers because it's "an honor to be on this team," and acquisition.com on a resume means "you can get a job wherever you want after that." On the craft of selling he insists **there is no such thing as advanced sales** — "advanced salespeople literally never don't do the basics"; it's the consistency of the fundamentals (isms: "simple scales, fancy fails"; "replicate before you iterate"). He runs sales like the Patriots: one system everyone runs; go off script and you don't work there; a proven closer who wants changes must first meet the standard, then earns a controlled A/B test ("you change it, I keep the rest of the team the same, and if your close rate's higher, I'll put it team-wide"). He argues ~95% of organizations don't know how to train — they roll dice on hires and mistake a pre-built closer's success for their own system. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, in a strong culture the team polices itself: high performers ostracize ("black-sheep") those who don't uphold the standard because being on the team is treated as an honor.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames acquisition.com (and specifically his sales team) as brand equity for the employee — it goes on the resume and opens doors elsewhere.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims there is no such thing as "advanced sales" — advanced salespeople simply never skip the basics; performance is the consistency of doing the fundamentals.
- As of March 2026, his sales operating principle: delete/simplify so the team focuses on the few things that matter — did you follow the script, follow up, work the lead, make the offer if qualified — then read metrics across the team to diagnose problems fast.
- As of March 2026, he expects roughly 80–90% of the team to be the standard-setting norm (not just a top 10%), with a couple-week ramp; "if it isn't working for you, you are not working it."
- As of March 2026, he runs one system "like the Patriots": go off script and you won't work there; a strong closer who wants to change something must first replicate the standard, then gets a controlled single-variable test before any change goes team-wide.
- As of March 2026, he claims ~95% of organizations don't know how to train — they gamble on hires, and when a pre-built closer succeeds they wrongly credit their own (often nonexistent) system.
