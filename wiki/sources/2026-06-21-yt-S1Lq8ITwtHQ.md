# Why a $25M Exit Is Harder Than a $100M Exit

## Summary
In a live coaching exchange, Hormozi advises the owner of a two-location dance school (self-reported ~$2.7M revenue, five years in business, targeting $3M this year and a 20M+ exit) on how to actually reach that exit. His central counterintuitive claim is that a $10–30M sale is the hardest price range to transact — too small for buyers with real money, too big for buyers without it — so a $25M deal is harder to close than a $100M one. He then diagnoses the specific problem with service-heavy, talent-dependent fitness businesses: they are easy to offload cheaply but very hard to sell for a lot of money (Orange Theory is his closest example of one that nearly worked). His prescribed path to a real exit is to strip the model's complexity by ~80%, keep only the highest revenue-per-square-foot service, and franchise or license the cookie-cutter version — illustrated with the Alloy / Rick Mayo semi-private-training story. He warns that nearly every multi-location fitness operator he knows never sold the whole business; they piecemealed it out to trainers, customers, and competitors when they got tired.

## Key claims
- The hardest amount of money to sell a business for is between $10M and $30M: not big enough for buyers with real money, too big for buyers without it.
- A $25M deal is one of the hardest deals to get done; a $100M deal is easier to close, because the small-deal buyer has a bigger checkbook, is scraping the bottom, and doesn't care since it barely moves their needle.
- Highly service-based, talent-driven businesses (gyms, studios) are hard to sell for a lot of money because value rests on the quality of trainers/coaches/instructors.
- To his standard, there have been no super-successful high-service gym businesses; Orange Theory was the closest for a period before it declined during COVID and never recovered.
- Such a business can be sold easily to a buyer who doesn't know what they're doing (e.g., a doctor with money who thinks he understands business) — but only for a low price, likely "10 with a bunch of hair on it," earn-outs, and clauses.
- You can acquire gyms/studios (ballet, dance, etc.) for free because so many owners are in pain and don't want to run them — this is how he says he expanded.
- Service businesses sell for little for the same reason restaurants do: someone else always has the dream and thinks they'll make it work, so it's easy to get a "yes" — just not for much money.
- The three expansion paths are M&A (buying existing studios), de novo / organic (opening locations yourself), and franchise/licensing. Franchise and licensing are far more sellable than the other two.
- Prescription: look at the current model and ask "how could I cut the complexity by 80%?", then franchise it.
- Alloy (Rick Mayo) is his example: a $4M single facility with smoothie bar, merch, PT, semi-private, and large group; on franchising he measured revenue per square foot, found semi-private highest, fired every other client, and opened a pilot with only that service.
- The replicable unit is a three-person model — one manager doing mornings/evenings and sales, plus a morning and an evening assistant — running roughly $500–600K/year at ~50% margins that can be cookie-cuttered.
- The owner's instinct to NOT add more services ("I could add daytime / things on the side") is 100% right; instead take the most profitable times, pricing, and packages and strip to the fewest people who can run it.
- Licensing is essentially the same as franchising, just a different legal structure.
- Almost every multi-location fitness operator he knows never sold the whole thing — they piecemealed it (one location to a trainer, one to a customer, one to a competitor) when they got tired.
