# Building a $1,000,000 Business for a Stranger in 56 Mins

## Summary
A live-build/coaching episode in which Hormozi diagnoses and prescribes growth levers for Ashley Capps, founder of AC Styles, a luxury personal-styling service ($309K revenue, ~42% net margin, 40 low-churn recurring clients). Alex identifies that the business is economically healthy (16:1 LTV:CAC) and that the constraint is simply volume, then walks through four levers: tightening the avatar, fixing a leaking paid-ads funnel with a valuable lead magnet plus BANT qualification, 10x-ing a wildly efficient affiliate channel, and leaving the already-strong continuity model mostly alone. He math-stacks a projected 12x (a 2x funnel × 2x ad spend × 3x affiliates) to reach ~$3.6M. Only Alex's advice is persona data; Ashley's business specifics are context.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)
- Every quality business has recurring or reoccurring revenue; the goal is to acquire a customer once and have them pay you for the rest of their lives. Product/service quality is measured by the percentage of customers who buy again and again.
- Businesses that serve luxury clientele often undercharge because they "sell out of their own wallet" instead of pricing to the value delivered or the buyer's wallet.
- Know what percentage of customers comes from every acquisition source, then check whether your time allocation matches — people often spend half their time on a source producing 10% of customers. Double down on the productive source and ask whether it's tapped out (usually it isn't).
- With good core economics (proper price, enough gross margin, good stick rates, referrals), growth becomes a front-end problem: get way more leads in the door via advertising.
- A high volume of ad clicks with almost no sales is a conversion problem, not a traffic problem — you're skimming only the people in dire, immediate pain and leaking everyone not ready to buy today.
- Going after high-income earners is good; don't compromise quality to chase lower-end customers or invent "diet" mini-versions of what you love doing.
- Identify the trigger/life event that starts the buying cycle (baby, divorce, dating, a status-image mismatch) and market to it.
- Affiliates are independent businesses that tell their audience to buy your stuff in exchange for money, free stuff, or both — different from referrals because they run their own advertising. Turn one-time outreach into consistent monthly lead nodes; aim to go from 6 to 60.
- A lead magnet should be risk-free, easy, and fast — do the work on the front end so the prospect does none; "pre-digest the food." Between a more-valuable-but-slower magnet and a less-valuable-but-immediate one, prospects prefer the immediate one.
- Give a very valuable (even unscalable, one-to-one) lead magnet only to qualified people — the landing-page headline screens them, making it a "honey trap" for the right buyers.
- Move the repeated parts of your sales pitch to before the call (video on the landing page, a second video between booking and showing) so the call time is spent on the person's decision-making, not a script that tires the sales team.
- Qualify with BANT — budget, authority, need, timing — asking the fewest questions needed to decide whether a lead stays. Budget is usually the biggest.
- Distinguish good friction (raises lead quality, removes bad leads) from bad friction (slow page load — loses everyone). Photo upload is high friction; put it in the follow-up, not the funnel.
- A price anchor (a high-tier option) raises take rate on the tier below it even if only ~10% choose the anchor, so keeping it isn't a mistake.
- Let the market set price: keep edging the price up until people stop saying yes, then back down slightly — that's your price for that avatar/messaging.
- The first step of running ads is attribution tracking; otherwise you're spending in the dark and any return is by accident, not on purpose.
- If a machine returns $16–23 for every $1 in 30 days, there's no reason to cap the budget — put in as many dollars as you can. Many businesses sit on these untapped "treasure troves."
- The Chinese "rule of 3 and 10": systems break when they triple, so plan growth in triples.
- Sequence recommendations by effort: knock out click-of-a-button quick wins (raise ad spend, add a lead magnet, drop in the video) before the labor-heavy plays (affiliate outreach); for a small business with limited time and capital, pick the highest-likelihood, lowest-cost moves first and defer the rest until volume/data/cash flow justify them.
