# My Criteria for Buying Companies

## Summary
In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi lays out the acquisition criteria for his holding company (Acquisition.com): the "deal box" of size, quality, and category filters a target must clear before he'll do a deal. He explains why he prefers businesses that are excellent where his team is not (and vice versa), how his content engine feeds all deal flow, and how a multi-stage screening funnel narrows ~3,000 monthly applicants down to about two LOIs a month. He illustrates the thesis with a recently acquired 32-location teeth-whitening chain whose sales process his team rebuilt.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex states his minimum acquisition size is roughly $3 million in top-line revenue and at least $1 million in EBITDA — below that, a company is usually "still figuring stuff out" and lacks basic product-market fit, which his team won't redo.
- As of March 2026, his "deal box" filters include: is the company in the deal box; do they like the founders (founders are often replaced with a trusted operator); is the vision big enough; is the product good enough long-term. He says having many disqualifiers makes deals harder to do, not easier.
- As of March 2026, his target categories are consumer services and business services — either brick-and-mortar chains or national services (e.g. mortgage sales) — where he can see an "easy 5x."
- As of March 2026, Alex says his head of biz dev wants targets to be "very specifically incompetent" — weak exactly where Acquisition.com is excellent (e.g. bad sales process or weak demand-gen but high LTV and sticky retention), so his team's playbooks add obvious value.
- Alex claims (self-reported) his team bought a 32-location teeth-whitening chain and, in one rollout of their "first level" playbook, 5x'd LTV — which he framed as creating roughly $50 million in enterprise value once rolled across all 32 locations over 12 months.
- As of March 2026, Alex says Acquisition.com receives about 3,000 company applications per month and that the number keeps growing; the firm does not make more money by doing more deals, so the discipline is staying selective (invoking Warren Buffett's "wait to swing" idea).
- Alex says all of his deal flow comes from content: prospects consume videos, podcasts, the book, and the course — roughly 100 hours with him virtually — before applying, which pre-fixes a lot of low-hanging fruit. The teeth-whitening deal came through content.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes the screening funnel: automated screening weeds out ~90% (too small, wrong space, international); a qualifier verifies data accuracy; an analyst writes a one-page business case; qualifying deals pass to the managing director of business development who takes them to close — resulting in about two LOIs a month.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the holdco team (~30 people) is organized by function as subject-matter experts (SMEs): IT, finance, HR/recruiting, two marketing SMEs (one MarTech/media-buying/CRO, one copy/creative/VSLs/ads), a director of sales, and a director of customer success — each having scaled multiple teams larger than most incoming companies.
- As of March 2026, Alex says his team has codified about three playbooks that can reliably "10x" services companies, and he wants to get more efficient at pulling a few big levers to 10x a business, then decide whether to exit, double down, or do a dividend recap and reinvest.
- Alex says he'd let a proven ex-operator (e.g. someone who scaled and exited a $100M+ software business) plug into his deal flow as a separate "pod" rather than build their own deal machine, and expects the deal box to expand over time as such operators join.
