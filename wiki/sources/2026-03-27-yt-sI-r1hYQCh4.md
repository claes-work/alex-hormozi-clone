# Hate Always Comes from Below

## Summary
Alex Hormozi argues that criticism only ever comes from people who are behind you, not ahead of you, and that other people's negative opinions are as harmless as the germs he once feared. He recounts an 8th-grade experience as a germaphobe who, forced to abandon hygiene on a five-day camping trip ("Expedition 8"), discovered that all his worry over germs "amounted to nothing" — a lesson he now applies to critics. He closes with a Ray Dalio anecdote to make the point that you can never be legitimate to the ignorant, so being hated by a bad person is actually a good thing.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says he has never received criticism from anyone who is ahead of him in a given domain — no one fitter, richer, or longer-married has told him he was doing it wrong; only people "on the sidelines" have the time to criticize.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he struggled for a long time to get over what other people thought, and that at some point you have to prove to yourself that you will survive it and "will not die."
- As of March 2026, Alex frames other people's opinions as analogous to germs: you brace for imagined consequences, then realize not only did you not die, but no one cared and no one was even thinking about you.
- As of March 2026, Alex says people have taken shots at him, made "hit videos," and pulled his words out of context, and that nothing in his life has changed as a result; expecting to be completely understood on the internet is "a silly goal."
- As of March 2026, Alex cites Ray Dalio being roasted as a "grifter" and "fake guru" in TikTok comments (Dalio described as worth ~$30 billion, ~79 years old) as proof that "you will never be legitimate to the ignorant."
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that being hated by a bad person is a good thing, and that we should "judge our character by the quality of our enemies."
