# If you're ambitious but inconsistent, please watch this

## Summary

Solo monologue framed as a long-form answer to a young man who stopped Alex on Rodeo Drive, said he made $80,000 in sales but quit because it "wasn't his passion," and asked what to do with his life. Alex argues that "follow your passion" is true only in the vague and never in the specific: any business funnels the hardest, most repetitive, least enjoyable problems onto the owner, so passion is a poor decision filter. He uses his own fitness-to-$46.2M-exit arc to show that even chasing a passion means spending almost no time on the thing you love. The real levers are proficiency, frustration tolerance, and pain tolerance — learnable skills — plus the willingness to become the person you want to be through suffering you deem worthwhile.

## Key claims

- Alex left a management consulting job with ~$50,000 saved to pursue fitness; he built gyms, did turnarounds, licensed the model to ~6,000 locations, started a supplement company and a software company, and sold all of it to private equity at a $46.2M valuation.
- He still loves fitness but deliberately does not make it his business or talk about it much; he now owns a gym with no memberships that is not for profit ("holy grail").
- "Addiction is a narrowing of things that bring you joy. Fulfillment is a broadening of them."
- Passion is a "very bad judgment filter"; a business is "a funneling mechanism of the hardest and worst problems related to the thing that you supposedly love."
- The only way to purely do your passion and make a living is to not scale: do only the amount you feel like, only when you feel like it, be so good that people pay ever more, and just raise prices.
- Success comes down to executing daily, solving problems intelligently, making decisions that are harder short-term / easier long-term, and sitting in the discomfort of unsolved (non-urgent) problems.
- Sequence he offers: "Interests get you started. Proficiency keeps you going, but pain tolerance helps you finish."
- Frustration tolerance = the number of times you can be rejected and try again; it is learnable, therefore a skill.
- Getting better = finding ways to do the repetitive things you hate so you hate them less.
- Reinforcement (not reward) drives behavior: a negative event (taking a shock) becomes reinforcing if it spares your family; reward-based willingness (a shock for $1M) decays because reinforcement "needs more variety."
- "Nostalgia is memory minus pain" — people apply rose-colored glasses to hardship they actually hated living through.
- All failure besides death is psychological — self-drawn goal lines; society's rules are made up. You're not out of business until someone physically stops you from working.
- He has made ~5,000 ads this year (including the book launch) and has recorded ads every week for 14 years; a friend who built a high-end cookie business said he wasn't passionate about cookies but "passionate about being excellent."
- Personal admission: he is at a "weird transition," recently had "a huge [amount] just [go] out the back door," no longer needs to work financially at all, yet keeps going because the man he wants to become would be ashamed if he stopped.
