# Advice for Anyone with Employees

## Summary
Short solo clip on people-management. Alex's thesis: **to get someone's best professional work, you have to care about their personal life** — chiefly their home environment. His strongest (and, he grants, controversial) claim: if an employee's spouse/significant other is unsupportive of the work — "even if they're neutral" — that person "will never achieve their potential." Speaking "for men," he argues being solid on the home front gives you "full access to your brain" to "go after the hunt"; if you're worried about friction at home ("bitching and moaning," "why are you working?"), you start "pulling your punches" — cutting out early, dodging bigger projects, setting only "adequate" goals because you know the effort a big goal demands (he adds there's nothing wrong with that if you don't want to see how far you can go — not everyone does). Because of this, he now pays deliberate attention to which team members have supportive home environments/spouses, and looks for ways to *help win the spouse's support* so he can "unlock their discretionary effort toward the goal." He frames the stakes with a life-scale motivation: at 85 you remember only a few standout moments, so he tells the team that looking back, "you made history" — which only happens when "every single thing" is aligned (plus luck). Second management lesson: **hard workers run themselves into the ground** when the goal is big enough, and they look to the leader to signal when to rest — so it's the leader's job to gauge who's near "cracking" and explicitly tell them to take the day/weekend and unplug; permission from above is what lets them actually disconnect. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
