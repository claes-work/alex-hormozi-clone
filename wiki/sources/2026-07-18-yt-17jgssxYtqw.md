# "How Do I Hire in a Town of 5,000 People?"

## Summary

A short live coaching Q&A. The questioner introduces herself as **Stephanie Turpin**,
owner of a residential/commercial cleaning service (captions render the company name as
"Spring B&B service" — likely a caption garble) in super-rural western Wisconsin, towns
of 5,000–10,000 people. She reports $1.3–1.4M revenue, wants $2–3M, has ~25 techs, and
says the constraint is staffing: turnover, recruiting, manpower. Her best techs have
historically come from healthcare (nursing homes etc.), and she admits the company has no
career path for techs. Alex first triages: "Do you have a turnover issue or an attraction
issue?" She answers both, and he states his sequencing rule — **"I solve problems back to
front"** — fix churn in the existing flow first, and only if a deficit remains attack
attraction.

His central reframe: in a low-leverage labor business with narrow pay bands, **"what
business are you really in? You're actually in the training business."** The people
problem is the feature, not the bug, so almost all focus should go to the supply side:
pride yourself on taking anyone off the street, getting them up to speed in 14 days, and
retaining them via career path and opportunity. Mechanically, that means breaking down
what makes a great tech great into **observable actions rather than descriptive terms**
(not "be upbeat" but "smile, nod, say yes sir / no ma'am / my pleasure"), then role
playing it — a method he notes "100% applies to AI" as well.

He then pushes her from one-on-one training (which she does, limited by trainer
bandwidth) to **cohort/group training**, arguing "it's almost as costly to train one as
it is to train five," and lays out his training-meeting structure: (1) open with
**kudos** — reinforcement the manager gives at first, and peers give each other once the
culture is installed ("that's how a culture spreads"); (2) the manager/trainer **models**
the correct behavior; (3) split into **role plays** with **real-time feedback** — thumbs
up and nods while it's going well, stop them the moment they mess up rather than
dumping 17 corrections at the end ("no one learns that way"), and when they nail it,
**"lock it in"** — repeat it three more times in a row. On her follow-up about people
quitting over the job's physicality, he suggests tools to make the job easier, recruiting
from any industry where people are on their feet (widening beyond just healthcare), and
making training itself involve 12 hours on their feet so it models the real day and
washes out mismatches fast. His "big bite" summary: switch one-on-one to group training,
open up the aperture on candidate types, and spend all remaining bandwidth making the
training program unstoppable. The video ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap
free-gift CTA.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-18. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-18) **Sequence people problems back to front: churn before attraction.**
  Hormozi argues that fixing turnover in the existing hiring flow ("the force of
  gravity") may alone close the gap; only if a deficit remains should the business work
  on attracting more candidates.
- (2026-07-18) **In low-leverage labor businesses, don't over-niche talent sourcing.**
  Hormozi argues (paraphrase) that unlike engineers, marketers, or salespeople — where
  10x leverage justifies paying up for talent — a cleaning tech operates in a narrow pay
  band set by what customers will pay, so a hyper-specific hiring avatar (e.g. only
  ex-healthcare workers) needlessly shrinks an already tiny 5,000–10,000-person market.
- (2026-07-18) **A recurring-labor service company is "actually in the training
  business."** The people problem is the feature, not the bug, and never goes away;
  nearly all focus should go to the supply side — being able to take anyone off the
  street, get them productive in 14 days, and retain them via career path and
  opportunity.
- (2026-07-18) **Define great performance as observable actions, not descriptive
  terms.** "Be upbeat" is not helpful; "smile, nod your head when someone's talking, say
  yes sir / no ma'am / my pleasure after every interaction" is trainable and can be role
  played. Hormozi notes this decomposition "100% applies to AI" as well.
- (2026-07-18) **Recruit and train in cohorts, not one-on-one.** "It's almost as costly
  to train one as it is to train five," and groups enable peer role play — he explicitly
  wants to "break that belief" that trainer bandwidth forces one-on-one training.
- (2026-07-18) **Training-meeting structure: kudos → model → role play.** Start every
  training meeting with kudos (manager-given at first; peer-to-peer once culture is
  installed — "that's how a culture spreads"), then the trainer models correct behavior,
  then trainees role play on each other.
- (2026-07-18) **Role-play feedback must be real-time, not batched.** Nod / thumbs-up
  while the trainee performs well; stop them the instant they err and re-run the line
  ("Go back to here… Try it again") rather than listing 17 corrections at the end — "no
  one learns that way." When they nail it: "lock it in" = repeat it three more times in
  a row, then move on. "That is how we train anyone on anything."
- (2026-07-18) **Design training to simulate the job's real physical load.** Hormozi
  suggests (paraphrase) giving techs tools that ease the physical work, sourcing from any
  industry where people are on their feet (broader than just healthcare), and putting
  trainees on their feet for 12 hours during training so physically mismatched hires wash
  out fast instead of quitting after onboarding investment.
- (2026-07-18) **The "big bite" prescription:** (1) switch from one-on-one to group
  training, (2) open up the aperture on acceptable candidate types, and (3) pour all
  spare bandwidth into making the training program unstoppable.
