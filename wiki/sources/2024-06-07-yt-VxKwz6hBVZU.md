# How to Build a Valuable Company You Can Sell Someday

## Summary

Solo talk in which Alex Hormozi explains how to build a company that is actually
sellable, framed around the fable of the goose and the golden eggs: separate the thing
that *creates* value (the goose) from the things that *are* valuable and sellable (the
eggs), and orient the business to keep the goose while selling many eggs. He walks
through the drivers that make a business attractive to acquirers — no owner/keyman
dependence, recurring/sticky revenue, faceless and turnkey operations — and uses
several client examples (event-space coaching, Amazon-store coaching, a media/talent
company, an accounting-firm coach, a dental association) to show how to convert an
unsellable "teaching" business into a sellable rollup of the underlying assets. He also
covers the "volume premium" in M&A (bigger aggregations sell at higher multiples),
raising money against a goose you don't want to exit (selling a minority), and reframing
competition as collaboration.

## Key claims (sellability / enterprise-value drivers)

- **Goose vs. eggs**: distinguish what *creates* value (the goose) from what *is*
  valuable/sellable (the eggs). Sell the eggs, keep the goose laying. The goose is often
  incredibly valuable but *not* sellable.
- **Keyman risk kills value**: anything dependent on the owner makes the business
  significantly less valuable. Gym Launch became sellable because Alex removed himself
  from delivery and ads and put a leadership team in place.
- **Recurring / sticky revenue** and a **faceless, turnkey** operation are the two most
  important attributes he'd look for. Businesses with high revenue retention (accounting
  firms, franchise chains) have clear exit paths; small service facilities (micro gyms,
  personal-training studios) don't, because buyers see keyman risk at that level too.
- **Sell the underlying sellable asset, not the coaching business.** Coaching/education
  businesses built on top of a proven sellable asset (event venues, Amazon stores,
  accounting firms) should aggregate their students'/clients' businesses into a rollup
  and exit that, rather than trying to sell the (face-dependent, non-recurring) coaching
  company, which "no one is buying."
- **Set up rollups in advance**: pre-standardize students on the same colors, systems,
  and price points so the top ~10% can be rolled up and sold in batches of 20–50 every
  year or two — doing upfront the standardization a rollup would otherwise have to do.
- **Volume premium in M&A**: unlike bulk consumer discounts, investing pays *more* per
  unit of profit as the aggregation gets bigger, because "big money is lazy" and pays a
  premium to do one deal instead of a hundred. Firms selling at ~4x earnings alone can
  fetch ~12x in aggregate; even giving up 30% of a 12x, the operator is better off.
- **Verify demand with M&A activity**: Google the M&A activity for your business type.
  If little exists, the market may not be there; if it exists, study what those buyers
  like and re-orient your business toward those attributes. Treat your business as a
  product and the acquirer/investor as the customer.
- **Franchise-law nuance**: name + system + fee = a franchise; doing all three without
  registering is an illegal franchise. Gym Launch deliberately used systems + fee but
  **no** unified name to stay sellable while avoiding franchise law.
- **Raising money vs. exiting a goose**: if you're bigger than the company you're
  permanent keyman risk, but you can still sell a *minority* — a liquidity event, not an
  exit — to take chips off the table or fund an acquisition. Cited Mr Beast (raised at
  the holdco level) and Elon Musk (mostly public companies) as owners who let others
  participate without leaving.
- **Media/talent company**: the stars generate the impressions (goose); the eggs are the
  products sold through that distribution, or advertising space. Creators often have fame
  that far exceeds their wealth, so raising a small stake funds inventory/logistics for a
  product launch.
- **Reoccurring counts, not just recurring**: consumables people rebuy (hair gel, Coca-
  Cola) create sellable value even without a subscription.
- **Competition is mostly imagined**: the richest acquirers aggregate fragmented
  industries and don't care about local rivalries; they remove ego-driven founders and
  capture cost synergies. Collaboration makes far more money than competing.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "You're trying to sell the goose when you should be trying to sell the eggs."
- "You never want to sell the goose, you want to keep the goose and let it keep laying golden eggs."
- "There's something that creates value and then there's the thing that is valuable."
- "The goose often is incredibly valuable but not sellable, and that's the difficult part about this."
- "If anything is dependent on you for the business, it's inherently going to be significantly less valuable."
- "If you're bigger than the company you will always be keyman risk for the business."
- "It's a volume premium... because big money is lazy, and so they will pay a premium to not have to do as many deals."
- "You have to zoom all the way back out and think of your business as a product and an investor or potential acquirer as a customer."
- "We're not competing against each other, guys — we're competing against the couch, we're competing against Netflix... we're competing against chocolate."
- "The most valuable version of that business wasn't actually that business, it was the eggs of that business."
