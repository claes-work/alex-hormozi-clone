# How to Build a Brand in E-Com

## Summary

Alex tells an Indonesian menswear company that $10 million is too early to require new
customer categories. He recommends defining the intended brand, studying the largest
comparable business, identifying skill gaps, and converting paid-media arbitrage into a
brand that can command a premium and become sellable.

## Key claims

- The owner self-reports $10 million annual revenue after nine years and a $3 million
  starting reference that is unclear in the caption; only the $10 million figure is retained.
- As of March 2026, Alex says menswear alone has ample room for growth and that even large
  brands remain bounded by product identity.
- His benchmarking framework starts with the biggest version of the current business,
  then maps business model, monetization, integration, and missing skills between it and today.
- He uses Red Bull as an illustrative, uncited example of outsourcing much except brand,
  marketing, recipe control, and distribution.
- A prominent endorser can raise conversion and price premium, helping the company move
  from copying retail styles toward owned brand meaning.
- He recommends scaling the existing menswear engine toward $100 million before adding
  unrelated consulting, media, or private-equity ambitions described by the owner.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "What's the biggest version of the business that's currently here?"

> "Those skill deficiencies become the opportunity."

> "You need to go from that to building a brand."
