# Helping a Marketing Agency Fix Their Pricing

## Summary
Live consulting clip. The client runs a digital-marketing agency selling complete marketing services to Australian service-based SMBs (cleaning companies, yard work; client average revenue $500k–$2.5M). He pivoted from a fitness business, went 0→$500k in four months, sells ~10 clients/month at $450/week (~$2k/month), and wants to reach eight figures. Alex's diagnosis is a pricing-and-margin trap, not a fix-the-churn problem. Because the agency services SMBs, the SMBs' volatility and churn flow straight into the agency; at a ~$1,500–$3k/month price point the average client "stick" is only four to six months, so margins compress while CAC keeps rising ("CAC will never be cheaper than it is today"), and the founder's instinct to hire more people or take over the clients' sales is the wrong move. Back-of-napkin, selling 10/month at $2k with ~5 months of retention (~$10k LTV) tops out around $100k/month, then stalls. Alex's core prescription: **the mid-market is a dead zone — you must go up-market or down-market.** Down-market = go super cheap ($400/month or less) with a "nuisance" service that costs almost nothing to deliver (Google Maps ranking, review management, SEO), which yields high sticky retention (30–40 months), similar LTV, and very low CAC via high sales velocity — structured as a big-head/long-tail one-time setup into a small, high-gross-margin recurring monthly fee. Up-market = keep the high-touch service but only for businesses that already know their metrics, have a proven model, and a working sales process, so you're not paid to figure out what they never figured out. Closes with the standard $100M scaling roadmap promo (excluded from claims).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues an agency servicing SMBs inherits its clients' volatility: "their volatility will translate over to your volatility," so clients churn regardless of how well the agency performs "because SMBs suck [as customers]."
- As of March 2026, he says CAC only rises over time ("CAC will never be cheaper than it is today"), so as churn eats the base, revenue can keep climbing while margin compresses until it "feel[s] like running a nonprofit."
- As of March 2026, at a ~$1,500–$3k/month price point for an SMB the average client "stick" is about four to six months — "right in the sweet spot of churn."
- As of March 2026, hiring more delivery staff to fix churn, or taking over clients' sales/nurture because they're bad at their own business, is the wrong response to margin compression.
- As of March 2026, back-of-napkin: 10 sales/month × $2k × ~5 months retention (~$10k LTV) caps the business around $100k/month, after which it stalls and growth requires either more units sold or higher LTV — neither of which lifts easily in that model.
- As of March 2026, the winning move is to leave the middle ("in the middle it's just a dead zone where everyone dies") and go either down-market or up-market.
- As of March 2026 (down-market model): price at ~$400/month or less for a "nuisance"-style service that costs almost nothing to deliver — e.g. ranking a client in the top three of local Google Maps ($400/month), or review management + SEO (~$300/month) — which clients keep because "they can see it," yielding 30–40-month stick rates, similar LTV to the higher-priced model but far lower CAC from higher sales velocity.
- As of March 2026, he frames this cheap model structurally as a "big head, long tail": a one-time setup fee feeding a very small, high-gross-margin recurring monthly fee that must offset CAC.
- As of March 2026 (up-market model): keep the high-touch services but sell only to businesses that already know their metrics, have a proven model, and an existing sales process — so the agency isn't hired to figure out what the client never figured out themselves.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes he sees a disproportionate number of marketing agencies among his audience and has "seen every model under the sun," and that SMBs are structurally poor customers for a mid-priced high-touch agency.
