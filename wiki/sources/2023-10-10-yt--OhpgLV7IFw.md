# Alex Hormozi's Favorite "Hobby" is Business

## Summary

Short interview clip in which Alex Hormozi reframes his relationship to work: business is
not labor to him but the thing he would choose to do all day anyway, his form of play. He
rebuts "road to burnout" / work-life-balance concerns as other people projecting their own
theory of mind onto him, arguing that the person who genuinely loves the activity can
sustain a pace that looks like grinding from the outside but feels like play from the
inside. He closes with a blunt mortality-based dismissal of others' opinions about how he
should live.

## Key claims

- Alex frames everything he does around "the game": his podcast is called *The Game*, he
  draws business doodles all day, writes and makes content about business, and spends the
  rest of his time doing business. (Alex; paraphrase)
- He personally illustrates his books — claims 100% of the drawings in *$100M Offers* and
  the doodles in *$100M Leads* are his own (bags of money, animals). (Alex; self-reported)
- Business-as-play thesis: the right question is not "how do you work so much?" but "what
  can you do longer than anybody else, that looks like work to others but feels like play
  to you?" — and for him that thing is business. (Alex; paraphrase)
- Uses tennis player Steffi Graf as an illustration: tested young on two axes — desire to
  train and skill — she scored 10/10 on both, so she could out-work rivals without it
  feeling like work; that combination is "terrifying" for competitors. (Alex; paraphrase)
- Burnout critics are using *their* theory of mind — imagining how *they* would feel at his
  workload — rather than his actual experience. (Alex; paraphrase)
- He deliberately works at the pace he likes and wants to see where his limits are; pushing
  to find "how much harder can I go" is exciting to him, tempered by awareness of burnout.
  (Alex; paraphrase)
- Nods to the "ikigai"-style intersection (referencing James Clear / *Atomic Habits*): the
  sweet spot is what you love, what you're good at, and what you can be paid for. (Alex;
  paraphrase)
- People project their idea of what his life *should* look like onto him, and it's
  irrelevant; if he weren't married and in shape, even more people would raise work-life
  balance. (Alex; paraphrase)
- Mortality frame for ignoring critics: everyone dies and won't matter, so their opinion —
  from people who won't even show up to his funeral — carries no weight. (Alex; paraphrase)
