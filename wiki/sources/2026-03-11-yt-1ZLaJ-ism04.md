# Your Self-Limiting Beliefs Are Sabotaging Your Goals

## Summary
Solo monologue on identity and self-limiting beliefs. Alex argues that one of the "big nasty things" you must eliminate on the path to your goals is the **identity you claim about yourself** — the "isms" like "I'm a neat freak," "I'm not good at math," "I'm not techy." These statements feel like descriptions but function as constraints, and they don't serve you. His proposed fix is a single decision **razor** that rules all choices: *does this thing, person, or belief make it more or less likely that I hit my goals?* — applied to your actions, the people you spend time with, keeping your phone in the room, and the labels you repeat about yourself. He grounds it in his own case: he considered himself "bad at math" after a bad ninth-grade algebra teacher put him a year behind, and he carried that label until his 20s, when he decided it no longer served him. He asked what he'd have to do to *feel* good at math, decided mental math was cool, and stopped using a calculator for years — making many mistakes at first, then checking his head-math after the fact — until people now say he "rattles off numbers like crazy." The lesson: people are far more **pliable** than they think; an early disadvantage is fine, because you just have to get over it. He dismisses childhood-hardship as an excuse ("if you want to win the award for hardest childhood, congrats, you win… no one cares about what happened to you then, only what you can make happen now"). His core thesis: **identity is a lie; doing is being.** We only describe who people are by what they *do* — historically people were even named for their trades (John the carpenter, John the mason), and the first thing you ask on meeting someone is what they do. Therefore your identity is 100% under your control, because you control your actions; others describe you by them only after the fact. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
