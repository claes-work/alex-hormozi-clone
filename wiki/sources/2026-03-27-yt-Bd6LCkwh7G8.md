# Helping an Online Coaching Business Fix His Offer

## Summary
In this coaching Q&A, an online-coaching business owner asks Alex Hormozi how to remove key-man/unicorn risk from a performance-intensive delivery component (e.g. one-on-one support) while still increasing customer LTV. Alex answers with two ideas: replace key-man risk by chunking a unicorn's combined skills into separately teachable component parts, and — when delivery capacity is the constraint on something customers already love — solve the right problem by adding capacity or raising price rather than degrading the product. He warns that finding product-market fit is far harder than changing price, illustrating with a mistake from his early gym days.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames rarity as a function of combinations: what makes a skill (or offer) rare is the combination of components, and what commoditizes it is breaking it into chunks.
- As of March 2026, Alex says you remove key-man/unicorn risk by chunking one person's combined skills into three or four component parts, each of which can be hired for or taught more easily ("chopping the unicorn's horn off" and reassembling the capability from more readily available people).
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that if a delivery model is at capacity because customers genuinely love it, the delivery is not unscalable — you just need more people doing the thing customers like, not a redesign of the offer.
- As of March 2026, Alex says the two clean levers when a beloved service maxes out capacity are: add margin/capacity to support it, or raise price to increase LTV — and if margin can't support it, the offer is simply mispriced rather than in need of change.
- As of March 2026, Alex maintains that product-market fit is much harder to find than pricing, so when you have something everyone loves you can usually support a price increase without touching the product.
- As of March 2026, Alex warns against downgrading delivery (e.g. semi-private to group) to fit more people, because it can strip out the value and create a price-to-value discrepancy.
- Caller claims (unverified): he runs an online coaching business using Zoom, community, and a learning-management setup; the community has become the intensive part; he has a director of education who does the performance delivery; the model is already semi-private with a time-intensive component of reviewing specific case studies; and he believes he could reallocate expenses to fund more capacity.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "What makes things rare is combinations. So what makes them commoditized is breaking into chunks."

> "It's chopping the unicorn's horn off and saying, okay, I can find a rhinoceros. Then you're like, okay, now I just need a horse and I need some, you know, fireflies to get the glitter and there we go. It took three people to replace my unicorn with things that are more readily available."

> "You just chunk down what the skills that this one person has into component parts and then you can teach those component parts much more easily. So it takes the mysticism out of it."

> "I don't think your delivery is unscalable. You just need more people to do that thing that people like."

> "It's harder to find something that everyone really likes than it is to just change the price. So if you have something that everyone really loves, then you can probably support a price change."

> "So what I did, this was the mistake, is I took my sessions and I shortened them so I could fit more sessions in. And by doing that, I destroyed the thing that worked. So don't do that."

> "Product market fit is much harder to find than pricing."
