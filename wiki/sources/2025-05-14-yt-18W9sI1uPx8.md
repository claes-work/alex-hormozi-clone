# How to Use Your Time in the Most Effective Way - Alex Hormozi

## Summary

Alex Hormozi teaches a five-step process for allocating time, attention, and resources, which he says he "stole ruthlessly from Elon Musk": (1) question the requirement, (2) delete, (3) optimize, (4) accelerate timelines, and (5) automate. He argues most people (and teams) invent work that produces no output — his running examples are a media team wasting ~20% of its time on giant file uploads and color grading that don't move views, and evenly distributing editors across platforms when 87% of his traffic comes from just Instagram and YouTube. He frames prioritization as the fundamental job of the CEO/entrepreneur, especially when resources are scarce, and demonstrates how pulling deadlines forward and increasing communication frequency can make people move dramatically faster.

## Key claims (Alex's content only)

- Every self-made millionaire and billionaire started with **the same number of hours** and roughly no money; what separated them was getting **a better return on their investment** of time and resources.
- Most people spend most of their resources on things that don't drive the highest return on their time; work grows unless you apply **constant, ruthless prioritization** and re-examine whether each activity is truly worth it.
- People "invent work" and feel productive doing it — his media team spent ~20% of their time on slow big-file uploads and color grading, which he judged has **very little effect on views or view quality** ("We're not making Hollywood movies").
- Just because others in the industry do something doesn't mean you should — it can still be a **poor return**.
- **The five-step process** (attributed to Elon Musk):
  1. **Question the requirement** — "Why are we doing this?" With three sub-questions: (a) what does that mean / define the term, (b) how do you know that, (c) why do I care / how does it translate to growth. If it can't be tied to growth, it doesn't matter.
  2. **Delete** — hack away; most people can delete ~90% of their work because it doesn't drive the business. Deleting recovers 100% of that resource **plus** the switching costs between tasks (one of the biggest inefficiencies for human brains).
  3. **Optimize** — increase throughput of what remains, and reallocate to where returns are highest (activities *and* platforms). Only optimize after questioning and deleting.
  4. **Accelerate timelines** — pull deadlines forward; ask "how long in hours?" then "why can't you get it done today?" Two levers to make things faster: **move up deadlines** and **increase communication frequency** (meeting daily vs. weekly gets more output because people default to filling the time until the next check-in).
  5. **Automate** — either someone else does it, or software/AI/robots do it. Automate last (he notes people often "jump the gun" and automate too early).
- Starting out, the big input is **advertising** — letting people know about your thing "ruthlessly and consistently beyond the time of being bored" (references his "rule of 300").
- **Data on his own channels**: 87% of his traffic comes from two platforms (Instagram and YouTube); Instagram now drives more traffic to acquisition.com than YouTube, despite Instagram content being only repurposed. TikTok, with ~1M followers, produced 1 opt-in in 4 days vs. 187 from Instagram in the same period (3M TikTok posts/impressions vs. 1M on Instagram, with more posts on TikTok) — so he'd reallocate editors (e.g., 8 on Instagram, 8 on YouTube, fewer elsewhere) and might drop TikTok.
- Beware the **sunk-cost fallacy**: if the rules change or new data says you're wrong, stop. "The only thing worse than not knowing is getting [the data] and still doing nothing about it."
- **Prioritization is strategy.** The number one job of the CEO/entrepreneur is prioritization — the allocation of scarce resources. When you have few resources you must be even more sparing.
- Many people never leave the beginner stage because they **try to figure everything out at once** instead of doing one thing at a time (his example: get the about page set up first, then drive traffic to it, then improve the VSL — nothing else until the prior step is done).
- His department-fixing method: itemize problems, rank them, and **refuse to discuss problem #2 or #3 until #1 is fixed** — each got fixed in ~3 days.
