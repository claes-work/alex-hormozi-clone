# Million Dollar Equations

## Summary

Solo whiteboard-style lecture in which Alex Hormozi walks through eight
"investing concepts" / business-math equations he uses to evaluate, buy, and
scale businesses. The core is the LTGP:CAC ratio and its component parts,
extended into return on invested capital, payback period, and three forward-
looking prediction equations (sales velocity × LTGP, sales velocity ÷ churn,
and a TAM-based opportunity/risk framework). Throughout he stresses that all of
it is only addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division, and that using
gross profit (not revenue) is the mistake most owners make.

## Key claims (the equations/formulas)

- **LTGP : CAC ratio** — Lifetime Gross Profit compared to Cost to Acquire a
  Customer is "the fundamental economic unit of the business." A 1:1 ratio means
  you spend a dollar and make a dollar back; 20:1 means $20 profit per $1 in.
  Rule of thumb: **always want at least greater than 3:1**; 5:1 is great. Below
  that "you'll be too constrained to scale."
- **Lifetime (customer lifespan)** — For subscription/service businesses,
  average lifespan = **1 ÷ churn**. Back-of-napkin: customers at start of month
  vs. those same customers still there 30 days later. 100 → 90 = 10% churn;
  1 ÷ 0.10 = **10 months** average lifespan. Works on any interval (monthly,
  quarterly, annual). Product businesses instead use average number of purchases
  (e.g. Shopify reporting "average customer buys 4.3 times").
- **Gross profit** — Physical products: price − cost of goods sold. Service
  example: $10,000/mo revenue per employee (10 customers) − $4,000 employee cost
  = **$6,000 gross profit = 60% gross margin**. Absolute number and percentage
  are the same metric expressed two ways.
- **Lifetime Gross Profit (LTGP)** — $6,000 gross profit ÷ 10 customers = $600
  per customer per month; × 10-month lifespan = **$6,000 LTGP per customer**.
  This is all the money you have to acquire them AND run the rest of the
  business.
- **Revenue vs. gross-profit trap** — Pen example: sells 10 pens at $10 = $100
  revenue but $9 cost each = only $10 lifetime gross profit; spending $50 to
  "make a $100 sale" is really spending $50 to make $10 GP. Owners conflate LTV
  and LTGP because Silicon Valley/software has ~100% gross margins where the two
  coincide; everyone with people, inventory, or manufacturing must run off
  gross profit.
- **CAC** — Total cost including labor, not just ad spend. Example: 5 employees
  $50,000/mo + $50,000/mo software & ads = $100,000/mo total; ÷ 83 customers
  acquired = **$1,224 CAC**. With $6,000 LTGP that is a **~5:1 ratio**.
- **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)** — Same ratio at business/location scale
  instead of customer scale. Cost to open a location (e.g. $100,000 including
  marketing, buildout, staff, recruiting) vs. annual profit at capacity. Wants
  at least 3:1, ideally 5:1 ($100,000 in → $500,000/yr profit). Because his time
  and effort are involved, he wants ~300%/yr minimum, not passive-market 10%.
  Key sub-metrics: payback period, one-year return, profit at capacity.
- **Payback period** — How fast you recover CAC. Four levers to pull it forward:
  (1) charge first + last month upfront, (2) add a fee (onboarding/enrollment/
  setup/initiation), (3) add an upsell (a fee is mandatory, an upsell optional —
  functionally the same: drag revenue upfront), (4) third-party financing that
  fronts the full amount for a piece of the risk. Faster payback = healthier,
  more resilient business in a downturn.
- **Sales velocity × LTGP** — units sold per month × LTGP = where the business
  revenue evens out at its "hypothetical max" if nothing else changes. 100
  customers/mo × $6,000 LTGP → shows the steady state. Reveals whether a
  business will grow into or shrink toward its current revenue.
- **Sales velocity ÷ churn** — gives the hypothetical max number of customers
  (logos) the business will sustain. 100/mo ÷ 10% churn = 1,000 customers. Pairs
  with the previous equation (money at max vs. headcount at max).
- **Churn's leverage on LTV** — churn 5% → 10% cuts lifetime revenue in half;
  10% → 5% doubles it. Take averages over trailing 90 days / 6 months / 12
  months because small businesses are volatile.
- **Bonus #8 — Opportunity = (potential units × LTGP) ÷ risk** — TAM ("total
  addressable market," he says "adjustable") × LTGP sized against risk. TAM is
  "important but not knowable" (Munger/Buffett knowable-vs-important framing);
  companies routinely start niche and expand (Amazon = bookstore → everything
  store; Facebook = college network → everything social). Focus on the knowable,
  important risks.
