# 9 Minutes of Life-Changing Alex Hormozi Advice

## Summary

A short compilation of Alex Hormozi clips stitched together around mindset, effort, and consistency. Alex works through a chain of related ideas: framing decisions by "what would I do if I weren't afraid," treating success as "the only revenge," saying no to almost everything, the multi-year "rocky cut scene" of unrewarded work, playing the hand you're dealt, making hard work itself the goal (who you become), emptying the tank, resetting expectations so hard things feel normal, and consistency as the hardest but most decisive virtue. He leans heavily on borrowed quotes (Warren Buffett, Naval-style aphorism, David Goggins, Jesse Itzler, Charlie Munger) but wraps each in his own framing.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A useful decision filter: **"What would I do if I weren't afraid?"** — the honest answer is usually the big thing you most want but are avoiding, and that's what you should do.
- **Success is the only revenge**, not the best revenge — every other form of revenge is petty and proves you were thinking about those people, which means they win by default. The only real move is to win so big that others shrink into irrelevance.
- Paraphrasing Warren Buffett: the difference between very successful and the most successful people is that **the most successful say no to almost everything**.
- Many success habits are "so simple and so hard" — **do the obvious thing for an extraordinary period of time without convincing yourself you're smarter than you are** (attributes the line to a source he can't quite name — "Naval"/"Neil"; attribution uncertain).
- Build an internal "third-party scorecard": **work harder when no one is watching than when they are**. Going through hardship means you "bear witness to yourself of what you are capable of," which he finds deeply satisfying.
- The **"rocky cut scene"**: almost every successful person he's met went through not a month or a year but **many years of unrewarded work** — doing boring things and sacrificing what others enjoy — for the one thing they want most.
- **Use what you've got.** The perfect condition is whatever one you're in; those are the cards you're dealt, so play the hand. Every possession carries both an advantage and a disadvantage — having nothing makes you dangerous because you have nothing to lose.
- **Hard work is the goal itself**, not a means to an end. Any external reward you'll just grow accustomed to (hedonic treadmill), but your *capacity* to work hard is growable and resilience compounds. So the only true output is **who you become along the way**.
- Keep your **rate of perceived exertion (RPE) at 8–9–10**; the best days of his life were ones where he had **nothing left in the tank** (borrowing Jesse Itzler's "zero" idea). The goal is to empty the tank, not to control where you drive — just drive the car as hard as possible.
- **Only you can judge your own success**, because only you know how much was actually left in the tank.
- **Reset expectations to make hardship bearable.** When people expect a thing to be hard and you tell them "this is what hard feels like," they exhale — getting permission to feel bad makes them feel less bad, because it becomes their new normal.
- **Uncertainty is what makes the pursuit worthwhile** — if you knew you'd succeed, it wouldn't be worth doing. The fear that it won't amount to anything is a fallacy, because who you're becoming is what you're amounting to in real time.
- **Consistency** is, per Charlie Munger's "how to guarantee failure" inversion, one of the hardest virtues (humans are drawn to novelty) but among the most decisive — it's very hard for consistent people not to eventually succeed.
- **Never be fake / never be cringe.** Cites Kanye as someone who "does what he believes"; whatever people say about him, no one thinks he's fake — making "never be fake" a north star.
