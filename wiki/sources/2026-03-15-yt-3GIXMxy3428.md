# Helping a Pest Control Company Create Career Advancement

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A third-generation pest-control owner (~$50M revenue, ~20% EBITDA / ~$8M, growing ~20-21% organically via paid LSA/Google search, referrals, and current-customer upsell, with a smaller M&A and door-knocking mix) wants a path to $1B in sales. Alex reframes the ambition arithmetically — at ~$2M per branch, a billion means ~475 more locations — but the client's real constraint is **manpower on the delivery side**: A-players rocket up and run branches, while the company retains "no average talent," producing constant churn at the lower levels, especially in the hard first 30-45 days. Alex diagnoses the cause as a **too-short career ladder**: with only three promotions (team lead → branch manager → regional manager), a competent-but-not-exceptional employee gets promoted once or twice, hits the ceiling, and leaves citing "no career advancement." His fix is a **micro-promotion career ladder**: take each of the three existing rungs and split it into 9 or 18 sub-rungs by alternating two independent levers — **title** and **pay** — and layering in **perks** (no Saturday nights, better parking spot, choose-your-own calendar, better truck). Junior → junior-plus (pay bump) → a new title (perk) → pay bump → senior → senior-plus, then the whole ladder repeats at title two and title three. The design goal: every employee is always **three-to-six months from the next promotion**, so at the moment they'd otherwise quit there's always something to look forward to. He ties promotions to **objective total-volume milestones** and deliberately makes the **first win a "6-inch putt"** — e.g. 25 stars/jobs to hit junior-plus placed "right there" to survive the highest-churn early window — then expands the reinforcement cycle (next target 100, etc.) so the rewards stretch further out over time. Orientation should show the new hire the whole map ("you are here… this is where you'll be if you do this"). Alex frames this as one of many partial solutions, deliverable in six minutes; the client's takeaway: they simply hadn't been creative — "just give them more ways to win." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's career-ladder framework applies to any role repeated within an organization (sales, delivery, video editors, etc.) and rests on two independent levers you can advance separately: **title** and **pay**.
- As of March 2026, a common retention killer is a career ladder with too few rungs: an employee gets promoted a couple of times, hits the ceiling, and leaves — and "no career advancement" is often the literal reason cited on the way out.
- As of March 2026, the fix is **micro-promotions**: take a 3-rung ladder and expand it to 9 or 18 rungs by alternating title bumps and pay bumps (junior → junior-plus → new title → pay bump → senior → senior-plus), then repeating that sequence at each successive title level.
- As of March 2026, the design target is that every employee is always **three to six months from another promotion**, so career advancement always exists at the exact moment someone would otherwise consider leaving.
- As of March 2026, promotions should be tied to **objective measures**, ideally cumulative **total volume** (e.g. number of jobs/deliveries or "stars"), not subjective judgment.
- As of March 2026, the **first promotion should be a "6-inch putt"** — deliberately easy and placed right at the start — because the biggest churn hits in roughly the first 30-45 days; land an early win ("I think I like my job") and then expand the reinforcement cycle so each subsequent milestone is a little further out.
- As of March 2026, alternating **perks** with titles/pay strengthens the ladder: a title bump alone still motivates ("people will take a title bump and have a steak dinner"), but pairing it with perks (no Saturday-night work, better parking spot, pick your calendar, a better truck) adds cheap, scatterable wins.
- As of March 2026, orientation should map the whole path for the new hire ("you are here; this is where you'll be if you do this"), making the trajectory visible from day one.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames retention as "give them more ways to win" — many small, achievable milestones — and treats this micro-promotion ladder as just one of several elements of the retention solution.
