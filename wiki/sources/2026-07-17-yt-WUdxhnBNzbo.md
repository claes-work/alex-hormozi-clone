# "Don't Open a Second Location Yet"

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A. The questioner owns a **music lesson business** and is "making the
jump from one location to two" because the first is out of space; where she previously
filled a few spots at a time via word of mouth (a grand opening party, plus parent contacts
from a school she used to work at), she now has to fill an entire second facility in a
month, and asks Alex what her first step should be.

Alex's answer pivots on one diagnostic question: "Is the first one really profitable?" When
she admits it isn't, he reverses the premise entirely: "Then I wouldn't open a second
location… it's only going to get less profitable." He grounds this in his own operating
maxim — "we have a saying internally which is **nail it then scale it**" — and his
experience with local chains: his portfolio holds two very large local chains, he ran his
own chain, and he says he has "done relatively large scale three times within local chains"
and looked at far more than he bought. From a hundred-location perspective, "getting to two
does nothing. Nailing the one does everything." The first location has to **print money**
and run on the simplest, most efficient model; a business held together by a hundred tiny
manual tasks means the model itself needs fixing. Concretely: if she's at capacity, she's
mispriced — **raise prices**. Second, she must learn to advertise and acquire customers,
because the one thing every very successful chain shares (if he had to pick just one) is
"an insane **grand opening SOP**" for local; Gym Launch succeeded, he says, because he
could fill up locations in 30 days, and that was his shtick for a long time. The crazy
offer gets tested on the existing location. Then the math: work out LTV to CAC (his
example: $50 to get a kid worth ~$2k, 200 kids per location), divide the perfect-efficiency
number by ~three for realism, and expect **compression to cost a premium** — demand that
might cost $10k spread over a year costs ~$30k in 30 days, a figure that goes into the
grand-opening budget and the return-on-capital calc for the new location, giving a known
week-6/week-12 point where the location turns profitable. Finally, on running locations the
owner doesn't stand in: every corporate-owned service business he's aware of uses a
**10–20% profit share** for the on-site manager ("a boot on the ground who acts like an
owner") — ~10% if they get a base, up to 20% if lucrative — and the manager generally has
to clear **six figures a year** for the seat to be worth it. Standard
acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift outro (10 stages, quiz across product, marketing, sales,
customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance; thank-you page books a call, in-person Vegas
session if they can help).
