# How to Start a Business From Nothing (Thank Me Later)

## Summary

A solo, long-form retrospective in which Alex Hormozi walks through 26 numbered lessons he learned building his gym business — starting from when he made his first $100,000 at 23 opening his first gym location (Huntington Beach). He recounts the early money math ($50K saved, $10K into a gym mastermind before he even owned a gym, $34K on the build-out, ~$6K left with rent at ~$5K/month), the Facebook-ad seminar that bailed him out, and then distills the founding period into tactics on bartering, pre-selling, ad variation, the six-week challenge offer, guarantees, sales psychology, bonuses, partnerships, profit-share for operators, "nail it before you scale it," teaching vs. doing, simplicity, upsells, internal promos, agencies, absolute vs. relative return, lead nurture, channel risk, and managing people. Filmed in his current gym.

## Key claims (Alex-attributed; paraphrase unless quoted)

- **First $100K at 23** opening his first gym location; saved $50K from his job, spent ~$10K to join a gym mastermind *before* owning a gym, ~$34K on the build-out, leaving ~$6K with rent ~$5K/month — roughly one month of runway.
- His first month collected **$4,972 in cash**, which he says exactly matched his $4,972 rent — a coincidence he only discovered years later in his processing records.
- **When you're poor, barter.** He traded gym memberships/PT for cleaning, IT (first printer/scanner), food prep, laundry, and videography. He calls barter the most tax-efficient form of trade (neither party pays tax) and says he still does it.
- **Have no ego / ask for help.** Being 23 and broke let him ask shamelessly; people help beginners. Posturing on Instagram as if you've "got it figured out" makes people *less* willing to help you.
- **Buy access to people already doing it.** The gym mastermind ($10K, ~30–40 gym owners) saved him the entire cost he'd paid on day one via mistakes avoided (locations, square footage, equipment brands). He now always pays to get access to operators up front — "this is why I invested in Skool… communities are the future."
- **Pre-sell.** Market and sell as fast as possible, delay delivery as long as possible, because delivery is when costs start. A killer grand-opening strategy is a core scaling lever for brick-and-mortar.
- **Pay down location risk with ad tests**: spend $500–$1,000 across 5–10 markets to find the lowest lead cost before signing a lease — "pay $10,000 for pre-sale insurance" rather than rolling the dice. If you don't open, "ignore the leads" like 99% of businesses do — but **never take money for something you won't deliver.**
- **Variation in ads.** Local markets are small, so he made 20–30 ad variations a week — same core service, different "wrapping paper" (Lean by Halloween, Slim for Santa, Big Booty Bootcamp, 42-Day Detox, 6-Week Challenge). This taught him volume + variation of creative that later crushed lazy national competitors. Today "the creative itself is part of the targeting" (like attracts like).
- **Six-week challenge offer** = a "win your money back" offer (lose 20 lb in 6 weeks, or 10% of body weight under a BMI threshold) paired with an **unconditional money-back guarantee** ($500 of value or your money back). Over the years only **two people** took the unconditional; ~80% hit the goal at maturity (vs. only 15% on his very first challenge).
- **Guarantee formula**: "if you don't achieve X by Y time, I will Z." The power is in the "or what" (the stakes), not the word "guarantee." His two most-used types: **service-based** (keep working with you until you get there — powerful when delivery has real cost) and **unconditional**.
- The challenge let him collect ~$500 up front plus ~$200–$300 of supplements 48 hours later, so he could **wildly outspend** competitors stuck on $21-for-21-days low-barrier offers.
- **Best-case/worst-case close**: best case you change your life; worst case you work out ~6 weeks for free, tell me I suck, and I refund you — the only thing more guaranteed is that if you leave you won't get where you want to go.
- **Don't take money out too early.** He left cash in the business, poured it into location after location, felt "asset rich and cash poor," on a razor's edge — driven by an "artificial sense of rush" to prove himself to people who didn't care. The richest he ever felt was putting $100K into his personal account.
- Challenge adherence improved not via science but via **operations + accountability** (reminders, public weigh-ins, color-coded meal plans, removing calorie counting). His physician dad diagnosed early failures simply: "they're just not adhering."
- **Ask specific questions** to get honest answers ("How many days out of seven did you eat all three meals exactly to plan with nothing else?").
- **Sell the vacation, not the plane flight.** Talk about the pain of their current state and the experience of the result; don't narrate the painful process. "Sell at the point of greatest deprivation."
- **Don't discount the core product** ("we don't negotiate with price terrorists"). Instead add a bonus, or change the terms (refer 3 friends / leave reviews for the discount). Build a **stack of bonuses** matched to specific objections (travel meal plan, budget grocery guide, shakes guide, at-home workout guide).
- **Overspending mistake**: his 2nd location cost $250K (~8x the first, glass wall alone ~= cost of the whole first gym) and made the *same* money — because customers came for **accountability, training quality, and community ("the third place")**, not granite. Understand the real "job to be done."
- **Proof system**: collect *and* display social proof — screenshot reviews, buy $1 frames, cover the sales room in before/afters. Ask "how much proof would you need to see in order to believe?" to surface the real fear behind the objection (people are afraid of being made fun of, looking stupid). The **flywheel**: get customers → get results → use results to get more customers.
- **Partners (TEAM acronym)**: a partner should bring **T**ime, **E**xpertise, **A**ssets, or **M**oney you don't have. Worst partnerships = two friends with the same expertise. Take a reasonable **salary** if you're the working partner (he lived on ~$30K/yr for 2 years). **Early equity is the most expensive** — don't reflexively split evenly; slice by risk.
- **Owners / profit share**: every privately scaled brick-and-mortar he's seen gives operators a profit share (Chick-fil-A model). Method: set on-target earnings, make ~half base and ~half a percentage of location profit so operators feel top- and bottom-line impact and have a career path.
- **Nail it before you scale it.** Don't start location #2 until #1 runs without you *and* has grown without you for **6 months straight**. Pair **responsibility with authority** (e.g., up to $1,000 discretionary to solve problems; up to 100% of a customer's last-month spend to fix escalations). A one-month vacation is a great stress test.
- **Unscalable beginnings are OK**: he kept one PT client paying ~$4K/month (10 hrs/week) to live on and fund aggressive expansion — as long as it's just high-ticket time (not a second business, "not the woman in the red dress") and doesn't become part of what you "nail."
- **Doing ≠ teaching.** He had to shift identity from "best salesperson/MVP" to "best sales/marketing/ops *teacher*." The turning point: employees he'd trained closed sales without him ($750 in one day) and he pulled the car over, emotional — "I had tasted freedom."
- **Simple scales, fancy fails.** His entire sales training was ~1 hour 15 min; the **CLOSER** framework came from cutting until a beginner could close after watching once. Find the absolute minimum required.
- **Zero-cost upsells**: sell high-margin products alongside the service (supplements) to liquidate ad cost up front, so service revenue is "gravy." You can even *sell* a guarantee (extra 10%+) — near-zero cost since ~1 in 10 claims it.
- **Internal plays**: run a defined promo to your existing list ~quarterly (Big Booty Bootcamp: 30 people × $300 = $9,000 for six 90-min sessions, ~98% margin). Almost all profit because acquisition is already paid. Cites average micro-gym: ~$360K/yr revenue, ~12.5% margins, owner ~$30–40K working ~80 hrs/week.
- He deliberately avoids explaining **why** things work — "I have no idea, and I don't think anyone else does either" (no split tests/research) — and just collects a "big bag of things that do work."
- **Core four of a business**: only two things are truly the business — **acquiring customers** and **delivering to customers**; everything else can be outsourced. Leads Book lists 8 acquisition methods (warm outreach, cold outreach, content, paid ads, customer referrals, affiliates, agencies, employees).
- **Agencies**: use them to *learn* the skill, not to depend on them. He paid an agency owner $750/hr for ~6–8 one-on-one calls (~$6K, one month's retainer) and learned national campaigns + retargeting, then dropped him. "What would it take?" is the magic question — "It's American. There's a price."
- **Absolute vs. relative return**: he obsessed over ROAS (10:1 on $1,500) while a competitor spent $500/day at 4:1 and netted far more. Focus on **total profit**, not the ratio.
- **Don't break what works.** He cut session times/consolidated to fit more people and churned out ~1/3 of customers. If people love it and it's profitable, don't touch the product — open another location; if it's not profitable, fix *price*, not product. "Pricing is easy to change. Changing what you deliver is super tough."
- **Work your leads.** Nurture is a bigger lever than close rate. Speed matters — cites the "call within 60 seconds = 391% (~5x) increase" stat; a business owner getting 2–3 leads/day closed **55% of leads** by paying one person full-time whose only job is to "be available" and call instantly.
- **Never rely on another business to feed yours.** Single-channel risk (fine early, one channel scales to ~$1M/month) is different from another-business risk (Groupon dependency killed his mastermind peers when it stopped working).
- **People (24–26)**: (24) to get great people, **pay more** or grind through 10x more candidates; (25) **platinum rule** — treat others how *they* want to be treated (his best manager quit to be a trainer: "I'll just be poor then"); (26) you *can* be friends with employees if you keep a clear line ("friend hat" / "boss hat").
