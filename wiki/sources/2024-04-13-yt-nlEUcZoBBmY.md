# Alex Hormozi shares the blueprint to becoming more valuable

## Summary
In this clip from a multi-speaker conversation, Alex Hormozi explains the "give first" strategy he used to build his network and get into rooms he felt he "didn't deserve to be in." Early on, when the only skills he had were writing sales scripts and building sales processes, he treated every member of his networking groups (e.g. a gym-owner group) as if they were a paying client — doing real, over-delivered work reviewing their scripts and processes and handing it to them for free. Each act of giving, he argues, creates a one-sided "IOU" that compounds into goodwill he could later cash in (e.g. learning Facebook ads from someone he had previously helped). His core mechanic for networking upward: don't ask a busy person "what value can I provide you," because that offloads the work onto them — instead figure out what you can do for them, do it, and give it to them unasked. He closes on the idea that genuine "players" recognize one another by hunger and work ethic, not by rented Lambos or borrowed status symbols.

## Key claims
- The fastest way to become valuable in a network is to give first: pick your niche skill and over-deliver it to people for free, as if it were a paid project.
- Every act of giving creates a one-sided "IOU" — value someone owes you that is "incredibly valuable" and "can come in handy later down the road."
- Not everyone returns the favor, and that is fine: if they don't reciprocate, you've simply helped someone (which is what most entrepreneurs claim they want to do anyway) and they'll have nothing bad to say about you — you stay "net positive on everything."
- When networking with people ahead of you, never ask them "what value can I provide you" — that forces them to do the homework of figuring out how to use you. Instead, do the hard work yourself: figure out what you can provide, then just do it and give it to them.
- Preparation is a massive, underrated leverage point: doing homework before a call (roughly 20 minutes of prep) makes you seem dramatically more intelligent and capable.
- You should never show up to a call and only then start thinking about the other person's problem — do the homework beforehand.
- Over-delivering earns feedback and access: "anyone will help someone who takes initiative" / "starts helping themselves"; even if your work isn't good, you get valuable feedback.
- Real "players" recognize one another and are unimpressed by rented status symbols (Lambos, blue checks, Rolexes) — those only impress people who don't know what players actually look like.
- Young winners are hungry "savages" in the grind who give first to as many people as they can, over-delivering because they know the goodwill will compound.
- This single skill — giving first / over-delivering — is what let Alex get into rooms he felt he didn't deserve to be in.
