# I want to grow my business but I don't want to sacrifice family time

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A roofing/exterior-remodeling owner (~$6M this year, wants $100M) names his blockers as comfort, distractions, and fear — he's replaced himself (works 2–3 hrs/week), fears losing family time, and is scattered across side businesses (junk removal, real estate, gold/silver — he cites ADHD). Alex's core teaching: **regret is imaginary** — "we imagine the upside that we didn't get without also considering the downside that we didn't suffer to get it." Trade-offs are preference, not right/wrong (his "cookies vs. six-pack" analogy); dissatisfaction comes from wanting both, so "either want less or trade more." To go 6→100 without working more, take a short-term profit hit to buy high-level talent — you're "graduating into the who game," and "there's levels of whos" (he narrates his own ladder from minimum-wage labor → first $50k → six-figure → $250k → $500k → $1M+ employees). He quotes his president Sharone: "the best talent's always in the future." On the trade: give up short-term profit (recoverable) rather than family time (not recoverable long-term). On distractions: keep passive investments passive ("keep active active, keep passive passive"; don't let a hobby eat your days) — and "it's only a problem if you decide it's a problem." He closes with the Buffett 20-punch-card frame applied to entrepreneurs: he figures he has only ~30–40 productive years / "four or five big seasons," so he can't pursue that many things. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's regret theory: "regrets come when we imagine the upside that we don't have without taking into account the cost that we didn't suffer" — regret is "not real" because we picture the upside without the trade-off.
- As of March 2026, trade-offs are preference, not right/wrong ("if I like cookies… and I also want a six-pack, I just prefer cookies"); dissatisfaction "comes from wanting both," so "either want less or trade more."
- As of March 2026, to scale 6→100 without working more: "take a short-term hit in terms of profitability to bring in the level of talent" to expand on your behalf — feasible if you're the kind of person (character/vision) that talent wants to follow.
- As of March 2026, he frames hiring as "the who game" with "levels of whos," narrating his own ladder: minimum-wage labor → first $50k employee → first six-figure → $250k → $500k → first multi-million-dollar-per-year employee.
- As of March 2026, he quotes his president **Sharone**: "the best talent's always in the future" — "the best people are always ahead of you, not behind you."
- As of March 2026, the trade prioritization: give up *short-term profit* (recoverable long-term) rather than *family time* ("you won't make up family time in the long term. You can make the profit up").
- As of March 2026, on distractions/investments: "keep active active, keep passive passive" — don't make passive money active (favors REITs/funds with good partners); a side interest is only a distraction if it "changes… what you do" or eats your days.
- As of March 2026: "it's only a problem if you decide it's a problem" — you may simply enjoy the ADD-driven side pursuits and be sacrificing goals for them; "the cost of the big thing is all the new stuff you have to give up."
- As of March 2026 (personal, self-reported): he estimates "30 or 40 more productive years at most… four or five big seasons/runs in me left," so "I don't have unlimited shots on goal."
- As of March 2026, he applies **Warren Buffett's 20-punch-card** idea to entrepreneurship: if you had only 20 lifetime investments you could never sell, you'd choose far better — same for which business opportunities to pursue.
