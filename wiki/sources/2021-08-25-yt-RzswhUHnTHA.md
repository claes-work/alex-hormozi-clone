# I spent $2,000,000 and it didn't make me happy — 2021-08-25

## Summary

In this solo talk, Hormozi reveals that he personally gave away $2 million to the
After-School All-Stars charity and reports, candidly, that the gift did not make him
feel better — he had donated partly to *feel* something and it "didn't actually make me
feel that much better." He uses that admission to unpack a conversation with Dr. John
Berardi about meaning, arguing that insisting work or life "must be meaningful" creates
a deficiency between where you are and where you think you should be, and that giving
himself permission to *not* find meaning — acting from his values instead — has been
freeing.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021-08-25)

- He personally donated $2 million to After-School All-Stars: ~250,000 fitness bands /
  Fitbit-style devices to kids in underprivileged cities to encourage fitness, plus
  ~$1.2 million to fund staffing. (self-reported, 2021)
- He did it partly because he "wanted to feel better," and reports it did not make him
  feel much better — he shares this to keep the channel "100% real." (self-reported, 2021)
- A four-hour conversation with Dr. John Berardi (who sold the majority stake in his
  company for over nine figures) reframed his thinking; Berardi asked "why do you think
  work needs to be meaningful?" and "why do you think life needs to be meaningful?"
  (paraphrase, 2021)
- Framework: demanding that life "must be meaningful" creates a desire/deficiency between
  current reality and an imagined ideal — so you manufacture the very deficiency you were
  trying to escape. (paraphrase, 2021)
- Personal history of this pattern: in college he grew so sick of a girlfriend's
  happiness-obsession that he adopted the mantra "fuck happiness," just got the work
  done, and paradoxically felt more joy. Later he replaced "happiness" with "meaning /
  fulfillment" as a new placeholder — another deficiency equation. (self-reported, 2021)
- His resolution: give himself permission to *not* find meaning; act in alignment with
  chosen values in the present rather than chasing meaning cast into the future.
  (paraphrase, 2021)
- Legacy view: he believes that when we die, nothing happens, and that "legacy is a
  myth." He argues wealth without the skill of acquiring it "ruins people." (paraphrase, 2021)
- He and Leila signed their wills a few days prior; with no kids, they are donating the
  fortune when they die. Even if he had kids he wouldn't give it to them. (self-reported, 2021)
- Anti-inheritance / dilution argument: over ~100 generations a genetic line halves
  repeatedly, so descendants are barely related to you, won't remember you, and would be
  "ruined" by wealth they didn't generate. (paraphrase, 2021)
- His portfolio (acquisition.com) does ~$85 million/year; his model is to give away the
  material (books at 99 cents, courses) with the aim of investing in the businesses that
  succeed using it. (self-reported, 2021)

## Biographical note

This video documents a specific, dated philanthropic act — a **$2 million personal
donation to After-School All-Stars** (~250,000 fitness bands plus ~$1.2M for staffing) —
and the signing of his and Leila's wills (~August 2021), leaving their estate to charity
because they have no children. It extends the frugality/"haves-over-wants" thread from
[[2021-05-12-yt-vKnAWYqMJ10]] into philanthropy: money, and even giving it away, does not
buy the feeling he expected. Both this video and its companion [[2021-08-30-yt-R9gHvJxHRhA]]
invoke the Magic: The Gathering "burning wish" card and the "wealth ruins those who
didn't earn it" argument. All figures are self-reported.
