# Win So Big You Shrink Them Into Irrelevance

## Summary

Alex Hormozi reframes "success is the best revenge" as becoming so successful that the
old targets of revenge stop mattering. He says people can use whatever fuel they have,
including anger, shame, fear, or anxiety, but should later evaluate whether that fuel
still serves the life and outcomes they want.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex credits a ninth-grade tennis coach who taught him to lift weights with challenging
  his teenage revenge fantasies.
- He says real revenge is not showing up at a reunion to prove people wrong; it is
  winning so much that they become irrelevant.
- Thinking about an enemy every day means that enemy still has power.
- People should not overanalyze their fuel if it helps them begin useful behavior.
- Negative emotions such as revenge, shame, fear, anxiety, or anger can be used if they
  produce wanted action.
- Over time, a person can shift from the original negative catalyst to being rewarded by
  the behavior itself.
- Alex says he avoids moral judgment about other people's chosen lives as long as they are
  getting what they want and not harming others.
- He frames public criticism as attacks on an imagined person, not necessarily on the real
  individual.
- A belief's usefulness matters more to him than the emotional purity of its source.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Win so big that you shrink them into irrelevance."

> "Don't analyze your fuel."

> "If I actually thought about somebody every day, they won."
