# I Sell Roofing. How Do I Get to $10M per Year?

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A metro-Detroit roofing owner doing ~$3M/year (previously $5M two years running as a storm/insurance contractor) wants to reach $10M. Storms "vanished" from his market, forcing a pivot into retail/residential. For eight years 100% of lead gen was door-to-door; his limiting belief that buying leads was "sacrilegious" broke last October when he went "lead curious" and started purchasing leads — but he "got slaughtered," with CAC around $7,500 to acquire a customer worth ~$6,500 on average, which "does not work." Worse, when he stacked purchased leads onto his existing door-knocking reps' calendars, they got "lazy, fat" and stopped knocking because inbound was easier, tanking the outbound engine. Alex's core diagnosis: **outbound and inbound must be separate teams.** Inbound is what a rep *graduates* to — only the "absolute savages" who can close everything earn the right to be fed the expensive, hard-won leads, because those leads can't be wasted. He prescribes putting everybody back on the streets, taking the one best closer (or hiring one) as the sole inbound rep, and funneling all purchased/qualified leads to that one person so the owner can iterate faster with fewer new variables. He notes inbound commissions must be structured differently (you're paying for the leads) — more volume, lower per-deal, but more reliable income (a bad day is two closes, not zero), which appeals to reps with families, plus "you don't have to be in the sun." Finally he tells the owner to hire a real marketer — "probably pay more than you expect" — because a roofing business is fundamentally sales and marketing, so those core functions belong in-house. The owner concludes he'd been "working on the wrong problem." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's rule for scaling home-services sales: outbound (door-knocking) and inbound (fed leads) should be run as **separate teams**, not the same reps doing both.
- As of March 2026, mixing purchased leads into a door-knocking team makes the reps stop prospecting because inbound is easier — the "lazy, fat" failure mode that kills the outbound engine.
- As of March 2026, inbound is a role reps **graduate into**: only the top closers who "cannot waste" leads earn access, because bought leads are the most expensive and hardest-won leads a business has.
- As of March 2026, inbound commission structure must differ from outbound because you pay for the leads — it's more volume at a lower per-deal rate, but more reliable income (a bad day is two closes vs. zero), which suits reps with families.
- As of March 2026, when you're new to inbound, funnel everything to one savage closer rather than spreading across many new reps — fewer "news" means fewer things break, and it isolates the variables (offer/lead source, lead-to-conversation process) so you can iterate faster.
- As of March 2026, in this case CAC of ~$7,500 against ~$6,500 average customer value "does not work" and isn't sustainable — cited as why buying leads through outside agencies failed.
- As of March 2026, a roofing/home-services business is fundamentally sales and marketing, so the core functions (including a competent marketer, who you should "probably pay more than you expect") should be built in-house rather than outsourced to agencies.
