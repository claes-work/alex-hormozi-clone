# I SPENT $100,000 on courses, so you don't have to... (2021-08-09)

## Summary

In this early (2021) YouTube video, Alex Hormozi argues that courses fail to make
people money not because they are bad, but because a course only teaches one "brick"
in a larger bridge of skills a business needs — and buyers are usually missing a
different brick. He reframes buyer frustration as an expectations problem (comparing
online courses favorably to a four-year Spanish class or degree that produced no
usable skill), champions e-learning as the "new world" of practical education, and
shares two decision-making rules he and Leila use: "no diligence, no deal," and never
judging a person he does not know firsthand. Biographical texture: he states he bought
courses on YouTube ads, copywriting, sales (multiple), plus mentorships, workshops and
seminars, and cites paying Grant Cardone $120,000 for four hours as an example of
investing in his own education.

## Key lessons

- **Business is a bridge (or a pipe), not a single skill.** Each skill is one brick;
  one missing brick stops you crossing. Courses fail when they reinforce a brick you
  already have instead of the one you lack.
- **Attack the gap, not the fun.** Most people keep learning the topics they enjoy
  rather than the skills they lack; find the weak link and "attack it ruthlessly."
- **Know every function enough to be dangerous.** Even a specialist solopreneur/small
  team must cover acquisition, product and fulfillment — enough to judge whether a hire
  is good.
- **Frustration with courses is really an expectations problem.** Buying a course does
  not guarantee success; students carry responsibility, and by the bell curve half of
  buyers are below average and will get below-average results.
- **A good teacher shifts the bell curve but never removes the tails.** The better the
  teacher, the less skilled the student needs to be — but outliers on both ends remain.
- **Take responsibility for both sides.** As a seller: aim for 100% of customers to get
  value; blaming customers never improves a business. As a buyer: own your side too —
  "winners take responsibility for both sides of the equation."
- **Guarantee your own ROI on learning.** Hormozi says he always profits from a course
  because he will find something to learn — even bad courses teach him what not to do
  when building his own.
- **Don't judge people you don't know firsthand.** Your own past judgment was an
  idiot's, so is the judge's; writing off a whole person makes you "miss all the gold"
  they could offer. People exist on continuums, not binary good/bad.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021-08-09)

- A course rarely makes you money on its own because the skill it teaches is a subset,
  not the whole, of what earning money requires (subset/superset relationship). [2021]
- Business should be seen as a pipe turning eyeballs into money (headlines → ads → copy
  → clicks → conversions → sales → upsells); the narrowest tube limits total
  throughput. [2021]
- The "last course that finally worked" was usually just the last brick on a bridge
  earlier teachers helped build — so people should stop resenting earlier teachers. [2021]
- People rage at internet courses but not at four years of school Spanish they can't
  speak — the difference is expectations, not course quality. [2021]
- Humans and classes fall on a bell curve; roughly half of any course's students are
  below average, so about half getting bad results is statistically expected. [2021]
- Over half the companies in his portfolio are education/training companies; he
  specifically likes vocational niche training (how to run a gym, an auto shop, a
  plumbing business) because those skills aren't taught elsewhere. [2021]
- The traditional education system has failed and is wildly overpriced: a $100k,
  four-year degree can leave a student with debt and no additional job qualification —
  the same jobs are available before and after. [2021]
- acquisition.com's purpose (in his framing) is to build "the new world of education" —
  teaching skills people can actually feed themselves with. [2021]
- He graduated from Vanderbilt magna cum laude in three years and worked hard for good
  grades — he does not have the "teachers never understood me" entrepreneur origin
  story. [2021, self-reported]
- Nothing he learned in any Vanderbilt class was more valuable than any e-learning
  course he ever bought. [2021, self-reported opinion]
- He has bought courses on YouTube ads, copywriting, and sales (multiple), plus
  mentorships, workshops and seminars. [2021, self-reported]
- Spending $100k on the most-vetted e-learning over four years would produce
  incomparably more income than a $100k four-year degree. [2021, opinion]
- He and Leila reduce decision fatigue by codifying repeat decisions as if-then
  "algorithms," e.g. the money rule "no diligence, no deal," and both must agree or they
  don't proceed. [2021]
- His people-judgment rule: don't judge anyone he doesn't know firsthand — because his
  own judgment is "shaky at best" and both he and the person judging are "idiots"
  relative to their future selves. [2021]
- He paid Grant Cardone $120,000 for four hours (often cited by critics as $30k/hour);
  most critics have never met Grant, so they judge without firsthand knowledge. [2021,
  self-reported]
- Removing expectations makes him happier: expecting nothing leaves room to be
  pleasantly surprised and less room to be let down. [2021]
- At time of recording he states acquisition.com does about $85 million a year, with the
  goal of a billion-dollar portfolio and to "recreate the middle class in America." [2021,
  self-reported]
