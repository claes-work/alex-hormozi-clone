# Market to the Top, Sell to Everyone

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A. The questioner (introduces himself as **Luke Miller**) sells online
coaching, training and nutrition to **physique athletes**; team top-line was ~$900k last
year, projected ~$1.1M this year, and he wants $10M. He also runs "J3 University," a
coursework/education arm that built the brand's reputation. His stated problems: **lead
flow** (all lead gen so far is organic — daily content on his personal Instagram and
YouTube; paid ads run only on the university side, where they "do great") and doubt about
the **vertical itself** ("bodybuilders are not the richest people in the world").

Alex answers in four moves. **(1) Aspirational anchoring:** marketing to the top end of a
market is an asset, not a ceiling — you scoop up everyone *beneath* the anchor who aspires
to it; you only lose the people above it. He illustrates with North Face (a guy on Everest,
not a mom walking from the car to the door) and Range Rover (shown climbing mountains,
bought by people who think "it'll probably be okay if it snows"), and maps it to
Acquisition.com ("we have a portfolio… people who aren't necessarily portfolio-ready can
still get benefit"). **(2)** Luke's worry that he's no longer a competitor himself is
dismissed flatly — "0%. When I ran Gym Launch, I owned zero gyms"; customers care about
what you can do for them. **(3) The advertising flywheel:** build a UGC/testimonial loop
into every business — one one-time input of founder-made advertising gets customers, the
customers produce testimonials and reviews, and those spin the wheel of advertising, a
"self-licking ice cream cone"; once testimonial volume surpasses the creative volume you
need, advertising becomes self-sustaining. **(4) Front-end/back-end diagnosis:** Alex
retells the hair-extensions coach case — 13:1 LTV:CAC on her business-coaching offer vs.
37:1 on teaching stylists to do extensions, with a ~20x bigger TAM — where the fix was to
turn off ads on the low-ratio offer, pour spend into the high-ratio big-TAM front end, and
focus on **ascending** customers into the back end. Applied to Luke: the university (where
ads already work) *is* the front end; the missing piece is a **choreographed ascension
process from education into service**, which "becomes the business" and should make the
already-working education ads perform ~3x better via blended LTV off a single acquisition
cost. Because the university has different owners than the coaching business, Alex says to
treat it as an **arm's-length affiliate relationship with a kickback** — at that point the
coaching business gets effectively free customers whose only cost is the kickback. The
video ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift CTA (10 stages, functions
across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-16. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Market to the top, sell to everyone beneath it.** Anchoring your brand at the top end
  of a market (elite physique athletes) is a good thing: aspirational positioning scoops
  up everyone below the anchor who aspires to it; you only fail to capture the people
  above it. If aspirational non-athletes inquire and you take them as customers, there is
  no problem with branding that way.
- **Aspirational-anchor examples: North Face and Range Rover.** North Face shows a man on
  Everest, not a mom walking from the car to the door — the mom reasons "if it keeps him
  warm there, it'll keep me warm to the house." Range Rover shows mountain driving; the
  buyer thinks "it'll probably be okay if it snows." (Same analogy pair as in
  [[2026-03-26-yt-iEEJ3aVtaFs]] and the Everest-jacket proof logic in
  [[../topics/marketing/proof]].)
- **The founder not being a practitioner/competitor anymore is irrelevant to positioning
  — "0%."** Customers care about what you can do for them, not whether you still compete.
  Proof point: "When I ran Gym Launch, I owned zero gyms."
- **Build the testimonial→ad loop ("self-licking ice cream cone") into every business.**
  One-time founder input creates advertising → advertising gets customers → customers
  produce testimonials and reviews → those become the new advertising. With a cadence or
  incentive for customers to make testimonials/advertisements, and testimonial volume
  exceeding the ad-creative volume needed for new business, advertising becomes
  self-sustaining. Alex says this is "always my goal" and he encourages every business to
  have it.
- **UGC-led creative for the coaching offer.** For Luke's coaching-business ads, UGC "for
  sure would be great."
- **Two offers with shared customers are usually a front end and a back end, not two
  businesses.** The hair-extensions coach case: she thought she had two businesses to
  advertise; the diagnosis was a front end (teach the skill) and back end (business
  coaching) she hadn't recognized as such.
- **Put ad spend on the high-LTV:CAC, big-TAM front end; ascend into the back end.** In
  the retold case: turn off ads on the 13:1 business-coaching offer, turn on ads to the
  37:1 skill-teaching offer with a ~20x bigger TAM, bring everyone in there, and focus all
  effort on ascending customers from profitable acquisition into the higher-level back
  end — accepting that not all customers will be a fit, "and that's okay."
  > ⚠️ CONTRADICTION: In the earlier retelling of the same client story
  > ([[2024-06-30-yt-jzKpAtzKQ54]], 2024-06-30) the ratios are **34:1 front end / 7:1
  > coaching**; here (2026-07-16) they are **37:1 / 13:1**. Same story and same
  > prescription, inconsistent numbers across retellings — treat the exact ratios as
  > illustrative, not data.
- **The prescription for Luke: a choreographed ascension process from education into
  service.** The university ads already work, so the real gap is the ascension motion from
  education (front end) into coaching (back end) — "and then that becomes the business."
- **Blended LTV multiplies the return on the same acquisition cost.** Once the ascension
  is fixed, the already-good education-side results should do roughly **3x better**,
  because a blended LTV (education + ascended coaching revenue) sits on the single cost to
  acquire a customer.
- **Separate ownership? Treat the front end as an arm's-length affiliate.** Since the
  university has owners who aren't in the coaching business, structure it as an affiliate
  relationship with a kickback — then the coaching business's customers are effectively
  free, and the only cost is the kickback.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours of reviewing portfolio companies, distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz
  that locates a business and prescribes actions per function (product, marketing, sales,
  customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance); thank-you page books a call, with an
  in-person Vegas session if they can help.
