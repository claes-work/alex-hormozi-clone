# How to Scale Your Outbound System

## Summary

Short two-speaker clip in which an interviewer asks Alex Hormozi about scaling outbound and about *when* a company should start an outbound program. Despite the title, this is a **framing clip, not a mechanics tutorial** — it contains no concrete outbound-scaling levers (no volume/domain/AI-SDR/rep numbers). Two ideas dominate.

First, why outbound is uniquely hard to crack at scale: outbound goes after the **most valuable and therefore most heavily protected attention resource**, so it sits "one degree separated" from the laws that regulate it. The most effective outreach methods are essentially illegal — regulation deliberately **handicaps outreach** so people can't do it, otherwise everyone's phone would be spammed 24/7. Stripped down, all outbound is trying to do is **approximate someone you know reaching out to you** — and it takes "a ton of technology and a ton of legal skirting" just to accomplish the one core thing: texting someone an offer and getting them to read it.

Second, on *when* to start outbound (the interviewer frames a $10M–$20M-revenue executive who wants to launch a "hub" program): Alex refuses to give a revenue-threshold answer and refuses to make a testimonial for the interviewer's service. Instead he applies his standard **risk-adjusted-return meta-frame** — the same lens used for any scaling/channel decision (outbound vs. ads vs. content vs. affiliates): estimate the expected return, your confidence/likelihood it occurs, and the resources required; combine into a risk-adjusted return; then allocate attention to the **highest-return vehicle**, whatever it is. He extends this to **vendor/path selection**: he'll happily pay *more* for a provider with a proven track record of taking a business like his to where he wants to go, because higher confidence means a higher risk-adjusted return even at a higher price. He closes with a B2B price reframe illustrated by a **penny-stock-vs-Apple analogy**: a cheaper vendor is less likely to help you hit the goal, so it's actually the *riskier* move — a 10% return on Apple beats a "100x" return on a penny stock once you weight for probability.
