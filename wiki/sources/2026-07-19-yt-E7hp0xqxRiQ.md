# Helping a $7M Landscaper Charge Aspen Prices

## Summary
In this short coaching exchange, a business owner named Rio Crandall describes running a high-end residential landscaping and maintenance company in Aspen, Colorado, doing $7M in revenue with a goal of reaching $30M. She names two blockers: her pricing model and a shortage of talent to scale. Alex Hormozi quickly reframes the pricing issue as "the best problem," arguing that a luxury service sold to affluent clients is being priced from the owner's own psychology rather than the customer's, and that she can simply charge higher numbers immediately and let churn signal the ceiling.

On the economics, Hormozi notes the business ran 11% net margins last year and that a 20% price increase would roughly triple profits while being sustainable without materially changing operations. Because the revenue is roughly 70% recurring maintenance behind a large one-time upfront landscaping install, he argues the monthly maintenance fee is anchored against the large install price (e.g., $80K–$180K) and therefore feels trivial to the client — almost like insurance on what they just paid for. He advises concentrating the increase on the back-end recurring maintenance, which the owner also preferred.

On the talent shortage in a small, isolated market, Hormozi advises advertising nationally and paying for people to relocate via a moving package that functions like a signing bonus, framing the pitch around working outside in an appealing location. The clip closes with a promotional segment for the $100M scaling roadmap at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) When a service is priced from the owner's own sense of what feels expensive rather than the customer's, the owner is effectively "selling out of their own pocket" and is likely underpriced.
- (2026-07-19) Being underpriced for a luxury service is "the best problem," because the fix is simply to state a higher number — pricing is that easy to change.
- (2026-07-19) The right time to push pricing higher is now; the correct ceiling signal is rising churn, which marks the top limit.
- (2026-07-19) The company runs about 11% net margins. _(attribution: audience — caller, reporting her own numbers)_
- (2026-07-19) At ~11% margins, a 20% price increase would roughly triple profits — a material change — and could likely be sustained without significantly changing the business.
- (2026-07-19) Roughly 70% of the revenue is recurring maintenance, with the balance in one-time upfront landscaping installs. _(attribution: audience — caller)_
- (2026-07-19) In a landscaping business, the large involved upfront install (e.g., $80K or $180K) creates a natural anchor; the recurring monthly maintenance fee ($500–$1,000/month) feels trivial by comparison, leaving substantial room to raise it.
- (2026-07-19) Ongoing maintenance can be positioned as "insurance" on the expensive install the client just paid for, which justifies pricing power on the recurring fee.
- (2026-07-19) Given the anchoring dynamic, the price increase is best concentrated on the back-end recurring maintenance rather than the front-end install.
- (2026-07-19) A small, isolated local talent pool can be solved by advertising nationally and paying for candidates to relocate.
- (2026-07-19) A relocation/moving package functions like a signing bonus, is negotiable back and forth, and can be sold on the appeal of working outside in a desirable location.
