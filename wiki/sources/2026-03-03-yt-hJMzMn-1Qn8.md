# The Best ROI for Your Business

## Summary
Solo clip. Alex argues that the **best ROI in business is still talent** — the cost of acquiring talent versus the profit that talent generates. His worked example: a dentist whose specialty dentists each throw off $700k/year in gross profit was spending only ~$2,500 on recruiting and complaining he couldn't find them. Alex reframes it as an investment — if an asset would cash-flow $700k/year, you'd happily pay a large one-time sum for it, so paying $50k–$150k to a headhunter (plus relocation bonuses) to land that talent is obviously worth it. The dentist needed four such hires to double the business; Alex points out that taking this year's profit from $4.6M down to $4M to spend $600k landing four A-players (at ~$150k each) is trivially worth it. He generalizes: talent's return is *better than ever in the age of AI* because a star who can leverage technology gets even more output (cites Meta's ~$100M individual pay packages — not crazy if that person unlocks billions in value). Two angles: **micro** — bringing someone in gives you time back (work you no longer do); **macro** — you can't live someone's 10–20 years of experience, so you buy that lifetime of experience and apply it to your business. The bigger the business gets, the more it's about *assembling talent that builds the business* than personally building it. His limiter thesis: your rate of growth is capped by your ability to learn, because "no one will come work for you who is better than you" (caveat: better than you at *everything* — specialists are better at their function). **Excellence attracts excellence** — to attract more A-players, become a deeper A-player. Uses Elon as the archetype: his superstar skill is being so good at engineering that he attracts the best engineers, letting him build the best engineering companies across six firms he couldn't personally run. The bottleneck is almost always the owner: "we are the limiter of the business." Ends with the $100M scaling roadmap promo (acquisition.com/roadmap).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's stated best ROI in business is **talent** — measured as the cost to acquire the talent versus the profit that talent will generate.
- As of March 2026, he reframes hiring as an investment purchase: if an asset would reliably cash-flow $700k/year, a rational buyer would pay a large one-time price for it, so paying $50k–$150k to a headhunter to land talent of that caliber is clearly justified.
- As of March 2026 (dentist example): each specialty dentist produced ~$700k/year in gross profit; the owner (~$4.6M annual profit) was spending only ~$2,500 on recruiting; spending ~$600k to land four A-players (dropping this year's profit to ~$4M) to double the business is obviously worth it.
- As of March 2026, the return on talent is **better than ever "in the age of AI"** because a star employee who can use technology gets even more leverage; Meta paying ~$100M packages for single individuals is rational if that person unlocks billions in value.
- As of March 2026, talent pays off on two levels: **micro** — you get time back (work you no longer have to do); **macro** — you buy a lifetime of experience you could never live yourself and apply it to your business.
- As of March 2026, the bigger a business gets, the more growth is about **assembling the talent that builds the business** than about personally building it.
- As of March 2026, his limiter thesis: your rate of business growth is capped by your ability to learn, because no one better than you (at everything) will come work for you — though specialists who are better at their specific function will, if they respect your depth.
- As of March 2026, **"excellence attracts excellence"**: to attract more A-players you must become a deeper A-player; five of the best teammates in the world would make one of the most valuable businesses in the world.
- As of March 2026 (Elon example): his superstar skill is being so good at engineering that he attracts the best engineers, enabling the best engineering companies; he can't personally make the vast majority of decisions across six companies, so a strong brand and elite skill let the best in the world want to work for him.
- As of March 2026, the owner is the constraint: because you're not the best in the world, the best don't want to work for you — "we are the limiter of the business."
