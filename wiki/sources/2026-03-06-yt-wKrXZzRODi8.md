# It Just Takes More Work

## Summary
Solo monologue on effort, volume, and what separates people who win. Alex's thesis: people chronically underestimate how much work success takes — "you think you understand how much work it is," but when he's met people further ahead than him, he's watched them work five to ten times harder. He anchors the "champion mindset" on a scene from *Invictus*: playing isn't about waiting for perfect circumstances, "playing creates perfect circumstances" — you learn to play when the chips are stacked against you. Work ethic, like values, is only real when tested: the test is whether you can work when tired, distracted, or doing things you find unpleasant. He criticizes the modern intolerance for discomfort (a new sales hire quit after three days — "you don't even know what's for you") and positions himself as a deliberate contrarian in the entrepreneur space: passion is *not* what carries you through hard times — **desire** is, the want to drag an idea "from your mind into the world." He extends the metaphor to labor/childbirth: work is literally labor — painful, done kicking and screaming, with life on the other side. His central visual model: excitement → neutral → worried/stressed, and **work only begins once motivation stops** — borrowing the old-school bodybuilding line that "once you have achieved failure, that is where your working reps begin." Measuring effort from that point (after motivation fades) changes how you judge how hard you actually work. He closes on the "champion wall": the low point where most people quit is exactly why they don't win, and finding the "next gear" past it is what leaves everyone else behind. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
