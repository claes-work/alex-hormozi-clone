# Helping a B2B SaaS Scale (Hiring: Hunter vs. Farmer Salesperson)

## Summary
In this live Q&A, a UK-based business owner selling student-housing software to US universities asks Alex Hormozi whether to hire a "farmer" salesperson with 25 years of industry knowledge and a proven US track record, or hold out for a "hunter" new-business salesperson. Hormozi reframes the decision around whether the candidate will make money (yes) and whether he will cost the company in culture (maybe), and concludes that "maybe" on culture plus the owner's own reluctance means no. His core prescription: the owner has interviewed too few people and should "keep dating" until the right fit makes the decision obvious.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a hiring decision with two questions: will the person make money (or at least not lose money), and will they cost you in culture — a "maybe" on culture, absent a strong yes elsewhere, resolves to no.
- As of March 2026, Alex defines culture as the spoken and unspoken rules that govern behavior, and argues it is culture — not managers — that manages sales-team performance.
- As of March 2026, Alex warns that hiring an experienced person who is allowed to break the rules you enforce on everyone else silently resets the culture, signaling to A-players that the rules no longer matter and driving them to leave.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues most people hire under acute pain — "talk to three people and then marry one of them," the same way most people date — and that widening the funnel ("more dates") makes the right choice self-evident.
- As of March 2026, Alex says his teams work through roughly 500–1,000 resumes to fill one solid role, funneling down to about three qualified candidates before picking one.
- As of March 2026, Alex tells his recruiting team he wants a very hard final decision — an obvious/easy choice means they failed to surface enough good talent.
- As of March 2026, Alex uses a realtor analogy: agents who represent their own home wait longer and sell for ~10% more; for hiring the reverse discipline applies — resist immediate pain and wait for better talent.
- As of March 2026, Alex dismisses the candidate's "black book" of accounts as a hiring rationale, calling it short-termist, especially since the candidate's product sells for a fraction (~10–20%) of the owner's price point (a different audience segment).
- Caller claim (unverified): the owner's US segment of the business did ~$1.6M last year and targets $2.7M this year; eight months ago no US universities were buying their software; the candidate has 25 years in the industry and a demonstrable US track record but is a "teller, not a seller."

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "He'll make sales. Will you lose money on him? No. Okay, will he cost you in culture? Maybe. Okay, well then no. Like we could have just started with that one."
> "Sales team culture is what manages performance. Like it is the spoken and unspoken rules that govern behavior. That's what I define culture as."
> "You had me at 'I don't really want him.' Like then just find another one."
> "We're in this immediate pain, we talk to three people and then we marry one of them, which is also how the vast majority of people date."
> "If we want to fill one role that's solid, we'll work from between 500 and 1,000 resumes to get one."
> "I tell them I need you to give me a very hard decision. If it's an obvious decision, it means that you didn't bring enough good talent."
> "You're going to keep ping-ponging this, you just need to decide. And I think that you will decide really easily if you just keep dating. Because as soon as you find that other person you're like, 'Oh my god, thank god I went with this guy.'"
