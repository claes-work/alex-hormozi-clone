# Buying the $400K Machine That 10x's My Business

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a beef-protein-chip brand that has been supply-constrained by manufacturing. Alex says that if the founder wants to build a large brand around a novel product, owning manufacturing may be strategically right, but cash flow must be solved first. He recommends negotiating terms, using preorders or sellout demand to pull cash forward, validating the true cost and defensibility of the proprietary machine, and understanding the machine deeply because it may be the core operational moat.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - beef-protein-chip founder. His product, revenue, manufacturing bottleneck, influencer strategy, machine details, location, and facility considerations are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If the goal is a big brand and the product is novel, owning manufacturing can be a good idea.
- The first constraint is cash flow.
- Supplier terms such as net 30, net 60, or net 90 can materially change growth capacity.
- Preorders and deliberate sellout can pull cash forward while supply is limited.
- Existing manufacturer capacity can provide the cash runway to buy or finance the machine.
- The founder should investigate whether the machine is truly proprietary and whether the quoted cost is real.
- Machine builders may price by value created, not by build cost.
- If the machine creates the product's defensibility, the founder should know everything about how it works.
- The natural extreme matters: if one machine works, the company will eventually need more machines.
