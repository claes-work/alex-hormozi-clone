# Focus on Inputs, Not Outputs

## Summary
Solo short on delayed gratification and controlling inputs. Alex's thesis: "the world belongs to those who can keep doing without seeing the result of their doing" — the longer you can keep acting *before* the payoff arrives while still acting, the bigger you can win. His headline stat: to be a top-1% podcaster you only have to upload 21 episodes — 90% of people never get past one, 9% quit before 20, so a mere 1% reach 21+. Because so many people quit early, "the price for excellence has never been so low"; "try harder" usually just means "don't give up." He reframes the fear of humiliation: people so afraid of short-term embarrassment accept "the long-term humiliation of never amounting to anything," and no one watches your first 20 episodes anyway — but that's fine, because the output (downloads) isn't the point. **You are the asset you're building, not the downloads** — judge yourself on the gap between episode 1 and episode 20 (and 20 vs 100), i.e. your own improvement, not the metrics. He debunks his own survivorship story: he did NOT start with millions of downloads. He made two podcasts a week for four and a half years straight without missing, and at the end was getting only 2,000–3,000 downloads/month (~200 per episode). He reframed small numbers as wins — 200 downloads is an auditorium; even at 10–15 downloads early on, the listeners "are doing me more of a favor than I'm doing them" by giving him reps to practice voice and presentation. Things only took off at year 4.5–5, driven by two changes: (1) renaming the show from **Gym Secrets** to **The Game** so it applied to a broader business audience, and (2) — "the real one" — selling a company for $46 million, which conferred the credibility that made people listen. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
