# The Biggest Opportunity with the Agency Model

## Summary
Short Q&A clip in which Alex argues that the **agency model — historically a bad business — is becoming a genuinely good opportunity because of AI**, and that the window is roughly the next 1–2 years. His setup: agencies are perennially in demand (every business needs customers, so there's no product-market-fit question) but they've "perennially sucked" as businesses because they aren't *sticky*. He contrasts them with sticky categories — payment processors, banks — which are few in number precisely because they're sticky; agencies are legion because they aren't. He diagnoses two causes of the non-stickiness: (1) delivery operations are genuinely hard, and (2) agencies are **mispriced**. AI now attacks both, especially at the bottom of the market (the ~$400–$700/month price points), where AI agents can automate a lot of the delivery ops. He cites Y Combinator currently hunting for **vertically integrated marketing agencies** as evidence: with demand pre-proven, "it's literally just a pricing consistency question" and "it's all about ops," much of which AI can now automate. He extends the point up-market too — a friend's centimillion-dollar Fortune-100 agency **cut half its payroll** by putting AI automations in place. When a participant pushes back that current AI solutions aren't good enough to match a human, Alex concedes they're "not good out of the box" but insists that's exactly the point: his own operation is doing **20 sales a day with AI, no human**, and it scales. He reframes the friction as the moat — "the fact that there is more friction for you means there's more friction for everyone, which is where the opportunity lies. As soon as it's easy for everyone, the opportunity will be gone" — and compares "AI doesn't work" to "Facebook ads don't work": both work only if you do them well. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, the agency model has "perennially sucked" as a business but is becoming a significantly better opportunity because of AI.
- As of March 2026, agencies are always in demand because every business owner wants customers, so there's no product-market-fit question — the question is pricing consistency and operations.
- As of March 2026, agencies aren't sticky (which is why there are so many of them), unlike payment processors or banks, which are sticky and therefore few in number.
- As of March 2026, the two reasons agencies aren't sticky are that delivery operations are hard and that agencies are mispriced.
- As of March 2026, AI is most impactful at the bottom of the market (roughly the $400–$700/month price points), where AI agents can now automate much of what delivery required.
- As of March 2026, Y Combinator is looking for vertically integrated marketing agencies precisely because the demand doesn't need testing — it's an ops-and-pricing problem AI can now help solve.
- As of March 2026, AI helps up-market too: a friend's Fortune-100 agency cut half its payroll by putting AI automations in place.
- As of March 2026, current off-the-shelf AI solutions are "not good out of the box," but that's the opportunity — Alex's own operation runs 20 sales a day with AI and no human, and it will scale.
- As of March 2026, friction is the moat: more friction for you means more friction for everyone, so the opportunity exists only while it's hard; once it's easy for everyone, the opportunity is gone.
- As of March 2026, "AI doesn't work" is like "Facebook ads don't work" — both work only if you do them well.
- As of March 2026, Alex estimates the AI-agency opportunity window is roughly the next 1 to 2 years for those who get ahead of it.
