# 'I Own a D2D Pest Control Company, How Do I Sell It?'

## Summary
In this MoreMozi Q&A, a business owner who holds 45% of one branch of a door-to-door (D2D) pest control company (~$1M topline in year one) asks Alex Hormozi whether to keep his equity-plus-operations partnership or switch to a pure "marketing deal" where he is responsible only for sales, so he can eventually own his own companies outright. Alex reframes it as a "happy to glad" problem: both paths make money because the operator's real edge is building sales teams, and the decision reduces almost entirely to timeline. He argues the per-customer cashflow (~$300/customer to the guest) is a distraction — on the equity path the only number that matters is exit value, since these businesses "eat cash until you sell" but carry strong exit multiples (he pencils in a 10x). He sketches the math to a $100M outcome (five stores at ~$1.5–2M EBITDA each cobble to ~$10M EBITDA, times 10x), notes favorable long-term capital-gains tax treatment (~20%, netting "high 30s" percent), and stresses getting written alignment from the 55% partner on a sell trigger. Because the partner wants to hold for ~20 years and is older, Alex concludes the guest can reasonably start his own thing — while warning that the partner almost certainly does more than the guest thinks, and that going solo means earning "the chance to get kicked in the teeth." He points to a break-even point computable in Excel driven by how fast the guest can open new locations (realistically one/year now, potentially two-to-three), noting the first location only went ~$50K in the red to open.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed] The guest owns 45% of one branch of a D2D pest control company doing ~$1M/year in AR; he identifies primarily as a door-to-door sales operator who runs sales teams and is expanding into multiple markets next year. [self-reported anecdote]
- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed] His branch's unit economics: ~$1,000 average customer/year, 33% annual churn (≈3-year average customer life, ≈$3,000 LTV), ~30% margins, roughly no profit in year one (sales team cost), leaving ~$2,000 of margin-bearing revenue and ~$600 profit, of which his 45% is ~$300/customer.
- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed] The guest's stated goal is to own $100M in pest control; his gut plan is to take the marketing deal, make money next year, then buy pest control companies he owns 100% of.
- (2026-02-11) When either option would work, it's a "happy to glad" situation — don't agonize over it, because the only thing that fundamentally matters is being very good at building the sales teams.
- (2026-02-11) On the 45% equity path the per-customer cashflow is irrelevant; the exit value is the only number that matters, because these businesses eat cash until you sell.
- (2026-02-11) Every new branch goes negative on opening — "that's the whole game."
- (2026-02-11) Pest control has good exit multiples; Alex pencils in a 10x on EBITDA.
- (2026-02-11) Math to $100M: stores at scale do ~$5–7M topline and ~$1.5–2M EBITDA; five stores cobble to ~$10M EBITDA, which at 10x is a ~$100M exit — 45% of which is ~$45M.
- (2026-02-11) A sale of that type gets favorable capital-gains tax treatment (~20%), netting "high 30s" percent from the deal.
- (2026-02-11) Before committing, get alignment from the 55% partner and paper a sell trigger (e.g., "if we get an offer above X we take it"); most partners, asked if they'd sell at a $100M offer, say yes — if so you're aligned, paper it, and that's the sprint.
- (2026-02-11) Timeline is the deciding variable: shorter timeline favors staying equity; longer timeline favors owning your own.
- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed] The partner wants to run and not sell for ~20 years and is older than the guest — which Alex says means the guest can reasonably start his own thing.
- (2026-02-11) Warning: the partner probably does much more than the guest thinks (not just admin and finance) — "there's just always more."
- (2026-02-11) Going out on your own means you "deserve the chance to get kicked in the teeth" — Alex says he doesn't want to rob the guest of that pain and suffering (delivered as friendly ribbing, "not making fun of you").
- (2026-02-11) The wanting-to-own-your-own motive is really about control.
- (2026-02-11) There's a computable break-even point (Excel) for enterprise value driven by location-opening rate — realistically one location/year now, potentially two-to-three later; assume five locations over five years with no assumed ranking increase and a learning-curve drag.
- (2026-02-11) [guest-attributed] The guest's first location only went ~$50K in the red to open — which Alex reads as "not long" to fund the next one, concluding "I think you can do your own thing."
