# How I Lost $217,000 on A Day Trade & How I Trade Now

## Summary
In this solo video Hormozi confesses to a recent short-term trading loss (the title says $217,000; in the video he rounds it to "200 grand") and uses it to argue against active trading and in favor of buy-and-hold investing. He opens with a Fidelity/Charles Schwab study he cites from memory: the best-performing brokerage accounts belonged to investors who had died, and the second-best to investors who had forgotten their passwords — both because inactivity beat trading. He then walks through a $100 math example showing that because short-term trading gains are taxed as ordinary income (~43-45%) rather than long-term capital gains, a trader would have to grow $100 to about $245 just to net the same $180 that a buy-and-hold investor keeps after only paying capital gains. He frames his own mistake concretely: he sold a position that had gains, it dropped (so he felt smart), then it climbed back to exactly where he sold, leaving him worse off because he now owes ordinary-income tax on a within-a-year sale. He stresses this all happened inside the 3-5% "play money" slice of his portfolio, with 95-97% in "really really stable stuff," and closes advising viewers to get a hobby, let money compound, and "pretend you're a dead person."

## Key claims
- The highest-returning brokerage accounts in a Fidelity/Charles Schwab study belonged to people who had bought stocks and then died; the second-highest belonged to people who forgot their passwords and therefore never traded (cited from memory, self-reported).
- Inactivity and broad market exposure (indexing) beat active trading; "the people who do the least amount have the most money in the account."
- Short-term/day trading throws out favorable capital-gains treatment and makes gains taxable as regular income (he uses ~43-45%).
- Because of that tax difference, a trader would have to turn $100 into roughly $245 just to net the same ~$180 a buy-and-hold investor keeps after long-term capital gains — i.e. the trader's growth has to be ~45% higher just to break even against holding.
- Retail traders are competing against large quantitative firms with hundreds of analysts whose business is beating you; ~75% of people who try will lose money.
- He describes his own passive holdings as having outperformed his active trading, which he says confirms the whole thesis.
- If you have the "itch" to trade, wall off ~3% for pure speculation and treat it as "managing my idiocy" rather than real strategy; he does not recommend trading otherwise.
- Prescription: get a hobby, let the money compound, "pretend you're a dead person," and if you can't, throw away or hand off your password to someone you'd be ashamed to ask it back from.
