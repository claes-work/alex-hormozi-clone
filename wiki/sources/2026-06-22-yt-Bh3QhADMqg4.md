# I'm Clueless on How to Scale From $1.2M to $3.5M

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A in which Alex Hormozi advises a business owner who sells duct-cleaning equipment and supplies to duct cleaners. The owner does ~$1.2M/yr entirely on organic word-of-mouth and wants to reach ~$3.5M. Alex diagnoses that the owner has the hard part (a genuinely good product) but none of the machinery that actually makes such a business profitable, and lays out a three-part fix: a new customer-acquisition system, an offer change, and back-end re-engagement of existing customers. This is a coaching case; only Alex's advice and frameworks are persona-relevant.

## Key claims

- **Four ways to get customers.** Alex frames acquisition as a choice among four channels: outbound (door-knocking, email, cold call, Instagram DMs), paid ads, affiliates, or posting content. Pick the one you're best equipped to run.
- **Relying solely on word of mouth signals product quality but caps growth.** "The good news is... you probably are good at what you do because you've been relying solely on word of mouth." The bad news is a steep learning curve for demand generation, because "you don't do anything" to generate demand — there's no demand gen happening.
- **Three "wheels" / revenue prongs in an equipment business.** You sell (1) the machines, (2) the consumables the machines use, and (3) the service around the machines. Alex compares the model to auto dealerships, large equipment, and "ink and printers."
- **LTV extension lives in the second and third prongs, not the machine.** The more sophisticated version of such a business sells the machine at cost or at a loss because all the margin is in consumables and service.
- **Retention is the first priority (Step 1).** Make sure you retain on all consumables: every year ~95% of customers "have to buy it from somewhere and they should all buy it from me." That recurring consumables/service base becomes "the annuity of the business" — it rises over time and is super profitable. "That's the game."
- **Prefer selling bigger customers over making the individual customer more valuable.** Alex thinks it's hard to make an individual customer more valuable; easier to sell a higher-tier customer (someone who cleans more ducts, buys in higher volume, or buys for whole fleets) — but that's a different sales motion.
- **Either path requires a new acquisition system.** Whether you go up-market (new customer type) or deepen existing customers, you need a new acquisition system. Alex's preference ("if I had my druthers"): start with a new acquisition system for the existing customer base, because you already know that flow; go up-market afterward.
- **Do outbound to existing customers.** Re-engaging existing customers to tell them consumables exist ("We have things available for purchase. You should purchase them from us.") is "an excellent idea."
- **Sell recurring at the point of the initial sale.** Put consumables on a cadence or subscription bundled with the initial product sale; design maintenance programs and subscription incentives up front — "that's where a lot of the alpha is."
- **The diagnosis / reframe.** "You've been making the money that you are with none of the actual pieces that make your business make money make money" — i.e., the owner has the hard part (a profitable product/front end) without the profitable machinery (consumables + service annuity). Fixing that lifts profitability "a lot."
- **The prescription — three moves:** sales-motion change, offer change, and back-end re-engagement.
- **Context (owner, not persona):** duct-cleaning supply business, ~$1.2M revenue targeting ~$3.5M; ~80 new customers/month; strictly organic/word-of-mouth acquisition; equipment is high-profit but durable (low repeat), consumables are repeat but low-margin (~$6/unit, ~500/yr).
