# How to Win in a Commoditized Business

## Summary
Live coaching clip. Alex works through a commoditized home-services business (artificial turf / grass installation) with its owner, who is present as the coachee (the `>>` turns are the client's short affirmations and laughter; all frameworks are Alex's). His opening thesis: in a commoditized market — high demand, high supply, low skill required — bad players underprice and it becomes "a race to the bottom," so you must **de-commoditize** by winning on one of **three vectors of value: risk, speed, or ease**. Risk = being less risky to buy: long-term via brand/reputation ("a brand premium" makes people believe they're more likely to get the good result), and short-term via **covenants and guarantees** when you don't yet have a brand. Speed = doing the job faster. Ease = removing friction so the customer "can just drop the keys off" and not worry. Alex says his team's discovery process is figuring out which of the three a given business can win on. He then builds two concrete offers. First, an **on-time-on-budget guarantee**: raise price ~20%, guarantee the job finishes on time and under budget, and refund the 20% margin if it doesn't — competitors quoting less "for a dollar" won't guarantee it, so this wins every bid; a **no-change contingency** (the guarantee voids if the customer changes the scope, which they always do) protects the installer. Second, a **maintenance plan** priced low to seed future work (e.g., $200/year covering up to $500 in repairs), offered **free in exchange for a five-star review** — and the review ask lands hardest when the salesperson frames it as helping the crew ("the guys get a little spiff… it'd mean a lot to them and their families"). He then covers **career pathing** for retention: two parallel columns (title/perks and money), with junior/mid/senior tiers across multiple roles yielding ~12 progressions plus in-house mini-certifications, citing a nail-salon operator who lifted tenure from ~6 months to ~4 years and paid unlicensed staff ~$70k/year. Finally, on expansion, he warns against opening a second location to escape a weak first one; his **"scale zero"** step is removing the owner from day-to-day operations first, and expanding only "when you're making more money than you know what to do with, and you have more time on your hands than you know what to do with" — a mistake he says he knows because he made it himself. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says the core problem in any commoditized market (esp. home services) is high demand + high supply + low skill → bad players underprice → "a race to the bottom," so you must de-commoditize.
- As of March 2026, he frames three vectors of value to win a commoditized business on: **risk** (being less risky to buy), **speed** (doing it faster), and **ease** (removing friction) — and his firm's discovery work is figuring out which one a given business can win on.
- As of March 2026, on the risk vector: long-term you lower perceived risk via **brand/reputation** ("a brand premium" = people believe they're more likely to get the good result); short-term, before you have a brand, you use **covenants and guarantees**.
- As of March 2026, his on-time-on-budget guarantee mechanics: raise price ~20%, guarantee the job is done on time and under a stated number, and refund the 20% margin if you miss — since competitors won't guarantee time and budget ("I can do it for a dollar as long as I don't have to guarantee time and budget"), this wins essentially every bid.
- As of March 2026, the guarantee carries a **no-change contingency**: it holds only while the customer doesn't change scope; because customers almost always change their minds, the contingency both protects the installer and still lets him win the bid.
- As of March 2026, a low-priced **maintenance plan** (e.g., $200/year covering up to $500 in repairs; "we make up the prices ourselves anyway") is really a way to buy a repeat sales consult, extend product life, and protect resale value.
- As of March 2026, offering the maintenance plan **free in exchange for a five-star review** is a strong lever — and framing the ask as benefiting the crew ("the guys get a spiff… means a lot to them and their families") converts far better than framing it as benefiting ownership.
- As of March 2026, retention via **career pathing**: two parallel columns (title/perks and money), junior/mid/senior tiers across multiple roles giving ~12 progressions, plus purely in-house mini-certifications that unlock more complex, higher-billable work — keeping staff feeling they're always learning and progressing.
- As of March 2026, he cites a nail-salon operator whose tiered model lifted industry-typical tenure from ~6 months to ~4 years and had staff without high-school diplomas earning ~$70,000/year; he says the same tiering applies to salespeople, editors, "anything that's a repeated role."
- As of March 2026, his **"scale zero"** step precedes expansion: get the business to run at the same or better level with the owner doing zero, producing a duplicable model.
- As of March 2026, the big expansion mistake is opening a second location because the first isn't making enough — you split your time, revenue drops, overhead doubles, and you work twice as hard for less; expand only "when you're making more money than you know what to do with, and you have more time on your hands than you know what to do with." He says he knows this because he did it wrong himself.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Whenever you're in a commoditized business — obviously the goal is not to be in one — but it's like you either have to win on risk, as in you are less risky, which is what a brand premium does… the next one is speed… and then ease, right? Like how do I make it less friction for you."

> "There's huge demand for it, there's a lot of supply, and so people are going to make money without really a lot of skill… and then basically it's a race to the bottom, which sucks."

> "Did they guarantee time and budget? No one will… 'Oh yeah, well then I can do it for a dollar. As long as I don't have to guarantee time and budget.'"

> "With me, when you pay this, you know that it will be done by this time and it will be done under this number, period. And if it's not, I'll give you my profit, which I run 20% margins, I'll give you 20% back."

> "This guarantee is contingent on one very important thing, which is that they don't change their mind. Which of course they're going to change their mind."

> "Or you can just leave me a five-star review, and I'll throw it in."

> "If he says it about the guys and he's not the owner, it crushes… the guys get a little spiff, it means a lot to them and their families, and you'll get 500 bucks for free."

> "Expansion should happen when you're making more money than you know what to do with, and you have more time on your hands than you know what to do with. That's when you expand, not when you're like, 'I'm not making enough from this facility, so I'm going to open another one.'"

> "That is how so many people think about it, and I only say I know that because I did it."
