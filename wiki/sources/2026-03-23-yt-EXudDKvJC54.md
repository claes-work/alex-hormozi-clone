# Helping a $10M/Year Transportation Company Scale

## Summary
Alex Hormozi advises the owner of a last-mile transportation company that hauls polymers/chemicals to fracking companies (revenue grew $5M → $7M → $10M over three years) who is weighing whether to start selling the product to end users his producers won't serve, versus doubling down on the core trucking business. Hormozi argues against the diversification: adding product sales piles on cash-flow constraints and complexity, and the real cost is the growth he'd forgo in the winning model he already has. He shares his own Gym Launch / Prestige Labs mistake as a cautionary parallel and reframes the choice as a life question ("what you want to do") rather than a business one.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that if you already have a winning, growing model, the highest-return move is to get even better at it and compound your existing competitive advantage rather than chase adjacent opportunities.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames added ventures as opportunity cost: the roofing/contracting/house-flipping friend can't become a billion-dollar roofer *because of* the contracting and flipping — each side venture is what stops the others from scaling.
- As of March 2026, Alex says selling the transported product to end users would add another cash-flow constraint on top of the ~$1.5–2M already floating in receivables, since purchased product must be paid in ~30 days before he gets paid.
- As of March 2026, Alex cites his own Gym Launch + Prestige Labs experience as one of his bigger mistakes: layering a supplement company onto his gym distribution base slowed the growth of the core licensing business; the same energy doubled into the main business would have paid off more.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes "do I want to do it?" as a life question, not a business question — and says wanting to diversify is not sufficient business justification.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes the "woman in the red dress" dynamic: the more distribution, skills, and opportunities you build, the more attractive outside opportunities look and the more you must turn down.
- As of March 2026, Alex speculates that institutional investors buy at the ~$5M EBITDA level because that's where most owners feel "done forever" — driving heavy M&A activity — but says companies that reach $100M are usually run by a more stubborn founder, which he views as a good thing.
- Caller claim (unverified): the company did $10M revenue last year, ~20% margins (~$4–5M EBITDA), floats $1.5–2M in receivables, has kept core customers 6–7 years, and that hazmat transport firms typically get bought out around $15–20M revenue (naming Qualitex, Kenan; "Kenan F Dana" per caption).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you already have a winning model, to me, I'm like there's no reason to not just become even better at the thing that you're currently doing and compound the competitive advantage that you have."

> "So, what stops you from being a billion-dollar-a-year roofing company? And the answer is the general contracting and the real estate flipping."

> "'But that's — you know, didn't want to leave money on the table,' which is like my favorite entrepreneur statement ever."

> "I could have taken all of that energy and just gone double down into the thing that I should have done. It was one of the bigger mistakes that I've made."

> "You want to do it — that's a life question more than a business question."

> "The woman in the red dress, she just always is more and more attractive. The more distribution you build, the more skills you have, and the more opportunities you see."

> "Believe me, the amount of opportunities that I have to turn down now is like sickening."

> "Taken to the natural extreme, can I build a hundred million dollar per year trucking business? If the answer is yes, then — what risks that? Everything that isn't that."

> "It's usually just a more stubborn founder who does it for different reasons, which I actually think is a good thing."
