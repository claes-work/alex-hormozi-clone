# Apply to 300 Grants, Not 3

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a Stanford research student working on leukemia treatments who needs funding to continue preclinical research. Alex treats grant funding like a sales or deal-closing constraint: if the research is existentially dependent on money, the highest-likelihood known path should be pursued with unreasonable volume before searching for new strategies. His prescription is to move from three or four grant applications toward dozens or hundreds, because the alternative is the research stopping.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Carlos Rodriguez / coachee** - Stanford research student. His leukemia-research context, funding runway, and grant assumptions are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Grant funding can be approached like closing deals: insufficient application volume may be the core constraint.
- When the alternative is losing the research, the team should ask what would make failure unreasonable.
- The highest-likelihood path should be maximized before spending time inventing a different path.
- If 30 good applications would likely secure funding, the immediate answer is to make 30 good applications.
- The constraint of the research system is cash flow; the direct solution is volume of outreach / volume of applications.
- A team may need to temporarily shift focus away from the work itself if the work will stop without funding.
- Creative strategies may exist, but they should not distract from a known path with high probability if volume is the missing input.
