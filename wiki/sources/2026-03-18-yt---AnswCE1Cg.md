# Helping a Digital Product Business Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client sells low-ticket digital video products across two separate brands: (1) motion backgrounds/visuals to churches on a recurring plan ($348 or $708/year, ~2,000 churches, ~$750k/year, ~60% margins), and (2) video-editing assets (color presets, film transitions, film burns, grains, overlays) to filmmakers/editors as one-time purchases (~$350k/year, but only ~1 hour of his week — Alex notes that's ~$6,000/hour). He has ~170,000 leads accumulated over 7 years on the editing-asset side, and asks how to find the "right money model" to scale — worrying he needs a higher-ticket offer. Alex's diagnosis cuts against that instinct. First he clarifies the structure and confirms the church side is the real business. He identifies the constraint as **demand, not supply** ("you're demand constrained, not supply constrained") — the client bottlenecked himself on content because he's a video editor doing the work himself, and is only now delegating. Alex reframes the whole scaling question: in a low-capex, high-cash-flow digital/service business, the year-end profit "is not all yours" — it must be reinvested in the two things that function as the capex of such businesses: **talent and brand**. Asked how he'd spend ~$600k of profit, the client says heavy on team to build an internal content agency; Alex pushes back that in the current AI era he'd lean on **Claude and GPT instances rather than headcount**, because execution is being commoditized and leverage now sits in "skill, taste, and strategic thinking" — hire "fewer, better people." The client agrees his real hire is for *taste*, since AI-feeling competitor content "doesn't feel good" and customers come to him precisely for taste. Alex rejects the higher-ticket church-consulting idea ("then you become a church consultant… feels like a different business") and instead says to **just double down and 20x the existing thing** — with 300,000 churches in the US alone (plus international/Spanish-language acquisition), the market is huge. His closing principle: entrepreneurs habitually reach for "another thing" to make more money when they could simply do more of the thing that already works; the genuinely hard problem is just "how do I get more?" of the same.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex advises that a good, simple business with pricing power should usually be scaled by doing more of the same rather than inventing a new higher-ticket offer — "there's a lot of elegance to simplicity."
- As of March 2026, he distinguishes demand-constrained from supply-constrained businesses; this client is demand-constrained (has content capacity/ideas but isn't generating enough demand), so the fix is making more marketing content, not adding products.
- As of March 2026, in low-capex, high-cash-flow digital/service businesses the year-end profit "is not all yours" — it should be reinvested in **talent** and **brand**, which function as the effective capex of a service business (the investment required to keep growing next year).
- As of March 2026, in the AI era he'd staff for scale with "fewer, better people" plus Claude/GPT instances rather than large headcount, because execution is becoming commoditized and leverage has shifted to skill, taste, and strategic thinking.
- As of March 2026, he frames taste as the defensible asset in media/content: competitors making "AI type feeling stuff that doesn't feel good" lose to businesses customers choose specifically for taste.
- As of March 2026, he warns the main bottleneck to scaling media output is recruiting talent, because "media talent without a brand is very difficult" — good people can earn more than most employers pay them, so a strong brand is needed to attract them.
- As of March 2026, he rejects layering on a high-ticket church-consulting offer as drifting into a different business ("then you become a church consultant"); with ~300,000 US churches (and international/Spanish-language markets), he'd rather 20x the existing recurring product.
- As of March 2026, his general principle: entrepreneurs reach for "another thing" to make more money when they could just do more of the thing that works; if the answer to "how do I get more?" isn't immediately apparent, that *is* the hard problem worth solving.
