# The Two Voices That Hold You Back from Trying

## Summary

Alex argues that people are often okay with being bad at something but not okay with
being judged for being bad. He recommends redefining success as trying, naming the few
specific voices behind fear, and separating one's own beliefs from other people's
beliefs. He gives a self-reported example from considering a $46.2M company sale, where
he realized one person's imagined approval was influencing the decision.

## Key claims (Alex's self-report and advice)

- Alex says people are often more afraid of judgment for sucking than of sucking itself.
- He recommends redefining success as trying rather than achieving the desired outcome.
- Failure and success are paired; the goal is to pay down failure debt quickly.
- The judging voices in someone's head are usually one or two specific people, not
  "people" in general.
- Alex says that when considering a $46.2M offer for his company, he worried it was not
  enough to impress a specific person.
- Naming that imagined judge made the influence feel absurd and reduced its power.
- If someone does not know why they believe something, Alex says it is likely someone
  else's belief.
- Alex says it took him six months to quit his job and that he drove across the country
  before telling people from home because he feared their reaction.
- He quotes Leila's frame that fear is "a mile wide and an inch deep."
- He also uses the idea that, apart from death, all failure is psychological.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Redefine success as trying and not as succeeding."

> "If you don't know why you believe what you believe, it's not your belief, it's someone else's."

> "Fear is a mile wide and an inch deep."
