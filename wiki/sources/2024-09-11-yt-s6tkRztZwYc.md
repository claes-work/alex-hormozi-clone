# How to Articulate Your Thoughts More Clearly Than 99% of People

## Summary

Despite the articulation-themed title, this clip is an excerpt from a Skool "school day" Q&A (the questioner is one of the top-10 Skool winners) in which Alex Hormozi lays out his management and communication frameworks. He presents his "Management Diamond" — a five-reason diagnostic for why an employee didn't do what you asked — then argues that clear communication is really about **debundling** vague "bundled" terms (charismatic, patient, motivated, "being a dick") into observable activities. He closes with a "logic / evidence / utility" (what does this mean · how do you know · so what) filter for cutting through imprecise language, and a claim that his content outperforms because he defines terms other people leave undefined.

## Key claims

Alex-attributed (persona-eligible):

- **The single most valuable skill set is getting other people to do things for you** — master that and you can get others to build every other part of the business for you.
- **The "Management Diamond": five reasons someone didn't do what you asked.** (1) They didn't know you wanted it → fix with *communication*; (2) didn't know how → fix with *training*; (3) didn't know when → fix with *deadlines*; (4) weren't *incentivized/motivated*; (5) something was *blocking* them → *remove the impediment*. If more than one applies, fix more than one. He frames running through these as a script for hard conversations that makes them "not hard."
- **Delegation vs. abdication.** If a handoff makes the output worse, you abdicated ("here you go, good luck"); if it stays neutral or gets better, you actually delegated. Most entrepreneurs verbally dump instructions once, expect 100% retention, then are surprised weeks later.
- **Bundling / unbundling terms** is his core communication move. Soft-skill labels (charismatic, patient, "a dick") are bundles of concrete behaviors. Don't say "be charismatic" or "stop being a dick" — name the six things to start or stop doing (vocal range, pause for emphasis, say people's names, eye contact, nod). Behavior change comes from specific instructions, not insults or "be nicer."
- **Checklists** operationalize soft skills. Score people against an 11-item checklist; if a low scorer follows all 11 and still underperforms, the checklist (i.e., the manager's training) is wrong, not the employee.
- **"Soft skills and hard skills are all just skills"** — soft skills are only harder to teach because they're bundled language.
- **Behavior is driven by consequences, not antecedents** — people do things because of what happens *after*, not what happened before. Managers must artificially insert feedback loops into roles with slow feedback cycles (sales and editing self-reinforce because their loops are tight; nobody cold-calls for fun).
- **Motivation comes from deprivation** — reinforce with whatever a person is most deprived of (e.g., praise). Money is a "conditioned universal reinforcer."
- **He deliberately erased "feelings, intentions, beliefs" from his behavioral vocabulary** and focuses only on the observable. Told the "dick" employee: "I don't care if you're a dick... as long as you behave this way."
- **Logic / Evidence / Utility filter**: for any claim, ask *what does this mean* (logic), *how do you know* (evidence), *so what* (utility). He uses it to dismantle vague statements ("I'm stressed because I'm lonely," "make a customer worth more in three ways").
- **The more skilled the person, the vaguer the instruction can be.** Experts have more ways to be rewarded in the same context; low-skill people need highly specific instruction. Training usually fails because you assumed a skill the person didn't have.
- Says most of these concepts derive "from the ground up from rat studies" with a "Dr. Cashy," and denies leaning on "psychology" as such.
