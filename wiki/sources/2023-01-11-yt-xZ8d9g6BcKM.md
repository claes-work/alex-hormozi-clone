# Building a Frozen Yogurt Store in 11 Minutes

## Summary

Solo case study in which Alex Hormozi walks through the unit economics and consumer
psychology of the frozen yogurt store business — the venture he says he researched
extensively and nearly started at 21 instead of his gym. He breaks down revenue,
margins, franchise economics, pricing-by-weight psychology, default-option effects,
and low-cost customer acquisition, then distills six transferable lessons for any
business owner.

## Key claims

- **Topline vs. take-home**: The average frozen yogurt store does ~$2,000+/day (~$800k/year),
  but runs 10–15% margins, leaving roughly $328/day in owner pay. Cited example: the average
  Menchie's owner takes home ~$93k/year. High revenue ≠ high income.
- **Unit economics of a cup**: Average cup is 8 oz; a store sells ~500 cups/day to hit ~$2,100/day.
  Split is ~75% yogurt / 25% toppings by weight. Yogurt costs the store ~$0.08/oz but sells for
  $0.50–$0.60/oz — that spread is where the margin lives. Toppings cost $0.10–$0.40/oz and often
  break even or lose money (esp. fresh fruit); they are a draw, not a profit center.
- **Franchise economics**: Franchises take ~6% of topline off the top. At 10% margins that 6% is
  ~60% of the owner's take-home. Franchisors structure fees so operators make "just enough" to keep
  going / maybe open a second location, targeting a return that beats the stock market (10%) by
  reaching for 20–25% and skimming the rest. Offset: bulk purchasing power across 200+ locations —
  though franchisors often upcharge on equipment/supplies at startup anyway.
- **Buy foreclosed equipment**: Motivated/failed-business sellers let you buy a whole store's
  equipment for pennies on the dollar. Alex got his first gym equipment for $13k off a business
  foreclosure site; a cursory search found three failed froyo franchises, the priciest at $20k for
  everything inside. Franchises won't let you do this because equipment markup is where they profit.
- **Commoditized market / room to win**: Brands (Menchie's, Yogurtland, Golden Spoon, Pinkberry)
  are largely interchangeable — "there is no Chick-fil-A of the yogurt world." Market was competed
  by franchisors selling locations, not building brands. You out-compete by beating the bottom 25%:
  clean floors, stocked machines, staff who greet customers, better service.
- **Pricing by weight (self-serve)**: Charging by weight instead of by cup size shifts
  accountability to the consumer — they blame themselves ("I'm such a fat ass") for a $7 cup rather
  than blaming the store for being expensive. Consumer controls spend, so consumer owns the guilt.
- **Default-option / cup-size effect**: Removing small cups and offering only medium/large "bucket"
  cups raised the volume of yogurt people served themselves (reportedly ~20–30% sales lift),
  because a small amount in a big cup "looks weird" and people fill to the perceived standard —
  and the store isn't blamed because the customer's hand is on the nozzle. Parallel: fast-food
  "medium or large?" assumed close.
- **Self-serve item order = reverse cost order**: Highest-margin item (yogurt) comes first so
  customers fill up on it, then cheap dry toppings (crumbled Oreos), then expensive perishable fruit
  last when little room remains. Same mechanic as buffets putting salad first and seafood at the end.
- **Customer acquisition on a low ticket**: With tiny tickets and thin margins, paid ads (Google
  PPC) are no longer profitable for most; the only viable channels are word of mouth (referrals) and
  affiliates/other businesses that send customers. Alex's own plan: locate near colleges, run
  yogurt-eating competitions between fraternities/sororities for swag, "rinse and repeat" with
  companies and affiliations, and offer a strong incentive (e.g., "save 50% today") to capture text-
  list leads at ~$2 each for lifetime value.
- **Six distilled lessons**: (1) let customers pick their own pricing by usage — they blame
  themselves for overspending; (2) put the highest-margin item in front of the most people first;
  (3) more sampleable options → more bought and consumed; (4) buy failed competitors' equipment cheap
  from motivated sellers; (5) the power of the default option (cup sizing); (6) for low-cost products,
  paid acquisition rarely works — rely on word of mouth (and explicitly ask people to share/review)
  and affiliates.
- **Word-of-mouth via asking**: When Alex added a simple ask to his podcast — "leave a review and
  share this with somebody" — reviews 20x'd and growth "skyrocketed," just from asking.
