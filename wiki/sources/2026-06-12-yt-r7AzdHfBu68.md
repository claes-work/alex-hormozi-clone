# Insurance Is Dying in California. Should I Pivot My Business?

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a property-damage repair business doing about $13.5M that is being hurt by insurance carriers reducing California coverage. The owner is considering pivoting to a home-services membership. Alex calls changing the whole business the "Armageddon button" and recommends testing less destructive moves first: find customers who still have coverage, improve the cash/insurance sales process, and sell a membership on the back end of emergency work rather than trying to sell a recurring solution to people who are not in pain.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Changing the entire business model should be treated as a last-resort move, not the first response to industry pressure.
- Before pivoting, test whether there are still acquisition channels or geographies with customers who have full coverage.
- A sales process may solve part of the issue by taking cash up front and recovering insurance money later.
- Selling a one-time emergency solution to someone in pain is much easier than selling a recurring solution to someone who is not in pain.
- Alex has seen many home-services membership attempts and says he has not seen that model work profitably and well as a primary front-end.
- Memberships can work better as a back-end sale after the company has solved an emergency problem for the customer.
- If the business cannot sell the membership to existing emergency customers, it definitely should not switch the whole model to membership.

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

> "Changing the entire business is typically the Armageddon button."

> "Continue to respond to the inquiries that you're getting that are emergency based, but then don't let that be the only sale."

> "If you can't close those people, then definitely don't switch the model."
