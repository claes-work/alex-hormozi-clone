# How to Use Agencies Without Getting Gouged

## Summary

In this teaching clip, Alex Hormozi explains how he uses agencies as temporary expertise transfer rather than permanent dependency. Phase one is a basic agency that forces cadence and fundamentals. Phase two is a higher-end agency that teaches the advanced platform playbook. Alex recommends setting expectations upfront: pay for a defined period, pay extra for decision breakdowns, train the internal team in parallel, then transition to consulting and eventually cut the agency once the in-house team matches or exceeds agency output.

## Key claims (Alex's teaching only)

- A basic agency can be useful early because it forces a regular content cadence and handles simple production.
- After three to six months, the founder has enough context to know what the audience likes and what advanced skills are needed.
- As demands exceed a small agency's capacity, it can make sense to part ways rather than expecting one agency to change its business for one client.
- Advanced agencies should be hired to learn the nuanced playbook, not to create permanent dependency.
- Alex recommends telling the agency upfront that the goal is a six-month learning engagement, followed by a lower-cost consulting arrangement.
- The founder should pay extra for the agency to explain why it makes decisions and what steps it takes.
- During the engagement, the internal team should document the agency's process and co-create until internal output matches or beats agency output.
- An in-house team should eventually win because it has more time and more brand context; the only missing piece is expertise.
- A clear transition deadline helps the agency, the founder, and the internal team understand the outcome.
- If the internal team cannot match the agency, either the wrong people are on the team or the agency is not teaching the full process.
- Once the internal team matches the agency, downgrade to consulting as insurance, then cut the relationship if the calls stop adding value.
