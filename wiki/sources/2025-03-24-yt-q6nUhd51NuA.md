# Why The Best Business to Start in 2025 is a Skool Community

## Summary

Alex Hormozi's onboarding/pitch narration for [[../entities/skool]] and the Skool Games, aimed at people brand-new to online business. He makes the business-model case for starting a paid Skool community — no tech, no website, no inventory, no staff, recurring revenue — and lays out the core "game": create content on social, siphon the traffic into a Skool community, and let the community convert eyeballs into monthly recurring revenue. He also explains his own reason for partnering with Skool (complementary skill sets with founder Sam) and gives community-building tactics: help others first, find a partner, and make it fun. L2 light ingest; overlaps other Skool Games material.

## Why a Skool community (the business-model case)

Alex's argument for why a Skool community is a good first online business:

- **Turnkey / no infrastructure.** Everything needed to run a paid group is already built in: no Stripe account, no website, no inventory, no new hires. "School works out of the box." Works like social media, so there's nothing new to learn — log in and collect money in minutes.
- **Recurring revenue model.** A paid community generates monthly recurring revenue (MRR) rather than one-off sales.
- **Built for beginners.** Explicitly for people brand-new to business or online business. You do not need an existing audience — per Alex, half the top winners "didn't have an organic audience."
- **The goal Alex sets: $10,000/month.** He says this number kept surfacing from his community, and frames multiple paths to it: 50 people at $200/mo, 1,000 at $10/mo, 20 at $500/mo — "a lot of ways to skin this cat."
- **The core system ("the game"):** post content across platforms (Facebook posts, Instagram posts/reels, YouTube shorts/longs, TikTok) → siphon that traffic into the Skool community → the community converts it into money. "You post content. You siphon the traffic to school. School converts it and turns it into money."
- **His own partnership rationale.** Alex frames his Skool involvement as complementary-skill-set partnering: founder Sam loves building product and didn't want to make content all day; Alex has an audience with the exact problem the product solves and likes making content/teaching, but didn't want to build a product. (See self-reported stats flagged in Notes.)

## Community-building playbook

Tactics Alex gives for succeeding in / building a Skool community (via the Skool Games structure):

- **Get the first three paying members.** Getting three paying members unlocks the top-10 trainings; framed as "get the first dollar across the bridge" — proof it's possible changes what you believe you can do.
- **Leaderboards reward growth, not total.** Each monthly Skool Games ranks communities by MRR *growth* over the month, so newcomers can win/compete even against larger communities; if you don't win one month you reset and play again.
- **Top-10 mastermind loop.** The 10 fastest-growing communities each month are flown out together to share best practices and learn platform-wide patterns; those learnings are then distributed to everyone who hit three members.
- **Help other people first (relationship capital).** From his own mastermind days as "low man on the totem pole": he helped everyone one-on-one with the one thing he was good at, asked for nothing back, and later (a) was voted member of the year and (b) built "a rolodex of people that owed me one." Side benefit, not tit-for-tat.
- **Make it fun / find a partner.** Rule number one is "have fun" (cites Tony Robbins' *Money Master the Game* — top performers treat business as a game). Two ways to make it more fun: find a friend ("doing shitty stuff with a friend is still more fun than doing shitty stuff alone"), or partner with someone who has a *complementary* skill set — if you don't want to build an audience, find someone who has one; if you lack expertise, find or source it from your community.
- **Weekly live support.** Alex hosts live Q&A calls (says every Monday / weekly), typically 200–300 attendees, so members don't get stuck on any step.
- **Free trial / risk-reversal onboarding.** Start free; if you quit you keep the training; framed as a "risk-free test drive" — you can't make an informed decision from the outside, so get inside for ~two weeks and decide then. Cancel in one click from your profile.
