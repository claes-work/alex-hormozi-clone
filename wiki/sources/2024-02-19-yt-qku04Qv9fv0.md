# Alex Hormozi Explains How to Find The Best Business Opportunities

## Summary
In this short interview clip, Alex Hormozi answers an interviewer's question about what to look for when acquiring or investing in a cash-flowing business. His central thesis: the best businesses — especially in an inflationary period — have low capital expenses, high gross margins, and pricing power. He contrasts capital-heavy businesses (inventory, supply chain, manufacturing, heavy equipment) with low-capex businesses (services, digital, software) that can produce 10x more units without materially changing their cost basis. He points to insurance and banking as the highest-leverage businesses precisely because they have endured for 100+ years ("a great breadcrumb of like this thing has to print money"), cites Warren Buffett and Geico, and closes with practical rules of thumb — demand 80%+ gross margins (5x cost of goods) on a service business, and use niching down, avatar specificity, and productization to drive efficiency and margin.

## Key claims
- The best businesses, especially in an inflationary period, are those with low capital expenses; wealthy people choose higher-leverage opportunities.
- High-capex businesses to avoid: lots of inventory, supply chain, manufacturing, or heavy equipment — anything where you must constantly buy more stuff to increase capacity.
- Low-capex businesses: services and digital businesses. Software is "mixed" because the development team can sometimes count as a capital expense, depending on how it's built.
- The test of low capex: if you can produce 10x more units without phenomenally changing the cost basis, the business has lower capital expenses — and such businesses tend to produce more cash flow and have more pricing power.
- Insurance is "just math" — literally assessing/pricing risk with no real capital product (cites Warren Buffett and Geico).
- Business longevity is a signal of high leverage: insurance and banks (JP Morgan, founded 1800s) have lasted 100+ years through wars, famines, and depressions, which proves they "print money."
- Gross margin = the incremental cost to make one extra unit; the biggest businesses have phenomenal gross margins.
- A pill costs ~$100M to make the first one and a penny for every one after; sold at $10 each, that's a very high gross margin.
- Starting from a low (~50%) gross margin makes it very hard to make money, because after covering cost of goods you still must pay everyone else out of the remaining half.
- Rule of thumb for a service business: get gross margins above 80%, i.e. charge at least 5x cost of goods (if it costs $100/month, charge minimum $500).
- This reframes the question from "how much can I charge?" to "how can I provide value while making it cost as little as possible / being as efficient as possible."
- Technology (including internal process breakthroughs) takes something valuable and lowers the cost of delivering it, which is how things once reserved for the wealthy become accessible to the common person.
- Niching down and choosing a specific avatar lets you productize the service; doing everything custom (as most beginners do) makes efficiency and margin hard.
- Doing the same thing repeatedly makes you faster, cheaper, and more efficient; the extra margin then funds hiring the best people and investing in marketing. Little margin makes growth very hard.
