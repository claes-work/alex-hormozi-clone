# If You Don't Understand Margin, You Don't Understand Business (2021-02-18)

## Summary

In this early (2021) whiteboard-style video, Hormozi walks through the difference between
gross margin and net margin, framing gross margin as the single number he most examines to
judge a business's health and investability. He argues that small incremental gains in gross
margin percentage compound dramatically into net profit, sets an 80% gross margin as his rule
of thumb for any service business, and shows the two levers to reach it: decrease the cost of
fulfilling one additional unit, or increase price. He closes with a real mastermind example of
a coaching business sitting at 75% gross margin.

> ⚠️ EARLY FRAMEWORK (2021): This is proto-thinking that later hardens into the
> LTGP:CAC / gross-profit-not-revenue canon in [[../topics/business/business-math]]. Here
> Hormozi already insists you spend and think off gross profit ("what's left over"), not
> revenue, but the mature acquisition-math vocabulary (LTGP, CAC, the 3:1 ratio) is not yet
> present. Treat the 80% rule and the "each margin point doubles" claims as his 2021 position.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2021)

- Gross vs. net margin is the number-one thing small/medium business owners fail to grasp,
  and gross margin is the first number he looks at when deciding whether to invest. (2021)
- Gross margin = revenue − cost of goods sold (COGS); he stresses it is just subtraction, not
  hard math. (2021)
- For a service business, COGS is the *incremental* cost of one additional unit only — not
  front desk, HR director, rent, or overhead. (2021)
- Net margin is what's left after everything is paid — the take-home at the end of the month. (2021)
- Gross margin "begets" net margin, so gross margin is the lever he focuses on and fixes. (2021)
- Small gross-margin gains compound: moving a business from ~66% to 80% gross margin roughly
  2.2x'd the modeled net margin (12% → 26%) in his example. (2021)
- His rule of thumb: get any service business over 80% gross margin. (2021)
- Two ways to raise gross margin: (1) decrease the cost to fulfill, (2) increase the price. (2021)
- The biggest businesses in the world run ~99% gross margins, not 80%. (2021)
- Each step up the margin ladder roughly doubles profitability because it halves the cost:
  80→90 is twice as profitable, 90→95 twice again, etc. — meaning you can serve twice as many
  people for the same cost, which is how you scale. (2021)
- Entrepreneurs undercharge because they feel bad charging a lot for things that don't cost
  much; without high margin you can't profit, can't afford to acquire customers, and can't scale. (2021)
- Out of the gross profit you must fund customer acquisition, all remaining overhead, and still
  leave a profit; he references being willing to spend a set percentage of revenue on acquisition. (2021)
- Worked mastermind example: coach paid $4,000/month can handle 40 clients; clients pay a
  $1,200 package for 3 months = $400/month, so 40 × $400 = $16,000/month revenue; $16k − $4k =
  $12k left, giving 12/16 = 75% gross margin. To clear 80% he'd need to reach ~$12,800 in
  coach-cost-adjusted terms — i.e. charge ~$20 more per client per month, or pay the coach
  ~$3,200/month. (2021)

## Topic notes

This is an **early (2021) version of the gross-profit / margin principle** that belongs in
[[../topics/business/business-math]]. Points to carry forward when synthesizing that hub:

- **Gross profit, not revenue, is the operating number.** Hormozi already teaches that spending
  (acquisition, overhead, profit) must come out of "what's left over" after fulfillment cost —
  the seed of the later "you get paid on gross profit, not revenue" line and the LTGP side of
  LTGP:CAC. The mature LTGP/CAC vocabulary is *not* yet used here.
- **Service COGS = incremental cost of one more unit**, explicitly excluding overhead (rent, HR,
  front desk). This definition is consistent with his later teaching and worth pinning as his
  stable position.
- **The 80% gross-margin rule of thumb for service businesses** is stated flatly here as his
  investment/health screen. Flag as a dated 2021 heuristic; check whether later sources restate,
  raise, or nuance it.
- **"Each margin step doubles profitability because it halves cost"** (80→90→95) is the compounding
  argument he uses to justify pushing price. Note the framing: it's about serving twice as many
  people for the same cost, i.e. margin as a scaling enabler, not just a profit number.
- **Two levers — cut fulfillment cost or raise price** — with a clear lean toward raising price
  and overcoming the psychological barrier to charging for low-cost-to-deliver offers. This
  prefigures the pricing/value themes in [[../topics/business/pricing]].
