# Helping a Restaurant Owner Build a Franchise Model

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The client runs an American barbecue / smoked-meats restaurant group doing ~$9M revenue across four corporate locations plus one franchise open (two more signed), and wants to scale to $100M system-wide by franchising. He frames his problem as two businesses (drive consumer demand for venues; sell more franchises) and insists a franchise "can only be bought, it can't be sold" because it's a lifestyle change for the buyer. Alex disagrees with the no-selling stance but zooms out to his core franchising thesis: **every franchising problem is solved by one North Star metric — unit economics**. You have to fix the restaurant first. A compelling franchise offer ("costs $100k to open, makes $500k profit year one") sells itself; the client's trouble selling franchises is a symptom of an offer that isn't compelling, which is the *same* reason it's unattractive for him to keep scaling as corporate. Alex then walks the exit math to show the $100M-system goal is actually a small franchisor: at 7% royalty on $100M = $7M top line, 40% margins = $2.8M EBITDA, ~12x = ~$36M, minus a forced ~30% rollover = ~$22M to the owner *after* opening 100 locations. By contrast, staying "Mr. Panda" and going from 4 to 20 owned locations while improving per-unit profit (from $1.2M on four toward $2M, then ~$500k each × 20 = $10M bottom line, ~8x = ~$80M) is both more likely and worth far more. His prescription: spend 24 months going "down to the penny" to make each unit dramatically more profitable — if Panda Express runs 27% net margins carrying 3,000-store corporate overhead, this owner should be able to too. He notes the FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document) limits what he can *promise*, but he can state the real numbers of open locations honestly. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo (ignored for claims).

## Key claims

- As of March 2026, he argues a franchise that is trouble to sell simply has an offer that isn't compelling: strong unit economics ("$100k to open, $500k profit year one") make franchises sell themselves.
- As of March 2026, he ties the two sides together — whatever makes it unattractive for the owner to keep scaling as corporate is exactly what makes it unattractive for a franchisee to buy in.
- As of March 2026, he disagrees with the client's belief that a franchise "can only be bought, not sold"; you can and should sell people on transforming their lifestyle, while selecting for buyers who will succeed rather than ones who will "suck."
- As of March 2026, he notes the FDD constrains what a franchisor may *promise*, but the owner can still "state the facts and tell the truth" about the real metrics of already-open locations.
- As of March 2026 (exit math), he estimates a $100M system-wide franchisor at 7% royalty ≈ $7M top line, ~40% net margin ≈ $2.8M EBITDA, ~12x multiple ≈ $36M, minus a forced ~30% rollover ≈ ~$22M to the owner — after the work of opening 100 locations.

- As of March 2026, he cites Panda Express running ~27% net margins across ~3,000 stores with full corporate overhead as proof this owner should be able to run comparable margins.
- As of March 2026, he prescribes taking 24 months to go "down to the penny" and make each unit significantly more profitable, rather than three-step tactical tips.
