# The Key to Transforming Your Mindset

## Summary

In this short clip, Alex Hormozi reframes "revenge" for past hardship as an inward project: instead of blaming external people or circumstances, you take revenge on the lesser version of yourself that got you where you are, by doing the exact opposite of what that version did. He argues that wherever you point the blame finger, power follows — so blaming others hands them control over your life. The clip then turns to the dichotomy of "change yourself vs. change the world" and the self-acceptance movement, concluding that healthy self-acceptance means accepting the higher, ideal version you can live up to — not the current version that falls short.

## Key claims

- If you want revenge for the bad things in your life, start with the version of yourself that hasn't lived up to its potential — many people fight a daily "battle" against a lesser self.
- **Wherever you point the finger of blame, power follows**; whoever you blame for your life is the person you hand power over your existence and circumstances.
- Turning the blame inward is painful, but the only thing you actually control is the actions you take — and the only person in control of that is you.
- Even if others genuinely wronged you or you were dealt a bad hand, it doesn't matter, because you can only control your own actions.
- The "revenge" is identifying what the version of you who got you here did, then acting in the exact opposite way as often as possible — the pain of rejecting your old habits is the real revenge.
- On the dichotomy "accept the world and change yourself" vs. "change the world and accept yourself": when you change yourself, you change the world, because you change how you see it.
- People feel "icky" about the self-acceptance movement because it can mean accepting a version of yourself that falls short of what it could be — one that doesn't live up to its word or whose thoughts, actions, and intentions aren't aligned.
- True self-acceptance is accepting the ideal you can live up to — the potential/higher version — not the current lesser version; this reconciles both sides of the "change vs. accept" argument.
