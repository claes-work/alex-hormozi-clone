# Helping an Accounting Business Hire Better Talent

## Summary
In a live business Q&A, a guest (Ben) who runs a traditional accounting firm asks Alex how to acquire mid-level ("semi-senior") staff — accountants with or working toward a CPA/EA license. Alex offers two recruiting tactics (build a referral/"farm system" with the schools where those candidates train, and do direct outreach to other accountants) but quickly reframes the hiring problem as a pricing problem. By probing the guest's unit economics — ~$120K gross profit and ~$200K gross revenue per accountant (60% margins) and an 80% close rate — Alex diagnoses chronic undercharging that prevents paying above-market wages needed to attract talent. His prescription: with a supply constraint and high close rates, double or triple prices, which raises per-accountant economics toward his 80%-margin services rule of thumb (~$400K revenue) and frees up cash to pay accountants ~$150K. He tells the guest to test it by keeping the exact same pitch and simply doubling the number stated at the end of the call. The bulk of the exchange is Alex pushing the guest past the psychological block of "saying a different number," arguing that if the guest truly believed his service was valuable he'd already charge more.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-02-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-02-11) The guest runs a traditional accounting firm offering a standard suite — year-end taxes, bookkeeping, and monthly payroll — bundled for small-to-medium businesses in the ~$500K–$1M range. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-11) The guest's stated hiring problem is finding qualified mid-level / manager-level accounting staff (candidates with or on the path to a CPA or EA license); he finds junior hires easy. [guest-attributed]
- (2026-02-11) To recruit candidates on the CPA/EA path, Alex advises going to the schools where they train and setting up a referral program — a "farm system" — since those institutions want career opportunities for their people.
- (2026-02-11) A second recruiting channel is direct outreach to other accountants.
- (2026-02-11) The guest reports ~$120,000 gross profit and ~$200,000 gross revenue per accountant per year, paying each accountant ~$80,000 — i.e., roughly 60% gross margins. [guest-attributed figures]
- (2026-02-11) Alex's rule of thumb for service businesses is 80% gross margins or higher; at 60% the guest is undercharging.
- (2026-02-11) At an 80% margin target, the firm would need ~$400,000 of revenue per accountant per year.
- (2026-02-11) The guest reports ~80% close rates on customers. [guest-attributed figure]
- (2026-02-11) An 80% close rate signals a clear, "no question" opportunity to at least double — maybe triple — prices; that upside is "sitting right there."
- (2026-02-11) The core diagnosis: the guest is supply-constrained, and undercharging is what prevents paying above-market wages to attract the talent he needs — so the hiring problem is really a pricing problem.
- (2026-02-11) Doubling price would unlock roughly an extra $200,000 per accountant per year, which lets the firm offer accountants ~$150,000 and actually land them.
- (2026-02-11) Even paying accountants $150K, the guest would still make far more than he does now — and be less busy.
- (2026-02-11) To test the new pricing, keep the exact same pitch and simply double the number when you state the price at the end of the call.
- (2026-02-11) If fewer customers close after the price increase, it doesn't matter because the business is supply-constrained anyway.
- (2026-02-11) Alex argues the guest doesn't truly believe his own value: if he thought the offer was that good, he'd charge more — "you got to put your money where your mouth is."
- (2026-02-11) The real obstacle is psychological — the guest is intimidated by having to say a different (higher) number at the end of the call; the worst case is one person says no, and prices can always be changed back.
