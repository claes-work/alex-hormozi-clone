# How True A Players Act

## Summary
Solo clip on how to recognize elite ("A-player") talent. Alex's frame: as you climb the "entrepreneurial food chain," the level of talent you can attract and the level of mediocrity you repel both get more extreme — so your very *belief about what good talent looks like* keeps shifting upward. He illustrates this with his own hiring ladder from the gym days: his first $50k hire felt like "this is what talent looks like," then $75k made $50k look like nothing, then his first six-figure hire ("this guy better cure cancer"), then $250k, $500k, $1M, and multi-million — each tier a night-and-day jump in intelligence, work ethic, and experience. His central diagnostic: **the more senior and capable the person, the more general your directives can be.** A true C-suite hire needs no instruction on *how* to do the job — they should be bringing you things *you* don't know to make the function better; if you have to give directives or re-prioritize their work, they're "barely a director." He runs the ladder in reverse to show it: at C-level he can say "build out acquisition for this business" and stop; a manager/director needs the campaign, angle, and copy hooks specified; a junior copywriter needs the grade level and tone spelled out. Two more markers of real A-players: (1) past roughly $1M/year, they come with their own **teams, networks, and Rolodex** — they cherry-pick "savages" from prior companies and arrive with near-complete, pre-built solutions (controllers, chosen systems, playbooks), so the founder's input is "like nothing"; Alex says if recruited he'd only join if he could bring six people with him. (2) True leadership means handling the hard things yourself — if the boss has to have the tough conversations with *your* team because you can't, "you're not a leader." He also flips this into promotion feedback: wanting a promotion means all the things he currently does *for* you are things he should no longer have to do. Ends with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, as you move up the entrepreneurial ladder, both the caliber of talent you attract and the mediocrity you repel become more extreme; one of the biggest beliefs that changes is *what you think good talent even looks like*.
- As of March 2026, the clearest test of someone's level is how general your directives to them can be — the more senior/capable, the less you have to specify; you can tell how advanced someone is by how many directives and how much detail they need.
- As of March 2026, a genuine C-suite hire should need no instruction on how to do the job and should bring you things you don't know; if you have to give directives or re-prioritize their work, they're "barely a director."
- As of March 2026, past roughly $1M/year, hires typically arrive with their own departments — they cherry-pick top people from past companies and bring pre-built solutions (staff, chosen systems, playbooks), so the founder's input is minimal and the function stands up far faster.
- As of March 2026, Alex says if he were recruited to "stand up the revenue-generating function" of a business, he'd only go if he could bring about six of his own people.
- As of March 2026, real leadership means handling the hard things yourself — if the boss has to have the tough conversations with your team because you can't, you're not a leader; leadership is a single word containing many sub-skills.
- As of March 2026, wanting a promotion implies you should no longer need the boss to do the things he currently does for you — that dependency is itself the feedback.
- As of March 2026, Alex reports his own team includes roughly six or seven employees earning over $1M/year, and describes it as a "different caliber" and "night and day" better than the earlier Gym Launch team.
