# There Are Only 3 Things You Need to Focus On

## Summary
Solo clip. Alex compresses all of business strategy into **three strategic buckets** every company must allocate resources into: (1) **get more customers**, (2) **increase lifetime gross profit per customer** (make each customer worth more), and (3) **decrease risk** — i.e. increase the likelihood that the first two keep happening. He maps this onto a **70/20/10 resource split**: ~70% goes toward getting more customers and making them worth more (better retention "ladders up" to the second bucket), while the risk bucket is typically "the new thing" — taking 10–20% of profit each year to build out a future line before the current one decays (his example: if YouTube ever goes the way of legacy TV, you'd need to fund a new team early). Key execution mistake to avoid: **don't pull the stars off the thing that's working** to staff the new bet — hire people who can build the new thing, or you sacrifice the core and end up "totally screwed" on both. He frames the mature CEO as a **"flex player"** who can parachute into any division solving a complex problem, because the founder uniquely holds decision-making power and can allocate resources / say yes instantly without a committee — which is why it's unfair to expect the team to move as fast. He closes on his definition of strategy: **"strategy is just a fancy word people say when they mean prioritization"** — unlimited opportunities against limited resources. Ends with the acquisition.com 10-stage roadmap (0→$100M+) promo.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, every business has three strategic buckets for resource allocation: (1) get more customers, (2) increase lifetime gross profit per customer, (3) decrease risk (increase the likelihood the first two don't stop happening).
- As of February 2026, more customers grows the company "period"; alternatively, keeping customer count flat but making each customer worth more also grows it.
- As of February 2026, he applies a 70/20/10 split: ~70% toward getting more customers and increasing their value, with retention laddering up to the second bucket; the risk bucket (~10–20% of profit yearly) funds "the new thing" that ensures the future.
- As of February 2026, the core execution mistake is pulling the top people ("the stars") off the working line to build the new bet — you must instead find people who can build the new thing, or you sacrifice the core and jeopardize both.
- As of February 2026, the CEO should eventually become a "flex player" who parachutes into any division with a complex problem, leveraging the founder's unique decision-making power and ability to allocate resources / say yes immediately.
- As of February 2026, it is unfair to expect the team to move as fast as the founder, because the founder can write the checks and say yes without running it up the flagpole or checking a committee.
- As of February 2026, strategy is defined as prioritization: unlimited opportunities against limited resources, and how you prioritize the former against the latter.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Every business has three strategic buckets that it has to allocate its resources into. Number one is how do we get more customers?... Number two, how do we increase lifetime gross profit per customer?... Bucket three is how do we decrease risk? Aka, how do we increase the likelihood that the first two things don't stop happening?"

> "Don't take the stars that are making the one thing work and then push them on the other thing. You have to find the people who can build this."

> "You want to eventually become the flex player, which is that you can parachute in to a specific division or department that's solving a complex issue."

> "You can write the checks and because you can say yes. You don't need to check with a committee. You don't need to run it up the flag pole."

> "Strategy is just a fancy word that people say when they mean prioritization. That's all it means."
