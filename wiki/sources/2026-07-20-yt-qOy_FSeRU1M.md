# How Do We Win Trust in a New City?

## Summary
In this coaching exchange, a caller named Isaac Hamlin — founder and CEO of Better Blend, a chain of 13 macro-focused smoothie shops (high protein, low calorie, low sugar) doing roughly $7–8M in revenue, half company-owned and half franchised — tells Alex Hormozi his goal is $1M in revenue per location, and that the obstacle is a trust/CAC problem: when Better Blend expands into markets far from its Cincinnati home base, people either doubt the product tastes good or doubt it's healthy, so new stores struggle to get people in the door.

Alex first probes the "$1M per store" framing, then addresses franchising directly: he isn't opposed to franchising in general but is skeptical of it for low-skilled-labor service businesses (citing gyms — F45 and Orange Theory both out of business, and noting that businesses like CrossFit that stuck around did so because they're licensing plays rather than true franchise models with standardized delivery), and is more comfortable with it in food, where the product is easier to standardize. He then turns to the CAC problem itself, arguing every successful brick-and-mortar chain has a highly dialed grand-opening playbook — a detailed path to profitability within 30/60/90 days, with promotions scheduled day-by-day for months beforehand (using Chick-fil-A's opening-day lines as the reference point). His prescription: a standout giveaway capped at the first ~500 customers (citing Wendy's golden-keychain free-Frosty promotion as a model), which works because it recruits mostly local customers who then become "ambassadors" bringing friends who buy full-margin items alongside the freebie; marketing and list-building should start at least 60 days before a launch; and CAC-recouping offers (BOGOs, half-off kids' items) should be paired with a single trained staff upsell question at the point of redemption.

Alex states that the single most important metric for this whole strategy is what percentage of customers return after their first visit — his proxy for LTV. When Isaac says LTV is hard to calculate for a brick-and-mortar, Alex disagrees, calling it "super doable," and suggests a 30-day return window is a reasonable proxy for a smoothie business (if someone doesn't come back within a month, they weren't thrilled). He also recommends "running start" punch cards — pre-punching two or three of ten slots when handing the card to a customer — noting people are roughly three times more likely to finish a card that already has a head start. The video closes with Alex's standard promotional offer for the $100M scaling roadmap quiz at acquisitions.com/roadmap.
