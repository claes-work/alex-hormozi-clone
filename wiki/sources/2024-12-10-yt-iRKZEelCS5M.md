# How this investment changes education forever

## Summary

Alex Hormozi explains the reasoning behind becoming co-owner of Skool — which he
frames as his single largest investment to date, including putting his personal
brand and endorsement behind the company. He walks through a "Lollapalooza
effect" (Charlie Munger) thesis: the bet works not because of one silver bullet
but because several forces stack — a large, fast-shifting education market, his
personal fit (he built companies serving creators), and mission alignment with
Skool's founder. Central macro claim: money is crisscrossing from shrinking
formal education into growing alternative/online education, flowing "down" to
individual creators who teach niche skills.

## Key claims

- His stake in Skool is his single largest investment to date, and he is also
  putting his brand behind it and endorsing the company (self-reported).
- Investment framing uses Charlie Munger's "Lollapalooza effect" — explosive
  growth comes not from one "silver bullet" but from "a hundred golden BBs":
  multiple incremental factors that multiply into an outsized outcome.
- Market sizing (his cited figures, approximate/directional): global education
  ~$10 trillion; US ~$1.3 trillion; formal education (2- and 4-year degrees)
  ~$850 billion; alternative / e-learning / online education ~$200–250 billion.
- The "crisscross" thesis: formal education is shrinking ~6% per year (losing
  ~$50 billion/year) while alternative education grows ~20% per year (gaining
  ~$40 billion/year) — roughly the amount one loses equals what the other gains.
- Underlying driver is a shift in buying behavior: people increasingly see that
  formal education doesn't deliver the promised ROI, and can instead learn
  income-producing skills directly from creators.
- Money is being pushed away from the "mediocre middle" of universities (he
  argues top schools are protected "for a while") down to creators who teach a
  proven niche skill ("I did this thing, it worked, here's how you can do it").
- Personal-fit factor: his first sold companies (Gym Launch, Prestige Labs)
  served creators; coming from a direct-response marketing / media-and-licensing
  background to brick-and-mortar gyms, he says he built the "university" that
  didn't exist for gym owners.
- He says he "lived through this huge shift in wealth," having (self-reported)
  taken home ~$16 million in income around age 27 by capitalizing on this trend.
- Audience-fit factor: his audience is split between business owners and
  aspiring business owners; his stated mission is "to make real business
  accessible for everyone," which he says aligns with Skool's mission to make
  business "fun and easy for everyone."
- He says he chose Skool over other promotable things (e.g. drop shipping)
  specifically because of mission alignment, and spent six months talking with
  the founder about the vision before investing.
- Decision-making framework offered to viewers: model the thinking (macro trend
  + personal fit + mission alignment stacking together), not a single tactic.
