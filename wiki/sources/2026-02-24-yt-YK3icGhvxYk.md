# This Mistake Cost Me $50 Million

> ★ **L3-CANDIDATE — new biographical detail.** Alex recounts a specific, dated, dollar-figured mistake from his **Gym Launch days**: reacting to a low-price competitor by cutting his own price to his existing base, which cost ~$6–7M/year in profit and ~**$50M** once the multiplied loss was baked into the eventual sale. ## Summary

Solo Alex (with a brief interviewer prompt at the end) tells the story of what he calls one of his **two most costly business mistakes**. A competitor undercut him during the **Gym Launch era**: the competitor recruited/partnered with ~10 of Alex's own top customers — evangelists he had converted into "semi-employees" and used as big ad testimonials — who then started running ads for the competitor instead (he likens it to the Verizon guy switching to AT&T). The competitor was **cheaper** and offered **one-on-one coaching**, which Alex never did.

Feeling threatened ("wartime"), Alex did a large internal **relaunch to his existing recurring customer base** — a big value stack offering *more* for *less*. He cut monthly price from roughly **$3,000 to $2,500** per customer, dropping topline recurring revenue by **$500,000/month**. Because costs also rose, topline fell by **$6 million** and profit by **~$6–7 million/year**. He later sold the business (which "never recovered that profit"), and because the lost profit got multiplied at sale, the mistake cost him **~$50 million** in total.

The kicker: **churn stayed exactly the same** — the price cut made no difference to cancellations because both prices were past the "this is a lot of money" threshold. And the first customer reaction to the lower price wasn't gratitude but a complaint that he hadn't cut it earlier. His lesson: don't abandon your position to copy a low-price competitor — stay focused on the customer and let the competitor discover for himself that undercutting is unprofitable.
