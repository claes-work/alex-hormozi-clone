# My Customers Only Buy Once. How Do I Grow?

## Summary

In this coaching exchange, a caller who owns an e-commerce company selling men's wedding bands describes an LTV-to-CAC ratio of roughly 1.6 and struggles to identify his first constraint. The product is largely a one-and-done purchase (about a 20% repeat rate) in a shrinking market, and the business rode cheap Facebook ads in 2017–2019 before conditions tightened. He is unsure whether he faces a bigger marketing problem or a bigger product problem, and mentions a plan to expand average order value from ~$170 to $1,000+ by outfitting the whole groom (suit, ties, groomsmen gifts).

Alex reframes the situation rather than optimizing it. He argues a one-and-done purchase is fundamentally an arbitrage or "media" business — a smash-and-grab where the only real asset is the ability to acquire customers cheaper than they cost, not a durable "business business." Because the caller lacks unique manufacturing, sits in a shrinking market, and admits he isn't emotionally invested, Alex says (unusually candidly) that he agrees the business isn't compelling. The good news he offers is that the caller's skills — a page that converts and the ability to run Facebook ads — are highly transferable to a better next venture.

Alex closes on sellability: the business won't be sellable until it becomes a brand with demonstrable repurchase rates. He ties this back to the caller's realization about the value of revenue retention, and suggests that recognizing this now may save him another two years of effort. The exchange ends with the standard acquisition.com scaling-roadmap offer.

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-19. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- (2026-07-19) One-and-done purchases are fundamentally arbitrage businesses: you know how to acquire customers cheaper than it costs and run as much volume as possible through that mechanism.
- (2026-07-19) An arbitrage/one-and-done model is really a media business — a "smash and grab" — not a durable "business business."
- (2026-07-19) To become a real business, a one-and-done product would have to become a brand built around a style or identity of man, leveraging a core manufacturing capability.
- (2026-07-19) The men's-jewelry / decked-out-groom expansion (raising AOV via suits, ties, groomsmen gifts) is a plausible route, but Alex flags the suit/tie extensions as "way out of scope" relative to the core.
- (2026-07-19) The caller's weak position is compounded by a shrinking market, lack of unique manufacturing ability, and low personal interest in the category.
- (2026-07-19) The core transferable assets the caller built are a landing page that converts and the ability to run Facebook ads — skills he can carry into a smarter next venture.
- (2026-07-19) Alex declined to give a motivational speech, judging the caller wasn't genuinely invested; being "super real" was more useful than encouragement.
- (2026-07-19) A business like this is not sellable until it becomes a brand and can show repurchase (retention) rates.
- (2026-07-19) Recognizing the value of revenue retention early can save the owner years of misdirected effort.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The company is a men's wedding-band e-commerce business with an LTV:CAC of ~1.6, price points of $100–$500 (avg ~$170), and a ~20% repeat rate.
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ The bands are the company's own designs, manufactured overseas (not drop-shipped), but there's "only so much you can do with a wedding band."
- (2026-07-19) _(attribution: audience — caller)_ He bought the business (a former agency client) two years ago, is a marketer by trade, and isn't emotionally attached to it.
