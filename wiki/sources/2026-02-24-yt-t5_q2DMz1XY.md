# The Secret to Staying Focused

## Summary

A short solo clip in which Alex Hormozi reframes productivity as an act of
subtraction rather than addition. He defines commitment as the elimination of
alternatives (marriage as the analogy) and focus as the state of doing nothing
but the one thing — the most focused person does the fewest things outside the
thing they are focused on. He argues productivity "hacks" fail because people
try to add activities, which is the opposite of focus; real focus is getting rid
of everything (including environmental distractions like windows, foot traffic,
door knocks, Slack notifications) that isn't the work. He uses a "wall" analogy:
you can only get over the wall by concentrating all your ladder rungs on one
climbing point instead of spreading four half-ladders across several — any one
opportunity works, but none works unless you commit to one. He illustrates with
a cyber-security founder he met (good margins, retention, easy customer
acquisition) whose only real problem is boredom and the urge to start more
things. Zooming out, he says 20 years of reps on a single project makes you good
regardless of which project it is, so the objective is simply to pick one thing
and stick with it. The hard part of a plan isn't making it or following it — it's
sticking with it. Closes with a promo for his free acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims (2026-02-24, paraphrased)

- Productivity comes from all the things you choose not to do. (paraphrase)
- Commitment is the elimination of alternatives (e.g. marriage eliminates all
  alternatives to your spouse), which is very similar to focus. (paraphrase)
- The hypothetical extreme of focus is doing literally nothing but one thing —
  not even eating or sleeping — so the most focused person does the fewest things
  outside the thing they're focused on. (paraphrase)
- Focus is measured by the number of things you say no to. (paraphrase)
- People chase productivity hacks that add things to their lives, which is
  counter to what focus means; focus is getting rid of everything that isn't the
  thing. (paraphrase)
- Environment matters: windows, people walking past, door knocks, and Slack
  notifications all break focus because none of them are the work. (paraphrase)
- Wall analogy: you need a critical mass to get over the wall; spreading effort
  across four separate half-built ladders never reaches the required height, but
  stacking all those rungs into one ladder gets you over. (paraphrase)
- The fallacy is "I'll do three or four things and see which one works" — any of
  them will work, but none of them will work unless you work on just one.
  (paraphrase)
- This happens at all levels of business — beginners starting five things, and
  established owners (e.g. a cyber-security founder with 50% margins, strong
  revenue retention, no trouble getting or delivering to customers) whose only
  problem is boredom and the urge to start more things. (paraphrase)
- Someone who improves every year on the same project for 20 years will
  inevitably be very good — and it doesn't matter which project it is; 20 years
  of reps on one thing, doing nothing else, makes you good. (paraphrase)
- Since being good after 20 years of practice is a near-certainty, the objective
  is just to get 20 years of practice in one thing and stick with it.
  (paraphrase)
- Your plans aren't failing because the plans are wrong; they're failing because
  you're not working on the plan. The hard part isn't creating or following the
  plan — it's sticking with it. (paraphrase)
- Promo: acquisition.com/roadmap is a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+
  that fewer than 1% of companies finish, broken down by eight business functions
  with the constraint symptoms and the steps taken to graduate each stage;
  validated across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar
  businesses. (paraphrase)
