# Why you should NEVER listen to your parents at any age (2021-07-28)

## Summary

In this short 2021 monologue, Hormozi argues against blanket-trusting parents' (or anyone's)
advice on ambitious pursuits, framing it as a risk-perception problem rather than a slight
against family. His rule: only take advice from people wealthier than you *want* to be, because
cautious naysayers are statistically right most of the time yet wrong on the rare high-stakes
decisions that actually change a life. He ties this to a macro-risk-vs-micro-risk distinction:
individual failures are cheap, but never taking the risk at all is the only guaranteed way to
fail.

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- One of his first "rules of life": never listen to someone poorer than you — or poorer than
  you *want* to be. Age doesn't exempt parents; the test is whether they've achieved what you
  aspire to. (2021)
- Cautious people default to the less-risky side because most ventures fail (he cites "95% of
  businesses fail within 5 years"), so naysayers are right ~99% of the time — which
  self-reinforces their sense of being right. (2021)
- But they are 100% wrong on the 1% of decisions that matter; the one bet they'd talk you out of
  is the one that changes everything. Analogy: if a friend dislikes every person you date,
  they're right about everyone except the one you marry. (2021)
- Micro-risk vs macro-risk: the micro risk (this business fails) is survivable and expected; the
  macro risk (never taking a shot, so never succeeding) is the real danger. Not taking the risk
  is the only guaranteed path to *not* succeeding. (2021)
- Cites Warren Buffett: most people only need to make four or five really good decisions in their
  life — so people should spend far more thought on the major forks (career, city, marriage,
  business partner) than on trivial choices like the next Netflix show. (2021)
- "Mistakes love a rushed decision" — take time over the few high-leverage decisions. (2021)
- Invokes Gary Vee's "micro speed, macro patience"; adds "money loves speed, wealth loves time"
  and "you get paid for what you do, you get returns on what you own." Building wealth means
  building an asset, which requires time. (2021)
- Explicitly frames the video as a response to younger followers who feel insecure about
  disapproval from parents/friends who earn less than they aspire to. (2021)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "One of my first rules of life is never to listen to someone who's poorer than you or poorer
> than you want to be."

> "They are 99% of the time right, but they're 100% wrong."

> "The only way that is guaranteed to not get to success is to never take the risk to begin with."

> "The biggest risk is not doing anything at all."

> "You get paid for what you do, you get returns on what you own. And what we're trying to build
> here is an asset that can pay us over time. Because if we cannot learn how to build that asset,
> we will work until the day we die."

## Biographical note

Hormozi does **not** reference his own father or a personal father-conflict in this video. The
"parents" framing is generic/rhetorical, delivered as advice to young followers rather than
autobiography. The only personal biographical references are: he considered investment banking as
the one job paying what he wanted but rejected the ~20-year life-cost trade-off; he quit a
consulting job and sank his savings into opening a gym against much advice that it was "stupid";
and he notes the first gym was not the business that "set him free" — it was "four or five
businesses from there," after assembling skills one by one. Opens by stating he owns a portfolio
of companies doing $85 million/year in revenue. (See [[persona/biography]] for the father-conflict
story from other sources; it is not corroborated here.)
