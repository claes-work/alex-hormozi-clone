# You can make way more money than you think

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex argues that the "my market is saturated / too small"
excuse is almost always false and is really a cover for a lack of skill in
advertising and lead generation. He reframes total addressable market (TAM):
most entrepreneurs have tapped less than 1% of their market because they only
advertise on one platform, in one medium, to one level of awareness — ignoring
the vast remaining pie across channels, ad formats, and outreach. He then covers
why red oceans are opportunities, how "niche down" reconciles with going broad
(puddle → pond → lake → ocean), five directions to move a market (up, down,
adjacent, broader, narrower), the cost of switching opportunities, "features not
bugs" of different business models (info, SaaS, e-com, service), and closes with
a mindset argument about owning the outcome and choosing "the cooler story."

## Key claims

- Alex has been in business 14 years; his portfolio, acquisition.com, did over
  $250M in aggregate revenue last year.
- The #1 excuse entrepreneurs give for not growing is that their market is
  saturated / too small / too competitive — and 99% of people who say this are
  wrong.
- Unless you sell to ~140 people in a remote rural town, your market is likely
  100x–1,000x bigger than you think; the average entrepreneur has tapped less
  than 1% of the actual market.
- "Saturated market" is usually ego protection and a mistranslation of "I don't
  know how to advertise / get more leads / scale."
- Failure to scale ads past ~$1,000/day is usually a skill gap in levels of
  awareness, not market saturation.
- Story: while at Gym Launch, he interviewed a lead sales rep from a gym-software
  company doing ~$10M/month purely on outbound — a company he'd never heard of,
  doing 5x his revenue at the time — which broke his belief about market size.
- Your current advertising is one tiny slice of a much larger pie: content
  across all platforms (Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, YouTube, X, radio, TV,
  Taboola, Google search, direct mail, email, Skool), plus paid ads, plus
  one-on-one outreach (DMs on every platform, phone calls, direct mail).
- On a single platform there are still many untapped formats (stories, images,
  carousels, YouTube long-form, community posts, etc.).
- Red oceans are good: "the bloodier the water, the more fish are there" — the
  fiercest competition often has the biggest rewards.
- Reconciling "riches are in the niches" with going broad: artificially constrain
  your pond early so you can be "the biggest guy in a puddle," then expand puddle
  → pond → lake → ocean as skill and experience grow.
- Niching down caps upside (usually far higher than you think) but grants pricing
  power and profit because there are fewer comparisons.
- His own trajectory: trainer → nutrition → gym stuff → business.
- Five directions to move a market from your current dot: up-market, down-market,
  adjacent, broader, narrower (gym example: franchisers up; trainers down; chiros
  adjacent; health & wellness broader; spin/dance studios narrower).
- Most small business owners never define their market and accept anyone "with a
  pulse and a credit card"; unclear targeting confuses customers.
- Path to omnipresence: more content in more places, better, consistently, for a
  long time — then add ads, then pair outreach.
- Local-business tactics to make more from one location: (1) expand the location
  (knock down walls, add seats, increase efficiency — he cut gym classes 45→30
  min to fit 1.5x more classes); (2) add channels; (3) open another location.
- His rule "one avatar, one product, one channel until $1M/year" has an exception
  for local businesses in small (C/D) markets — under ~50,000 people in a 10-mile
  radius you'll likely need more than one channel.
- A single store isn't a $100M opportunity, but duplicating and scaling a proven
  model is; $10M/year is achievable in almost any business by opening new markets.
- Cost of switching: starting a new opportunity resets you to year one while the
  old one keeps compounding; the head start comes from sticking with something,
  and growth is easier when you're bigger.
- "Features, not bugs" (credits the phrase as not his invention): every business
  has an inherent difficulty that is why you're compensated — cleaning = attract/
  retain talent; fitness = attract customers + retention.
- Business-model shapes: info/coaching starts fast but is hard to scale (little
  revenue retention — "once you learn something, you learned it"); SaaS starts
  slow, scales fast; e-com scales fast but has cash-flow/inventory/supply-chain
  strain; service is slowest but steady.
- Supply- vs demand-constrained: if you're the inverse of your industry's typical
  constraint, you're doing something either really right or really wrong; usually
  you match your industry.
- Reframe excuses into statements you control ("I don't have the skill to get more
  leads / hire a good salesperson") so you become "source" and own the outcome.
- You could be right that circumstances were against you — but "some prizes aren't
  worth winning"; the story of winning despite hardship is stronger and serves
  others who follow you.
- Story razor: at 85, split between two choices, pick the cooler story; and pick
  the hard thing, because if the easy thing were right you'd already have done it.
