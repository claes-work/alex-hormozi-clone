# Helping a Real Estate Developer Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip with a former Alan-software (white-label social-media agency) client whom Alex had told, ~4 years earlier, to quit his failing real-estate lead-gen agency, go "get good at real estate," and "find rich people, figure out their problems, and solve them." The client did: he now sells real estate to high-income W-2 earners and business owners as a **tax play** — buy real estate (Airbnbs) to leverage depreciation and offset large tax bills, closing ~90–95% of prospects, doing ~$55–60M/year (heavily stacked in Q4), doubling annually. His business has three divisions: acquisition (commissions, the real money), property setup (~$10k done-for-you, should be ~$20k), and back-end done-for-you management (15% fee). His dilemma: the management/ops side (which he "hates," was dumped on him when a property manager quit, and even spun off an accidental cleaning company) is capping him — how does he go from $60M to $100M? Alex's answer is a focus/subtraction play: **lop off the management**. The client's actual product is the acquisition/economic arbitrage (buy below market → instant ~58% return on money that would otherwise go to taxes); management is "gravy," not the core, and it's eating half his time. Sell houses without bundling management, make intros to third-party management companies, and reset expectations up front so clients don't expect turnkey. The freed time goes into scaling the real lever: his lead channel is ~4 CPA/accountant strategic partners (each firm ~$500–700k/yr in commissions, ~60–70% of revenue, ~88–90% margins). Alex: go from 4 CPAs to 40. Even dropping to a 3-of-4 close rate (a "home run" offer vs. a "grand slam" turnkey offer) and losing ~25% of business, 10x lead flow still ~7.5–8x's the business. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the client's core product not as houses or management but as an **economic arbitrage**: buying real estate below market to convert money that would be taxed into a depreciating asset, delivering roughly a 58% "instant increase on free money."
- As of March 2026, Alex's scaling move is subtraction: cut the management/ops line that the client hates and that caps him, because it is "gravy rather than the core thing" and is consuming half or more of his time.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues you don't need legal control of an ancillary service to have leverage over it; the client can hand a third-party manager "100%" (forgo his ~30% cut) precisely because he's the one who owns the client relationship and the front-end sale.
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes bundling as expectation-setting: you can still pitch strongly ("I'll get you a house below market, lock in the 58%; management I can introduce you to, but it's hit or miss and on you") without owning the management yourself.
- As of March 2026, Alex identifies the true point of leverage as the lead channel: since ~100% of *new* business comes from ~4 CPA firms (each worth ~$500–700k/yr in commissions), the entire game is going from 4 CPAs to 40 (or 400).
- As of March 2026, Alex accepts a lower close rate as the price of a simpler offer: dropping from a ~100% "grand slam" turnkey offer to a ~75% (3-of-4) "home run" offer while 10x-ing lead flow still multiplies the business ~7.5–8x.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes each CPA firm being worth $500–700k/yr means the client "can get really aggressive in attracting them" — the high per-partner value justifies heavy acquisition effort.
- As of March 2026, Alex distinguishes repeat/referral business from new business when diagnosing a channel: repeat customers "will come back," so you carve them out and ask specifically where *new* customers originate.
- As of March 2026 (recounting his earlier advice, ~2022): he told the client to stop running the agency he "sucked at," get good at the underlying skill (real estate), and "find rich people, figure out their problems, and solve them cuz they pay a whole lot better."
