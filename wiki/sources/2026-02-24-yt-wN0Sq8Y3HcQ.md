## Summary

Alex lays out how he hires senior talent by starting from the assumption that he is
*bad* at hiring (biases, knowledge gaps), which pushes him to **decentralize the hiring
decision**. Practically: build an interview panel of ~5 people — one may be you, the rest
the best operators you personally know in that function (e.g., for a salesperson, "the
best salespeople you've ever encountered," including whoever sold you your house). The
host adds that the number of references should scale with the importance of the role.

He then bridges hiring to a **"rapid learning" / consulting method**: source candidates
(and knowledge) through a **referral cascade** — ask each expert who the five best people
they know are, then ask those people the same, and keep going until certain names recur
as "god tier." He illustrates with Skool: non-technical founder Sam interviewed ~600
developers via this cascade to find Daniel, who became CTO/co-founder. Alex ties this to
his own past as a 22-year-old consultant who had to brief four-star generals on weapon
systems he knew nothing about.

For senior interviews he runs the candidate through **the real problems of the role**
("how would you solve these?"), which yields free consulting *and* a read on the person.
His evaluation rubric has **three parts**: (1) the quality and quantity of **metrics**
they track, (2) the concrete **behaviors** they'd use to move those metrics, and (3) how
those metrics and behaviors **ladder up to revenue**. Experts think in funnel components,
not binary "it worked / it didn't"; and specific, action-level answers signal real
capability, while vague answers get drilled down ("what does that actually mean?"). Ends
with a CTA for the free acquisition.com/roadmap (10-stage 0→$100M+ roadmap across 8
business functions).

## Key claims

_All dated 2026-02-24. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked (Host)._

- **Assume you're bad at hiring, then decentralize the decision.** Alex operates on a
  permanent assumption that he is bad at hiring (personal biases, things he doesn't know),
  which motivates him to distribute the hiring decision rather than trust his own judgment.
- **Build a multi-person interview panel of the best operators you know.** For hiring a
  salesperson, create a process with ~five interviewers — one may be you, the other four
  the best salespeople you've ever personally encountered (e.g., the person who sold you
  your house) — and ask them to take part in the interview.
- **(Host) References should scale with the role's importance.** The amount of reference-
  checking you do is proportional to how important the role is.
- **Referral-cascade sourcing finds elite talent.** To find a "god-tier" hire, ask one
  person for the best people they know at a skill, talk to each of those, ask *them* the
  same, and repeat — mapping the information/talent ecosystem until top names recur.
- **Skool example: ~600 developer interviews to find Daniel.** Sam, the non-technical
  founder of Skool (Skool.com, a community-building platform Alex publicly invested in
  outside acquisition.com), knew world-class talent was needed for an exceptional software
  product. He interviewed ~600 developers via the referral cascade; Daniel's name kept
  surfacing as elite, and Daniel ultimately became CTO and a co-founder of Skool.
- **Hiring senior talent mirrors the consulting "rapid learning" process.** As a 22-year-
  old consultant, Alex had to brief four-star generals on weapon systems he knew nothing
  about ~12 months after "chugging beers"; the method that let him sound intelligent maps
  directly onto interviewing high-level candidates.
- **Talk to experts first because information is abundant, not scarce.** You can Google
  anything; the problem is too much information. Experts have spent years filtering for
  what matters, so consuming their most-filtered knowledge is the highest-leverage move —
  then you reorganize it yourself.
- **The analyst distillation workflow:** raw notes (e.g., ~600 pages of interview notes on
  a space/cyber project) → recategorize by category → distill/consolidate into the core
  "truths" or most-distilled beliefs → generate inferences (what "right" looks like, what
  the problems are, potential solutions) → decide what to do next.
- **Use the interview as free consulting.** Present a senior candidate the actual problems
  of the business they'd control and ask "how would you solve these?" Asking successive
  candidates the same yields free consulting *and* reveals their thinking.
- **Metrics tracked reveal skill level; experts think in components, not binaries.**
  Beginners have binary outcome thinking ("it worked / it didn't," "ads don't work").
  Experts diagnose a failed marketing campaign by component: low click-through rate,
  unqualified traffic, weak page conversion, drop-off at a specific step of a multi-step
  funnel.
- **Specific beats vague; drill answers down to actions.** When a candidate gives a vague
  answer ("I'll coach the guys up," "I'll improve the sales team"), keep asking "what does
  that mean? / how do you do that?" until it becomes concrete ("meet the team every
  morning, run role-play, start from the script"). The more detailed the answer, the more
  Alex believes they can actually do it — if someone can't dial a plan into specific
  actions, they're unlikely to take them.
- **Three-part evaluation rubric.** Assess candidates on: (1) the quality and quantity of
  metrics they track, (2) the behaviors they'll perform to influence those metrics, and
  (3) how those metrics and behaviors ladder up to revenue in the business. A candidate who
  can explain all three clearly is "a winner." ★
- **"How do you make the company money?" is a diagnostic and a teaching tool.** Ask any
  role (even a customer-support rep) how their work makes money; if they can't explain it,
  they probably aren't doing it. A strong answer (great support → more referrals +
  retention + cross-sell into more revenue) signals someone operating above their title —
  "probably a director," not a frontline rep.
- **CTA:** Free 10-stage roadmap from $0 to $100M+ (which <1% of companies finish), broken
  down across 8 business functions with the constraint/symptoms and the steps taken to
  graduate each stage, at acquisition.com/roadmap.
