# Watch This To Become More Confident in 2024

## Summary

Alex Hormozi lays out his framework for building genuine confidence, which he defines as being "secure in who I am." He argues confidence should be earned through irrefutable proof — stacking facts about what you have actually done — rather than manufactured through charisma or "fake it till you make it." He explains how domain-specific confidence (e.g. being good at video games) can be transferred to general confidence by chunking a proven trait or skill up to a general principle and back down to a new domain. The back half develops a related theme: valuing your own opinion of yourself over the "fictitious" voices of others, using frames like his imagined 85-year-old self and "the person with the longest time horizon wins" to lower external pressure and act in accordance with the identity he wants to build.

## Key claims (his confidence framework)

- Confidence follows proof. You are rightly not confident if you lack evidence, and an accurate read of your standing is good because it shows you what to improve.
- Present facts, not bravado. "Here is the evidence" is an unrefutable position — no one can deny what has happened, including yourself.
- The hardest person to convince when starting out is yourself; give yourself irrefutable proof that you've done the work the person you want to become would do.
- He rejects "fake it till you make it" because confidence must be based in reality — a "house of identity" built on facts no one (including you) can refute.
- Everyone has already done things. You transfer confidence from specific to general by chunking up a past accomplishment to a principle, then chunking it back down to the new skill (asking girls on dates → approaching strangers under high stakes → sales).
- Repeated transfers generalize the skill set until you can say "I am good at acquiring skills quickly" because you've done it many times.
- Traits are skills. Patience, etc. are just stimulus-response patterns; repeated exposure to the same stimulus trains a new response, so any trait can be learned and proven by behavior, not by what you say.
- "Everything must be hard before it can be easy" (Chinese proverb he cites) — doing more of something makes you "suck less."
- Confidence = security in who you are. He is more secure at his current age than at 20 because he has fewer things to prove to others, having proven more to himself.
- Be your own witness (Epictetus). Seeking outside approval compromises your integrity; your opinion of yourself should have the highest standing because you have the most context — you lived it.
- Building confidence is, over time, weighing your own opinion more than other people's. Most external criticism is "people making noise with their faces" and has no real impact.
- Filter feedback: if there's truth in it, fix it; if not, learn nothing from it — because some learning is the wrong learning.
- The negative voices are usually one to three specific people replayed in your head; ask whether you want to give that fictitious version power over your life. The answer is usually no, so give the power to yourself instead.
- Frames that lower pressure: the 85-year-old / nice-old-person version of himself already knows the answers and doesn't care about most things; "the person with the longest time horizon wins," and on the longest horizon none of it matters.
