# How I Let Go of Needing Validation

## Summary
Interview clip (an off-screen host asks the opening question; all substantive content is Alex's). Asked how he got past the "chip on his shoulder" about his relationship with his father, Alex traces the arc of his ambition through his dad's approval. His goals escalated in lockstep with seeking that approval: first "make as much as my dad," then "make more than my dad," then "make more than my dad had ever made" — and he realized the approval he chased "was always going to be moved." He recounts a landmark phone call: after roughly five years of minimal contact following his departure to start the gym business, once Gym Launch was "printing money" (he says he was taking home ~$1.5M/month around age 27), his father cold-called him out of the blue, told him to sit down, and said "I'm sorry… everything." Alex says he felt **nothing** in the moment, found that curious, and — instead of accepting the olive branch — told his father he'd never thank him from an awards stage because "you weren't [there] and you didn't believe in me," to which his dad replied "we'll see how long it lasts." The realization that followed is the core of the clip: everything he'd built to that point was an attempt to **beat his father at his father's game** — a money-driven scorekeeping he'd absorbed growing up (his dad introduced people by how much they made, tying worth and name together). He frames this as a second layer of the same insight as dropping the "blame finger": he'd stopped blaming his dad, but was still **playing his dad's game and winning someone else's contest, not his own**. Letting go of validation meant taking on more responsibility — he now has to **define the game, its rules, and what matters most to him** himself. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
