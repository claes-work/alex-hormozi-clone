# "Scaling an In-Person Personal Training Business"

## Summary

Live coaching Q&A. A solo personal trainer and boxing instructor doing "a little under
$100,000" with 10 active clients asks Alex Hormozi how to structure a long-term vision,
then how to grow, and finally how to escape self-employment. Alex reframes each question:
kill the long-horizon vision talk (you can't have vision with bills to pay), make the
only goal getting from $100K to $1M in a year, and forget passive income entirely — the
trainer doesn't even have strong active income yet, so his real fight is "against
irrelevance." Because this is Alex's origin domain (he came up training gym owners and
trainers), the advice leans heavily on his fitness-business fundamentals: fitness is
really a marketing-and-sales business because "no one wants to get in shape," personal
training is "the absolute worst product ever" (explained via his value equation), and the
only two free growth levers are posting content and cold outreach. He prescribes his
Rule of 100 (four hours of *actual* work per day for 100 straight days), a "three fours"
daily structure, selling a $3K–$5K goal-based package instead of per-hour sessions, and
positioning as "the architect, not the technician." He also sketches the online-coaching
skill stack (VSL, application, landing page, phone sales) as a year-long learning bet.

## Key claims (2026-02-17, Alex's advice only, paraphrased)

- The person with the longest time horizon wins — but only if that horizon is reverse-
  engineered to some level of reality (cites Elon Musk having wanted rockets/cars/space
  since age ~7 as why he's the richest man; Alex says his own horizon is only ~3–5 years,
  "which is probably why I'm not as rich as Elon").
- You can't have vision when you have bills to pay; get your personal finances situated
  first, which buys the breathing room to think further out.
- For this trainer the only goal that matters is going from $100K to $1M/year, and that's
  doable in a year — don't worry about anything else (explicitly waves off both the 3–5
  year planning and the passive-income question as premature).
- Clearing the first level (100 → 300K → 1M) gives you more resources and skills, which
  changes how you see the board; you'll "see the game played differently."
- The best players at the game are the ones who see reality most clearly; if you've done
  something a long time and it hasn't produced results, it's usually because you see
  distortions, not reality. Going up in wealth = getting better at predicting what will
  happen.
- Entrepreneurship is one of the best sources of personal development because you "get
  slapped really quickly" to find out whether you were right or wrong.
- In fitness you are actually in the marketing-and-sales business, because "no one wants
  to get in shape" — you have to persuade and sell to get anyone to do it.
- ★ The only viral / word-of-mouth growth product in the wellness space is GLP-1 (weight-
  loss drugs) — "and it's not fitness"; it's near-impossible for personal training to
  outsell "take the shot, be down 20 pounds in five weeks, do whatever else you want."
- Personal training is "the absolute worst product ever" to sell, framed via the value
  equation: you pay today, the dream outcome takes 1–2 years (low likelihood of
  achievement), long time delay, and huge perceived effort/sacrifice (immediate physical
  suffering, giving up foods you love, one bad day ruining a week). All four value-equation
  levers point the wrong way.
- The trainer's core problem is diagnosed bluntly as a pure advertising problem: "no one
  knows you exist." The fight is against irrelevance, not a strategy or product problem.
- The only two free ways to advertise: post a lot of content, and do cold outreach. If
  your day isn't spent on one of those two things, you're wasting your time. (You can also
  reach out to the people who responded to your content.)
- ★ Cold outreach to affiliates / centers of influence gives more leverage — partner with
  other businesses whose customers overlap with yours (example: swap coupons with a
  smoothie shop, health-interested traffic both ways). "A zillion gorilla strategies"
  exist, but the two that depend on no one else are posting more and outreaching more.
- ★ "Three fours" daily structure Alex says he used in that position: first 4 hours =
  all promotion, second 4 hours = all delivery, third 4 hours = all learning (12 hours/day
  — "welcome to growing").
- Rule of 100 (from *$100M Leads*): do 100 units of primary outreach/advertising activity
  per day for 100 straight days and the business will grow; most people who truly commit
  see the business change well before day 100. The four hours must be four hours of *work*
  — set a timer, pause it when you walk away — not four hours of elapsed time.
- Growth isn't about discipline vs. laziness. He reframes "discipline": it takes him zero
  discipline to hang out with his wife (Leila) because he likes it; people who need
  discipline for that just "have a shittier life." The trainer likes boxing/lifting so he
  does them — but growing requires exercising a muscle he hasn't used: frustration
  tolerance = the ability to do things you don't want to do, without immediate reward, for
  an extended period.
- Frustration tolerance is a skill, not a trait — it can be learned by ratcheting up
  (10 outreaches today → 20 → 30 → 50 → 100).
- ★ Pricing: stop selling one-off hours (he was at $175/hr). Sell a goal-based package at
  $3K–$5K; you can down-sell to half the price for half the duration. Example structure:
  $3K for three months, "sell the goal," keep it simple.
- ★ Position as "the architect, not the technician": "I'm going to design this whole plan
  for you, you're going to adhere to it, and I'll give you $1,000 back of your $3,000 if
  you actually hit your goal" (guarantee tied to adherence + result).
- ★ Staffing-up warning: growing the in-person business to ~20 clients roughly doubles
  the money but halves your time and leaves you exhausted — he'd rather the trainer make
  a bit less, live below his means, and reinvest the freed time into learning higher-
  leverage skills.
- Online coaching is the same core sales motion as in-person, but adds a required funnel:
  outreach → they respond → they watch a VSL → fill out an application → then they talk to
  you. You have to position yourself as the prize.
- ★ Online-coaching skill stack (realistically ~a year to learn): online/phone sales,
  building a landing page, making a VSL, writing a sales script, asking for a lot of money
  over the phone and being okay with it, working leads, and making content.
- On escaping self-employment / passive income: "you don't even have active income right
  now" — it's not even a question yet. Making more active income is genuinely fun at his
  level because each income increase materially changes your life; the trainer doesn't
  like the game yet, which is why he's already asking how not to work.
- On the *$100M Leads* book: the steps are literally in the book — reread it and just do
  the steps.
