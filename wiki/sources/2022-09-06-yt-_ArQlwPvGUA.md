# A Magic Business Genie Grants You 3 Wishes...

## Summary

A solo thought-experiment in which Alex frames three ways to double a business as
"wishes" granted by a "business genie," then argues which one an owner should actually
pick. Using a model business (100 customers × $100/mo = $10,000 top line, $8,000 cost,
$2,000 profit), he compares doubling new customers, doubling retention/duration, and
doubling price — concluding that raising price is the strongest profit lever because it
drops through almost entirely to the bottom line. He closes with a step-by-step process
for raising price (Van Westendorp survey, internal-then-external communication, clearing
the pipeline, letting the math settle) applied to a hypothetical "grow your calves" program.

## Key claims

- The three "wishes" (each doubles the business a different way), in the order he ranks them:
  - **Wish A — double new customers/month:** 100 → 200 customers doubles top line to
    $20,000 and (scaling costs proportionately) doubles profit to $4,000. Doubles the
    business, but you paid to acquire twice as many customers.
  - **Wish C — double how long they stay (retention/LTV):** 3 → 6 months. Reaches the same
    equilibrium top line as Wish A but yields more bottom line, because the later months a
    customer pays are more profitable than the first (acquisition and onboarding costs are
    front-loaded), so you didn't have to spend as much on marketing. He picks C over A.
  - **Wish B — double the price:** same 100 customers, same $8,000 fulfillment cost, but
    revenue → $20,000, so profit goes from $2,000 to $12,000 — a **6x** on profit from one
    change with nothing altered about the product.
- **Pricing is the number-one lever on profit.** People split-test pages, scripts, and
  offers but forget to test price, which is the strongest of the three levers (customers,
  retention, price).
- Raising price often *increases* perceived value and buyer volume: when something becomes
  more expensive people perceive higher value, and a higher price tag can be more aligned
  with a strong personal brand.
- Even when higher price does reduce demand, it usually doesn't drop proportionally: double
  the price and you might sell a third fewer, yet still triple the profit of the business
  (6x profit per customer at two-thirds the volume).
- **Price-raising process he lays out:**
  1. Survey the existing audience with the **Van Westendorp pricing survey** — first third
     profiles the customer/avatar (why they bought, income, job, how they found you); then
     the four Van Westendorp questions (too expensive / too cheap / a stretch / a bargain);
     plus a value-stack question (which single deliverable would you keep, which could you lose).
  2. Communicate internally first (team, sales, security, marketing).
  3. Then communicate to existing customers — past/present/future framing: what we did, why
     it matters, what we're building, that we're reinvesting to fulfill promises.
  4. Then hit the lists / new incoming customers with urgency before the change.
  5. Officially set the new price.
- Expect fewer total sales at first because you "cleared the pipeline" before the change;
  it takes guts to hold — let it settle, then "let the math do the talking."
- Opening anecdote (context): "we doubled the profit of a five million dollar business in
  less than eight weeks" following a single specific process.
