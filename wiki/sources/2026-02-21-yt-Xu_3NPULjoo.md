# What Jobs Set Me Up Best for Entrepreneurship?

> ★ **L3-candidate for biography** — adds concrete early-job color (Smoothie King, Orthodox Jewish caterer, fur-coat brusher) and a vivid dated data point about quitting his white-collar job (age 22–23, ~$50k saved, took six months, "most afraid I've ever been").

## Summary

In this short clip, Alex Hormozi answers which of his earliest jobs most shape his current day-to-day — and says, realistically, none of them. He lists several early low-skill jobs he held (blending smoothies at Smoothie King, working as an Orthodox Jewish caterer, and brushing fur coats in a warehouse) and argues none taught him real business skills; where he tells stories about learning from them (e.g. the fur-coat dealer), he says he only understood what was happening *retroactively* after learning business, because he lacked the context to judge it at the time. He illustrates this with the Smoothie King daily sales tally ($2–3k/day) never registering to him as money, revenue run-rate, or margin — it was "just the number."

He then makes his central point: he rejects the idea of the "born entrepreneur." People get better at what they are reinforced for doing; so-called natural entrepreneurs are just people who got reinforced for entrepreneurial behaviors early. He frames himself as the opposite — a lifelong security-driven person who followed the "trodden path" (jobs → college → a prestigious white-collar job) because he'd been rewarded over and over for doing what he was told. That reinforcement history is exactly why quitting his white-collar job was so terrifying — it took him six months, he was 22 or 23 with ~$50k saved, and it remains the most afraid he's ever been. He contrasts himself with people who failed out of school and became entrepreneurs: they were never rewarded for following directions, so breaking away was easier for them.

The clip closes with a promo for his free acquisition.com/roadmap (10-stage 0→$100M+ roadmap). Solo Alex throughout; an off-camera interviewer only asks the opening question and interjects briefly.
