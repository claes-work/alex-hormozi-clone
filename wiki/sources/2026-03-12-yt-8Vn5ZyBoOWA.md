# Pain Fades...

## Summary
Solo motivational monologue. Alex argues that the pain of pursuing a goal is **impermanent**: once you succeed, the difficult period "compresses in your mind" and gets "a significant discount on the punishment you endured" — all that remains is the accomplishment, not the memory of the work. He builds the case through his father's biography, a story his father told him "multiple times." His Iranian father left the country on a government scholarship, was stranded abroad when the revolution hit, and tried to enter medical school in Paris without speaking French — failing two full years in a row. He switched to Belgium, passed on the third attempt (by then he understood the language), and graduated as a surgeon after ~7 years. He met an American woman (Alex's mother), moved to the US, but his MD didn't transfer — he spent ~2 more years working minimum-wage courier jobs (running radiology slides) while being a fully qualified surgeon, until he secured a residency. Net: "four or five years of his life where there was basically no net gain in progress." The lesson his father drew at 50: "No one asks me how long it took. They just know what I have now." Alex reframes failure as skill-acquisition rather than an innate deficiency ("I'm not smart enough") — sometimes you lack **prerequisite skills** (calculus without arithmetic) and must "take two steps back to build the foundation." He layers on his signature lines: **resourcefulness over resources** (his father learned general contracting to build his own surgical center from Home Depot rather than pay $250k), and radical **self-made responsibility** ("Every self-made person in poverty is self-made… if you want to feel better, blame others; if you want to get better, blame yourself"). He then applies it to his own life: after losing everything a second time (built a gym from sleeping in it → 6 locations → sold them → put the money in a new thing → gone), the only thing he had to show was the **education** — "the only thing no one can take from you." Coming from a family whose assets, land, and homes were all seized in the revolution, he sees material things (studio, lights, platforms) as ephemeral. He closes on the counterintuitive claim that **failure raises the probability of future success**, not lowers it: failure is a "requisite probability for taking action," and action → feedback → getting better → winning. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims the pain of difficulty is impermanent: after you succeed, the hard period "compresses in your mind" and gets "a significant discount on the punishment you endured" — you keep the accomplishment, not the memory of the work.
- As of March 2026, he argues failure often reflects missing **prerequisite skills**, not an innate deficiency ("I'm not smart enough") — like attempting calculus without arithmetic — so sometimes you must "take two steps back" to build a foundation.
- As of March 2026, he frames his father's ~4–5 years of "no net gain" (two failed med-school years in France, ~2 years as a minimum-wage courier despite being a surgeon) as invisible in hindsight: "No one asks me how long it took. They just know what I have now."
- As of March 2026, he restates **resourcefulness over resources**: his father learned general contracting and built his own surgical center from Home Depot rather than pay a third party ~$250k.
- As of March 2026, he asserts radical self-made responsibility: "Every self-made billionaire is self-made… every self-made person in poverty is self-made"; you may have had real disadvantages "and now what?" — blame others to feel better, blame yourself to get better.
- As of March 2026, he claims **skills/education are the only truly durable possession** — everything else (clothes, belongings, assets) can be taken, grounded in his family having all their land, homes, and wealth seized after the Iranian revolution.
- As of March 2026, he says you "can lose it all and gain it back," citing his father arriving in the US with ~$1,000 and this being a common cycle in entrepreneurship — provided you understand and respect risk.
- As of March 2026, he claims failure **increases** the likelihood of future success: failure is a "requisite probability for taking action," so anyone who has failed has taken more action → gets feedback → gets better → wins.
