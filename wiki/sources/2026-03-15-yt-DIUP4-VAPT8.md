# Helping an E-Commerce Owner Build a Brand

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A former TV producer (27-year career) who launched a handmade, all-natural skincare brand at age 50 in 2017 now does ~$1.3M/year revenue at ~$275k profit, ~70% of it from Meta and Google ads (all paid, plus email), and wants to at least double sales in the next 12 months. Her original growth came from celebrity friends doing free endorsement videos — a "mini influencer launch" — but those ads no longer perform. Alex first tells her to "erase key man from your vocabulary for now": key-man risk mainly matters when selling the asset, and at $1M nobody's buying it, so it's a distraction at her stage. He reframes doubling into three levers — **sell twice as many customers, raise average order value (AOV/cart), or raise repeat-purchase frequency** — any one doubles the business, all three roughly 8x it. Mechanically, to double ad spend profitably she needs **UGC campaigns with a wide variety of avatars/ICPs** feeding the modern (Andromeda-style) ad algorithm, which rewards "many micro niches rather than big hero ads." But his central point is strategic: **without a clear, compelling brand you're just in the arbitrage business** — buying clicks for a dollar, selling for two, chasing colder and colder audiences until margins compress and you're "doing $10M a year and making no profit," the fate of countless e-com businesses that grew as media buyers instead of brand builders. Brand delivers what e-com needs: **cheaper clicks, higher conversion rates, premium prices, and higher return/repeat rates** — one asset covering the whole business. The classic way e-com builds brand is borrowing associations from admired people (get cool people in front of it so others buy to be cool) — hence selective UGC with influencers who represent her values. He'd also attack front-end **email flows** and layer ad **hooks by awareness level** so the whole campaign's blended ROAS rises even as individual ad sets fall — a campaign approach, not hunting individual winners. He confirms she does *not* need an operations manager yet: e-com is low operational drag versus a service business, just harder on the marketing side. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.
