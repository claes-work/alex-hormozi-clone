# What Happens When You Win at the Wrong Game

## Summary

Alex Hormozi reflects on trying to beat his father at his father's money-and-status game.
He says that after reaching the goal of making more than his father had made, he realized
he had not asked what game he actually wanted to win. The clip adds a dated father-story
detail: after years of limited contact, his father apologized, and Alex rejected the olive
branch in the moment.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex says that around age 28 he realized he had been trying to prove himself to his
  father for a long time.
- He says he and his father are currently on good terms.
- After Alex left the conventional path and his father disapproved, they did not talk much
  for about 5 years.
- His father later called and apologized "for everything," which Alex says was culturally
  significant given his father's Iranian/Middle Eastern background.
- Alex says he responded harshly rather than accepting the olive branch.
- His early money goals escalated from making as much as his father to making more than
  his father had ever made.
- Once he won that game, he realized it had been his father's game rather than a game he
  had chosen.
- He says many people try to win games whose rules they did not set.
- He later reframed his father's role: if he likes who he became and his father raised
  him, his father may have been the perfect father for producing that outcome.
- Negative micro-events can become macro wins if doubled down on and integrated.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I was trying to beat him at his game."

> "I like who I am. He raised me."
