# Helping a Marketing Agency Scale

## Summary
Alex Hormozi coaches the co-owner of a custom-development/marketing agency that builds websites for enterprise clients, where roughly 90% of revenue comes from two "whale" accounts and new-client acquisition has stalled. The owner frames it as an outbound-sales problem, but Alex reframes it as a partnership/accountability problem: the co-founder responsible for sales isn't producing new business, and no one truly owns new-business development. Alex's core advice is to have a direct, unflinching conversation with the co-founder using clear division of roles and fact-based criticism rather than personal attack. The caller reveals they've already restarted a lead-generating partnership with a hosting provider.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that a stated "sales problem" is often actually an unresolved partnership/accountability problem beneath the surface.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that division of roles between co-founders is essential, and that partners must not "step over each other's territory."
- As of March 2026, Alex claims that when no single person owns new-business development, the company predictably won't generate new business.
- As of March 2026, Alex reasons that because the owner previously landed a replacement whale client quickly, they already know how to acquire clients and simply aren't doing it — so the real question is "why."
- As of March 2026, Alex frames unaddressed misalignment between partners as inevitably curdling into either direct conflict or resentment.
- As of March 2026, Alex distinguishes insult (an attack on character) from criticism (a factual observation of the gap between what was stated and what is real), and recommends framing hard partner conversations as criticism plus a plan.
- As of March 2026, Alex advises not "pulling punches" in the co-founder conversation, and taking partial ownership ("that's my fault too because I haven't been clear") while still being explicit about expectations.
- As of March 2026, Alex positions a clean division-of-labor offer — "I'll take over 100% of delivery if you go get us customers" — as a way to force clarity, with the implicit consequence that if customers don't come, one partner isn't necessary.
- Unverified caller claims: the agency serves enterprise clients (billion-dollar / ~1,000-employee companies with 1M+ page views/day, fully custom builds); two whales make up ~90% of revenue; a prior whale was lost to a merger but replaced quickly; outbound sales "dried up" over the past year; they had to buy out a third partner; a hosting-provider partnership is now bringing in some leads.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "There is a way to get new clients. I promise you."

> "No one owns new businesses, which is why you don't have new business."

> "If you need to go get business, you know exactly what to do to go get business. You just aren't doing that. Right? And so the question is why?"

> "That conflict turns into direct conflict or it turns into one of you becoming resentful."

> "So you guys know the difference between insult and criticism? Helped me a ton. So an insult is an attack on someone's character, right? You suck, you're an idiot, whatever. A criticism is: you said this, you did this. These are not the same. It's a discrepancy between stated and reality. That's all it is."

> "You are responsible for sales. We have no sales. This gap is the problem that we have right now."

> "Either you change what you do or I stop being here. Just being really real."

> "You presented with a sales problem, but I think you have a hard conversation with the co-founder to have."

> "Don't pull the punches. If you're going to have the conversation, be like, this is what I want. You're not doing that — and that's my fault too because I probably haven't been clear about it. But let me be clear now."

> "If and if you don't want to do that, fine. Let's figure out a way that we can just work on separate things, 'cause I want to keep growing."
