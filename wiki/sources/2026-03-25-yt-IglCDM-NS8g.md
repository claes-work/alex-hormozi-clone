# I Have No Idea What I Am Doing

## Summary
In a live coaching exchange, Alex Hormozi advises a caller who runs a six-month-old
tech-and-operations consulting firm selling to law firms. The caller weighs two things:
whether to "productize" his high-skill service, and whether to accept an incoming $500k
investment. Alex counters that productizing would make the business harder to sell,
that he almost certainly does not need outside capital, and that the choice between
service tiers should be driven purely by which one maximizes units sold times gross
margin (with the best LTV:CAC).

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex advises against productizing a high-skill professional
  services firm if the goal is to eventually sell it, arguing productizing "will create
  a business that's significantly harder to sell."
- As of March 2026, Alex frames high-skill professional services as supply-constrained:
  such businesses are easy to sell (in-demand) if you are good, but hard to staff
  because good people are hard to find — "this is the trade," and building such a firm
  produces a very valuable company.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues a profitable service business rarely needs outside
  capital because it earns money today on work done today, can sell work before hiring
  the person to fulfill it, and carries no negative cash-flow/inventory cycle.
- As of March 2026, Alex reasons that taking a $500k investment to fund hiring is a bad
  trade — giving up a permanent equity stakeholder "for the cost of one deal" when the
  caller already has deals worth ~$500k; better to just sell another deal and keep the money.
- As of March 2026, Alex's tier-selection rule: choose the offer that maximizes number
  of units sold times gross margin, favoring a lower-touch offer you can sell 4x+ more
  of over a high-touch offer that quickly hits a capacity constraint.
- As of March 2026, Alex prefers offers with strong revenue retention and strong gross
  margins because those can be sold aggressively and push out the capacity constraint.
- Caller claims (unverified): the firm is ~6 months old, sells to law firms, offers
  packages at 36–80k and 300–600k; the 200k offer runs a ~50% gross margin (implying
  thin net margins); fulfillment currently relies on an expensive certified outside
  contractor whose work they white-label; a $500k investment is being considered at the
  boss's request.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You have the same issue that Justice has, which is like, I have this hard thing in front of me, can you tell me there's an easy way?"

> "It is easy to sell high end professional services if you are good at what you do. It is hard to find other people who are good. This is the trade. But if you build a firm like that, you build a very valuable company."

> "Why do you need money? You don't run a capital expensive business. Like, what are you going to use the money for?"

> "Your business makes money today on work it does today. And if anything, you can sell the work before you even have the person."

> "Why would I give up a chunk of the company to have somebody who's permanently going to be involved in my business for the cost of one deal? It's like just sell another deal."

> "If you have a $100,000 thing that cost me 30 and I make 70, or something that cost $10,000 that cost 100, I'd rather just sell six more of the 10k things provided I have the ability to sell that."

> "Basically, we're just looking at number of units sold times gross margin. The one that makes the most money."

Synthesis candidate: Compact decision rule for tier/offer selection — maximize (units sold × gross margin), preferring high-retention/high-margin low-touch offers to push out the capacity constraint — plus a clean articulation of why profitable service businesses rarely need outside capital.
