# The 10 Minute MILLIONAIRE entrepreneur advice for people starting out (2022-02-21)

## Summary

In this short solo talk, Hormozi answers a recurring Instagram question — "how do I
pick my niche?" — by reframing it as a mistake beginners make: they try to teach or
sell a niche they have never actually worked in. His core argument (2022) is that
there are no shortcuts; you must accumulate real trench experience in an industry
before you can build a durable business or teach it, and you should expect roughly
five years of work before anything meaningful happens. He ties this to Y Combinator's
preference for founders with domain experience, and to his own credibility (he could
only write a book on offers because he had made a ton of offers). The overriding
theme is respecting the time horizon and doing the boring, high-volume foundational
reps instead of injecting an artificial timeline.

## Key claims

- (2022) You don't need a new or original idea; you can win by simply doing an
  existing thing better than everyone else (e.g. dry cleaning, lawn care). Novelty in
  the industry itself is overrated.
- (2022) The common mistake in niche selection: people pick a niche and sell "how to
  do the niche" without ever having done it themselves — trying to skip to a
  higher-leverage opportunity without the requisite work.
- (2022) Y Combinator looks for past experience within the space when investing,
  because most "new" businesses are slight iterations on old ones, and domain
  experience means you understand the lingo, the avatar, and the problems.
- (2022) Business is fundamentally solving problems that exist and making sure the
  math works: find where the problem is, how big a problem the customer thinks it is,
  and whether you can solve it and charge for it.
- (2022) How well you solve the problem dictates how much virality/word-of-mouth
  growth you get.
- (2022) Advice he gave a struggling agency owner in ALAN (whose clients weren't
  getting results): close the agency, get a realtor's license, generate your own
  leads and build a real estate business first — then, once you've actually done it,
  teach others. Better still, a realtor who succeeds can just grow a team of 100
  realtors rather than run an agency.
- (2022) A client's real problem is often that they aren't good at the thing yet —
  e.g. asking "how do I fix churn / scale to $100k/mo without testimonials" when the
  actual issue is they're not good, which is why there are no testimonials. They make
  the goal the number ($100k/mo) instead of making the goal being good.
- (2022) Struggling is not the same as failing. As long as you're getting better,
  you're making progress; getting better *is* the success. If you don't stop for 10
  years, you'll be really good; if you're good, you'll like it.
- (2022) About a third of the questions he gets come from too short a time horizon
  ("why am I not a millionaire? it's been 90 days"). You can't master
  entrepreneurship in 90 days when a chemistry degree takes four years.
- (2022) Learn from the bottom — not because it's the only option, but because
  understanding the entire client/industry journey gives you trench knowledge and
  depth you can later apply across skills (finance, sales, recruiting, marketing).
- (2022) He is against reading books on a skill before doing it: start selling first,
  then read sales books, because then they'll actually make sense.
- (2022) How you start a business: get a bank account, get an LLC, and figure out what
  you're going to sell. It starts with promotion and selling because you can't do the
  rest until you can do that.
- (2022) Banks won't loan to people without industry experience — example of a
  would-be gym owner rejected by 10 banks, who then worked at Bally's for 3-4 years up
  to personal-training manager, and by the time he started his gym he'd saved money,
  understood the business, and didn't even need the loan.
- (2022) Every entrepreneur he knows put in about five years before anything really
  happened. His advice for picking a niche: pick something you're not repelled by and
  that you see others making money in, then stick with it for an inordinate amount of
  time without convincing yourself you're smarter than you are.
- (Self-reported, 2022) He started his entrepreneurial path at age 23; states
  Acquisition.com does about $85 million a year.
