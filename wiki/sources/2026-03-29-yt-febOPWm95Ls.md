# How To Fix a 12 Month Ad Payback Period in SaaS

## Summary
In a short advisory exchange, a founder of a vertical SaaS for coaches (~$50/month, ~2,000 free trials and ~6,000 leads/month, 12-month ad payback) asks Alex Hormozi how to scale low-ticket software. Hormozi argues the problem is not a money/ad-spend problem but a data-and-onboarding problem: scaling low-ticket SaaS depends on virality and retention, not paid acquisition. He walks through an M12 (month-12) retention analysis to find the onboarding actions that predict long-term retention, then engineering the onboarding flow to drive those actions, and finally building in referral virality.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that scaling low-ticket SaaS is fundamentally about virality — the product must compound on its own through word of mouth, product quality, and built-in growth factors — rather than through paid ads.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames a long ad payback period as the wrong problem to solve; customers who arrive organically (word of mouth) have an immediate payback period, so the lever is increasing the organic/viral share, not reallocating ad budget.
- As of March 2026, Alex's method: run an "M12 retention" analysis — take everyone who stayed 12 months, work backward to identify the actions and demographics that raised their likelihood of reaching month 12, and rank-order those factors by correlation.
- As of March 2026, Alex illustrates (hypothetical numbers) that an onboarding action like getting a site live could 4x the likelihood of reaching M12, and adding a custom URL could 8x it — so the onboarding flow should be engineered to drive those high-correlation actions while removing friction at each step.
- As of March 2026, Alex says this is "a data problem first, and then an engineering issue" — it requires accurate in-product action tracking and a capable data engineer, not more money.
- As of March 2026, Alex describes real SaaS growth visually as: of 100 sign-ups, ~50 never leave, so the retained base compounds and growth becomes unstoppable.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues churn framed as "I didn't make money coaching" is partly a marketing problem: promising customers they'll make money invites them to blame the software when they don't; positioning the product around building a community (achievable for free) sets a success definition the product can actually deliver.
- Caller claim (unverified): the founder's product is vertical SaaS for coaches (payment, scheduling, client comms, website) at ~$50/month, with ~2,000 free trials and ~6,000 leads/month, ~$30k/month ad spend, a 12-month ad payback, and roughly half of sign-ups arriving without marketing.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "So basically, if you want to scale a low ticket software, it's all about virality. It has to be able to compound on its own based on word of mouth, based on quality of the product, and based on the growth factors that you put into the actual software and customer experience."

> "Basically, what you do is you look at something called M12 retention. So month 12, right? So you're like, okay, let's look at all of the people who have stayed for 12 months. Now, let's reverse that to the present today. What did these people do? Actions? And then what do they look like? Demographics?"

> "So it's the data problem first, and then it becomes an engineering issue for how you do onboarding."

> "I actually don't think this is a money thing."

> "100 people sign up. That's what growth looks like... These 50 people never leave. And then you just can't stop growing. That's how it actually looks for growth."

> "If you frame it as, if you use our software you're going to make money, you will get people who if they don't make money, they will think that your software failed. If you say this is the thing that helps you build communities, and you can build a community for free, then they were successful if they got 20 friends to do skateboarding in an online community."

> "There's a reason that billion dollar companies don't get out of the sky. It's hard."
