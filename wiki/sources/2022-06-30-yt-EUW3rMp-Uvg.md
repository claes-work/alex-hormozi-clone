# Biggest mistake that stopped me from getting past $30M

## Summary

Solo lesson on why mass-market consumer-product, business-opportunity, and mass-market
service businesses plateau. Alex argues that once you hit product-market fit, doing more
of the same paid acquisition is a trap: three "eventualities" (rising CPMs, colder/less-
converting audiences, higher infrastructure costs) inevitably compress margins until a
high-revenue business becomes a "massive nonprofit you're 100% liable for." The fix is to
pause around $1–5M/year and build the "invisible hand" of positive word of mouth by
raising product quality, customer experience, and referrals — which lowers acquisition
cost and eventually unlocks brand-based pricing power.

## Key claims

- **The mistake:** after reaching product-market fit, assuming that simply doing *more* of
  what got you there ("more of that thing") is how you grow. It works — until three forces
  compress margins to razor-thin. You won't know you made the mistake until it's too late.
- **Three eventualities** that hit every mass-market business as it scales: (1) CPMs / cost
  per impression rise over time; (2) you must reach colder and colder audiences, so
  conversion rate falls; (3) infrastructure / fixed costs rise. All three compress margins
  even while revenue grows.
- **The lesson / fix:** you must *pause* in the ~$1–5M/year range and do something entirely
  different — stop doing the thing that got you there — to build an acquisition channel that
  doesn't behave in those three ways.
- That channel is **warm word of mouth**, the "invisible hand," which is *quadratic* (one
  tells two, two tell four) and drives acquisition cost *down* at scale.
- **Negative word of mouth** is diagnosable: it exists when your cost of acquisition rises
  *faster* than cost per impression (e.g. CPM up 10% but CAC up 40%). Negative experiences
  are told to ~10x more people than positive ones.
- Build word of mouth by increasing product integrity/quality, a customer success function
  tracking time-to-value, NPS, and customer health scores — because referrals are "the one
  thing that matters most."
- **Set modest expectations and over-deliver.** Short-term sellers raise expectations to
  close the sale, then can't live up to them, creating a negative expectation-to-delivery
  gap that drives negative word of mouth. Widen the gap between expectation and delivery in
  the customer's favor (movie-hype analogy).
- **A brand is "what people say about your product behind your back"** — a reputation. A
  brand earns pricing power: for mass-market B2C, a 10–20% price bump with no demand change
  can double/triple/quadruple profit. Without a brand you're just "media arbitraging."
- "Go slow to go fast" — think 10 years, not 10 days.
