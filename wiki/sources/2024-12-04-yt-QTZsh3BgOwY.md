# You're WASTING Your Chance to Make INSANE Money

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video laying out the "six Horsemen" — the six recurring problems he
says plague most businesses he sees at Acquisition.com (a portfolio he states does over
$250M/year). The six: serving too many avatars, underpricing, overcompensating staff,
overextension (the "Woman in the Red Dress"), scaling via arbitrary growth/multiple
businesses without a "who," and having no data. He gives tactical fixes for each — most
notably a step-by-step price-raise script for new vs. existing customers, and a
"measurement as intervention" argument for tracking. The back half is a real
portfolio-company case study where fixing the back end (team, data, retention,
ascensions) took profit from 5% to 30% and added ~$2M in revenue in roughly 90 days.

## Key claims

**Problem 1 — Serve too many avatars** (paraphrase)
- Saying yes to anyone with money forces marketing, sales, and delivery to serve too
  many expectations; "you have to be everything to everyone which means you're nothing
  to no one." Most common under ~$3M/year revenue.
- Fix: narrow the front end (messaging, lead magnets, call-outs). Narrower messaging
  gets a higher response rate from that segment; LTV:CAC can actually improve. Pick the
  avatar with the best LTV:CAC vs. market size; cut or bridge from the others.
- If he invested in a marketing agency, he'd only buy a niche one (e.g. only painters,
  only plumbers), never a generalist "we do ads" shop.

**Problem 2 — Underpriced** (paraphrase)
- Most people price by taking the market average and offering a bit more value for a
  bit less money; "everyone's broke" — the average business owner makes very little for
  a lot of risk.
- Fix new customers first: just raise the price. You get more nos but can make more
  money — doubling price while close rate falls from 30% to 20% is a great trade.
- Then existing customers via an email sequence: (1) a "brace yourself" teaser email;
  (2) remind them of value already delivered; (3) state the raise; (4) frame it as
  reinvesting to keep promises (WIIFM), not greed; (5) soften with a discount equal to
  their current price for 3–6 months, then the new price; (6) a PS inviting anyone
  genuinely hurt to reach out. People accept a disappearing discount more easily than a
  price going up, and it pushes the pain into the future.
- Onetime-purchase businesses: just raise it, no ceremony. His *Gym Launch Secrets* book
  contains a recurring-customer price-raise letter; he's walked hundreds of gyms through it.

**Problem 3 — Overcompensation** (paraphrase)
- Paying labor too much, typically via rev-share/profit-share on top line with
  high-skilled labor who don't own overhead, acquisition, or retention. Hardest lever;
  he'd rather fix the business model (price, upsells) first.
- Fix mirrors the price raise but as a decrease, done on new hires first: post the job at
  the new comp, prove you can attract equal talent, then use that as precedent with
  existing staff. Give long-tenured people 3–6 months' notice; define what it would take
  for them to keep their current pay by doing more.
- "Your biggest expenses walk on two feet" (a saying he cites). Example: a $1M business
  at 25% margin makes $250K; a $100K hire eats 40% of profit and must generate ~$400K in
  revenue just to break even. He calls $1–3M revenue "the swamp."

**Problem 4 — Overextension / "The Woman in the Red Dress"** (paraphrase)
- His most-used analogy (from *The Matrix*): shiny new opportunities distract you from
  the core business and are "sent to destroy you." Says it's the problem he personally
  struggles with most, 13–14 years in.
- "You will grow bigger by getting better. You will not grow bigger by choosing to get
  bigger" — choosing bigness makes you bloated.
- Value framework: once you have a claim people want, everything else is removing
  friction — delay, effort, sacrifice, risk, and inconsistency. "The iPhone is what's
  left after you remove everything that sucks from a phone." Easier to spot problems than
  to define value; remove all problems and what's left is value.

**Problem 5 — Overextension via arbitrary growth / multiple locations** (paraphrase)
- Entrepreneurs chase arbitrary next-notch revenue goals and add locations/product
  lines before ready; a second location often drops the first's profit, and by three
  you can be doing 3x the work for zero profit.
- Litmus test: the business must run and maintain/grow for six months without you.
  Removing yourself from delivery/sales doesn't mean the business runs without you — you've
  moved to a higher-leverage job. Vacation-for-2–4-weeks-phone-off is level-one proof.
- Fix is a "who": someone to own the new thing or take over the current thing.
  Overextension happens when you lack a good enough who and do it anyway — out of greed or
  envy. Fix your core economics first rather than bolting on new revenue lines.

**Problem 6 — No data** (paraphrase)
- Constant confusion = no tracking. "If you don't track you don't care." Guessing assumes
  randomness beats thought.
- "Measurement as intervention": weighing yourself daily causes weight loss; tracking
  profit/close rate causes them to improve. Most business growth is common sense, but you
  can't act on common sense without data.
- Track two funnels: click-to-close (CTR, traffic, opt-in, schedule, show, offer, close,
  cash collected) and close-to-resell (activation, time-to-value, churn, gross profit per
  product line, ascension rates by cohort). Do the resell/back-end one first if forced to
  choose.

**Portfolio case study (back-end data only; front end covered in a separate video)**
- Starting state: 5% profit, ~2% revenue retention, 0–1 ascensions/month; problems were
  team churn, no data, no revenue retention, no ascensions.
- Team fix: the department was *underpaid* (opposite of Problem 3, and easier to fix),
  with a "cancerous" CS leader turning the team against the founder. Hired an experienced
  CS leader, fired ~90% of the baseline team, hired aggressively with higher comp + PTO +
  benefits (a "grand slam offer" for hires), using the "green dye in diluted water"
  method so good hires displaced bad ones without disrupting the business.
- CS/ascension fix: install notes so customers don't repeat themselves; drive an
  activation point / time-to-value; onboarding restates goals and ties them to actions.
  Deletion over addition — the #1 cause of churn is overwhelm. Ran a "if I got rid of
  everything except one thing, which would you keep?" survey; deleted a 35-person tech-
  support team at Gym Launch with zero effect on churn or sales (parallels a Carl Icahn
  story about firing an 11-floor division with no consequence).
- Metrics tracked: NPS (−100 to +100; Apple ~61, Chick-fil-A ~58, average company ~−2),
  time-to-value, activation, churn, ascension rates (qualified vs. total).
- Ascension timing: sell at open "loops" — immediate/24h, time-to-value, halfway,
  first/big win — not at the "last chance." CSMs paid for sets, closers for the sale.
- Results: profit 5%→30%, revenue retention 2%→30%, ascensions 0–1→5–6/month, ~$2M added
  revenue, in ~90 days. He builds businesses "back to front" — make the back end so
  profitable he can outspend everyone on acquisition.
