# Why you should quit college. (2022-08-27)

## Summary

Alex uses the true story of Jacob — his 18-year-old Austin next-door neighbor whom he walked around the block for an hour and talked out of college — to argue that the college decision must be treated as a big, irreversible choice weighed by *opportunity cost*, not social expectation. He lays out a decision-making framework (what problem are you solving? which path maximizes future income potential?), a re-imagined skills-based education mapped to business departments, and closes with an on-camera interview of Jacob, now 20, earning ~250k/year in sales with ~210k saved.

## Key claims

- Big life decisions should be evaluated on two axes — reversibility and long-term impact — and college (4 years, 50–200k cost plus lost income) is a big, irreversible one most people make only because it's "the next natural step." (paraphrase)
- The decisive factor is **opportunity cost** — the loss of potential gain from the alternative not chosen — which he calls "the single greatest cost that people do not take into consideration." (paraphrase)
- If you want to make money you need income-generating skills; general-education classes (ancient Chinese literature, Aztec culture) don't add value, and big tech (e.g., Google) has dropped degree requirements. Real majors should map to business departments: marketing, sales, delivery (product/service), and support functions (finance, HR, legal). (paraphrase)
- The "social experience" argument fails on efficiency: you can work all day and party every night, ending up debt-free with savings and the same social life. (paraphrase)
- The best people at anything are self-taught; you don't need certification or permission to learn — "the world is your oyster." (paraphrase)
- For big decisions, chew on it until you can compress it into a single question — for Jacob: "four years from now given the cost, which of these two paths gives me the most potential for my future?" (paraphrase)
