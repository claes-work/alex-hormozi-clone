# $100M Hooks Playbook (2025)

**What it is.** A free Acquisition.com playbook by Alex Hormozi, titled on the cover "$100M Playbook: Hooks" with the subtitle "How to Double Your Marketing in Under 5 Seconds" (`PDF p. 1`). Roughly 26 PDF pages of instruction plus Hormozi's "121 Best Hooks" swipe file across ads, content, email and Twitter, ending with a hook checklist (`PDF p. 3`, `PDF p. 22`, `PDF p. 26`). Raw text: `raw/books/2025-100m-hooks-playbook.md`.

## Thesis
The hook — whatever a person sees or hears first — is the single highest-leverage element of any ad, post, or outreach, because if nobody notices, nobody buys (`PDF p. 6`). Mastering hooks alone can 5x–100x the attention a business gets for the same effort, so Hormozi argues you should spend the majority of your marketing time on the first few seconds (`PDF p. 6`).

## Structure and content
- **Opening story (`PDF p. 4`–`p. 5`)**: Dean Graziosi's Larry King infomercial, transcribed in Dean's own words — changing only the opening sentence turned a bombing ad into one that "sold millions of books."
- **What's a hook / why it matters / how it works (`PDF p. 6`–`p. 7`)**: definition, the value case, and the two-part anatomy (call out + condition for value); the "cocktail party effect."
- **Types of hooks: verbal and nonverbal (`PDF p. 7`–`p. 8`)**: eight verbal categories with examples, plus visual/sound hooks.
- **Process for finding and creating hooks (`PDF p. 8`–`p. 11`)**: Google's 70-20-10 innovation rule applied to hooks, with a swipe-file spreadsheet and outlier-tracking workflow.
- **Testing the hooks (`PDF p. 11`–`p. 12`)**: record-count rules per format (ads, shorts, long content, email) and the "let the data do the teaching" author note.
- **The 121 Best Hooks swipe file (`PDF p. 13`–`p. 22`)**: Ads (1–22), Content/Alex YT (23–43), Leila top 7 YT (44–50), IG/Alex (51–83), Leila IG top ten (84–92), Email book launch + scaling workshop (93–110), Bonus ten Twitter outliers (111–121).
- **Why to improve hooks + CTA (`PDF p. 23`–`p. 24`)**: benefits/costs lists and a book-a-call CTA to acquisition.com/scale.
- **Hook checklist (`PDF p. 25`–`p. 26`)**: first-time-through vs. every-time-after steps, plus a chiropractor example rendered in every hook format.

## Key claims
- Hooks have two parts: a **call out** (gets the prospect to think "This is for me") and a **condition for value** (the promise); the call out can be verbal or nonverbal, the promise explicit or implicit (`PDF p. 6`–`p. 7`, 2025).
- A person consumes content only if they judge the cost of consuming to be less than the benefit they expect — and the hook sets up those conditions (`PDF p. 7`, 2025).
- Good hooks should be **specific enough to get the right people and broad enough to get as many of them as possible** (`PDF p. 6`, 2025).
- Mastering hooks can 5x, 10x, or 100x the people who pay attention — "you could 5x, 10x, or 100x your business" changing nothing else (`PDF p. 6`, 2025).
- Of ten hours available to advertise, spend the first eight on hooks (`PDF p. 6`, 2025).
- Eight verbal hook categories: **Labels, Questions (yes / open), Conditionals, Commands, Statements, Lists or Steps, Narratives, Exclamations / Provocative Statements** (`PDF p. 7`–`p. 8`, 2025).
- Hook creation follows **Google's 70-20-10 rule**: 70% proven/core hooks, 20% "winner-adjacent" concepts modeled from other niches, 10% brand-new risky experiments (`PDF p. 8`–`p. 11`, 2025).
- **The hook dictates the content, not the other way around** — build a large hook dataset first and you never run out of content ideas (`PDF p. 11`, 2025).
- Testing cadence by format: **record ~10 hooks per 1 ad**; reuse every winning tweet as a short (then expand the hook); record 3–4 hooks for long content for the team to pick in post; run active split tests on every email campaign and document winners (`PDF p. 11`–`p. 12`, 2025).
- "Let the data do the teaching": no matter how bad your content, some will be top-10% — make more like the top, less like the bottom, repeat forever (`PDF p. 12`, 2025).
- The swipe file contains Hormozi's **top 121 hooks over the past year**, sorted by views (content) and performance (ads/emails), bucketed into categories and charted (`PDF p. 13`, 2025).
- Nailing hooks has "2x-5x-10x'd many businesses overnight," while misunderstanding them has done the opposite overnight (`PDF p. 24`, 2025).
- Checklist protocol: first time, isolate hooks from top performers and write down your favorite 50; every time after, take the top 5 (top 10%) and make 70% of the next batch from them, 20% permutations, 10% completely different (`PDF p. 25`–`p. 26`, 2025).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> A hook is whatever gets the attention of your audience. (`PDF p. 6`)

> If nobody notices your ad, then nobody buys your stuff. Nothing else matters. Your entire business hinges on these first few seconds. (`PDF p. 6`)

> The longer I have been advertising, the less I care about anything else. If you have ten hours to advertise, focus your first eight hours on the hooks. (`PDF p. 6`)

> And since the hook dictates the content, and not the other way around, you no longer need to worry about what content to make. (`PDF p. 11`)

> My promise is, no matter how bad your content is, some of it will be in the top 10% of that content. Look at the hooks for that content. Make more like that. Look at the bottom 10%. Make less like that. Repeat until the end of time. (`PDF p. 12`)

> These playbooks aren't museum pieces. They're meant to be used. (`PDF p. 13`)

> The first impression is the strongest impression with every human, let alone, every customer. It's either a tailwind or headwind for your goals. The hook is that first impression. (`PDF p. 23`)

> I try to make hooks specific enough to get the right people and broad enough to get as many of them as I can. (`PDF p. 6`)
