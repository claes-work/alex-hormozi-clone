# How to Build a Lead Magnet That Converts

## Summary

Alex gives a B2B technology PR firm several lead-magnet concepts, including a podcast
invitation, a free press release, a results guarantee, and fundraising support. His
central test is whether the giveaway is a service prospects would otherwise pay for,
then he recommends split-testing multiple concepts across the firm's outbound volume.

## Key claims

- The owner self-reports about $2.1 million in revenue, 100,000 cold emails per month,
  and a follow-up gap of roughly six months; these figures are unverified.
- As of April 2026, Alex proposes inviting target CEOs onto a podcast because the hour
  of conversation creates access and relationship value before a sales discussion.
- A free press release can work when it is presented as a normally paid deliverable,
  rather than as a thin informational giveaway.
- He proposes a deadline-bound placement guarantee with free service as the remedy if
  the firm misses the promised result; its feasibility is not established in the source.
- He defines a strong lead magnet as something competitors charge for and prospects
  would knowingly pay for, offered free to initiate the relationship.
- For fundraising clients, he suggests building a venture-capital network and offering
  pitch-deck help or introductions as adjacent value.
- The existing email volume is sufficient to split-test five concepts rather than
  debating the perfect lead magnet in advance.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "A good lead magnet is something that people would normally charge for."

> "You should split test five of these."

> "Anything is better than nothing."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: paid-value test for lead magnets; podcast access as B2B acquisition; use outbound volume to test several offers concurrently.
