# I Have a Construction Company and I'm a Firefighter. Should I Go All In?

## Summary
Live Q&A clip. The client is a 18-year professional firefighter (a high-seniority captain, ~$168k/year, working roughly 10 days a month on 48-hour shifts at an affluent, low-call-volume department) who also runs a design-build ADU / general-contracting company in San Diego with his 68-year-old father. The company does ~$5M/year (targeting $10M), nets ~$1.2M split across an ~80%-margin design/planning side and a ~30%-margin construction side; the client is currently 70/30 with his dad but could move to 50/50 and effectively runs everything. He asks for **objective criteria** to decide when to leave the fire department. Alex reframes it as a "what do I do with my life" question, not a money question — the client already admits money is a non-issue. Alex applies a **replace-income downside test**: the break-even to justify quitting is making up the ~$168k salary (×2, since profit gets split with dad) — roughly $340k of extra profit, which at ~25% margins means ~$1.2–1.5M of additional top-line; Alex asks "can you do that?" and the client says yes. Alex also introduces a **double-negative productivity test**: if you become *less* productive by leaving the job, quitting is a double negative — but here the client is on "study hall" at the firehouse and admits cutting the parachute would force him to make it happen. Alex diagnoses the real issue as fear dressed up as rationalization ("you'd like to quit, you're comfortable, you think you can grow this, and you're afraid to do it"), plus a deeper meaning question — Alex's view: "a man must have a quest," work is inextricable from a good life ("before God gave man a wife, he gave him a job"), so leaving one job just means the client will build or seek another. Also surfaces the client's **key-man risk** (he circled every key-man risk item; the business is supply-constrained; his dad is a paper-and-pen holdout; they've never marketed or paid for a lead) and the point that 10 more full days a month would likely let him solve those constraints. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex reframes the "when should I quit my job" question as fundamentally a "what do I do with my life" question, not a numbers question — especially once the person admits money isn't the real constraint.
- As of March 2026, Alex's replace-income downside test: quitting is justified once the other venture can reliably make up the salary being given up; here that meant covering ~$168k, doubled to ~$340k because profit is split with the partner (dad), which at ~25% margins implies ~$1.2–1.5M of additional top-line as the break-even, then asking "can you do that?"
- As of March 2026, Alex's double-negative test: if you become *less* productive as a result of leaving the job, that is a "double negative" and argues against quitting — but if the job is low-productivity "study hall" and cutting the parachute would force focus, that objection dissolves.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that "a man must have a quest" and that work is inextricable from how we live, citing that "before God gave man a wife, he gave him a job" — so leaving one job doesn't end the meaning problem; you'll build or seek another.
- As of March 2026, Alex diagnoses the client's stated objections as rationalized fear: comfortable, capable of growing the business, wants to quit, afraid to — and the other "reasons" are cover for that fear.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes that reclaiming ~10 full days a month would likely let the owner solve existing constraints (key-man risk, supply constraints) that the business currently can't fix.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the whole exchange as "like a therapy session" — the client already knew the answer and mostly needed permission and a downside check.
