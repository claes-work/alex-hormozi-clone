# Helping an E-Com Company Doing $1M/Year Solve Their Supply Chain

## Summary
Live coaching clip. An e-commerce founder sells beef-protein chips to athletes and outdoor enthusiasts, doing ~$1M/year in revenue and wanting to reach $12M/year (~$1M/month). Their bottleneck has been supply: 6–9 months in "supply-chain purgatory" finding a manufacturer willing to make their decommoditized (non-jerky) product without a price race to the bottom. They finally landed a new manufacturer who can produce ~20,000 units/month (~$280k/month in revenue) using a proprietary machine that spits out a tray every 12 seconds — but the machine is too large for the manufacturer's facility, and the machine reportedly costs ~$400,000. Alex's guidance splits into two threads. **Cash flow / working capital**: since inventory-heavy e-com is cash-constrained, he leads with **renegotiating supplier payment terms** (net 30 / net 60 / net 90) to pull cash forward — citing a company he looked at investing in that went net-zero to net-90 and grew from $2M/year to $5M/month in 18 months. He stacks additional cash-forward levers: run a **sold-out / scarcity play** to pent up demand, take **pre-orders**, and meet the existing volume while ramping into the manufacturer's larger caps — the "double" sitting in front of them. Because the founder says it won't take many months of that doubled volume to save up for the machine (with terms and lending), Alex frames the plan as already self-spelled-out. **Manufacturing as defensibility**: for a brand aspiring to be big, owning the manufacturing of something novel is worth it. He is skeptical of the $400k machine price — a machine builder prices off value, not cost (his friend's machine business did ~$70M/year selling machines that cost ~$17k to build for ~$400k each) — and urges the founder to bring in trusted "machine guys" (posed as a marketing partner) to inspect it and learn exactly how proprietary it really is, since the machine is the core defensibility of the business. On location: founder is San Diego, manufacturer in LA, 3PL in Georgia; Alex dislikes California for a facility and notes the founder's native Montana would be a "much more appetizing" location, ideally something central for easier shipping. Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's first move for a cash-constrained inventory business is to renegotiate supplier payment terms — push from net-zero toward net 30 / 60 / 90 — because extended terms pull cash forward and fund growth.
- As of March 2026, he cites a company that hard-negotiated from net-zero to net-90 and then grew from ~$2M/year to ~$5M/month in 18 months (explicitly flagged as "not a promise or guarantee," just illustrative).
- As of March 2026, additional cash-forward levers for an inventory-constrained e-com brand: run a "sold-out" scarcity play to build pent-up demand, and take pre-orders — both pull cash forward without new capacity.
- As of March 2026, the growth path he endorses: meet existing volume, ramp into the new manufacturer's larger caps (the "double"), and use the extra months of doubled volume plus terms and lending to fund the machine purchase.
- As of March 2026, for a brand that wants to become big, owning the manufacturing of something novel/differentiated is "probably a good idea."
- As of March 2026, a proprietary machine that is the only thing differentiating you is a "huge point of leverage" and the "core defensibility" of the business — so learning everything about how it works is critical before scaling to more machines.
- As of March 2026, he is skeptical of the quoted $400,000 machine price: a smart machine builder prices off *value* (e.g., replacing a $2M workforce for $400k is a steal even if it costs $20k to build), so the buyer should inspect it to find the real cost delta.
- As of March 2026, he cites a friend's machine business doing ~$70M/year, selling machines at an average ~$400k that cost ~$17k to build ("printed, destroyed").
- As of March 2026, tactical due diligence: bring in "really good machine guys," introduced as a marketing partner, to inspect the supposedly proprietary machine and gauge how proprietary it actually is.
- As of March 2026, on location for a manufacturing facility: he "hate[s] California stuff," prefers central for easier nationwide shipping, and considers the founder's native Montana "much more appetizing" than California.
