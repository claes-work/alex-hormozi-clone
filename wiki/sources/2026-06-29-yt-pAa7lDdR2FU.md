# Why a $200M Builder Can't Hit $300M

## Summary

In this short coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a new-construction homebuilder doing about $200M who is constrained by finished lots. The builder can find raw land but does not want cash tied up for years while land is developed. Alex says the company may be large enough to vertically integrate land development by acquiring a developer, buying the team, or stripping key talent out of the developer market. He defines the quantified constraint as the resource allocation with the highest return.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Homebuilder** - the coachee. Her revenue, market, land sourcing, and development timeline are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If finished lots are the bottleneck and the business builds every lot it can acquire, then land development is the true constraint.
- At $200M scale, the company can consider buy-or-build vertical integration rather than merely improving land outreach.
- The first path is to identify all developers the company has bought from over the past decade and explore deals with older/tired owners.
- Another path is to recruit the key talent from developers rather than buying whole companies, if the relationships are transportable.
- Alex compares this to AI companies paying huge signing bonuses for key people because acquiring talent can be cheaper than acquiring whole companies.
- If relationships and permits are deeply entrenched, buying the whole development company may be better.
- Older owners may accept long seller-financing / earnout structures that feel like annuities.
- Large businesses often become businesses of businesses, like Amazon's AWS, marketplace, logistics, and media arms.
- A quantified constraint is the allocation of resources that yields the highest return; solving a true constraint can create order-of-magnitude improvement.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Your size you can just do the acquisition."

> "It actually is more efficient to give one person or the four key people in the business $100 million than buy the business for $4 billion."

> "Constraints often times are not like 10, 20, 30% improvement. They can be like order of magnitude improvements."
