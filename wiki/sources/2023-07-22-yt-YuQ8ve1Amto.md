# Networking Will Make You Millions... If You Do it Right

## Summary
In this multi-speaker clip, Alex Hormozi lays out the "give first" networking strategy he used early in his career to build goodwill and access. When his only skills were writing sales scripts and building sales processes, he reached out to every member of his networking groups (e.g. a gym-owner group), treated each person as if they were a paying client, and over-delivered free work reviewing their scripts and processes. Each act of giving, he argues, banks a one-sided "IOU" of goodwill that compounds and can be cashed in later (e.g. learning Facebook ads from someone he had previously helped). His mechanic for networking upward: never ask a busy person "what value can I provide you" — instead do the homework yourself, figure out what you can give them, then do it and hand it over unasked. He closes on real "players" recognizing one another by hunger and work ethic rather than rented status symbols.

## Key claims
- The way to become valuable in a network is to give first: pick your niche skill and over-deliver it to people for free, treating it like a real paid project.
- Early on, the only things he knew how to do were write sales scripts and build sales processes, so he offered to review every member's scripts and processes and did it as if it were a paid engagement — showing up with pages of notes.
- Each act of giving banks a one-sided "IOU" of goodwill that is "incredibly valuable" and "can come in handy later down the road"; he cashed his in when he needed a deeper understanding of Facebook ads than a weekend workshop gave him.
- Not everyone reciprocates, and that is fine: if they don't, you've simply helped someone (which is what most entrepreneurs claim they want to do) and they'll have nothing bad to say — you stay "net positive on everything."
- Giving first without return is itself the personal brand most entrepreneurs would want to build anyway.
- Anecdote: after doing this in a networking group, the group "invented an award" for him — member of the year — because they wanted everyone to do what he did (take every call, do the homework, over-deliver).
- The big point on calls: you don't show up and only then start thinking about the other person's problem — you do the homework before the call.
- Preparation is a large, underrated leverage point: roughly 20 minutes of prep before a call makes you seem dramatically more intelligent (framed in the clip as a restatement of a recent tweet of his).
- When networking with people ahead of you, never ask "what value can I provide you" — that offloads onto them the work of figuring out how to use you. Instead do the hard work of figuring out what you can provide, then do it and give it to them.
- Over-delivering earns access and feedback: "anyone will help someone who takes initiative" / "starts helping themselves"; even if your work isn't good, you get valuable feedback, and most people will give you their time to tell you what you could do better.
- Example: an editor who went through his old YouTube clips, cut 20 short reels unasked, and sent them in — which is how that editor got hired.
- Real "players" recognize one another and are unimpressed by rented status symbols (rented Lambos, blue check marks, Rolexes) — those only impress people who don't know what players actually look like.
- Young winners are hungry "savages" in the grind who give first to as many people as they can, over-delivering because they know the goodwill will compound.
- This single skill — giving first / over-delivering — is what let him get into rooms he felt he "didn't deserve to be in."

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "You basically plug into everyone else's network and skills by giving first. Now some of them aren't going to return the favor, but you're net positive on everything."

- "If you're a carpenter then build the most amazing table of all time and send it to somebody, and you know what they're going to do — they're going to get it and be like, 'this is too much, I can't believe somebody did this.'"
- "Anyone will help someone who starts helping themselves. Anyone will help someone who takes initiative."
- "That single skill has served me so well because that's how I've been able to get into rooms that I didn't deserve to be in — was by just saying, okay, what am I good at that I can help this guy out with."
