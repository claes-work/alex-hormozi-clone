# If I Were Single and Starting Over, This Is What I Would Do

## Summary

Despite the "starting over" framing, this is Hormozi's playbook for **partner
selection**, not business — he argues the person you marry has the largest single
impact on who you become, what you achieve, and how happy you are, and that most
people put less thought into it than buying a car. He reasons about mate selection
using his business/investing mental models (sales pipelines, the "big domino,"
asset-vs-liability, arbitrage, business-partner fit) and lands on a core thesis: hold
yourself to a higher standard than you hold a partner, be brutally honest about
tradeoffs up-front, and pick someone who wants you to be *more of who you are* (growth)
rather than *someone different* (change). Recurring anchor examples are his own
relationship with Leila and advice from his father.

## Key claims (start-from-zero partner playbook)

- **Weight the decision correctly.** Cites a college economics graph claiming a ~0.71
  correlation between subjective well-being and the strength of your relationship with
  your significant other; over a lifetime, time spent alone and with a spouse rises
  while time with friends/family/kids falls to near-nothing. So the spouse is the human
  you'll spend the most time with by a wide margin. *(Statistic is self-reported from
  memory of a class — not independently verified.)*
- **Have obscenely high standards — but only if you hold yourself higher.** "High
  standards second only to the standards you hold yourself to." He mocks two failure
  camps: (1) "I'll take anyone with a pulse" → scraps of society; (2) "I'm a mess but
  they should see the amazing me deep down." Camp 3 = chasing dating hacks/posturing,
  which are mostly deception and get exposed over time.
- **Solve the "big domino."** Instead of 100 tiny profile hacks to land someone out of
  your league, make yourself so worthy you can attract anyone — the same one-big-thing
  approach he uses in business and fitness. (Concede: spend ~5 min on good profile
  photos since it's an ad; the real work is being someone worth being with.)
- **Date outside your "type."** His dad told him you'll date plenty of your type but
  probably won't marry your type — if you did, you'd have a wife, not a type. Dating a
  more diverse range than he befriended taught him traits he didn't know mattered.
- **Judge people — that's a skill.** Assessing someone accurately is necessary and
  human; the error is measuring your worth against them. Pattern recognition only comes
  from exposure: lots of bad first/second dates over an extended period ("go on 100
  dates").
- **Know your own metrics and priorities.** Identify the traits that actually matter to
  you (he learned he needed a woman who respected him, followed traditional gender
  roles, and did not see his ambition as a "take"). Make sure your partner judges you
  on the same metrics you judge yourself on — otherwise you can never win.
- **Find someone who already loves what you do** rather than changing yourself to fit
  them — far less work, and prevents resentment. Someone else out there genuinely loves
  the thing you're being asked to change into.
- **Advertise your negatives (dating arbitrage).** Borrowing Rory Sutherland's realtor
  point: state your downsides; the strong match is the person for whom your negatives
  are positives or neutral. "You want to get a steal on a partner."
- **State priorities explicitly.** You can't call everything a priority — same as
  business strategy. If a partner sacrifices your #3 to keep doing your #1, you can't be
  upset; that was the tree you chose. (Leila's #1 attraction was his ambition — the
  thing that won't change; he admits he forgot her birthday the first year, and his own.)
- **Own the tradeoffs; don't complain about the price after you chose the Nikes.** State
  "the cost of this is this — are you okay with it?" up-front. A partner unwilling to pay
  the cost isn't supporting your long-term goals; you'll end up resenting them.
- **Novelty→loyalty, exploration→trust, the chase→a journey together.** The three trades
  of committing. Loyalty defined as *predictability of behavior* (acting in accordance
  with stated intentions), which he values arguably above love. If you prefer novelty,
  exploration, and the chase, you shouldn't marry — no judgment either way.
- **Be ethically honest / cut it off early.** The moment he decided he couldn't marry
  someone, he stopped seeing them — saved both parties years. Condemns men who dangle
  vague "maybe later" while the woman waits; state the facts and tell the truth.
- **Growth over change; asset over liability.** A spouse marries who you are *and* who
  you want to become — make sure they love both. Wanting you to *grow* = more of who you
  already are; wanting you to *change* = someone different (treating a partner as a
  "project" is a terrible way to live). "Liabilities decline in value over time, assets
  increase — choose the latter."
- **Measure of a successful relationship = the Delta.** How much more valuable the sum
  is than the individual parts; a good partnership makes both people better together
  than apart. Most relationships, one partner is a net weight and the other would be
  better off alone.
- **Divorce stats are misleading.** The "half of marriages end in divorce" figure is
  skewed by remarriages; he claims first marriages run ~30% divorce, second ~70%, third
  ~80%. *(Self-reported figures, not verified here.)*
- **Alignment on goals is the whole game.** If you and a partner want different things,
  it's a perfectly fine reason to break up *before* marriage — but finding out after is
  the problem. Not knowing what you want with your life is "a you problem" to solve
  first. Uses Gary Vaynerchuk's analogy: a good spouse cheers from the sidelines and
  never asks the quarterback to leave the game when it's on the line.
- **Better people attract better people.** Improving marriages come from becoming better
  people, which requires honestly facing where you're deficient and starting with the
  most important gap. Formula: big goals + big work = ambitious; big goals + lazy =
  entitled — most people feel entitled to an amazing spouse without being ambitious about
  earning one.
