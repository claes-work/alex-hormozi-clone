# "My Employees Don't Take My Business Serious...Why?"

## Summary

Hormozi inverts a management problem for comedic effect: he lays out the three
best ways to *guarantee* employees never do what you want, then reverses each into
a positive management prescription. The framework — he calls it the "acquisition
triangle" — is communication/expectation-setting, training, and motivation; whenever
someone isn't doing a task, the cause is one of those three. He credits Andy Grove
(Intel, *High Output Management*) for the motivate/train pair and Leila for the
ego-protecting diagnostic script.

## Key claims

- Three levers determine whether an employee does a task: (1) they didn't know you
  wanted it (expectation not set, not recorded, not asked about frequently, no
  metric); (2) they don't know how (not trained on the task, on prioritization
  within their workload, or on your preferences); (3) they aren't motivated
  (not compensated, work feels pointless/unseen, not tied to purpose/values/self-interest).
- To set expectations properly: communicate it, put it in writing/record it, repeat
  it, ask about it frequently, and give them a metric to track.
- Motivation should be layered — global business impact, impact on coworkers they
  care about, impact on the customer, alignment with the person's own values, and
  finally direct money (a bonus tied to the metric, e.g. call notes on 95%+ of calls).
- The diagnostic conversation protects the employee's ego by attacking the activity,
  not the person: "it's not like you to not attend to the details — is it that you
  didn't know, you don't know how, or you don't want to?"
- Andy Grove's premise: the boss's job is two things, to motivate and to train.
  Hormozi adds the third (they didn't know you wanted it) as his own contribution.
