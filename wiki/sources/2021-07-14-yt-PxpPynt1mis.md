# Here's Why I Always Give Away a % of My Business

## Summary
In this solo video Hormozi explains why and how he shares business ownership with key employees, centering on "phantom equity" — equity that is not vested but that vests on a defined trigger (typically a sale or change in liquidity). He walks through the mechanics from both the owner's and the employee's side (vesting schedules, cliffs, forfeiture on departure, tax treatment, liability, and the separation of equity from profit distributions) and frames the whole practice around his rule that making other people money makes him money. He cites Sam Walton's regret at not starting Walmart's employee stock program sooner and traces his own arc from being loose with equity, to hoarding it, to a "middle path" of giving proportional phantom equity only to key players.

## Key claims (2021; paraphrase unless quoted)
- Growing his own slice comes from growing the pie and making more people wealthy; his "rule of life" is the more money he makes other people, the more money he makes — and this applies to employees, not just customers. (Alex; says it took him much longer than hoped to learn)
- Phantom equity = equity that is not vested but has trigger events that cause it to vest; the trigger is typically a sale or change in liquidity/ownership. (Alex)
- Its objective is to get owner-like thinking, engagement, and behavior from people who are not majority owners, and to retain key people so they keep growing the business. (Alex)
- Terms are all negotiable: whether employees can participate in a majority-ownership sale, vesting schedules (e.g. 5% over 5 years = 1%/year), cliffs (e.g. first 1% vests at one year, then 0.25%/quarter), and forfeiture if the employee leaves. (Alex)
- Tax framing (self-reported / general, not tax advice): a true equity transfer is a taxable event for the employee because they receive something of value; phantom equity avoids that because it does not technically vest until a trigger, so the employee is not taxed up front — though it typically triggers as regular income at sale. (Alex, self-reported)
- Phantom equity also shields employees from personal liability, unlike real equity ownership. (Alex, self-reported)
- Equity and profit are distinct variables: one can hold equity without profit distributions, or receive profit distributions without equity; owners conflate them because owners usually have both. Alex says he tends to keep profit-sharing to himself — a stated preference, not a claimed rule. (Alex)
- Rough sizing from his mentors: senior/executive-track leaders might get ~0.5%–2%; a true executive leading nearly half the company might get 3%–5%, depending on business value, seniority, and experience. (Alex, relaying mentor guidance)
- He first encountered phantom equity as an affiliate: he was an affiliate of a large software company and told the CEO he'd need upside/equity to make it a focus; the CEO offered phantom equity instead. He says he has since used phantom equity in "just about every company" he owns. (Alex, self-reported bio detail)
- His personal evolution: very loose with equity at the start, then swung to hoarding it, now a middle path — give proportional phantom equity to key players rather than disproportionate chunks (he "ended up paying for" the early over-giving). (Alex)
