# Entrepreneurs Get Stuck For 3 Reasons (2021-07-30)

## Summary

Prompted by a call with an online-marketing business owner doing ~$1.5M/month who believed no one
in his space exceeds $2M/month, Hormozi argues that a perceived growth ceiling is almost never a
real market cap — it's a personal deficiency in one of three areas. In this 2021 monologue he lays
out his diagnostic: when you're stuck, you're missing a **belief**, a **trait**, or a **skill** —
and you usually need all three.

## The three reasons entrepreneurs get stuck

1. **A belief that is incorrect** — you don't believe what you need to believe (e.g. that the
   ceiling is real, or that your product is good enough to push past your own needs).
2. **A trait you lack or need to change** — e.g. patience to see it out, or the shift from
   self-centered to other-centered motivation.
3. **A skill you lack** — you don't know how to do (or how to hire someone to do) what growth
   requires (e.g. opening a new sales channel).

## Key claims (paraphrase, 2021)

- Getting stuck at a level is caused by a wrong belief, a missing/wrong trait, or a skill
  deficiency — and you generally need all three; coaches tend to over-index on just their own
  favorite (character people talk traits, skill people talk skills, "woo" people talk beliefs).
  (2021)
- "No one in my space does more than $X" is usually a belief issue, not a market fact. When he
  entered the gym space no one exceeded ~$3M/year; he grew his company to $30M+/year, 10x the
  biggest player — like the four-minute mile, undone-doesn't-mean-impossible. (2021)
- Diagnostic math: if you have 10 competitors each doing $2M/month, one operator better than all
  of them could do $20M/month. He sized his own gym target by adding up all competitors' revenue.
  (2021)
- Real market caps do exist (e.g. only ~50,000 chiropractors in the US), but mass-market/consumer
  businesses have the whole marketplace as their base; if you've sold 0.1–1% of your market, you
  haven't capped your market — you've capped your skill, belief, or traits. (2021)
- A hidden belief block: many people don't genuinely believe their product is good enough, so
  subconsciously they stop pushing once their *own* financial needs are met. Growing past that
  requires believing you're helping others — shifting from self-focus to other-focus. (2021)
- On skills: you can either acquire the skill ("how do I do it") or use the wealthier mindset of
  "who can do this" — but you must know what needs to happen before you can find the who. (2021)
- On bringing in an operator too early: someone making ~$50–100k/month asking who should run the
  business "for them" is likely masking that they don't really believe in what they're selling —
  if you believe in it, you want to be the one driving it. (2021)
- Diagnostic sequence when stuck: (1) is there an actual market cap and have I sold most of it? If
  not, (2) could I capture all/half my competitors' combined revenue if I were better? then (3)
  identify whether it's a trait or skill I lack, and attack that one. (2021)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The reason we get stuck in our business is because we either have a belief that is incorrect,
> there's this character trait that we lack, or there's a skill deficiency."

> "Just because no one has done it doesn't mean it's not possible."

> "If you have 10 competitors and each of them are doing 2 million a month, then it means that one
> of you could be better than all of you and make 20 million a month."

> "You haven't capped your market, you just have capped your skill or your beliefs or your traits."

> "You can always acquire the skill, which is how do I do it — or you can have the probably
> wealthier mindset of, well, who can do this."
