# Raising $100M Almost Got Me Kicked Out of My Own Company

## Summary

Despite the biographical framing of the title, this is a **coaching Q&A**, not a story from Alex Hormozi's life. Alex advises the founder of **BioXcelerator**, a stem-cell / exosome healthcare company doing **$24M revenue** and targeting $250–500M, who wants to raise **$100M in cash at a $500M valuation** (selling ~20%) without losing control or getting "kicked out." Alex reframes a capital raise as fundamentally a sale — "a sale on you and a sale on the story" — and argues that valuation is priced off how many unproven assumptions remain: the fewer assumptions investors must believe, the higher the valuation. He coaches the founder to build the narrative around what has *changed* since the last ($50M) round to justify a 10x step-up, and reassures him that control can be retained via structure (dual-class voting, minority-with-control) even after dilution. The video is a MoreMozi clip and closes with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap CTA.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- A capital raise is **fundamentally a sale** — "this is just a sale" — and success depends less on the facts and more on the **story** built around them.
- The core question to answer for each investor is **"what would you need to see in order to believe?"** — map what each potential partner needs to believe/see before committing.
- His own process: talk to **10x the number** of potential investment partners you need, learn each one's "need to believe," then narrow to the realistic ones and size the raise accordingly.
- Sometimes the right move is a **tiny raise** to satisfy one "need to believe," then **ladder** into a larger round once that's proven.
- Reframe: "find my customers, find out what they want, and then build the thing they want" — investors are customers. This is *not* about derailing the vision or building someone else's company.
- Investors are almost always just **paying down risk** — "that's all they're paying down" — either de-risking themselves or wanting you to de-risk for them.
- **Control is manageable structurally**: you can be a **minority shareholder and still control** the company via dual-class / super-voting shares (cites **"Zuck has 100-to-one voting rights"**), provided investors trust you and understand the business needs the capital.
- Valuation is priced off **unproven assumptions**: good VCs discount a multi-billion-dollar outcome by the number of assumptions that must still come true; **the fewer assumptions left, the higher the valuation** (higher likelihood).
- To justify a **10x step-up** ($50M → $500M), you must articulate what **fundamentally changed** since the last round — a proven technology/lab, a de-risked assumption, favorable regulatory direction — so the raise is now a **"capital constraint, not an assumption constraint."**
- Position the biggest remaining-but-now-proven risk as the reason for the largest valuation step-up ("this was the riskiest of the four that have to happen... that's why we have the biggest step up").
- **Don't pitch like a scientist**: investors "don't know the science," their "eyes glaze over" on nuts-and-bolts detail — "do not be a scientist for the pitch." They just want to know they're going to make a lot of money.
