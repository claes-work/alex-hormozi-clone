# The Only 3 Things That Actually Make You Money

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says scattered attention usually comes from fear of being left behind. For
most small businesses, he reduces the money-making system to gathering attention, pointing
it at a conversion mechanism, and delivering on the promise. Once something works, he says
to keep repeating it until it stops working and to use first-party data rather than
thrashing on every outside trend.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Scattered attention often comes from fear: fear of being left behind, becoming
  irrelevant, or missing a new tool.
- Alex trusts that if a change becomes important enough to affect his business, the
  business data will show it and he will adapt.
- He reduces the core business system to gathering attention, pointing attention at an
  offer, and delivering the promise.
- For many sub-$1 million businesses, most time should go into promotion because delivery
  and conversion are often already simple or leveraged.
- A simple daily allocation can be promotion, building the thing, and delivering the
  thing.
- Focus means doing one thing; everything else reduces focus.
- If something begins to work, keep doing it until it stops working instead of stopping
  because of fear it might stop.
- Alex repeated the same presentation on day two of a book launch and says it produced
  about $25 million in day-two sales.
- First-party data from doing the work should eventually outweigh generic third-party
  advice.
- Do not change things when a solution is already in motion and needs time to produce
  data.
- Many beginners lack enough traffic or repetitions to run meaningful A/B tests.
- Alex often uses "cowboy testing" internally: change something, then watch whether the
  number goes up or down.
- Earlier funnel changes, especially creative and click-through rate, can have more
  leverage than small page changes.
