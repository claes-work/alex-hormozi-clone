# Big Business Owner Shares 1 YR of Wealth Lessons in 63min (2021-01-26)

> Solo retrospective. Only Alex Hormozi speaks. Sequel to his "2019 lessons and
> failures" recap. He reads from a running email thread he keeps to himself, logging
> lessons as he learns them throughout the year.

## Summary

In this ~63-minute year-in-review, Hormozi walks through the business, wealth, and
mindset lessons he took from 2020. The through-lines: transitioning himself from CEO to
"investor/owner" so the company can scale without him ("scale zero"); the outsized value
of A-players and the corresponding realization that most employees add little; the pain
caused by years of poor data/CRM systems; and a personal shift toward long time horizons
and away from chasing "happiness." He shares his personal investment allocation
transparently and reflects on marriage, purpose, and having become financially
independent. He frames 2020 as his single biggest year of personal growth despite lower
profit than the prior two years.

## Key lessons

**Scaling / operating role**
- **"Scale zero" / simple scales.** To grow beyond a plateau, the founder must be able to
  do *nothing* in the business and still have it grow profitably. Any value that requires
  the founder present becomes the ceiling once the team outgrows him.
- **Transition from "what" to "who."** Early on the founder must learn to do everything;
  as you scale it becomes about finding the right people. But founders reach for the "who"
  too early, before they've learned what actually makes the business work.
- **Hire people "pre-loaded with solutions"** ("batteries included") who have already
  solved the problem several times, so you can point them at it.
- **Adopt an investor's operating playbook.** He studied how private equity firms take
  over and improve a business (100-day plan, pick 2-3 KPIs, track meticulously, cut costs,
  form 2-3 focused task forces, then let management run it rather than meddle). He admits
  he tends to meddle and that meddling hurts the business by removing consistency.
- **Excess cash flow** is the KPI PE firms track first — and it's the number he personally
  cares most about — yet his own company wasn't tracking it. He plans to reorganize around
  it and align management incentives to it.

**People / hiring**
- **One A-player does more than five B-players; most people effectively do nothing.** He
  believes the 80/20 is fractal (roughly 1 in 5 employees provides the value).
- **Growth after shrinking.** Multiple times he cut a team or whole department (e.g. one
  built to fix churn) and found revenue, retention, and profit unchanged or better — profit
  up. He wants to test how far the company can grow with *fewer, smarter* people (cites
  Berkshire Hathaway / Warren Buffett running a giant company with ~19 employees).
- **Busy ≠ productive.** "Cutting grass with scissors" — activity that isn't value. If he
  never hears an employee's name, that's the sign they aren't moving the business.

**Data / systems**
- **Poor data management was his single biggest three-year mistake.** No properly set-up
  ERP/CRM. He estimates it cost ~$30M in left-on-the-table revenue. Adopting HubSpot
  (~$36k/yr) finally let them track "click to close." Advice: invest early in good data
  systems.

**Focus / product**
- **Simple scales.** The simplest solution — not the "best" (most complex) one — is
  actually best because it can be realized. Simplifying Gym Launch's product got better
  client results than during peak Facebook arbitrage.
- **Sell what they want to buy, not what you want to sell.** For gyms that's leads and more
  money; almost everything else is a waste of focus.
- **Three-year product life cycle.** Gym Launch reinvented its product suite ~twice (six-week
  challenge → higher-ticket hybrid bootcamp → same packages via outbound acquisition).
- **Multiple acquisition channels.** Built an outbound (cold-call) channel in 2020; took
  ~90 days to get right; inbound + outbound firing side by side is stable and reliable.

**Decision-making / mindset**
- **Right decision vs. right outcome.** You can make the right decision and be wrong (COVID
  hits), or the wrong decision and be right (betting savings on black and winning). Judge
  yourself by the information available at the time; don't unlearn good habits because of a
  bad outcome or reinforce bad habits because of a lucky one.
- **Reversible vs. irreversible decisions** (via Bezos). Starting a company is largely
  irreversible — hence his regret over running three businesses at once.
- **Entrepreneurship runs in cycles and seasons** — seasons of sowing and reaping, plus
  literal seasonality (Q1 strongest for fitness/weight-loss businesses).
- **Know your "game beyond the game."** Once you have a large stack of money, allocating it
  becomes its own game that can drain attention from the business — where your highest
  "return on attention" actually is.
- **Expand your time horizon** (his single most-emphasized lesson). Think in 5- and 10-year
  terms, not months. Long horizons let you nearly eliminate risk and make steadier, calmer
  decisions; the rich get richer partly because they can think this way. Ties to
  fulfillment: long horizons lower unrealistic short-term expectations, which raises
  fulfillment and paradoxically speeds progress.
- **"F*** happiness."** Chasing happiness sets an unrealistic expectation that leaves you
  perpetually deficient; abandoning it made him more content. Do meaningful work instead.
- **Financially "done" requires a bigger purpose** — money stops incrementally changing
  life, so meaning must come from the work itself.

## Key claims (paraphrase; dated 2021-01-26)

- Company hit **$100M in total sales over ~3.5 years** (reached the cumulative $100M mark in
  2020) — NOT $100M in a single year. Yearly breakdown he gives: **$7M (2016), $28M (2017),
  $28M (2018), $37M (2019), ~$31M (2020)** (2020 books not final). [Note: he lists two $28M
  years; the aggregate and his "stuck at ~$30M for three years" framing are the load-bearing
  points.]
- He and Leila took **~$35–36M out of the business in direct distributions** over 3.5 years
  (excludes the equity value of the companies).
- In **2018** they took home **$17M in profit**. Made **less profit in 2020** than in the
  prior two years.
- **Prestige Labs** paid out **over $10M in commissions**; he calls it a good/real business
  but says starting it was not the correct decision at the time (started it to allocate Gym
  Launch cash into an ancillary business sharing distribution/shared services).
- Runs **three businesses**: Gym Launch, Prestige Labs, ALAN — which he says was a mistake
  from an *attention* standpoint, but it forced his transition into the investor/owner role.
- Built and then **killed a gym CRM project after spending ~$1M** — starting it was a bad
  decision; shutting it down (eating the sunk cost) was a good one.
- Spent **~30% of the year figuring out how to allocate money.**
- **Advisory work:** took on companies doing $1–5M/yr; one company he began advising went
  from ~$50k/month (stuck at ~$500k/yr for two years) to ~$150k/week (~$600k/month) in six
  months.
- **Investment allocation (2020):** whole/permanent cash-value life insurance (go with the
  "big four": Guardian, New York Life, Northwestern Mutual, MassMutual; watch agent
  commissions; ~4%/yr guaranteed growth; designed to break even by year 3–5; look up COLI/BOLI);
  B- and C-class multi-family apartment syndications (downside mitigation over upside);
  broad index funds; a **5% hedge in crypto** (not to make money — insurance against dollar
  devaluation/loss of reserve-currency status).
- **Financially independent:** growing current net worth at ~10%/yr would reach **~$700M
  (half a billion+) by age 60**; says he could stop working now.
- Q1-2021 outlook: bullish — lots of savings/cash on the sidelines, expects stimulus and an
  economic boom near-term; notes both inflationary and deflationary pressures.
