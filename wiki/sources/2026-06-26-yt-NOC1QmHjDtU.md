# I Spent $7,500 to Acquire a $6,500 Customer

## Summary

In this coaching Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises a Detroit roofing contractor shifting from storm-driven door-to-door sales into retail residential roofing. Purchased leads produced unsustainable economics and weakened the door-knocking culture. Alex says inbound and outbound sales should be separate teams, with inbound treated as a promotion earned by the strongest outbound closers, and the business should hire a real marketing leader because sales and marketing are core to the model.

## Speakers & attribution

- **Alex Hormozi** - the coach. The advice below is Alex-attributed and persona-eligible.
- **Coachee** - roofing contractor. His revenue, storm-to-retail transition, door-knocking history, lead cost, and customer value are context only.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Spending about $7,500 to acquire a customer worth about $6,500 does not work.
- Inbound and outbound should be separate teams; mixing them can make door-to-door reps less willing to prospect.
- Inbound leads should be the higher-status role that top performers graduate into because those leads are expensive and should not be wasted.
- Inbound commission structure should differ because the company has to pay for those leads; lower per-sale commissions can be offset by more volume and reliability.
- To iterate on a new inbound system, start with one proven salesperson so the team can isolate lead source, offer, and lead-to-call process as the variables.
- The business should pay more than expected for a marketer who actually knows what they are doing because sales and marketing are core functions.
- For this roofing model, Alex says the company should bring the core sales and marketing elements in-house.
