# Why Does Life Need to Be Meaningful?

★ **L3-candidate for `persona/beliefs.md`** — articulates a durable, distinct belief:
meaning is a *choice*, not a *demand of the universe*, and dropping the unspoken "should"
(life should be meaningful, I should be happy) is precisely how he first experienced those
things. Complements — does not duplicate — the earlier "meaning = learning / output of a
process" belief.

## Summary

A solo Alex Hormozi clip (excerpted from a podcast appearance — a second speaker interjects
"Mhm" once and Alex references "Williamson," i.e. Chris Williamson; no substantive claims
come from the second speaker, so everything here is Alex-attributed). Alex recounts asking
an acquaintance who had sold his company for hundreds of millions of dollars how he derives
meaning from life. The reply — "Why do you think that life needs to be meaningful?" — shook
him. It exposed an unspoken "should" he had been carrying: an inherent *demand of the
universe* that his life be meaningful and that he be happy. He argues these hidden shoulds
(the beliefs so deeply held you never think to question them) are the ones that change you
most, and cites his favorite quote (a permutation of Orson Scott Card): we question all our
beliefs except those we truly believe and never think to question.

His resolution: stop demanding meaning and happiness as external things to be pursued (which
creates a gap you can never close), and instead *choose* to focus on his degrees of freedom
and responsibility — the things within his control — doing his best to make others' lives
and his own better. He frames this as a behaviorist doing what he's been rewarded for in the
past, and cautions against projecting one's own sources of joy onto others. The clip closes
with a plug for the free acquisition.com/roadmap resource.

**Overlap / distinctiveness:** This overlaps thematically with the recent "What I Think the
Meaning of Life Is" (meaning = learning / meaning as the output of a process). What is
**distinct** here is the anti-framing: rather than *defining* meaning, this video argues for
*dropping the demand* for meaning altogether — treating meaning as an optional choice, not a
requirement — and the specific mechanism that pursuit creates an uncloseable gap. The
"shoulds / demands of the universe" and Orson Scott Card framings appear to be new material.

## Key claims

_All paraphrased and Alex-attributed unless quoted; dated 2026-02-23._

- An acquaintance who sold his company for hundreds of millions, asked how he derives meaning
  from life, replied "Why do you think that life needs to be meaningful?" — a question that
  genuinely shook Alex. (2026-02-23)
- Alex realized he had been carrying an unspoken *demand of the universe*: that life should
  be meaningful and that he should be happy. (2026-02-23)
- The "shoulds" we don't even know we hold are the ones that change us most; the beliefs we
  never think to question are the most powerful and the hardest to see. (2026-02-23)
- His favorite quote (a permutation of an Orson Scott Card line), which he says may go on his
  tombstone: we question all our beliefs except those we truly believe and never think to
  question. Much of his entrepreneurial and human journey has been hunting for those
  invisible beliefs. (2026-02-23)
- History sweeps essentially everyone away within 100–1,000 years; over 10,000–100,000 years
  we are almost certainly forgotten. The desire to be remembered is itself just another
  unexamined "should" — why must we be remembered? (2026-02-23)
- His "solving" is for degrees of freedom and responsibility: focus on what is within his
  control and do his best to make other people's lives, and his own, better. This is a
  *choice*, not a demand made of the universe. (2026-02-23)
- Demanding that life be meaningful and that he be happy placed those things *outside* himself
  and created an inherent gap he could never close; only by trying to forget/eliminate the
  demand did he first actually experience them. He keeps the demand out of his head so he can
  be *inside* those states rather than in pursuit of them. (2026-02-23)
- He holds a behaviorist view: everything reduces to behaviors; he does what he has been
  rewarded for and enjoyed the results of in the past, and continues those behaviors.
  (2026-02-23)
- He encourages others to keep doing what has produced joy for them — but cautions against
  projecting your own sources of meaning/joy onto others, who may not get the same result.
  (2026-02-23)
