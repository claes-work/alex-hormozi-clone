# My 4-Part Investing Model (with real example) (2021-06-14)

> ⚠️ **Not financial advice; self-reported, US-specific, illustrative.** Every deal figure,
> return, and tax rate below is Alex Hormozi's own account of a specific private-lending deal
> and a competing deal pitched to a portfolio CEO. Figures are rounded and unverified against
> any registry or third party.

## Summary

Hormozi lays out a four-lens framework he uses to evaluate any investment — **yield, capital
preservation, tax advantage, and equity growth** — and contrasts a bad deal (a startup asking
$450K for a 10%/yr return at 12 months) against a hard-money bridge loan he says he personally
did ~60 days earlier. He describes writing a $2.5M note to fund a buyer purchasing a
foreclosure building already worth ~$5M, structured so he collects ~4%/month (~$100K/month)
with personal guarantees and a minimum carry. The video's stated purpose is to teach viewers
to run any deal through the four lenses so they can spot predatory or lopsided structures.

## Key claims (2021, paraphrase)

- (2021) Evaluate every investment through **four lenses**, each on a scale rather than
  yes/no: (1) **yield** — how much cash flow comes to you monthly; (2) **capital
  preservation** — how likely your principal returns to you; (3) **tax advantage** — capital
  gains treatment vs. ordinary income; (4) **equity growth** — whether the thing you buy
  becomes more valuable.
- (2021) The startup deal ($450K → $495K at 12 months, i.e. 10%/yr) scored badly on all
  four: unlikely capital preservation (a startup), no upside participation, ordinary-income
  tax treatment, and — for a high earner taxed ~45% — an effective real return of only ~5.5%,
  "barely enough to cover inflation." *(self-reported figures.)*
- (2021) When you lend money you get only guaranteed cash and participate solely on the
  downside — so the downside protection has to be excellent.
- (2021) His own deal: he acted as **the bank**, writing a $2.5M note to secure a building
  out of foreclosure that already had four offers between $5.1M and $5.6M, with the top buyer
  having put $300K earnest money down; he also took **personal guarantees** from both buyers
  of the building to protect his capital. *(self-reported.)*
- (2021) He structured the note as ~4%/month (~$100K/month on $2.5M), with a **minimum carry**
  of two months (so he makes at least ~$200K even if repaid in a day) and up to a 90-day term.
  He frames this as **hard money / private lending / private placement** — borrowers pay a
  high rate because they need liquidity fast to close a below-market deal. *(self-reported.)*
- (2021) Because it is ordinary-income yield (bad tax treatment), the deal has to be
  exceptional; he cites an after-tax return of ~27%, which for him "still makes sense." He
  notes he is taxed at the highest bracket because he has "a ton of income." *(self-reported.)*
- (2021) Access to such deals compounds: the more deals you do, the more people know you have
  capital ready, and the higher-quality deal flow you get.
