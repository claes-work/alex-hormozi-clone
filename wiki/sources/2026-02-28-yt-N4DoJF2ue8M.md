# What Do You Have to Lose...

## Summary
Solo motivational clip on **shame, fear, and the asymmetry of trying**. Alex's thesis: if you have no money and want to make money, you should have **no shame** — "knock, call, email, text, DM, ask," because "life-changing doors do not open themselves." He argues shame is an illusion invented to keep the have-nots from acting, and that trying carries an **asymmetric risk/reward return**: worst case is a "no" you learn from, best case is a sale — either way you come out ahead. He frames unlimited attempts as scratching lottery tickets paid for with *time* rather than money: lose one, pay more time, get more tickets. He distinguishes **guilt (breaking your own rules) from shame (breaking other people's rules)** and pushes the listener to ask *whose* rules and whose voice they actually fear — usually one or two specific people, not "society." He uses his own decision to sell Gym Launch for ~$46M (down from a pre-COVID ~$150M valuation, so it "felt small") as the case: he realized he was withholding the decision to avoid one particular friend's judgment, i.e. handing that person total power over his life. His counsel: even if critics are right that you had an advantage, they'll disqualify your success regardless ("good genetics," "great parents," "inherited wealth," "born in America"), so you may as well **play the cards you're dealt** — capped with his father's Shaq analogy (Shaq didn't refuse to play basketball because he was 7 feet tall). Also restates his **fog-walking** idea: you can't see the whole path, and shouldn't try to plan 17 steps ahead because both the game and you-as-player will change; just take the visible step in front of you. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of February 2026, Alex claims that if you have no money and want to make money you "should have no shame" — you must proactively ask (knock, call, email, text, DM) because "life-changing doors do not open themselves."
- As of February 2026, he argues shame is an illusion "invented by people who have to prevent people who have not from taking action."
- As of February 2026, trying has an **asymmetric risk/reward return**: worst case is a "no" you learn from and get better; because life gives "unlimited shots while you're alive," reaching out to a stranger always leaves you better off — you gain either a sale or an experience.
- As of February 2026, he reframes attempts as lottery tickets paid for with **time, not money**: a failed attempt just costs time, so "pay more time, get more tickets, keep scratching."
- As of February 2026, he distinguishes **guilt (breaking your own rules) from shame (breaking other people's rules)** and says fear of being ashamed should prompt the question "who wrote the rules you're choosing to follow?" — which is never the successful people, since they publicly say it's "totally different once you get in the pool."
- As of February 2026, he restates the **fog metaphor**: you can only see a few steps ahead, and planning the whole path rests on two fallacies — that you have the vision to see that far with current skills/resources, and that neither the game nor you will change; the only move is to take the visible step, after which more steps appear.
- As of February 2026, he advises writing down what's stopping you and, specifically, *whose voice or judgment* you fear — it's usually one or two people, not "society."
- As of February 2026 (Gym Launch case): he considered ~$46M "small" because Gym Launch had been valued at ~$150M before COVID; he realized he was avoiding the sale to escape one particular friend's judgment, which amounted to giving that person "complete power" over his life — a terrible reason not to decide.
- As of February 2026, he claims most people don't want you to win, and even when you win they "reverse-excuse" it with justifications (good genetics, great parents, inherited wealth, connections, born in America, speaks English) — so critics will disqualify your success regardless, making their opinion useless as a reason not to act.
- As of February 2026, his conclusion (via his doctor father's Shaq analogy): you "play the cards you're dealt" — refusing to use an advantage just wastes it and still doesn't earn respect.
