# Brutally Honest Advice to my Poorer Younger Self

## Summary

Solo monologue in which Hormozi tells his younger self what he wishes he had understood about money, growth, and time. Two central regrets: he over-optimized for fast-feedback sales/marketing and under-invested in **product** and **brand**, the two things that create compounding. He reframes wasted time as "ignorance debt" everyone owes and must pay down through action, and attacks morning-routine culture, "30 is the new 20," dead-end jobs, self-labels, and other people's opinions.

## Key claims

- "People obsessed with their morning routines make less money than people obsessed with making money" — copy what a successful person did on the way up (in the grind), not what they do now at the top; correlations to their current habits (Buffett drinks Coke, etc.) are mistaken.
- Optimizing for **earning** in your 20s over **learning** is a mistake — seasons of earning vs. seasons of learning; taking a high-paying dead-end job forecloses far larger long-term potential. He cites turning down a comfortable path to work for $13/hr at a gym.
- The most expensive thing you pay for is the information you don't have but should ("ignorance debt"). If you make $50k, not knowing how to make $1M costs you $950k/yr — so education is worth the difference.
- The two lessons his younger self most needed: (1) **product** — spend upfront time going from good to great (each step 8→9→9.5→9.75 is ~10x the work); a good-enough product does its own marketing. (2) **brand/reputation** — a strong brand means people buy the next thing sight unseen; both reduce to the one concept he didn't grasp: **compounding**.
- Cut friends who only talk about your past, not your future; don't take opinions about your life from people whose interests aren't aligned (only "future you" is fully aligned). Reject self-labels ("I'm bad at math," "I'm a hothead") — labels change behavior more than behavior changes labels.
