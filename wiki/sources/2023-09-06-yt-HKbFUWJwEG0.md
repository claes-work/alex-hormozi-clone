# I Reverse Engineered the Perfect Business

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi video in which he "reverse engineers" the perfect (maximally
*valuable*, sellable) business by painting a "Mosey Lisa" — his pun-portrait of a
masterpiece company — and identifying the ten pieces that make a business into an
**asset an institutional investor would buy**, not just a job that makes money. The
spine is Acquisition.com's **"value acceleration method"** and the equation
**Enterprise Value = number of customers × lifetime gross profit ÷ risk**; each of the
ten criteria increases customers, increases lifetime gross profit, and/or decreases
risk. Origin story: gym launch went $0 → $4.4M/month in 20 months but "also sucked" and
an investment-banking friend told him it could sell for millions only after he fixed a
list of things — which became this framework.

## Key claims (the ten perfect-business criteria / filters)

All paraphrase unless quoted. Every criterion is framed as a *risk-reducer / value
multiplier* against the EV equation.

1. **Leadership team in place running the day-to-day** — the owner can leave and the
   business maintains *and grows* in their absence. Most small businesses die if the
   owner leaves, so no one can buy them. An operator is someone who can **lead** the
   business (not just manage systems); litmus test: after they arrive you have *more*
   time/bandwidth. Good operators use as *few* systems as possible to decrease work
   globally; bad ones add tasks without removing any. Prefer to under-title (manager →
   director of ops → VP of ops → COO); don't over-credentialize. If you 10x in size, the
   leadership team you had at 1x is "virtually non-existent" at 10x. Hire **for a
   purpose** (a specific problem to solve), and weight **track record / relevant
   experience** over "would we be friends." His personal litmus: step away and the
   company grows for **six straight months** without him jumping in.
2. **Marketing without the founder** — if you *are* the ads/content, that's a job, not
   an asset. He transitioned gym launch ads in stages over ~12 months: Alex only → Alex
   + Kale (his CEO) together → Kale alone. Brand = associations, so you deliberately
   **transfer the association** to someone else. Also: keep your *personal* brand
   separable from your primary income source if you ever want to sell.
3. **Delivery without the face of the founder** — replace yourself not with one clone
   (impossible) but with **two-to-five "subject-matter experts"** who each own a slice
   (sales, retention, marketing) — a "Hydra." Edify them to customers; give the highest
   ones small equity (0.1–0.5%), which also makes non-competes hold weight. Buyers pay
   for future value the company delivers *without you*.
4. **Multiple reliable acquisition channels** — "multiple lines in the water." One
   channel = one platform ban away from death. Both **multiple** and **reliable** matter.
   Tactics: one-offer / one-avatar / one-channel until **$1M/year**; then "more and
   better" on the working channel before adding a second; **don't kill your business
   trying to save your business**; **look at progress not outcome** when standing up a
   new channel (it can take months to first sale); start new channels with *someone
   else* owning them; pick the next channel by **what others of your size in your
   industry already use profitably**.
5. **Reliable recurring revenue** — one-off sales plateau; recurring revenue compounds.
   The real goal is **net negative churn** (revenue grows even with zero new customers,
   e.g. Salesforce expansion). B2B should build in expansion revenue; consumer (Netflix)
   mostly hopes you don't cancel. **Nine C's of stickiness:** consumption, collateral,
   cost of switching, choice (scarcity/patent), control (of money flow), cause,
   community, contracts/commitments, communication. EV = a **multiple on earnings**;
   multiples (P/E) reflect how reliable/growing future revenue is (cites Netflix ~44x,
   Microsoft ~29x, Amazon ~310x on slim-by-design margins); small businesses <$10M
   typically 1–4x.
6. **Diverse customer base** — no whale should be too large. Ideal: **no customer >20%**
   of revenue; his personal preference **<5%**. A customer that's 20% of revenue can be
   ~100% of *profit*, so losing it breaks you even. A whale forces you to rebuild the
   business around it. Two decisions: (a) take the whale on at all? (b) if yes, then
   chase minnows or a whole herd of whales (10–100 whales = diversified again).
7. **Automated metric tracking** — "if you don't have data you can't make good
   decisions." Move off scattered Google Sheets into a real CRM (Salesforce/HubSpot).
   Interview tell: how many and how *nuanced* the metrics someone tracks. Depth on the
   back end (product/delivery) should match depth on acquisition. Process: (1) pick
   platform, (2) pick the data, (3) one owner ("one chest to poke"), (4) game-plan via
   **theory of constraints** — add a fixed % to each metric and attack the one that
   moves total throughput most (usually the lowest number first).
8. **High cash flow, profitable, growing — with a story** — Newton's first law: a
   business in motion tends to stay in motion, so buyers prefer growth already underway.
   He's a **cash-flow investor**. Distinguishes profit-on-paper from cash. Encourages
   founders to take a fixed monthly amount out to de-risk personally. A **story** should
   track a **trend** (e.g. AI tailwind/defense). Insider move on brick-and-mortar:
   audit each product's **gross profit and gross margin** and reorganize the sales
   process to push the best ones. "No silver bullets, many golden babies."
9. **Audit-ready financials** — a third party can independently recompute your profit
   and it matches. Levels: (1) have financials at all (outsourced bookkeeper), (2)
   upgrade to an accountant + move **cash-basis → GAAP** (smooths revenue recognition),
   (3) audit-ready + "quality of earnings" (a "blue check mark" on your numbers). "You
   will literally not sell your business for anything material" without this.
10. **$5M+ in EBITDA** — most institutional investors won't buy smaller; reaching $5M+
    EBITDA usually *requires* a real management team, and analyzing a small deal costs
    about as much effort as a big one. Portfolio stats he cites: within 12 months
    revenue **+1.8x**, profit **+3.01x**; within 24 months revenue **+2.8x**, profit
    **+4.7x** on average. His sweet spot: businesses already ~$1–2M+ (esp. brick-and-
    mortar chains doing $1M+ profit) where he can "plug the holes" and unlock 50–100x.

**Umbrella claim:** "You get paid for what you do; you get returns on what you own."
Value comes down to selling more customers, making them worth more, and making sure
those keep happening **without you**.
