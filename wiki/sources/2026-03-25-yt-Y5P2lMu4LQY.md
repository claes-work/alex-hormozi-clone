# Stop Caring About What Other People Think

## Summary
In this MoreMozi clip, Alex Hormozi and a co-host discuss how people criticize you both before and after you succeed, framing all such criticism as others casting their preferences onto you. Hormozi offers a two-part filter for deciding which feedback to actually heed — aligned incentives and domain competence — and argues that criticism-sensitive people must learn discernment rather than ignoring everyone. He closes on the idea that success is simpler than people admit but harder than they expect, and that willingness to persist through discomfort beats most competitors.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames nearly all criticism as one message: "you live your life in a way that I would not prefer" — critics cast their own preferences onto you and then expect you to care.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues you must learn to listen to feedback because reflexively ignoring all opinions ("middle finger my way through life") is impractical; products, podcasts, and sales all improve by responding to feedback.
- As of March 2026, Alex's two filters for whether feedback is worth heeding are: (1) aligned incentives — does this person benefit from you doing better; and (2) competence — do they have relevant domain expertise. He says adding more criteria only makes the decision harder.
- As of March 2026, Alex distinguishes "criticism hyperresponders" who over-index on others' input, and warns that a stray comment ("a flicked penny") may be a joke, a fake, or laced with arsenic — discernment when you're unused to feedback is a hard skill.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims people underplay how simple success is and then wrongly infer it must be easy, leaving them disappointed; he cites a friend whose money-back guarantee (write daily intentions and a recap for six weeks) had a completion rate under 1%.
- As of March 2026, Alex echoes a Kobe Bryant realization that many who "made it" stop being willing to sacrifice to keep winning.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Before you win, everyone will ask why you're working so hard. And after you win, everyone will remind you how lucky you got."

> "At the end of the day, they just cast their preferences onto you. And then somehow they expect you to give a [expletive]."

> "The ability to be discerning when you're not used to receiving feedback is a difficult skill."

> "Number one: aligned incentives — does this person benefit from me doing better. Number two is competence — do they actually have sufficient domain expertise where their feedback would be relevant."

> "The first one ensures that the person is pointing in the same direction as you. The second one ensures that they actually know how to row a boat."

> "Success really just boils down to this: you got to want it more than you hate what it takes to get it."

> "If you're willing to suck at anything for 100 days in a row, you can beat most people at most things."

> "We underplay how simple success is and extrapolate an expectation of how easy it must be from that. And then we're disappointed or dissatisfied when it doesn't meet those expectations. It is harder than we expect, but also the rewards may be also greater than we expect."

## Notes
Synthesis candidate: The two-filter feedback-discernment model (aligned incentives + domain competence) and the "critics cast their preferences onto you" reframe are a crisp mindset addition worth promoting.
