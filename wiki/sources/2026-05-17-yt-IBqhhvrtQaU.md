# How to Raise Your Minimum Standard for Work

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says the absence of fear comes from knowing he has done everything under
his control to prepare. He describes success as input-based: even a good outcome feels
tarnished if he knows he did not prepare fully. The clip also explains how his minimum
standard for work has risen, from single-day slide decks to hundreds of hours, repeated
rehearsal, recordings, edits, and game-tape review until the presentation is refined.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- If Alex steps in front of a huge audience after doing everything in his control, he
  says there is nothing to fear.
- The only voice he expects to hear during performance is his own judgment about whether
  he prepared.
- Defining success by controllable input makes success feel more under one's control.
- The downside is realizing that a person can do very few things at a true best-effort
  standard.
- Alex says his later-career returns grew because his minimum work standard multiplied
  dramatically.
- He recommends measuring serious work in hundreds of hours.
- Doing more reveals that a person is capable of doing more.
- His presentation process is iterative: make it, perform it mentally, perform it aloud,
  watch the recording, edit, and repeat until it is "glass."

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "The only person whose voice I'll hear is mine."

> "Measure in hundreds."

> "I just keep doing it until it's glass."
