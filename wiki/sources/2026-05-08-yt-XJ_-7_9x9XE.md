# Why Most Pricing Ladders Don't Actually Work

## Summary

Alex coaches a luxury wellness/interior-design business owner away from a three-tier
pricing ladder. He says the tiers are not differentiated enough, rich buyers want
speed and flexibility, and the business is bespoke enough that pricing should be job
specific. He recommends a small annual continuity plan to stay in the relationship and
capture future projects.

## Key claims (Alex's advice, dated 2026-05-08)

- Alex says a pricing ladder may not fit a bespoke ultra-high-net-worth advisory
  business.
- He says fractal pricing needs much larger jumps than roughly 20% to make tiers feel
  meaningfully different.
- He says 10- or 15-year commitments can feel too heavy for very wealthy buyers.
- He says rich buyers tend to want options, flexibility, speed, and not having to redo
  work.
- He recommends making the annual retainer de minimis compared with the project price
  and positioning it like insurance or strategy.
- He says the continuity plan gives the provider a recurring reason to meet the client
  and discover future acquisitions or projects.
- He says small jobs can lead to big jobs with rich clients.
- He says $80 per square foot may mean little to the buyer, and the business can price
  internally while communicating minimum estate size or overall expectation.
- He recommends bespoke job pricing and continuity rather than the proposed ladder.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I'm not actually sure if a ladder is the approach."

> "The small jobs get the big jobs."

> "Sell whatever you can get away with."
