# You'll Find This Video When You Need it Most

## Summary

A solo, personal video in which Alex Hormozi shares a four-part framework for moving forward through hard times, prompted by what he calls some of the hardest days of his life this year (2025). The four parts are: (1) learn the greatest skill — reframing reality; (2) redefine pain as the price of what you want; (3) endure what others won't; and (4) zoom out and keep going. He weaves in personal disclosures (a multi-eight-figure bill due in 72 hours, a season of difficulty he and Leila are going through, a cancer scare with Leila) and a large set of quotes, parables, and mental models he uses to stay resilient.

## Key claims

- The single greatest skill you can develop is the ability to stay in a great mood in the absence of things to be in a great mood about.
- If you can be in a bad mood for no reason, you might as well be in a good mood for no reason.
- Hard days follow a standard distribution: a top 10%, a neutral middle 80%, and a bottom 10%. You need the bottom-10% days to recognize the top-10% days.
- The most important skill is reframing reality — asking "in what world would this be an amazing thing?" and how you'll tell this story 40–50 years from now.
- A utilitarian test for loss: ask what a loss actually changes about your life (food, home, work, actions). Often it changes nothing except an expectation held in your mind.
- Nothing in this world belongs to you; you are only a steward "renting the chips" — you cash them out when you die.
- Most suffering happens only in the mind, to things that never actually occur.
- In a bad season, aim only to have a good day; stringing enough good days together turns a bad season into a good season ("bite-size victory").
- Big goals come with equally large obstacles; obstacles can take a year or years to get around, so you must manage your headspace over long time horizons — make "a little bit of progress every day."
- Alex's "good day formula": (1) eat with people I like, (2) lift with people I like, (3) write something. The "super good day" upgrade is doing these without a clock / without being in a rush.
- He is willing to trade money/upside for consistently better (unrushed) days at this point in his career.
- Redefine pain: to achieve a goal you must accept boredom or pain, and the bigger the goal the more of both. Pain reframed as "the price of the thing I want" quadruples pain tolerance (cites shock/family research).
- "The works on you more than you work on it" — work always changes you, so it is never wasted even if the outcome is destroyed (bankruptcy, failed partnership).
- You can only start from scratch once; after that you start with experience. "A man with an experience is never at the mercy of a man with an opinion."
- Hardship doesn't define you, it reveals you; you can't claim loyalty, patience, or resilience until it's tested — otherwise it's an opinion, not an experience.
- Nearly every hateful comment reduces to "he lives his life in a way that I would not prefer." You don't have to catch what someone hurls at you.
- Endure what others won't: you can beat 99% of people not by being smarter or luckier but by being willing to endure pain and uncertainty longer.
- Most people don't know what really trying feels like; the highest levels of effort are so rare you can unlock most things by trying "like your life depends on it."
- It's rarer to have a six-pack over age ~30 than to be a millionaire (~9% of Americans have a net worth over $1M) — he explicitly says "don't cite the sources," he heard it and believes it.
- Sadness comes from a perceived lack of options (hopelessness); anxiety comes from many options but a lack of priorities (paralysis). Solve sadness with knowledge, anxiety with a decision.
- Three actions covering every situation: if you don't know what to do, learn; if you know, do it; if you're already doing it, never stop — none of which depend on how you feel today.
- Resilience = returning to baseline behavior faster; toughness = how much it takes before you decline; a third vector = how low you go.
- Leila's most-known quote: "F*** your mood, follow the plan." He calls Leila one of the most mentally resilient people he's ever met (poorer family, worse home situation, arrested six times before 18–19).
- Zoom-out frames: the veteran frame (if it happened 1,000 times, would it still bother you?), cosmic relevance (we're a tiny marble in a galaxy), and the grandfather frame (imagine being 85 and waking up in your current body — a gratitude generator).
- Gratitude is operationalized by imagining something terrible and then realizing it hasn't occurred (his real example: an EA's message wrongly implying Leila had cancer).
- Your life every day is living proof that you can endure everything that has come your way.
- The Solomon frame / Solomon paradox: we give better advice to others than to ourselves; he writes to his imagined wiser 85-year-old self ("a DM to me in the future") and finds it more helpful than therapy because he has all the context and full confidentiality.
- First rule of entrepreneurship: use what you have; become more resourceful. Whether shame/reward/punishment fuels you ("clean vs. dark energy") doesn't matter — define things in observable behaviors, not amorphous labels.
- Bad things don't come in threes; people fail to cope and let one bad thing snowball. Isolate the "cancer" fast and don't use a bad day as an excuse to misbehave.
- The Buddhist two-arrows parable: the first arrow (the event) is often outside your control; the second arrow (your reaction) is optional.
- "If it has not occurred yet, I will not worry about it." Ruminating on negative futures is harmful; when problems arrive they won't look as imagined and you'll have different resources.
- In some seasons, maintaining is winning — "just not losing ground is the W."
- Judgments like "this was a bad year" only happen zoomed all the way out; zoom in and you usually find mostly good days with a few bad moments.
