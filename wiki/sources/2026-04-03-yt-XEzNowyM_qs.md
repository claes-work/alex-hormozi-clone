# You Don't Need an Upsell

## Summary

Alex explains that upsells work best when they satisfy a new deprivation created by the
first purchase, not when they add more of the same value after the customer is full. For
a sticky weekly trivia service, he concludes that the owner should scale the core offer
through more outbound sales activity instead of forcing a short-lived upsell.

## Key claims

- The owner self-reports $1.1 million in revenue, 12:1 lifetime-value-to-acquisition-cost
  economics, 25% margins, and three-month churn on a music-mashup upsell; these are unverified.
- As of April 2026, Alex says an upsell should be timed at deprivation: dessert after a
  steak is attractive because it supplies a different experience, while a second steak is not.
- Solving one problem can create the next sellable problem; his example is generating
  leads first and then selling lead nurture.
- Because the core trivia night is recurring and reportedly retains well, he recommends
  selling more of the core rather than adding another product merely to have an upsell.
- He considers 100 calls per day too low for the current representative and proposes
  roughly 300 daily calls plus three additional representatives at similar activity.
- The owner says adjacent bars compete with one another, limiting local referrals; Alex
  compares this with Gym Launch referrals across noncompeting geographies.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You don't need an upsell."

> "Upsells are all about timing deprivation."

> "You just need to do more of the thing that's working."

## Notes

Synthesis candidate: upsell on a newly created value vector, not more of a satiated one; do not manufacture product complexity when the recurring core works.
