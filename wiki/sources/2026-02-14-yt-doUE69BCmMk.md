# How I Think About Pricing

## Summary

A short clip in which Alex lays out his core pricing heuristic: **sell extremely
expensive to a select few, or super cheap to everyone — the middle is where people
die.** He ties the choice not to the product itself but to **scalability**. If the
offer requires humans and cannot scale (service-type work), price it at the absolute
highest gross margins and sell up-market to people who can afford the ticket. If you
want to serve the mass/low-money market, you first need a truly scalable product with
near-zero incremental cost per customer — and typically enormous capital (he cites
Amazon raising "$680 billion" to serve the masses; likely a garbled figure).

The distinctive angle here is his **customer-psychology argument against the bottom of
the market**: for low-money buyers, even a small price is a large share of their income
or savings, so they carry the expectations of "someone who gives you their life
savings" — impossible to satisfy, and they demand you promise everything for their $10.
Serving them drains reputation and team morale. His personal preference: set customer
expectations at zero and pleasantly surprise, rather than promise the world and fail to
deliver. Practical takeaway for the under-capitalized entrepreneur: do the same work
but charge ~10x, because an audience exists that would pay 10x more — "they just don't
know you exist."

Speaker: solo Alex (a second voice interjects only brief acknowledgments — "right?" —
in an interview/course setting; no substantive claims attributed to it). Overlaps
heavily with existing high-ticket-first / value-equation / go-up-market material.

## Key claims (2026-02-14, paraphrased unless quoted)

- ★ **Barbell pricing rule tied to scalability:** sell extremely expensive to a select
  few, OR super cheap to everyone — "the middle is where people die." The determinant
  is whether the offer is scalable, not the product category.
- If an offer is **not scalable** (requires humans), price it at the **absolute highest
  gross margins** and sell to people who have lots of money and can afford the ticket —
  "get the money from them."
- Serving the **low/no-money mass market** is only viable via a **truly scalable
  product with almost zero incremental cost of adding an additional customer**; only
  then can you sell to the masses.
- As a **service provider you maximize the likelihood of making more money by going
  up-market first**; you can descend to the masses later, once you've built retention
  and near-zero marginal cost.
- Serving the masses at scale usually demands **massive capital**: Amazon is an
  exceptional business but "had to raise $680 billion of capital" to build it. [figure
  likely garbled — flag on synthesis]
- The common failure mode: **under-capitalized, unskilled entrepreneurs decide to serve
  the masses** but lack the capital and skill to actually scale it — that's the trap.
- **Pricing-psychology reason the bottom of the market is brutal:** for those buyers a
  small amount of money is a huge percentage of their income/savings, so they hold the
  **expectations of someone handing over their life savings** — almost nothing satisfies
  them.
- Low-ticket bottom-market buyers tend to **demand you promise everything to get their
  $10**; serving them **drains your reputation and your team's morale** because nothing
  is ever enough.
- Alex's **expectation-management preference:** set customer expectations at **zero** and
  let them be pleasantly surprised, rather than promise the world and fail to fulfill.
- **10x pricing opportunity:** if you're doing the same work anyway, "you might as well
  get paid 10 times as much" — an audience exists that would pay 10x more; they simply
  "don't know you exist."
