# Be a Perfectionist

## Summary
In a clip with an interviewer, Alex Hormozi pushes back on the popular "don't be a perfectionist / perfectionism is just procrastination" advice, arguing that the label is usually misapplied. He distinguishes true perfectionists — who are obsessively working toward finishing and can see their thing improving — from procrastinators who hide behind the word while producing nothing. He then reconciles perfectionism with his signature high-volume content philosophy: reserve uncompromising standards for the few highest-contribution areas, and treat volume itself as the standard everywhere else.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that most self-described perfectionists are actually procrastinators; the label is a socially acceptable cover for not doing anything.
- As of March 2026, he defines a real perfectionist as someone who wants to finish, works on the thing constantly, and can see it getting better — "if you don't know how your thing is getting better, you're not a perfectionist, you're just ignorant."
- As of March 2026, he holds that planning, thinking about, or getting angry online about the thing are all not doing the thing.
- As of March 2026, he says perfectionists (or people with high standards) must pick their battles: apply uncompromising standards only to the highest-contribution areas, because demanding perfection everywhere kills the velocity needed to make progress.
- As of March 2026, he contrasts high-stakes work (e.g., a Skool announcement release, links, video quality) where a typo or pixelated link is unacceptable, with low-stakes "sawdust"/freebie short-form content where an occasional typo is tolerable.
- As of March 2026, he reframes content perfectionism as perfectionism about volume: because you can't predict what goes viral, making 10 pieces beats trying hard on one, and volume compensates for one's own ignorance.
- As of March 2026, he restates his core lesson that "volume negates luck" — high output brute-forces the perspective and data needed to eventually make the best work.
- As of March 2026, he cites Tiago Forte's take that perfectionism lets people avoid producing at the rate required to learn what works, and the pottery-class parable (the quantity-graded group produced both more pots and higher quality than the one-perfect-pot group).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Perfectionism is procrastination masquerading as quality control."

> "Most people who claim to be perfectionists are not perfectionists. They're actually procrastinating cuz they're not doing anything. And so it just is a socially acceptable label."

> "If you don't know how your thing is getting better, you're not a perfectionist. You're just ignorant."

> "Planning to do the thing isn't doing the thing. Thinking about the thing isn't doing the thing. Getting angry at people on the internet that have already done the thing isn't doing the thing."

> "Picking your battles as a perfectionist, I think actually, or someone as someone with high standards, is super important, because you can't have that degree of high standards at absolutely everything, because if you don't pick your battles, you're not going to make sufficient movement at the velocity you need to actually make progress. So, find the areas that are the highest contribution. And don't compromise on those."

> "We know that if we make 10 pieces of content, it is more likely that we will have more people see it than if we try really hard at one. And so we make 10 because the net benefit of the 10 is greater."

> "Volume negates luck. You can try to be lucky and pick the one perfect thing and try and make it, but if you don't want to try and be lucky, you can just do so much work that you will brute force your way to figuring it out."

> "Doing the volume gives you the perspective to then have the best podcast at number 1,000 or 1,001."
