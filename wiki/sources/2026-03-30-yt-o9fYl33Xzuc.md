# Why Most Accounting Firms Stay Small

## Summary
In a live Q&A, Alex Hormozi advises Joel, owner of a virtual CPA/accounting firm (~$2.5–3M revenue, ~30% margins, serving all 50 states) that wants to scale, raise prices, and improve ~70% annual retention. Hormozi argues accounting firms are highly sticky, high-LTV, roll-up-friendly businesses that stay small mainly because no one in them knows how to market and sell. He reframes the caller's churn theory ("clients learned our tax tricks and leave for someone cheaper") as almost certainly weak onboarding and poor client communication, and lays out a two-step fix: tighten the outside experience first, then recruit an elite tax specialist.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex characterizes accounting firms as very reliable, sticky, high-LTV businesses with heavy buyer/roll-up activity; he says nearly everyone he knows who runs one makes money, so demand is strong.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that the main reason accounting firms stay small is that no one inside them knows how to market and sell — growth is all word of mouth — and that hiring good accountants is the second constraint given high demand for talent.
- As of March 2026, Alex distrusts stated cancellation reasons: what a client says on exit ("we already got the value / learned the tricks") is not necessarily why they actually leave; "I already got the value" is what everyone says when they simply want to leave.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues clients cannot judge the quality of the internal tax work, so they gut-call on the external experience — poor communication and expectations lead them to assume the internal work is sloppy too, and vice versa.
- As of March 2026, Alex's diagnosis for the caller's ~70% retention is bad onboarding: the delta between what's sold and the early experience, plus weak communication cadence; he suggests accounting firms should be able to retain over 80%.
- As of March 2026, Alex's prescribed fix is proactive, personalized, frequent, pushed communication — proactively notifying clients each time work is done on their behalf (not asking them for things), so clients perceive constant effort and won't leave for a cheaper competitor.
- As of March 2026, Alex says accountants typically don't know how to deal with customers and need retraining on communication cadence and structured touchpoint calls.
- As of March 2026, Alex affirms accounting firms are scalable, citing EY, KPMG, and PwC as publicly traded examples.
- As of March 2026, Alex's two-step sequence: (1) fix all the outside/client-experience stuff first because it makes more money today from existing clients; (2) then recruit an elite tax specialist from a top firm if the owner is more CEO than "tax nerd."
- Caller-reported (unverified): Joel's firm did ~$2.5M revenue last year (~$3M projected), ~$900K net last year (~$1.1M projected, ~30% margin), minimum fee $1,950/quarter, quarterly tax planning mostly for businesses, ~70% annual retention.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "It's a super reliable business. It's a viable business. Like accounting firms get rolled up left and right. There's tons of buyer activity there. Like, it's very — people don't churn out of their accounts."

> "The issue with accounting is most of the time there's no one in there who knows how to market and sell. It's like all word of mouth and no one knows how to market."

> "I think what someone says on the exit versus why they actually cancel aren't necessarily the same thing. 'Cause 'I already got the value' is literally what everyone says when they want to leave anything, and it's just 'cause they just want to leave."

> "Fundamentally, from an accountant you want somebody who will get you to pay as few taxes humanly possible and keep you out of jail. And that's pretty much the task."

> "I get pissed off if I feel like I'm talking to somebody they don't know much about my business and I'm paying them $8,000 a year to run my taxes."

> "You want frequent communication as long as it's pushed. So it's not me asking for you — 'cause then I hate you. But if it's like, 'Hey, just letting you know we ran this today. This thing is moving forward. Everything's good. Nothing from you. Just keeping you in the loop.'"

> "If you do 100 things, you want to let them know every time you're doing one of those things on their behalf, and they're like, 'Dude, these guys are working for me all the time.'"

> "If this is my external experience, I'll bet you they're not crossing their T's and dotting their I's on my taxes. And so if you get all of the other stuff tight, they will just guess that your internal stuff is tight."

> "I'm more of a CEO than I am a tax nerd, so go get a tax nerd. Recruit somebody from one of those companies who reads tax law for fun."

> "Step one though, fix all the outside stuff. Step two, go get that guy. 'Cause that stuff will make you more money today with what you already have."
