# Alex Hormozi shares the #1 lesson he's learned from the wealthy

## Summary
In this short interview clip, an unnamed interviewer asks Alex Hormozi about pivotal early-career moments and patterns he learned from wealthy people. Hormozi says the single most valuable thing he has done is consistently get around people who make more money than him, to observe what opportunities they pursue and what they say no to. He then breaks down the "games" he saw the wealthy playing — chiefly the private-equity model, which he identifies as the highest concentration of billionaires. He walks through why PE produces such outsized outcomes: stacking multiple layers of arbitrage (buying assets below intrinsic value, third-party debt/leverage, and raising outside capital) so that a small amount of capital can control and grow large businesses over a 5-to-7-year span. He contrasts this with real estate (a more efficient market, more millionaires but fewer 100x outcomes, lower risk) and with high-leverage business models generally, arguing that products with zero cost of replication — media, software, licensing — have more leverage baked in, so finding experts in those domains helps you move faster.

## Key claims
- (Alex) The most valuable thing he has done for his own thinking is to consistently get around people who make more money than him — to see what they think about, what opportunities they pursue, and what they say no to. This hasn't changed over his career.
- (Alex) He rejects the premise of a single pivotal early moment; the trajectory change came from proximity to wealthier people rather than one event.
- (Alex) The largest percentage of billionaires come from private equity; he treats this as an observed anomaly worth studying for its arbitrage.
- (Alex) Real estate has made more millionaires than anything, but it is a far more efficient marketplace, so it rarely produces 100x outcomes; business can produce them in a much shorter period, at higher risk and higher reward.
- (Alex) A key risk asymmetry: a business can go to zero, but a building doesn't really go to zero — the structure remains and can still be rented.
- (Alex) Private equity stacks multiple levels of arbitrage that combine to create 100x outcomes: (1) buying businesses at a significant discount to their worth, more so than real estate; (2) leverage via third-party/bank debt; (3) further leverage by raising outside capital.
- (Alex) Illustrative math (his hypothetical): someone with $1M raises to $100M, puts a third down on three ~$100M businesses (~$300M total) with bank debt for the rest; at ~20% growth over ~5 years those businesses roughly double, and after paying the hurdle rate to partners and repaying debt, the managing partner is left with a large sum (~$100-200M) to redeploy and "play the game again."
- (Alex) Very few paths let you achieve that kind of outcome in a 5-to-7-year span with so little capital.
- (Alex) The trust/experience barrier is real: investors typically back people with a track record (e.g. ~10 years and two funds at a PE firm), so someone starting at 20 could credibly run their own fund by ~35.
- (Alex) Software is a major leverage opportunity because you can go from zero to a billion-dollar valuation in ~3 years — impossible with a chain of dry-cleaning stores.
- (Alex) Products with zero cost of replication — media, software, licensing — have more leverage baked into the business model; you work just as hard to build a restaurant or dry cleaner as a software company, but you need different knowledge.
- (Alex) Finding people with expertise in high-leverage, zero-replication-cost domains helps you move faster.
