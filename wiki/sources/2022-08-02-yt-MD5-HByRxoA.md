# 1.2M Followers in 6 Months… My Content Marketing Strategy REVEALED

## Summary

Alex Hormozi lays out his early (Aug 2022) content-marketing framework: the **five phases of building a content marketing machine** and the **two objectives** content can serve — lead nurture (phases 1–2) vs. lead generation (phases 3–5). He argues most businesses only need phases 1–2 (post recently, look legitimate) unless they intend to make organic content their actual customer-acquisition engine, which requires vendors, in-house hires, and years of patience. He discloses that putting out ~160 pieces of content a week for Leila and himself costs about $70,000/month (self-reported), which he frames as a massive arbitrage against ~$2M/month in equivalent paid impressions.

## Key claims (the content-marketing method)

- **The five phases of a content machine:**
  1. **Post something** — make one thing and post it, anywhere. Most people haven't even done this.
  2. **Post consistently** — pick one platform you already use, set a cadence/calendar, repeat.
  3. **Post reliably on all platforms** — big volume increase; learn how to *package* the same idea per platform (a tweet → TikTok → YouTube Short → Reel: same subject, packaged differently).
  4. **Maximize each platform** — find the max each platform tolerates and crank volume on all simultaneously (short-form like Twitter/TikTok can take 5–10/day; newsfeed audiences fatigue and cap at ~1–2/day). "Most people never even get to phase four."
  5. **Capture and create** — deliberately create content (e.g. a call-in show) and capture it to repurpose across all channels, extending reach.
- **Two objectives split the phases:** Phases 1–2 = **lead nurture** (fodder, testing hooks, sales support). Phases 3–5 = **lead generation** (generating new customers directly from content).
- **Lead nurture litmus test:** when a lead comes in via ads/affiliates/outbound, they check your profile to see "is this person legit?" — you just need to have posted something recent that isn't garbage; they consume 2–4 pieces and conclude you're a legitimate, still-operating business.
- **Build a "best content of all time" master Excel sheet** — some content converts better (knowingly or not). Two uses: (1) sales team can proactively feed it to prospects between call one and call two to close; (2) tells you which hooks/headlines to lead with on paid ads and cold email (split-test winning organic hooks against your control).
- **Content = low-risk hook/headline testing** for what gets people to buy.
- **Conversion still needs CTAs** — even when platforms serve you to new audiences (more followers/likes/impressions), you convert via calls-to-action; the rest of the time you just provide value. Don't oversell small demand.
- **Costs are threefold — time, long-term, money.** Expect it to take a long time: he made content for Acquisition.com ~18 months before real deal flow/growth (self-reported). Patience is the competitive advantage.
- **Leverage via labor:** even phase three exceeds one person's capacity. Start with (more expensive but faster) vendors/specialists per platform, then bring employees in-house, then scale volume internally.
- **Vendor warning:** most vendors are data-driven and obsess over clicks/views/subscribers. "Just because it's easy to quantify doesn't mean it's important." Values, message, and *who* you reach matter more than vanity metrics; resist chasing hits that aren't core to who you are.
- **Niche down for authority:** "How narrowly can I define the problem that I solve so that in that tiny world, in that pond, I am king." Don't compete with Gary Vee / Grant Cardone / Ed Mylett; be the fastest toilet fixer in Tuscaloosa competing against three guys. Evidence (fixed 500 toilets) makes you "beyond reproach." Then ladder up: toilets → plumbing business → local business → marketing → enterprise → business expert.
- **Volume rules:** "There's no such thing as too long, only too boring," and "quantity wins over quality" (quality comes from reps).
