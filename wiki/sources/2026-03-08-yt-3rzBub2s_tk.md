# What is LTV:CAC?

## Summary
Solo educational clip (live-stream format, Alex reading chat guesses aloud). Alex defines the **LTV:CAC ratio** and argues the famous SaaS-world "3:1" rule of thumb is badly overapplied. LTV (which he treats as **lifetime gross profit** — how much gross profit a customer generates over their entire lifetime) is "how much you make"; CAC (customer acquisition cost) is "how much money it cost you." In plain speak, the ratio measures "how much money does it cost you to make more money." His central reframe: the right target ratio depends on **how many humans are in the loop** across the three stages of a business — **attraction** (getting people in the door), **conversion** (getting them to pay), and **delivery** (fulfilling). Using a binary 0/1 scale (0 = zero operational drag / unlimited scale, e.g. software; 1 = a human in that stage), he gives a ladder: all-automated (pure SaaS: ads → checkout → software delivery) can live at **3:1**; **one** human stage → **6:1**; **two** human stages (e.g. a manual sales call plus a service delivery — "many of you guys") → **9:1**; all **three** stages human → **12:1**. The reason is padding/cushion: the moment you add a human you get "lumpiness or inconsistency," and scaling means hiring more people who are worse than your best (a new salesperson caps out, a new rep/technician is less effective), so you need slack in the economics to absorb that cost while you scale. This ties to his "**nail it then scale it**" mantra — most people overexpand (more locations, more reps too fast) because ego is tied to the number rather than the fundamental economics. He then reframes "12:1 is crazy" as actually the *minimum* if you want to scale big: the biggest companies have "absurd" LTV:CAC, and there are only two long-term winning strategies — drive **CAC toward zero** (massive brand, or a viral product; Facebook as the CAC≈0 example — get billions of users for ~$0 and make a couple hundred bucks each) or drive **LTV toward infinity** (Salesforce as the high-LTV example — $1M–$5M/yr enterprise customers, but expensive to acquire). To hit extraordinary ratios, one side must approach zero or infinity. He shares a personal proof point: the first year of Gym Launch his LTV:CAC was **100:1** — spent $100k, made $10M — and notes most of the money he's made came in four distinct "windows of opportunity" (arbitrage windows) each above 30:1, which is why he keeps "beating up the system" / tweaking the money model (the subject of his book *Money Models*). Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex defines the ratio using **LTV as lifetime gross profit** ("how much gross profit you make over the entire lifetime of the customer") over **CAC** ("cost of getting that customer in the door") — plain-speak: "how much money does it cost you to make more money."
- As of March 2026, the traditional SaaS "3:1" rule is true only "under specific conditions which only apply to like 5% of businesses" — despite big-tech CEOs treating it "as though it's a rule of law."
- As of March 2026, the correct target ratio scales with the number of humans in the three business stages — attraction, conversion, delivery — scored 0 (automated/unlimited scale) or 1 (human): all-zeros → 3:1; one human → 6:1; two humans → 9:1; three humans → 12:1.
- As of March 2026, the reason for higher ratios with humans is **padding/cushion**: adding a human creates "lumpiness or inconsistency," and scaling requires hiring people who are worse than your best (a capped salesperson's next hire "won't be as good"), so you need slack to absorb that while scaling.
- As of March 2026, most people try to scale before the business model is proven — "nail it then scale it" — overexpanding (more locations/reps) because ego is tied to the number rather than the fundamental economics; what people say they'd do (nail first) differs from how they behave.
- As of March 2026, a business at 6:1 can comfortably make "a million bucks, a couple million bucks a year," but if you want to scale big, 12:1 is the *minimum* — the biggest companies in the world have "absurd" LTV:CAC.
- As of March 2026, there are only two long-term winning strategies: drive CAC toward zero (via massive brand or a viral product) or drive LTV toward infinity — to get extraordinary ratios, one side must approach zero or infinity.
- As of March 2026 (examples): Facebook is a CAC≈0 business (billions of users near-free, a couple hundred dollars each); Salesforce is a high-LTV business ($1M–$5M/yr enterprise customers, but costly to acquire, bidding against other CRMs).
- As of March 2026, in the first year of Gym Launch his LTV:CAC was 100:1 — he spent $100k and made $10M; most of the money he's made came in four "windows of opportunity" each above 30:1, which is why he keeps tweaking the money model (his book *Money Models*).
- As of March 2026, to classify a stage: manual outreach = human, but ads/content = scalable (attraction); a checkout page = scalable, a phone/in-person sales team = human (conversion); services = human, software/media/physical products = not human by his definition (delivery).
