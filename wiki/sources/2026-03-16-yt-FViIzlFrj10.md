# Helping an Online Service Business Doing $30M/Year Get to $100M

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A in which the owner of an online fitness-coaching/info business (~$33M revenue, selling mobility and injury-prevention coaching to athletes and fitness enthusiasts) tells Alex Hormozi his LTV:CAC has compressed to ~2:1, leaving no cushion to keep scaling his sales team, and asks how to reach $100M. Alex diagnoses it as fundamentally a sales business with direct-response media on the front end, and argues the path to $100M is scaling through training rather than through selection — building a repeatable recruiting-and-training machine so ordinary reps ramp fast and margins hold. Only Alex's advice and frameworks are persona-relevant; the owner's figures are context.

## Key claims

- **This is fundamentally a sales business with direct-response media on the front end.** The core problem isn't ads or funnel conversion — it's the sales motion. "You have a sales business with direct response media on the front end." The fix is a recruiting-and-training problem: better reps, ramped faster.
- **Scale through training, not through selection — that's where the margin advantage is.** Alex claims ~95% of sales organizations scale by hiring as many people as possible and letting the bad ones die out (scaling through selection). When you scale through training instead, you can scale with higher margins because you don't have to overpay "mercenaries" — you can take anybody off the street and have them sell immediately.
- **Training-based scaling is the competitive advantage in an info/coaching space.** Competitors who scale through selection must hire mercenaries who jump from offer to offer and demand a "king's ransom." A business that ramps ordinary reps can pay less, ramp them higher, and get more of them — that is the durable alpha.
- **"You're not going to increase LTV, you're going to increase dollars per lead."** When the owner frames the 80/20 as "increase LTV = better sales team," Alex corrects the framing: fixing the sales function raises dollars per lead, not LTV per se.
- **The two most profitable pieces of business are free/organic customers and renewals.** Customers who come in for free (organic) — which was effectively the entirety of this business's margin — and customers who renew (no CAC to re-acquire) are what drop to the bottom line. "Everything in the middle is literally just to facilitate these two things." The play: double down on those two while lifting the sales function.
- **A realistic LTV:CAC target for this model at this scale is ~5:1 (not 10:1).** Alex pushes back on someone's "10 to 1" suggestion as unrealistic here; 5:1 is a good target. Getting the sales function right gets you to ~3.5–4:1; the remaining ~20–25% lift comes from front-end conversion-rate optimization, media, and back-end renewal rate.
- **Getting to ~4–5:1 LTV:CAC is what unlocks the path to $100M.** "If you get to four or five, then you can get to 100."
- **Add education prior to the close to make reps' jobs easier.** Beyond dialing in the sales motion, add elements of education before the close so reps can succeed with less ramp.
- **Low close rate is partly a ramp artifact, but training is still the lever.** The owner's 20% company close rate is depressed because he scaled from 4 to 40 reps in ~3 months and most reps are still ramping; the goal is to get the best guys to 40–45% close and the median to 30–35%.
- **Context (owner, not persona):** ~$33M revenue online info/coaching business, low-ticket-to-high-ticket engine, selling mobility/injury-prevention to athletes; ~70% of clients from paid, ~30% organic; ~20% margins; LTV:CAC squashed to ~2:1; scaled 4→40 reps in ~3 months; sales training built by the owner and sales director; wants to reach $100M.
