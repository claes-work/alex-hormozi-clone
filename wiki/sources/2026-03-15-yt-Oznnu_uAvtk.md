# Helping a Rental Equipment Company Scale

## Summary
Live coaching clip. The owner of a Mexican rental-equipment company (~$7M/year revenue, the top-rated buyer for the dealerships he sources machinery from, growing ~30%/year, currently ~$1M/month in sales, wanting to reach ~$1.5M/month) is blocked not by operations but by a **psychological fear of debt** — he has a personal debt-percentage ceiling he won't cross for "financial health," yet growth in his capital-intensive business requires financing more assets. Alex frames it as an unavoidable trade: you want to grow, you don't want the thing growth requires (debt), so **one of them has to give** — either growth slows, or you re-appraise how you analyze risk. His prescription for the fear is one word: **math.** Debt increases risk because it raises the chance you miss a payment to a lender who can then take control; the way past the emotion is to *quantify* it — model seasonal volatility, the cash flow the business spits off relative to debt covenants, and what coverage you can comfortably commit to over the period. He notes good business lenders already do most of that diligence/modeling before extending "good debt," and distinguishes **consumer debt from business debt**: a $100k truck financed over 5 years at a ~$20k/year payment that generates ~$400k/year in gross profit is not risky — the asset pays for itself many times over. Risk tolerance is individual (Dave Ramsey runs 0% debt; even 1% debt / 99% cash feels too risky to him, and that's fine). Crucially, Alex validates the owner's caution rather than pushing leverage: if he wants to be in this business 20–30+ years, being **appropriately risk-averse** is correct — grow a little slower but survive every bad year and you end up gigantic, whereas one debt-covenant breach in a bad year sends you back to zero. His analogy: slow-and-steady **Chick-fil-A** kept compounding while **Boston Market** blew up, IPO'd, and imploded. The move is to strip the emotionality, estimate the probabilities (a vendor leaving, machines made obsolete by robots), and make deliberate bets — "we all make bets; it's just part of the game." - As of March 2026, good business lenders already perform most of that diligence/modeling for you before granting "good debt" — if they lend, they've largely underwritten the risk themselves.
