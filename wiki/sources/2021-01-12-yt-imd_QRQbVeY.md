# Negative Emotional Motivation

> Source: YouTube (@AlexHormozi), "Negative Emotional Motivation", 2021-01-12
> [[../../index]]

## Summary

Reflecting on a dinner conversation with Nick Bailey, Hormozi argues that so-called
negative emotions — specifically envy — are adaptive, evolutionary survival tools that,
while they do not make a person happier, do make them successful. He advocates first
*naming* emotions (distinguishing envy from jealousy) to create space between emotion
and action, then judging behavior by its outcome rather than judging oneself for the
motive behind it.

## Key claims (paraphrase; dated 2021-01-12)

- **Envy vs. jealousy are distinct and worth naming.** (2021) Envy = desiring something
  someone else has that you lack; jealousy = the threat that someone will take something
  you already have. Naming the specific emotion is the first step to analyzing it.
- **Envy "gets a bad rap" but drives success.** (2021) He concedes envy does not make you
  happy, but insists it makes you successful — a signature nuanced position.
- **Envy is adaptive / from the "old brain."** (2021) Animals feel it too; when one member
  of a group brings more to the table, envy elevates the rest of the group to achieve the
  same. Good for the collective's survival, not for individual happiness.
- **Admitting envy openly disarms it.** (2021) Telling someone "I'm envious of you"
  creates candor and vulnerability; people usually respond with "let me help you" rather
  than defensiveness. Faking being happy for someone reads as disingenuous.
- **Judge outcomes, not motives.** (2021) He would rather deal with people who "do the
  right thing for the wrong reasons" than the wrong things for the right reasons — the
  self-interested EMT who wants the best stats will save your life better than the merely
  compassionate one.
- **Widen the gap between emotion and action.** (2021) Naming an emotion increases the time
  between feeling and acting; the larger that gap, the more rational and higher-quality the
  decisions, since humans are never fully rational.
- **"Wanting is reason enough" (attributed to Dan Sullivan).** (2021) Giving yourself
  permission to desire things — without interrogating *why* — freed up attention he used
  to spend judging himself, as long as the desire hurts neither others nor himself.
- **Self-described as "a very envious individual."** (2021) He acknowledges this framing may
  partly be rationalizing his own negative emotions, but says it has made him feel "less bad"
  and stopped him berating himself for having desires he thought were wrong.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I think envy absolutely does not make you happy. But I do think that envy will make you
> successful."

> "Envy is adaptive. Right? As human beings, this is actually from our old brain... if in a
> group one being brings more to the table you are now envious of that person and what it
> does is it elevates the rest of the group to go and achieve the same."

> "I would rather deal with people who do good things for bad reasons than [the wrong] things
> for [the right] reason... I'd rather someone who does the right outcome."

> "Would you rather have the compassionate EMT or would you rather have the self-interested
> EMT who is doing it for status? ...I'd rather have the guy who wanted to pride himself on
> being the best in the world and didn't even care about me at all because I knew his result
> was going to be more pro-me."

> "The further that time gap is [between emotion and action] in general, the better the
> quality of the decisions we make because the more rational we are."

> "Wanting is reason enough because so many times we ask, but why do we want that? We're
> probably not going to change the desire."

> "Candidly I am a very envious individual and maybe this is me just rationalizing my own
> negative emotions. Who knows?"
