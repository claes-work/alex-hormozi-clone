# How to Outlearn Everyone

## Summary

Solo video in which Alex Hormozi lays out his personal, step-by-step method for learning
any skill fast. He defines learning as "same condition, new behavior" and intelligence as
the speed of that change, then walks through deconstructing skills, defining success by
measurable behaviors, "ignoring the black box" (focusing on observable inputs/outputs
rather than psychological explanations), observing the top 1-10% to model what they *do*
(not what they *say*), iterating in coats-of-paint layers, and repeating until people call
you a natural. He frames rate of improvement as the only thing that matters on a long
enough time horizon and argues for removing "luck" from your vocabulary.

## Key claims (his learning method)

- **Learning = same condition, new behavior.** Doing something different in the same exact
  situation. Intelligence is the *rate* (speed) of that change — how few iterations, or how
  fast you run the iterations.
- **Two vectors to out-learn smarter people:** fewer iterations to change behavior (raw
  intellect), OR compress more iterations into less time ("lock in," do 7 reps in a day
  instead of one per week). He beat academically superior Vanderbilt peers by working
  9am-9pm (library, cafeteria, gym only) — doing his 10 iterations faster than they did 3.
- **A skill is a chain of adapted behaviors.** Repeated units of same-condition/new-behavior
  chained together.
- **Step 2 — Deconstruct the skill.** Chunk broad terms down (e.g. "business" → marketing,
  sales, product, hiring → sub-skills) until you reach quantifiable behavioral units you can
  change within. Some skills are generalizable (reading, writing, speaking, hand-eye
  coordination, learning itself); specificity is a matter of degree, not binary.
- **Step 3 — Define success.** Identify the specific behaviors/actions that demonstrate
  mastery of each sub-skill; quantify what "good" looks like in practice. "If you don't
  track, you don't care" — no tracking means no way to know if you're improving.
- **Step 4 — Ignore the black box.** Don't try to explain *why* something works
  (psychology, emotionality, "biomolecular gobbledygook"). Focus on observable inputs and
  outputs. Good teachers tell you what to do with your mouth or your body; if they can't,
  they aren't giving clear directions.
- **Model the top 1-10%: observe what they DO, not what they SAY they do.** Most people who
  are good don't know why they're good (Michael Jordan wasn't a great coach). Teaching is a
  separate skill from doing.
- **Volume → first-party data.** After enough modeling and reps, you learn from your own
  work: compare your top 10% against the rest, find 1-2 differences, run another batch,
  repeat. Observation density increases "like coats of paint."
- **Step 6 — Analyze and iterate.** **Step 7 — Repetition** ("repetition is the father of
  skill"). Repeat until people call you a natural — a "natural" is just someone who observed
  and learned unconsciously (like a toddler modeling rewarded behavior; testimonials work
  the same way).
- **Rate of improvement is the only thing that matters** on a long enough time horizon,
  independent of starting point. The team that gets better fastest wins.
- **When entering a new field, hire experts** to start you at "iteration 9 as iteration 1,"
  then out-iterate them with a faster feedback loop.
- **The only "why" he commits to:** people do things because they were reinforced (rewarded)
  or punished for it in the past. Everything else, he only observes *that* it happens.
- **Remove "luck" from your lexicon.** Calling someone lucky makes you better in zero ways;
  99 out of 100 times someone better than him had simply done something he hadn't.
