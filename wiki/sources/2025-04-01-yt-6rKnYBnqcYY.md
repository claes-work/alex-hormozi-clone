# Why Alex Hormozi Values Maximizing Over Work-Life Balance

## Summary

In a conversation on @TheSkoolSchool, Alex Hormozi argues that he is a "maximizer," not an "optimizer": rather than fine-tuning inputs, he wants to do as much of a working equation as humanly possible. He frames optimization habits (sleep tracking, morning routines, supplements) as fragile "superstitions" that become competitive weaknesses, and holds up a laptop-and-a-foldout-chair, "anything else is extra" mindset as his north star. He extends the theme to identity and relationships — that being unapologetically yourself, and being willing to sacrifice what rivals won't, is what produces outsized returns.

## Key claims

- He describes himself as a **maximizer, not an optimizer**: given an input/output equation, his instinct is to scale the input as far as humanly possible rather than tune it.
- He views competitors who depend on optimizations (eight hours of sleep, morning routines, supplements, coffee) as **fragile and easy to break/exploit** in competition.
- Optimizations, taken too far, **become superstition** — romanticized rituals you can't function without.
- His "north star" is being able to make money with just an internet connection, a laptop, and a foldout chair; **everything beyond that is "extra."**
- Over-tracking can backfire: obsessing over "sleep PRs" made his sleep worse than not tracking at all (he still believes in tracking progress in general).
- Hard-push periods are **"for then and not forever"** — temporary sacrifice (weight gained during a grueling work period) is acceptable in service of the goal.
- Competitive sacrifice principle: *the things you aren't willing to give up for your dreams are exactly what the person who beats you is willing to give up.*
- **Absolute dedication to maximization, made part of your identity/character**, is one of the biggest returns of his life.
- On relationships/authenticity: being genuinely yourself filters for a compatible partner; the alternative — someone who falls in love with a role you play — locks you into performing forever.
