# $100M Pricing Playbook (2025)

**What it is.** One of the twelve companion playbooks released around the $100M Money Models 2025 launch, subtitled "Instant Ways to Make More Profit in Any Business" (PDF p. 1). A ~55-page tactical guide (numbered body runs to p. 55, ending with a recap and a call-to-action to book a call at acquisition.com/scale) collecting ten low-effort pricing tactics Hormozi says can add 26.8%–63.8% to revenue with minimal or no change to conversion (PDF pp. 21, 55, 60). Raw path: `raw/books/2025-100m-pricing-playbook.md`, cited by `PDF p. N`.

## Thesis
Pricing is the single highest-leverage profit lever in a business — Hormozi cites Profitwell data on 512 companies claiming a 1% pricing improvement is ~2x as efficient at increasing profit as improving retention and ~4x as efficient as improving acquisition (PDF pp. 15, 60). His guiding principle: "Profit is unnatural. You must force it into existence" (PDF p. 3). The book argues most owners price by copying competitors — "the recipe for break even and burnout" — and instead pushes value-based pricing plus ten "instant profit" hacks designed to affect sales minimally while dropping straight to the bottom line (PDF pp. 18, 21).

## Structure and content
- **Pricing To Make The Most Money** (PDF pp. 1–11): the "business genie" parable comparing 2x customers vs. 2x purchases vs. 2x price, worked through a sample business; concludes doubling price 6x's profit vs. 3.5x for the other two levers (PDF pp. 7–15).
- **Three Models Of Pricing** (PDF pp. 12–15): cost-plus, competitor-based, value-based; the three metrics for value-driven pricing; a price/conversion/LTV table (PDF pp. 18–21).
- **My Rules For Pricing** (PDF pp. 16–18): ~15 pricing beliefs/heuristics that underpin the tactics (PDF pp. 22–24).
- **Just Raise It** (PDF pp. 19–21): the restaurant 4% processing-fee anecdote and the "Small Percentages, Big Changes" argument (PDF pp. 25–27).
- **Ten Pricing Plays** (PDF pp. 22–53): #1 28-day billing; #2 processing fees + 2nd payment form; #3 sales tax; #4 annual price increases; #5 annual billing; #6 round up; #7 annual renewal fee on top of monthly; #8 automatic continuity; #9 ultra-high-ticket anchor; #10 guarantee/warranty upsells. Each with "How I Learned This / How It Works / Examples / Steps / My Advice."
- **Why You Should Actually Do This + recap** (PDF pp. 53–55): benefits of right vs. wrong pricing, and the summary table (PDF pp. 55, 60).

## Key claims
- The "business genie" model: doubling customer count 3.5x's profit, doubling purchase frequency 3.5x's profit, but doubling price 6x's profit — because a price increase adds revenue while costs (acquisition and delivery) stay flat (PDF pp. 13–15).
- Value-based pricing beats cost-plus and competitor pricing; you can charge 2–5x market rates or more if you make something the customer actually wants, and can keep raising as you add value (PDF pp. 18–20).
- The "perfect price" is the one that yields the highest total lifetime gross profit, not the most customers or the highest first purchase — "I used to say the highest price I could get people to buy at. But now I want the price that people will keep paying at" (PDF pp. 20–23).
- **Play #1 — 28-day billing:** billing every 28 days gives 13 cycles/year instead of 12, an instant, permanent 8.3% revenue lift at the same conversion; a 20%-margin business rises to ~26.1% margins (a ~41.5% profit jump). Display price weekly, bill every 4 weeks (PDF pp. 28–29).
- **Play #2 — processing fee + 2nd payment form:** add a ~3.99% card fee after price agreement; if they balk, offer to waive it for a second form of payment. The real prize is reduced involuntary churn (1.2%–1.7%/mo from card declines), which can raise LTV 31%–51% on a $100/mo, 5%-churn service (PDF pp. 24–26, 31).
- **Play #3 — sales tax:** charge tax as a separate line ("plus tax") instead of absorbing it; eating a 5% tax on 20% margins gives away 25% of profit. Alternatively reincorporate in a no-sales-tax state (PDF pp. 27–29).
- **Play #4 — annual price increases:** bake fixed annual CPI-style increases (5%–15%) into contracts; keep it under 15% and few balk. Cites Warren Buffett raising See's Candies prices 50+ times over 51 years, sometimes 17% in a year (PDF pp. 30–32, 38).
- **Play #5 — annual billing:** less frequent billing lowers churn; Profitwell data — annual billing = 2% monthly churn / 5.35x LTV vs. monthly = 10.7% churn. Sell a short 6–12 week term first, then upsell prepaid annual; a 16% annual discount is taken by 10–15% on a page, 30% if it's the default, 35–40% by phone (PDF pp. 33–35, 39–40).
- **Play #6 — round up:** change prices ending in 7 to 9 and add .99; in his gyms $47→$49.99 etc. added $104–$155 per client/year (4.25%–11.1% price increases) with no drop in close rate. Exception: luxury goods should end in round 0 or 5 (PDF pp. 36–37, 42–43).
- **Play #7 — annual renewal fee on top of monthly:** advertise a low monthly rate ($39) but add an annual renewal fee (e.g. $99), lifting effective rate to ~$47/mo (+20% revenue). Pick a fee 1–3x the monthly rate; frame it as "rate protection" (PDF pp. 39–41, 45–47).
- **Play #8 — automatic continuity:** at the end of a fixed term, roll customers into a stripped-down, high-margin version at 5–20% of the main price (disclosed up front); one workshop speaker made $750k/yr from it, Hormozi ~$150k/mo of "pure profit." Modeled 32% LTV lift (PDF pp. 43–47, 49–51).
- **Play #9 — ultra-high-ticket anchor:** add an item 10x+ your core offer's price; even a 10% take rate roughly doubles blended LTV ($440→$890 in the example) and makes the core offer look cheaper, lifting its close rate (PDF pp. 48–49, 54–55).
- **Play #10 — guarantee/warranty upsell:** after purchase, offer a warranty for 5–30% of the price (learned from AppleCare); price it near cost-of-goods so you never lose money. In one portfolio company it covered nearly half the sales team's commissions (PDF pp. 50–53, 56–57).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Profit is unnatural. You must force it into existence. (PDF p. 3)

> People usually price by simply looking at what everyone charges. This is the recipe for break even and burnout. In other words, you run a nonprofit with none of the benefits! (PDF p. 18)

> I used to say the highest price I could get people to buy at. But now I want the price that people will keep paying at. (PDF p. 23)

> Hear more nos, make more money. You have to be willing to delay changing your price back when you get your first no. (PDF p. 22)

> The more often you bill the more often people cancel. The longer between billing cycles, the longer you have to provide value. (PDF p. 23)

> Display price in the smallest increment, bill on the longest increment. How you display price and how you bill don't need to be the same thing. You can say "this costs $5 per day" but bill $1825 per year. (PDF p. 24)

> We might as well pluck the juicy profit fruit from the low-hanging price tree while it's right in front of us. (PDF p. 11)

> Monthly billing gives you 12 cycles a year. Weekly (or every 28 days) gives you 13. That extra cycle? Pure profit. (PDF p. 28)

> PS - Changing prices, more than anything, requires guts. Have some. And reap your reward. (PDF p. 55)
