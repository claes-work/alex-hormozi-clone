# Why Skool is the Easiest Business Model to Start

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi lays out what he calls the simplest business model in the world, built entirely on the Skool platform: get traffic, send it to your "about" page, and convert it into a paid community people like and recommend to friends. This installment focuses on the two front-end parts of that three-step funnel — traffic and the about page (the "what's inside the community" step was covered in earlier videos). He enumerates six traffic sources (warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, content, influencer partnerships, referrals) and gives about-page copy advice centered on demonstrating value ("just show people what's inside"). He closes by framing the Skool Games as a monthly, low-stakes competition where the only way to lose is to quit, because "business is a game of endurance."

## Why the community model works (business-model reasoning)

- **Radically simple funnel / low operational complexity.** The entire model reduces to three steps: (1) get traffic, (2) send it to an about page, (3) have a community people like and tell friends about — "and then you make money. That's the business." The simplicity itself is the pitch.
- **Zero external tooling / low-capital, all-in-one.** "You don't need any external plugins, tools, resources. It's all here and it's built to work seamlessly together." One platform hosts the community, the about (sales) page, and the traffic call-to-action, removing the usual stack of funnel software.
- **Free customer-acquisition options exist.** Of the six traffic sources, warm outreach, cold outreach, content, and referrals require no ad spend — so the model can be started with time rather than capital.
- **Referral / word-of-mouth as compounding, zero-cost growth.** He cites his own books as proof of the referral engine: they "sell zillions of copies... 3,000 plus copies a day" with no direct marketing, no ads, no outreach — purely people telling friends the book is good (self-reported). The lever: make the community "awesome relative to what you charge," and growth becomes self-sustaining.
- **Influencer arbitrage / leverage.** Many influencers have "hundreds of thousands of followers and make very little money." You bring the productized community (Skool's features/value), run it, take a percentage, and monetize an audience that already exists — skipping the slow work of building attention yourself. He frames a concrete deal: an influencer making $500/mo off 100k followers could plausibly make $10,000/mo together, split by negotiation. Start with a low percentage; once you have proof you've done it, take a bigger cut on the next deal. "Big secret here, this is what I'm doing with Skool."
- **Retention driven by product quality, not lock-in.** The retention/growth thesis is that a community that is genuinely great relative to its price keeps members and generates referrals — quality is the retention mechanism.
- **Endurance economics — staying in business is the win.** "You don't win at business. The point of business is to keep the business going. It's to stay in business." The monthly Skool Games reset means a new chance to win every month; the only way to lose is to quit. Compounding small wins (a few members this month, a few more next) is the path.

## Key claims

- The simplest business model in the world is: traffic → about page → likeable community → money.
- There are six traffic sources to feed a Skool community: **warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, content, influencers, referrals.**
- **Warm outreach** = directly messaging people you already know (phone contacts, past emails, social followers/friends/subscribers) about your community.
- **Cold outreach** = same motion to people who don't know you, sourced via bought lists, scraping software, or shared-interest targeting on social.
- **Paid ads** work on any platform "that accepts money in exchange for eyeballs or earballs" (Facebook, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube).
- **Content** works by making free posts whose bio/copy call-to-action points interested viewers to the community's about page.
- **Influencer partnerships** are "one of the easiest and fastest ways to grow a group" because the audience already exists; structure it as a revenue split and increase your percentage over successive deals once you have proof.
- **Referrals** require making the community great relative to price; word-of-mouth then drives growth (his books are his cited proof point).
- The about page has only two components: **media (video/images) and words**. Pro tip: demonstrate value — "just show people what's inside" — because demos let buyers see value before purchasing.
- About-page copy should use **bullets, not big paragraphs**; state facts and tell the truth — "and if the truth isn't that compelling, then change what's inside until it is."
- Model the *structure* of the biggest groups' about pages, but never copy their actual content.
- The instructions are deliberately brief to "practice what we preach" — crowdsource answers and learn by doing, not by watching.
- **Learning requires changed behavior in a new condition**; doing the same things in the same space for a long time means "you have learned nothing."
- In the Skool Games, winners share lessons monthly — recordings from the top 10 fastest-growing members are dropped each month so members have the most current working tactics.
- Business is a game of endurance, not a sprint; the only way to lose the games is to quit, and there's a new chance to win every month.
