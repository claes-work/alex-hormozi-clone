# Helping a Coaching Business Scale

## Summary

Alex advises a fast-growing New Zealand coaching company whose leadership bonus
structures stopped fitting the business. He distinguishes leadership compensation from
technical/individual contributor tracks, recommends annual reviews, and suggests
leadership bonuses based on a mix of company and individual objectives. For fast-growing
companies whose KPIs keep changing, he prefers tying compensation to quarterly objectives
until metrics stabilize. He also warns that purely individual incentives can destroy
teamwork unless paired with quality or pull metrics.

## Key claims

- Compensation structures often need annual review because fast-growing companies outgrow
  them within three or four quarters.
- High-level leaders can participate in a profit-share pool, such as 10% of profits set
  aside for leadership seats.
- Mid/high-level leaders can have bonuses around 20-100% of annual salary depending on
  experience and scarcity; lower levels might have 10-35%.
- Alex often likes 60% of a leadership bonus tied to company performance and 40% tied to
  individual performance.
- Company performance can include revenue, profit, and growth.
- In very fast-growing companies, quarterly objectives may be more useful than KPIs until
  stable baselines exist.
- Purely individual metrics can create competition and reduce teamwork.
- Incentives should pair push and pull metrics, often quantity plus quality, such as
  sales minus early backouts or tickets plus customer rating.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Most compensation structures in general, you want to review annually."

> "I prefer to tie it to something not as precise, but more useful, which is our quarterly objectives."

> "You want a push metric and a pull metric."
