# Helping a $6M Finance Business Scale to $30M

## Summary

Alex coaches a finance business selling car loans to entrepreneurs. The founder wants to
grow from $6M to $30M but sees CPA rising from roughly 400-500 euros to 900-1,000 euros.
Alex says scaling paid acquisition usually breaks for one of three reasons, two of which
he explains here: LTV is too low for colder audiences, or creative quality/volume is too
low to expand reach. He says CAC tends to converge across similar competitors, so the back
end and creative become the arms race.

## Key claims

- On 2026-04-17, Alex says colder audiences cost more as a business leaves the honeymoon
  phase of the easiest, highest-interest targeting.
- He says the first fix is often increasing LTV so the company can afford more expensive
  acquisition.
- He says another cap is creative quality: many businesses think they capped the market
  when they actually capped the creative.
- He cites the Old Spice campaign as an example of S-tier creative broad enough to show
  to almost everyone, with self-reported market-share impact.
- He says companies spending around $100K/day may create 50-100 ad pieces per week.
- He describes a 70/20/10 creative allocation: 70% from historic top-performing hooks,
  20% adjacent remixes, and 10% wild ideas.
- He says CAC is often similar between competitors in the same market; outsized returns
  come when one business has 10x the LTV.
- He says he usually fixes a business back to front, then scales creative volume and
  iterates from the top 10%.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You get out of your honeymoon phase of the easiest targeting."

> "In reality, it's you've capped your creative."

> "The back end becomes the arms race of every business that spends money to acquire customers."

## Synthesis candidate

Synthesis candidate: paid acquisition caps from LTV or creative, not always market size;
70/20/10 creative allocation; CAC convergence and LTV as backend arms race.
