# How to Start Monetizing as a Beginner

## Summary

A monologue Alex Hormozi delivered to a Skool community (referencing the "Skool Games" and the "$100 test"/"speedrunners" group he peeks into every night), arguing that the core deficiency of most beginner entrepreneurs is prioritization, not effort. He reframes speed as distance-per-move rather than moves-per-minute (citing Peter Thiel's "A problems vs. B+ problems" and Frank Slootman's "one thing" doctrine), then prescribes a concrete beginner monetization playbook: beat obscurity by advertising more, make things valuable before scalable, and give away large amounts of unscalable one-on-one work for free to generate leads, harvest reciprocity ("blank checks"), and build frameworks/IP. He closes with the math that a beginner's time is nearly worthless until they can convert it to money, so trading free hours for leads is a winning trade.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- **Prioritization is the primary skill/deficiency.** People (including solo founders — "you're an employee of your own business") solve easy B+ problems instead of the most valuable A problems, crossing the hard items off their to-do list for momentum.
- Speed should be measured as **distance per move, not moves per minute** — "how much you get per revolution," not revolutions per minute. The fastest-moving people don't move fastest; they get more per step.
- He credits Leila and himself moving disproportionately fast not to a frantic pace but to the leverage/return on each action.
- A leader's primary job over a team is to **prioritize their to-do list and remove everything that isn't the one thing**; telling a report "do all of them" reveals the leader lacks prioritization skill.
- Invokes **Frank Slootman's "one thing" MO** (three-time IPO CEO): if you can't pick one priority you don't know how to prioritize. Accomplishing the top item re-orders (and often makes irrelevant) items 2–5, so doing them first would have been disguised procrastination.
- In the speedrunners group, **88% of participants had already made money** — presented as strong proof the system works and a shame/deprivation prod to the audience.
- If things are working, **do more of that thing**; if not, do different things.
- **The enemy is obscurity, not competition.** No one can buy from you without first knowing you exist, so most beginners simply need to advertise more / let more people know about their stuff.
- Beginner rule: **do not make things scalable before you've made them valuable.** Worrying "this won't scale" before you have customers is like refusing to lift weights for fear of getting too jacked.
- **Selling something unscalable and inherently valuable** lets you charge more, run less infrastructure, and make more per customer — then redeploy that money to build capacity when you actually need it.
- Beginner monetization play: **make your offer, then give the free version away** — offer 3–5 hours of one-on-one work for free.
- Time-value math: divide last year's income by 2,000 (waking hours: by 8,000) to get your true hourly rate. At the US median household income of $60,000 ÷ 8,000 = **~$7.50/hour**, so 5 free hours ≈ $40 of your time — and you'd gladly pay $40 to acquire a customer who buys a $1,000 offer, yet you won't spend the time equivalent.
- The market doesn't value a beginner's time because they **lack the skill to turn time into money**; giving time away is therefore a rational trade (no leads → leads → customers).
- Free-work discovery process: get permission up front to present a paid option at the end, then ask the client's **goals, current state, and obstacles** — you get paid to solve the obstacle.
- Free work lets you **"load the dice"**: solving one problem creates the next problem (fix a site's conversion → they now want traffic; build an offer → they now want help selling it). This is the **classic upsell** ("you can't have fries without ketchup").
- **Reciprocity/"blank check" mechanic:** giving someone something they accept obligates them; you later cash that on your terms for more than it cost you. Give away so much that people feel bad taking from you.
- Give away **unscalable value that visibly costs you** ("pounds of flesh") — it's almost more important that they know it cost you a lot, because that's what makes them inclined to buy.
- One-on-one work is how he started his business; it works early because you don't need bonuses/features/wording — "2011 midwit marketing stuff" only matters once your time is maxed out.
- Until you're **spending 40 hours/week promoting or delivering** (admin doesn't count), scalable concerns don't matter. Doing that customer-facing volume teaches you your customers and lets you extract repeatable **frameworks → intellectual property**.
- Service = giving people their time back: having solved a problem more times than they ever will, you deliver the result **faster, better, and easier**, which is what people pay for.
- Mindset: **hardship is the point.** Trust-fund kids are miserable ("wealth therapists"); the value is in the earning. "Hunger is the best sauce." Wish for the good fight, not for money to magically appear.
