# How To Know Which Friends to Cut

## Summary
Alex Hormozi argues that as ambition rises, the quality bar for friendship should rise too, and that better friends are busier and rarer, so having fewer is normal. He offers five questions to evaluate whether to keep or cut a friend, plus a two-part filter (same scale of goals, same dedication to them) that he says he used for his most important friend, Leila. He reframes the loneliness of ambition as definitional to being exceptional, and closes with the idea that big life decisions should be guided by the people closest to your goals rather than those closest to you.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex claims "better friends are busy friends," and that the people who were always available were the ones he did not want to become more like.
- He offers five litmus questions for keeping/cutting a friend: (1) would being told you're like them be a compliment; (2) are you truly fulfilled or just avoiding loneliness; (3) can you be unapologetically yourself; (4) do you like who they are today or only who they could become; (5) would you want your future child to be friends with someone like them.
- As of March 2026, Alex holds that rare people are by definition rare, so having one or a few good friends is normal; having many friends more likely means the friendship bar is too low.
- His two-part filter (stated as used for Leila): a friend should have (1) the same scale/level of goals (not the same goals) and (2) the same level of dedication and commitment to them — "walk the walk" and "keep up with my pace."
- As of March 2026, Alex claims ambitious people cycle through friend groups, often transitioning from a group into no friends before finding one or two people at a new level; lonely seasons are inherent to being the exception.
- He argues the ability to delay gratification is a function of intelligence, drawing an analogy to training primates: expanding the reinforcement window while still abstracting the reward back to the action is the sign of intelligence, and practicing delay increases one's rate of learning.
- As of March 2026, Alex claims you become like the people you want to impress, not the people you admire.
- He cites a Harvard study (unverified as summarized here) on "reference group" as the best predictor of long-term material success, and argues it has been miscast as "the five people you spend the most time with" when it is actually the five people you compare yourself to.
- Alex advises that for big life decisions you should listen to the people closest to your goals, not the people closest to you — and that these "heroes" may just be people you follow on the internet, whose decision-making calculus you transpose onto your life.
