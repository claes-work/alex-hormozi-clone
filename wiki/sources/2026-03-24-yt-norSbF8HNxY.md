# Helping a Coaching Business Scale to $5M/Year

## Summary
In a live Q&A, a coaching-business owner (unnamed) tells Alex Hormozi his company does over $1M/year in revenue and, "being pessimistic," aims for $5M in the next 12 months, with people/hiring as the main constraint. Hormozi reframes the classic build-vs-buy dilemma — raise money and pay cash vs. give equity — as a single math equation comparing the cost of equity given to employees against the cost of equity sold to investors to fund cash comp. A second questioner asks what "winning" means to him; Hormozi answers that it is simply "the game," and describes his three-part definition of a good day.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the decision to fund hiring via investor capital vs. employee equity as a math equation: compare the equity cost of giving equity to an employee (the delta between full-cash comp and cash-plus-equity comp) against the equity cost of selling that same stake to an investor to raise the cash needed to pay full cash comp.
- As of March 2026, Alex's illustrative example: raising at a $100M valuation and selling 5% yields $5M cash; the question becomes whether that $5M can pay everyone in cash for a net loss of only 5% of the business — versus whether you could instead get everyone you need for less than 5% in equity directly, skipping the raise.
- As of March 2026, Alex says his definition of "winning" is simply "the game" — he enjoys playing the game itself, and that is the win.
- As of March 2026, Alex restates his optimization process: do a lot of volume of whatever it is (ads, sales calls, content), look back at the last hundred or thousand units of output, isolate what the top 10% have that the other 90% don't, and do only those things in the next batch — repeating that loop across everything.
- As of March 2026, Alex defines a good day as three things: working out with someone he likes, eating food with people he likes, and writing something; most of his life is trying to repeat that day as many days in a row as possible.
- Alex recounts (biographical, self-reported) that at age 21 he worked a job he hated, and his boss told him after a good weekend, "the key to happiness is living as many days in a row like that as you can" — which he found profound because it made happiness operationalizable.
- Caller claim (unverified): his coaching business does over $1M/year in revenue and targets $5M in the next 12 months, citing "people" as the main bottleneck and noting he has investors happy to provide capital.
- Alex promotes the free "$100M Scaling Roadmap" (acquisition.com/roadmap): a 10-stage diagnostic quiz built from ~200 hours reviewing portfolio companies, mapping what to do across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, and finance.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "There's actually a pretty simple math equation for this, though."

> "It's what's the equity cost if I give it to the employee ... the delta between what their compensation would be at full cash comp versus cash plus equity, compared to that cost of equity if I were to sell it to an investor to cover the cash."

> "If you raise it a hundred million dollars and you sell 5% of the business and you get five million dollars in cash, can I use that five million dollars in cash to pay everyone cash compensation and net only a loss of 5% of the business? ... Can I get everybody I need for less than 5%? ... So, it's a math equation."

> "You're pessimistic, so you're saying only a 5x in a year. Cool. Got it."

> "What is win for you? ... The game. I mean, I can tell you that now. It's the game. I enjoy playing the game."

> "I do a lot of volume of whatever it is. ... And then I look at the past hundred or thousand units of effort or output and I say, 'What do the top 10% have that's different than the other 90? Can I isolate that?' And the next hundred, I just want to do the things that I have there. And I just continue to repeat that process in everything."

> "I'm pretty sure the key to happiness is living as many days in a row like that as you can."

> "For me, a good day has three things. I work out with someone that I like. I eat food with people I like. And I write something. If I have those three things, I had a good day."
