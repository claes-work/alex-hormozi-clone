# Watch these 55 minutes if you want to be a millionaire in 2022

> Source: YouTube long-form (~55 min), published 2021-12-31 on @AlexHormozi. The video re-airs a

## Summary

A flagship year-end 2021 keynote in which Hormozi lays out his core thesis that **time allocation
is the only thing that matters for material success**, and that **leverage is the multiplier on
time**. He organizes the talk around four frameworks he says he uses to evaluate and scale both his
own and portfolio companies: (1) scale the entrepreneur, (2) scale the market, (3) scale the
deliverable, and (4) scale the business. Throughout he uses his own trajectory — gym owner → gym
licensor (Gym Launch) → supplements (Prestige Labs) → software (ALAN) → Acquisition.com — as the
worked example, arguing each jump came from adding a new form of leverage. He states Acquisition.com
is a portfolio doing ~$85M/year, that he and Leila exited two companies in 2021 and sold their house
and car, and that he is in a "consolidation" season distilling his learnings into "artifacts"
(frameworks) before his next chapter. The talk doubles as promotion for his book (then 99 cents) and
his free acquisition.com course.

## Key principles

### Premise: time allocation and leverage
- **Time allocation is the only thing that matters** for material success (explicitly not happiness).
  Master time and you master material success.
- **Money is a denomination of time.** Every transaction buys some percentage of your life; wealth is
  a measure of how little of your life you must trade for what you desire. Money is "an IOU from
  society for future goods and services."
- **Leverage is the key multiplier on time.** The size of opportunities you pursue is directly
  proportional to the amount of leverage you can employ. "Rich dads" steer their kids toward
  high-leverage vehicles (real estate, funds, acquisitions, business ownership); "poor dads" steer
  toward low-leverage ones. Most people fail from ignorance of leverage, not lack of desire.
- **Speed comes from fewer wrong turns, not more activity.** Most entrepreneurs are "micro fast,
  macro slow" (fast bursts, constant direction changes); the goal is "micro slow, macro fast"
  (consistent forward motion, no detours).
- **The 1% of decisions** (borrowed from Naval Ravikant): only ~1% of decisions matter; the
  difficulty is identifying which. The four frameworks exist to shape those 1% strategic decisions.
- **The opportunity equation:** fortunes = Entrepreneur × Market × Deliverable (delivery vehicle) ×
  Business acumen. "Opportunity" itself is a function of Market × scalability of the Deliverable.

### Framework 1 — Scale the entrepreneur (skills, traits, beliefs)
- Every business is bottlenecked by the entrepreneur in one of three ways: a missing **skill**, a
  missing **character trait**, or a missing **belief**. Visualized as a ladder — the lowest missing
  rung caps you regardless of the others (the theory of constraints).
- All three (skills, traits, beliefs) exist on a **continuum, not a binary** — not "can you manage a
  sales team" but "how well."
- Skills, traits, and beliefs all **compound**, producing outsized "overnight" returns.
- **Skill stacking:** stacked skills create asymmetric returns (bookkeeper → accountant → controller
  → CFO → rainmaker). His own stack: fitness → nutrition → sales → local marketing → operations →
  scaling sales teams → national B2B marketing.
- Learning skills comes from **repetition and feedback (volume)** — one reason he wants to "make
  active income cool again."
- **"Your work works on you more than you work on it"** — the work builds character/capacity.

### Framework 2 — Scale the market
- The single biggest lever separating peers doing more revenue: they **picked better markets**
  (typically mass-market, expensive product, world-targetable).
- **Four attributes of a good market:** (1) pain — they desperately need it, (2) purchasing power —
  they can afford it, (3) easy to target, (4) growing (ride the tailwind).
- **The "starving crowd" (hot dog stand):** the most valuable competitive advantage is a starving
  crowd, not location, ingredients, taste, or marketing.
- VC saying he cites: "Great entrepreneur, poor market — market wins. Poor entrepreneur, great
  market — market wins."
- **Five ways to scale a market:** up-market, down-market, adjacent, broader, or deeper. Going
  **deeper** in the existing market (buy competitors, add channels, raise ad budget) is his favorite.

### Framework 3 — Scale the deliverable (leverage + delivery)
- **Four C's / forms of leverage** (partly from Naval): **Labor → Capital → Code → Content/Media.**
  Labor and Capital require permission; Code and Media are **permissionless** and zero-cost to
  replicate — "that's where the new fortunes are." Top operators (Bezos, Zuckerberg, Dorsey) use all
  four.
- His income made a ~10x jump at each leverage change: employee (labor for others) → trainer
  (self-employed) → gym owner (uses labor) → licensor (labor + media, ~$1M/month income) → adds
  capital (heading toward 8 figures/month) → full stack (9 figures/month, undefined).
- **The delivery cube** — six dials to change value/margin: (1) 1-on-1 vs small group vs one-to-many,
  (2) DIY vs done-with-you vs done-for-you, (3) level of support, (4) consumption format
  (written/audio/video/live), (5) speed & convenience, (6) three thought experiments: 10x the price
  and ask what you'd deliver; deliver more value at 1/10 the price with zero-replication-cost adds;
  and "if the only new customers came from existing-customer referrals, how different would your
  experience be?"
- **"Everything is for sale" — the base units you can sell:** products, services, access, media,
  risk, and money. Each has a physical and a digital form. Complex offers (experiences, businesses,
  titles, IP) are just combinations of these base chunks.

### Framework 4 — Scale the business (business acumen by revenue stage)
- **$0–1M:** one product, one avatar, one channel. Problem: you don't yet know what you're doing;
  objective: consistently sell something people actually want. Stop running multiple businesses under
  7 figures.
- **$1–10M:** objective is to increase **lifetime gross profit per customer** (he prefers gross
  margin over "LTV"); add higher-leverage deliverables.
- **$10–50M:** consistency of delivery drops; you must **professionalize** (taxes, payroll, tracking,
  finance) and hire experienced high-level operators.
- **Framework X — attract talent:** always active. You must become the entrepreneur each level
  requires; growth needs other "stallions" running their own P&Ls (a big company is many businesses
  "globbed together," like Amazon).
- **Six business functions, external mirrored by internal:** external = lead gen, lead nurture,
  sales, customer success, ascension, resell. The internal HR/hiring funnel mirrors it (applications
  → booked calls → interviews → hires → onboarding → training → ascension → retention). Illustrated
  with the "10 BDRs" story: the real bottleneck to hiring wasn't sales math but HR not running enough
  ads to generate interviews — "you're one amazing hire away from all the growth you ever wanted."
