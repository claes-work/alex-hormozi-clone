# Helping an Online Coach Get to $1M/Month

## Summary
Live coaching clip. A creator serving overseas Chinese entrepreneurs (Skool community; ~20k book-club subscribers; founding-member lifetime deal at $1,000, later $1,000/month; ~$75k so far) wants to reach $1M/month. Alex delivers his core **recurring-revenue-for-education** framework: the big mistake in education businesses is treating something with *high one-time value* as if it's *consumable*. "The day before you know how to advertise, advertising is unbelievably valuable. The day after… why would I pay for something I already know how to do? It goes from huge to zero." To make recurring revenue actually recur, you must create/demonstrate **consumable** value. His model: inputs → black box → output(money); the *one-time* teaching (the box) is very valuable but you can only charge for it once, so **sell the inputs to the machine** on a recurring basis. Examples: a low-churn 3D-printing Skool that uploads fresh trending blueprints weekly; a wholesaling community delivering pre-vetted weekly deals; news subscriptions; and **Gym Launch itself** — they pre-filmed/tested ads and handed gym owners only the winners each month, which gave it "super high margins" and stickiness because it was functionally *media*, not education. He notes a big one-time-vs-recurring price discrepancy is fine if the recurring value stays ~3–4x the price, and that added members should *add* value (network effect), not dilute it. On the second question (becoming his "global Chinese partner"), Alex's answer: **credibility/proof** — "the messenger is 80 or 90% of the context within which the message is received"; authority is compelling because it decreases risk and cognitive load (Buffett, Elon examples); he'd only partner with someone "bulletproof from a proof perspective." Ends with the roadmap promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex's central education-business claim: education has "super high one-time value" but people wrongly "build for it like it's consumable" — value goes "from huge to zero" the moment the buyer learns the thing.
- As of March 2026, to make recurring revenue recur you must "create or at least demonstrate consumable value"; news is the archetype (last week's news is worthless, this week's isn't → subscriptions work).
- As of March 2026, his model: inputs → black box → output (money). The one-time teaching (the box) is very valuable but chargeable once; instead **sell the inputs to the machine** on a recurring basis.
- As of March 2026 (examples): a low-churn 3D-printing Skool uploading fresh *trending* blueprints weekly; a real-estate wholesaling community delivering pre-vetted weekly deals; **Gym Launch** pre-filming/testing ads monthly and handing owners only the winners — "media functionally rather than education," which drove its high margins and stickiness ("what do you need next month? More winning ads").
- As of March 2026, "niche-down teaching is so valuable because you can actually create the machine and then sell the inputs" — you can't test/produce inputs for 100 different businesses, so a narrow beginner niche lets you sell inputs; for medium/large owners the recurring value is instead network, access, and group-negotiated discounts (valued less than the one-time value that makes them money).
- As of March 2026, pricing guidance: a big gap between the one-time charge and the small recurring fee is fine "provided it's sticky" — a $10k up-front then $500/month works if ongoing value exceeds the $500 "usually by three or four X."
- As of March 2026, the ideal community design is a **network effect**: "each additional member does not detract or dilute the value… but actually adds value."
- As of March 2026 (on partnering / authority): "the messenger is 80 or 90% of the context within which the message is received"; the Acquisition brand "was built off of a $100 [million] network prior to this" — proof is "the backdrop that everything stands on."
- As of March 2026, why authority is compelling: it "actually decreases risk" and cognitive load (following Buffett on investing is efficient); high-agency people *should* separate messenger from message, "but no one does" — quoting Napoleon, "I'd rather have lucky generals."
