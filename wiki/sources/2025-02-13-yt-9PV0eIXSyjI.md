# How to Make Money from an Audience: Strategies for 2025

## Summary

Alex Hormozi outlines the four ways to monetize an audience — affiliates, sponsorships, partnerships (equity), and starting your own brand — and places them on a continuum of difficulty, risk, and exit value. He explains how compensation, leverage, and sellability differ across the four, and how deals can blend elements from each. He frames the whole talk as a shared "construct" for both sides of a deal: the influencer with eyeballs and the business owner who wants to buy, license, or rent that audience.

## Key claims (audience-monetization strategies)

- There are **four ways to monetize an audience**, sitting on a continuum of increasing difficulty, risk, and exit value: (1) affiliates, (2) sponsorships/endorsements, (3) partnerships (usually equity), (4) starting your own brand.
- The behaviors across affiliates, sponsorships, and partnerships are more or less the same; the difference is **how you get compensated**.
- **Affiliates**: you get paid *after* you make a sale. Fastest way to make money immediately, low-risk for both parties, no cap on upside (lets you prove your audience is a good fit). Programs usually have predetermined, standard terms you can sign up for automatically.
- Affiliate revenue is **not typically valuable at exit** — its value is a multiple of the cash flow, discounted by how reliable/trustworthy the underlying business is (affiliate of the US government = sound; affiliate of an arbitrage info product that vanishes tomorrow = near-zero value).
- **Sponsorships**: the business pays you *first* to advertise; you're a traffic source paid on CPMs (cost per impressions). Two forms: (a) posting to your own audience, (b) **whitelisting** — the business runs your posts as their own ads to audiences far beyond yours.
- Hormozi likes whitelisting as a win-win: the business spends money advertising your brand (grows you), and gets higher conversion because you're better on camera and have a warm, recognizable audience.
- Sponsorships are **harder to come by** than affiliate deals (more à la carte) and hinge on the **leverage balance** between influencer size and company size (huge influencer + tiny company = great deal; small influencer + Nike = maybe no deal).
- Sponsorship revenue only becomes exit-valuable if you build a **true media company** (an army of influencers under umbrella deals — similar to a talent agency); a personal brand's sponsorship revenue is very hard to sell.
- **Partnerships**: you take **equity** in a business that already has product and infrastructure. Can be minority or majority (0.1% up to 99%), fully negotiable; often structured as performance-based equity (e.g., 1% per 1,000 customers brought, capped at 10%).
- When taking equity, get **something upfront** no matter what, because the association you make with the brand is **permanent** — you transfer your goodwill the moment you publicly declare it.
- Deals commonly include a **time/vesting** component (e.g., 25% upfront, 25% on performance, 50% vesting over ~4 years) so the business gets sustained promotion, with forfeiture on a breakup.
- Define deliverables **up front** using the W's (who/what/where/when/why/how): how often to post, what format (e.g., links in bio one week/month, one long-form mention, five short-form posts/stories, monthly ads for whitelisting). Vague "I will promote the business" doesn't work well.
- The four options are **not mutually exclusive** — a partner can still earn affiliate revenue and sponsorship cash simultaneously.
- Personal-finance rule of thumb: the **more money you already have, the more you should lean toward equity** over sponsorship cash — taking cash comes "at the expense of equity"; if you believe in the product and a future exit, delaying for equity makes sense but carries more risk, so demand a disproportionate reward (stock/options).
- **Starting your own brand** (highest risk, you start with 100% equity): two routes — **white label** (put your logo on an existing manufacturer's product; faster, cheaper) or **custom formulations** (custom product/materials/cuts no one else has; costs more).
- Hormozi personally prefers **custom over white label** because he doesn't want anyone able to sell an identical product and hold pricing power over him; a generic slap-a-label product "is not a good look" — though he's clear there's nothing *wrong* with white label.
- Framework for navigating any deal: balance **exit value, cash in/out, amount of work, and risk exposure** to find the right point on the continuum for both the person and the product.

### Hormozi's own track record cited (self-reported)

- **Prestige Labs** (2nd company): built to 5,000+ affiliates generating $20M+/year; still operating.
- **Allan / ALAN** (software, 3rd big company): 1,000+ affiliates who were agencies white-labeling the software.
- **$100M Leads** book launch: 30,000 affiliates promoted the launch.
- Has also invested in companies he personally endorsed.
