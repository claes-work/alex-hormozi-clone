## Summary

A short mentorship-style Q&A clip. An audience member says they have a patent
that will be pending in eight weeks and asks how to prepare (they've already been
sourcing the cheapest manufacturer). Hormozi responds with a "good news / bad
news" answer whose core message is that a patent, by itself, is close to
worthless: patents and IP are only defensible in practice if you have a
dedicated legal budget to enforce them. He illustrates with his own operation —
a five-person full-time in-house team whose only job is takedowns of
impersonators, scammers, and people reselling his material. He reframes patents
as a *later-stage* concern: you still have to build the business, market the
product, and make money first; duplicates ("dupes") only start flowing once
you're successful, and *that* is the point at which you allocate capital to
defend the IP. The clip cuts off mid-sentence ("...the patent means").

## Key claims

_All dated 2026-02-21, paraphrased (Alex Hormozi), unless quoted below._

- A patent doesn't mean much unless you have a legal budget to defend it. In the
  eyes of the law, IP/patents you do not actively defend are "not that
  defensible."
- Enforcement is an ongoing operational cost, not a one-time filing. Hormozi runs
  a **five-person full-time in-house legal team** whose sole job is takedowns —
  removing 50 to 200+ infringing accounts per week (impersonators, scam sellers,
  people reselling his material), getting them delisted, ad accounts shut down,
  removed from social media, and Shopify pages taken down.
- Business is "much more ruthless and cutthroat than you think." People will
  simply steal your work and dare you to do something about it — and proving
  infringement is a long, expensive legal process (how close is the copy? is it
  fair competition?).
- A patent guarantees nothing. You still have to market the product; success is
  not conferred by the patent.
- For a patent to matter, it has to protect something **truly core to the product
  that no one else can reproduce around** — otherwise competitors design around it.
- Sequence: first build the business and make it profitable; the duplicates start
  flowing *after* you become successful; only then do you allocate capital to
  defend the IP.
- Practical stance: until the business itself is successful, the patent
  effectively means nothing (sentence cut off at the end of the clip).
