# Alex Hormozi Explains Why You're Not Successful Yet

## Summary

In this interview clip, Alex Hormozi unpacks his mental frameworks for enduring the difficulty of building a business and a life. He traces his signature line "this is what hard feels like" to his time as a fraternity president, where resetting pledges' expectations to match reality made their suffering evaporate. He then covers reframing negative feedback (bringing self-doubt "from the shadows into the light"), the difference between measurable burnout and self-inflicted emotional burnout, and his belief that radical self-blame ("my fault") is the first lesson of escaping poverty because "power follows the blame finger."

## Key claims

- Hardship feels bad because expectations don't match reality; once you accept "this is what hard feels like," you get "permission to feel shitty," which paradoxically makes the bad feeling stop.
- "This is where most people stop and this is why they don't win" — most quitting happens at the point of maximum difficulty, which sets the competitive bar very low for anyone who persists.
- Statistic he cites often: ~90% of podcasts don't make it past episode 3, and of the 10% that do, ~90% don't make it past episode 20 — so making 21 episodes puts you in the top percentile of podcasters ever. He calls this not even "hard," just "consistent."
- There is a difference between real burnout (measurable: output per unit of time decreases) and emotional burnout, which he argues people mislabel — it's usually a failure to reframe reality (e.g. fixating on one negative comment).
- His reframing method (crediting Byron Katie's "The Work" and a Tom Bilyeu clip): name the fear out loud, ask "is it true?", then confront "what if they're right?" — following the logic down to "base," where all that remains is your next action.
- "Power follows the blame finger": whoever you blame holds power over your outcomes; taking ownership ("my fault") reclaims that power. He frames this as the first lesson of leaving poverty.
- Acknowledges the risk of being "so good at dealing with suffering" that you push past your limit; the "art of not burning out" is easing off the gas (sauna, sunshine, food) before you break.
- On disadvantage: people who suffered real inequality/abuse would be "completely justified" in underachieving — but they also have the opportunity to be a local "point of proof" that it can be overcome, because someone has always "had it worse and done it better."
- A personal reframe from early in his career: he didn't think he was a good person, and found relief in realizing "I can still do the things that create success and not deserve it and still get it."
