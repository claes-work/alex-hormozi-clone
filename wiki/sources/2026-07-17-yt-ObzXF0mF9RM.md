# "Everyone Wants the Guy on the Door. Now What?"

## Summary

A live coaching Q&A. The questioner introduces himself as **Austin Dunbar**, owner of
**Durham**, a design studio in northern Kentucky just outside Cincinnati doing **~$3.5M/yr**
and stuck in a "$3 to $4 million glass ceiling"; he wants to know how to break to $10–15M.
His self-description: he is "Mr. Bottleneck" — he built the company 12 years ago and clients
want to talk to *him*, "like running a dentist's office — they want to see the dentist whose
name's on the door." The business is (his words) "a thousand percent word of mouth," selling
brand identity, motion/video storytelling and naming to enterprise/Fortune-500 logos (Old
Spice, TaylorMade, Bud Light), with clients retained 10–11 years — but budgets are shrinking
("we're in the business of nice-to-haves, not necessities; the first thing that gets killed
if your stock's down 25% is marketing"), AI has the industry "freaking out," and he has had
acquisition inquiries. He also owns a Kentucky distillery (co-owner), a solar battery
company in Missouri, and real estate — and is CEO of the rest. His question: ride the horse,
get more horses, or build a sales model that runs without the owner?

Alex's diagnosis starts the moment the portfolio comes up: "Or are you the CEO? … Yeah,
that's the issue." He contrasts his own setup — effectively **zero direct reports** ("that's
not true — Leila's father is technically my direct report, he manages my books… we meet once
a month") — then runs the answer Socratically ("You appear to be a bright guy. What do you
think I'm going to say?"), letting Austin self-prescribe a founder-independent sales model
before reframing the real problem as **attention, not sales**. The argument: (1) Austin has
"special sauce" spread over several ventures, so everything grows a little but nothing
grows enough; he has been *reinforced* to believe making more money means doing something
new rather than **doing more of the thing**. (2) Even passive ownership taxes cognition —
the "shower time" effect: one call or text and the next hour and a half of thinking cycles
belongs to another business (same bandwidth concept as the legal-battle version in
[[2026-03-04-yt-HVHeZN2p07o]]). (3) Alex is "a big believer in **thinking surface area**":
solving problems elegantly is painting a masterpiece in multiple coats, which requires
uninterrupted attention. (4) The blunt read: Austin has "gotten away with" running multiple
companies because he's competent, but he's making significantly less than he should because
he hasn't had the discipline — there's a day you start a business and a day you **go pro**,
and Austin is "under his ability." Rome analogy: "Rome wasn't beat head-on, it was beat by
barbarians nibbling at the edges — you can still be Rome, you can still lose."
Prescription: **recoup 100% of attention, then go all-in on the marketing business** (the
one he likes most / highest return on attention). On the AI fear, Alex flips it into
opportunity, citing (attribution his own, "I can't remember who said this from A16Z") the
line that the next trillion-dollar company is a **technology business masquerading as a
service business**: agencies were traditionally terrible businesses, but the founder who
uses AI instead of fearing it should be the one *they* fear. More "thinking revolutions"
would "put the fear in them." Standard acquisition.com/roadmap free-gift outro (10 stages,
quiz, functions across product, marketing, sales, customer success, recruiting, IT, HR,
finance; thank-you page books a call, in-person Vegas session if they can help).

## Key claims
_All dated 2026-07-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Alex-attributed unless marked._

- **Being CEO of multiple companies at once is "the issue."** When Austin confirms he's CEO
  of the agency, the solar company and the rest, Alex's diagnosis is immediate: that
  structure is the problem, and "that's tough."
- **Bio/structure fact: Alex runs with effectively zero direct reports.** He corrects
  himself to exactly one — Leila's father, who "technically" reports to him and manages his
  books, meeting once a month — and contrasts that deliberately minimal structure with
  Austin's CEO-of-everything setup. (Refines, consistent with, the "deliberately no direct
  reports" information-flow setup described in [[2026-02-14-yt-O4xXOK4MQ24]].)
- **Spreading "special sauce" across ventures makes everything grow a little and nothing
  grow enough.** The founder's mojo is a finite input; distributed over several companies,
  each grows "a little bit but not as much as you want."
- **The "do something new" reflex is a reinforcement error.** Austin has been "reinforced…
  that in order to make more money you do something new rather than doing more of the
  thing" — and unwinding the resulting portfolio is harder than never building it.
- **Attack the problem by regaining 100% of attention first, then deploying it into the
  highest-return-on-attention thing.** Sequence matters: recoup attention *before*
  reallocating it; for Austin that means going all-in on the marketing business, the one
  he likes most.
- **Passive ownership still costs thinking cycles ("shower time").** Even with a team in
  place and "not really doing that much," one call or text hijacks the next ninety minutes
  of thought. The cost of a side venture is cognitive, not just calendar time. (Same
  bandwidth concept, applied to litigation, in [[2026-03-04-yt-HVHeZN2p07o]].)
- **"Thinking surface area": elegant problem-solving needs uninterrupted attention across
  multiple passes.** Alex's stated mental model — a masterpiece isn't painted in one
  sitting; you need multiple coats of paint to solve problems elegantly and well, which
  fragmented attention makes impossible.
- **Competence lets you get away with lack of focus — and hides the money you're leaving on
  the table.** Austin has gotten away with running multiple companies because he's
  competent, but is "making significantly less money than [he] should" for lack of
  discipline.
- **Everyone has a day they start a business and a day they "go pro."** Going all-in means
  different things to different people; some can half-ass to $10M/yr, and Alex knows people
  who half-ass to $100M/yr — it's about competence. The diagnostic: Austin is "under his
  ability," and Alex bets he feels the discrepancy between his numbers and his capability.
- **Rome analogy for founder attention: you lose by nibbling, not head-on.** "Rome wasn't
  beat head-on. It was beat by barbarians nibbling at the edges… You can still be Rome, you
  can still lose." Many small attention leaks, not one big competitor, kill the winner.
- **Agencies become good businesses when rebuilt as technology businesses.** Quoting an
  A16Z line he can't source exactly: the next trillion-dollar company is "a technology
  business masquerading as service business." Agencies were "traditionally very terrible
  businesses to run," but the AI transition changes that for whoever leads it.
- **Flip the AI fear: be the one they're afraid of.** Rather than fear AI cannibalizing the
  agency industry: "Let everybody else be afraid of it. I'm using it. They should be afraid
  of me." AI is "just a tool, like anything else"; more "thinking revolutions" put the fear
  in competitors — potential Austin has "if you decide to do it."
- **"How long do you ride the horse?" — "As long as it's running."** Alex's one-line answer
  to the keep-vs-sell lifestyle-business question.
- _(Outro, promotional)_ The "$100M scaling roadmap" free gift at acquisition.com/roadmap:
  ~200 hours reviewing portfolio companies distilled into 10 growth stages with a quiz that
  locates a business and prescribes actions per function (product, marketing, sales,
  customer success, recruiting, IT, HR, finance); thank-you page books a call, with an
  in-person Vegas session if they can help.
