# The #1 Way To Increase Profits

## Summary

Hormozi explains **arbitrage** as the underlying mechanic of all business: you buy in
one market cheap and sell in another market dear, and the size of that spread dictates
how much you make. He layers on the economics distinction between accounting profit and
economic profit (opportunity cost), then draws his central pricing conclusion: what you
charge should be tied to what the market will bear, never to what the thing costs you.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2020-12-10)

- Arbitrage = buying in one market at one cost and selling in another market at a higher
  cost, exploiting a marketplace inefficiency. Every business — even a service business
  buying labor and reselling it — is fundamentally an arbitrage. (paraphrase)
- How much a business makes is dictated by how big the arbitrage spread is. (paraphrase)
- A supply chain is just a series of markets buying-and-selling in a row; the business
  owner in the middle is arbitraging all of them. (paraphrase)
- **Accounting profit** = revenue minus all costs. **Economic profit** = revenue minus
  explicit costs minus the *opportunity cost* (implicit cost) of using your resources on
  this path instead of a better one. He uses a paper-mill/forest example (paper vs.
  selling timber/sawdust/wood). (paraphrase)
- As an entrepreneur/investor he evaluates which arbitrage opportunity best fits his
  existing resources (people, equipment, skills, software, team); answering that also
  answers "what can we be the best in the world at." Growing markets (e.g. online
  fitness) offer a greater degree of arbitrage because of rising demand. (paraphrase)
- **Proto-pricing-psychology / value-equation seed:** price should have *no relationship*
  to your cost and should be tied only to what the market will bear. Big businesses run
  on extreme arbitrage (cites iPhone ~$17 to make, Facebook selling eyeballs, Verizon
  adding a user for pennies). He notes he once had "weird morals and ethics" about how
  much he could charge and now rejects that limit. (paraphrase)
- Selling relies on an information advantage — the seller typically holds it over the
  buyer. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "All businesses arbitrage… and how much money you make and the extent to which you
> crush it will be dictated by how big the arbitrage is."

> "That price that you charge should never be in any way related to what it costs you

> "For a very long time when I was earlier on in my career, I had these weird morals and
