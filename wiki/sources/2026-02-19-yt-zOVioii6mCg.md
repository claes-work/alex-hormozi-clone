# How to Create "Good" Content

## Summary

In this short solo clip, Alex Hormozi gives his working definition of what makes content — and education more broadly — "good." His central claim: "good" is not an outcome you can directly aim at; it is achieved *tactically* by systematically removing everything that sucks for the user. Every piece of content has an implicit promise (make money, find love, lose weight), and anything that gets in the way of delivering that promise is what you work on. The theoretical ideal ("nirvana") is a product so frictionless the user "hits a button" and gets the outcome; the closer you get to that, the better the product. The clip closes with an ad read for his free acquisition.com/roadmap resource (a 10-stage zero-to-$100M+ roadmap).

## Key claims (2026-02-19, paraphrased)

- ★ Making something "good" is not a direct outcome you can will into being; it is *tactical* — you make content good by systematically **removing the things that suck** for the user, and not stopping.
- Almost all creators have some **promise** (people want to make money, find love, lose weight); everything that gets in the way of that promise is what you have to work on, and fixing it is what makes content good.
- The "perfect nirvana" of a product is frictionlessness: the user "hits a button" and gets the outcome (loses weight / makes money / finds love). The closer you get to that, the better the product is.
- This subtraction principle applies to education in general, not just content — good education is the same removal-of-friction process.
- (Ad read) His acquisition.com/roadmap is a free 10-stage roadmap from zero to $100M+ that fewer than 1% of companies finish; he claims to have done it multiple times, broken down by eight business functions with the constraint's symptoms and the steps taken to graduate each stage, tested across software, physical products, services, and brick-and-mortar.
