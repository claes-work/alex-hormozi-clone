# How To Know If Your Business Is Actually Unscalable or Just Hard

## Summary
In a coaching exchange, a faceless short-form product-review creator (about 120 million views/month, 75% of revenue from brand sponsorships, ~50 products reviewed/month) tells Alex Hormozi his model feels unscalable and unfulfilling and asks for a roadmap. Hormozi reframes "not scalable" as a common disguise for "it's working but it's hard, so I want something easy," and argues the creator's own model is highly viable. He presses on why the creator can't simply do more of what already works (review 500 products instead of 50 at 80% margins), and introduces the idea of pricing "the hard" against the enterprise value it unlocks.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that when founders say a working business "isn't scalable," they usually mean it has become hard and they want an easy alternative instead — a reframe he says he hears "all the time."
- As of March 2026, Alex maintains you should confront the problem rather than switch to a new business to escape it.
- As of March 2026, Alex frames hard decisions by pricing them against the enterprise value they unlock: a hire adding $2M/year in profit at a 7x valuation creates ~$14M in tax-free enterprise value, which reframes a $500K salary or a difficult recruit as clearly worth it.
- As of March 2026, Alex cites a portfolio company where adding a tech/developer component could move valuation from ~$80M to the $200Ms — i.e., roughly $150M of value for doing the hard work of building a team, which he calls "hard and worth it."
- As of March 2026, Alex's advice to the creator is that a working, high-margin, faceless, formulaic model is not inherently unscalable: if 50 faceless reviews/month works at 80% margins, do 500 — "let's do more of that."

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I hear that sentence all the time. I have this business. It's working. I don't think it's scalable. I should do a different business."
