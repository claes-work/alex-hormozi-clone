# You Need To Focus

## Summary
In this live coaching exchange, a caller (Alex Rodriguez, a music/entertainment attorney from Puerto Rico who runs three businesses: a law firm ~$100k, an education platform ~$200k stuck for two years, and a contract-automation software product) asks Hormozi where he should focus. Hormozi diagnoses a classic split-avatar problem and recommends niching down. Against his usual preference, he advises going down-market toward the numerous, easier-to-acquire prosumer musicians rather than chasing enterprise labels, because the caller's ~40% one-year retention signals product-market fit. His core prescription: pour all profit into raising month-12 software retention and use the education side to fund customer acquisition.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the caller's core problem as having two very different avatars, arguing that pursuing either direction ostracizes the other; the fix is to niche down because unqualified leads are too expensive and cap advertising ability.
- As of March 2026, Alex says he *usually* advises going up-market ("sell to the rich, rich people pay better"), but in this case recommends going down-market to the smaller, more numerous customer.
- As of March 2026, Alex treats ~40% retention at the 12-month mark in a consumer/prosumer product as compelling evidence of product-market fit, citing best-in-class as 60%+ annual retention for that category.
- As of March 2026, Alex argues that getting customers is not hard; keeping them is hard, so the entire focus should collapse to one number: month-12 (M12) revenue retention, moving it from 40% toward 60% then 70%.
- As of March 2026, Alex says enterprise is not inherently more valuable than lower-market; it just tends to be stickier, which is what makes it more valuable. A large, easy-to-acquire market that also sticks is "a gold mine."
- As of March 2026, Alex recommends using the education/media arm as a liquidation mechanism to offset CAC (cost of acquiring a customer) so paid acquisition into the software cash-flows.
- As of March 2026, Alex notes that raising money in software exists precisely so a company can focus on one customer and build the product without split attention.
- As of March 2026, Alex second-guesses targeting the large legacy music companies, predicting they will struggle to pivot with AI over the coming years.
- Caller claims (unverified): the law firm grows ~20% year-over-year passively; the software has ~100 signups with ~40 active in year one; only ~4 active users pay ~$3,000/year while the other ~36 pay ~$600/year; education customers who stop education keep the software because it is effectively a one-time purchase.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Here's a very classic problem, which is that you're good at doing something and you have two very different avatars, and if you continue down either direction, you ostracize the other."

> "Getting those customers is not hard. Keeping them is hard. But if he can keep them, then he can probably blow the doors off this thing."

> "Enterprise in and of itself is not more valuable than lower market. It's just it tends to be stickier, which is what makes it more valuable. But if you can get a larger marketplace, easier-to-acquire customer to stick as well as a large enterprise customer, you've got a gold mine if that's true."

> "We don't care at all about the education. All of your focus, all the profit goes into just fixing one number, which is that you need to look at M12, so month 12 retention."

> "That's fundamentally the beauty of software once you get it right, is that it just keeps growing."

> "This is fundamentally why people raise money in software, so they can just focus on one customer the whole time, build the product, and then actually get it to work."
