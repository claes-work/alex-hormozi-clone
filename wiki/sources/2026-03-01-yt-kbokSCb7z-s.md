# It Won't Get Harder — The Start Is the Hardest Part

## Summary
Short solo motivational clip aimed at anyone in the "hard period" or the "start period" of building something. Alex's thesis: **the beginning is the hardest part, and it won't get harder** — later dragons are bigger, but by then you are far more equipped to slay them and you have allies, an audience, and resources behind you. In the beginning "it's just you with a stick against a bear," and that fight is arguably harder to win than beating a dragon "when you have a nuclear bomb and six nations behind you." The difficulty of the start is not just the size of the hardship but the near-total lack of resources: no leverage, no money to pay others, so you burn the one thing you have — **time**. He stresses that the core things that matter can't be outsourced: "no one can work out for you… no one can learn skills for you," which is why the early days feel so lonely ("you look around to see who can help you and then you're like… it's me again"). He reframes early struggle as compounding investment: like *Slumdog Millionaire* (12 seemingly meaningless life experiences that later answer every quiz question) or Steve Jobs' calligraphy class becoming Apple's fonts, the skills and context earned "in the weeds" become **arrows in the quiver** for slaying future, bigger dragons. Closing line ties it to expectation management: "expecting it to be easy is what makes it much harder than it ever is." Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap ($100M Scaling Roadmap) promo.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex frames the start of any hard endeavor as **the hardest part** — "it won't get harder"; if you make it through the beginning, "everything else is downhill."
- As of March 2026, he clarifies this is not because future challenges shrink — the "dragons" get bigger — but because you become far more equipped to face them and accumulate allies, an audience, and resources ("people in the stands cheering for you").
- As of March 2026, the beginning is uniquely hard because of scarce resources, not just hardship size: no leverage, no money to pay others, so you "burn the one thing you have, which is time."
- As of March 2026, the things that matter most cannot be delegated regardless of money — "no one can work out for you… no one can learn skills for you" — which is why early struggle feels isolating.
- As of March 2026, he reframes early low-stakes struggle as compounding advantage: skills and context won "in the weeds" become "arrows in the quiver" for later, bigger challenges (examples: *Slumdog Millionaire*, Steve Jobs' calligraphy → Apple fonts).
- As of March 2026, his expectation-management principle: "expecting it to be easy is what makes it much harder than it ever is."
