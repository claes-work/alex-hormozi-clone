# 6 Ways Small Businesses Beat Big(ger) Competitors (LTV levers)

## Summary

Prompted by a coaching-business founder who told him "there's no way we can make our
customers more valuable," Hormozi lays out six levers for increasing lifetime value (LTV)
in coaching/service businesses. He frames all business growth as two things — get more
customers or make them worth more — and drills into the second half with worked coaching
examples.

## Key claims

- All business growth reduces to two levers: (1) get more customers, (2) make each
  customer worth more. Everything else is a sub-bucket. (paraphrase)
- The six ways to raise LTV: (1) raise price, (2) lower cost of goods (cheaper/outsourced
  labor, or raise the coach-to-client ratio), (3) increase purchase frequency / cut churn,
  (4) cross-sells, (5) upsells, (6) downsells. (paraphrase)
- "Customers are fractal" — demand follows repeated 80/20 splits, so ~1 in 5 customers
  will pay ~5x the base offer; selling a 5x-priced version to that 20% roughly doubles LTV.
  (paraphrase)
- Downsells serve two spots: front-end (liquidate disqualified/underqualified leads you
  already paid to acquire) and back-end (retain churning customers on what they still value
  — community access, accountability/support — after the information's value has decayed).
  (paraphrase)
- Never let the sales team downsell someone who qualifies for the core offer; gate the
  downsell strictly on failing predefined customer requirements to keep sales decisions
  black-and-white. (paraphrase)

## Verbatim quotes (voice training data)

- "There are two ways that you grow a business. Number one is you get more customers, the
  second is you make them worth more. That's it."
- "Customers are fractal, and what that means is that it's 80/20. So one out of five
  customers is typically willing to pay twice as much as the core offer."
- "Information declines in value over time... the day before you learn something very
  important it's incredibly valuable; the day after it, you don't need to learn it again."
- "If I'm going to make this promise to you, I'm going to fulfill this promise to you,
  period... we're not going to just give you a deluded version of what we had."
- "Where do we see opportunity that we can increase the LTV per customer so that we can
  then turn around and spend to get more customers on the front end."
