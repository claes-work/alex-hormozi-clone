# If You're at Zero, Here's What You Need

## Summary

Interview clip (unnamed host asks the questions; Alex Hormozi answers). Asked what people
"at zero" really need to start a business, Alex argues the honest answer is not a tactic but
**courage** — the willingness to be wrong, to fail publicly, and to disappoint the people
whose opinions you care about. He grounds it in his own origin story: he was not a natural-
born entrepreneur, he had a "real opportunity cost" (a white-collar consulting job, a GMAT
score above Harvard's median, a clear path toward top MBA → consulting/banking → private
equity), and leaving it meant defying his father. He recounts realizing he "was living to
win someone else's game," concluding "his dream must die in order for mine to live," and
driving halfway across the country before calling his father to say he was gone — a rupture
that strained the relationship for years. He reframes fear as "a mile wide and an inch deep"
(a Leila quote) and closes on the distinction between failure and cowardice: cowardice is
letting fear change your behavior away from what you want, and he'd rather be a failure than
a coward. Ends with the acquisition.com/roadmap CTA (not persona content).

## Key claims (Alex-attributed, 2026-02-23, paraphrased unless quoted)

- What people at zero *think* they want is a tactic to immediately start a business; what
  they *actually need* is the courage to be willing to be wrong and to bear the shame of
  failing in front of people whose opinions they care about.
- The harder it is for you to break free of your self-made "mental prison" (real or
  imagined), the more compelling your story will be when you break through — it resonates
  with more people. Easy stories don't resonate.
- He explicitly disclaims the born-entrepreneur origin myth: no childhood lemonade stand, no
  "school failed me" grievance — he was good at school, had jobs in high school and out of
  college, and had a clear conventional career path.
- His opportunity cost was real: a white-collar consulting job and a GMAT score above
  Harvard's median, pointing toward a top MBA (Harvard/Stanford) then management consulting
  or investment banking, "eventually end up in private equity."
- Seeing the people 20 years ahead of him on that path, he didn't want their life — which
  made him question whether he wanted his own.
- His biggest life decisions have almost always come "at the doorstep of a parent['s]
  death"; he says needing death to force decisions may make him "a coward," and he has since
  tried to *operationalize* that trigger to make hard decisions faster without waiting for it.
- As a ~22-year-old consultant with a paid-off condo overlooking the city, he went through a
  period of hoping every night he wouldn't wake up — and this was precisely when his father
  was most proud of him, because he was following the plan. (Self-reported; sensitive.)
- Core reframe: he "was living [his] life to win someone else's game," and realized "one of
  our dreams had to die — either his or mine." He repeated "his dream must die in order for
  mine to live" as a crystallizing mantra.
- Measure of his fear of his father's judgment: he left the state and drove halfway across
  the country before calling to say he was already gone. His father had offered to "come
  over for lunch, we'll talk about it"; Alex told him he was already gone, and the tone (and
  the relationship) changed for years.
- He rejects the "I want to thank my mom and dad for always supporting me" Oscar-speech
  narrative — he can't say it, you might not be able to either, and that is not a reason not
  to pursue it.
- The concrete move: the family's "prodigal son" took a minimum-wage personal-trainer job
  cleaning floors at a gym "just to learn the gym business." Breaking free was both physical
  (leaving the area) and mental.
- He had to accept a possible timeline where his father never spoke to him again, and be
  okay with it, in order to pursue what he wanted.
- He "read tons of business books at that time" but couldn't use any of them because he was
  too afraid — knowledge is inert without the courage to act.
- ★ Fear reframe (Leila's quote he loves): "fear is a mile wide and an inch deep" — it looks
  like an ocean, but you take the first step and find ground under you; you don't drown.
  Friends you lose, you replace with new friends more on your path.
- This fear was the single hardest decision of his entrepreneurial career: across imagined
  parallel universes, "in like 90% of universes that Alex[i] exists, I'm just a consultant
  somewhere" — the split he was least likely to have taken.
- Humans are "allergic to uncertainty" and will choose the *certain misery* of their current
  situation over an uncertain path. (Note: this framing is stated by the host and affirmed
  by Alex.)
- ★ Cowardice vs. failure: he used the "non-palatable word coward purposefully." Being afraid
  doesn't make you a coward; you earn the title only if you let fear change your behavior in
  an aversive direction (away from what you want). Courage is acting *despite* fear, not
  without it. He has feared the "coward" label his whole life — more than the label of
  failure. "I'd rather be a failure than a coward."
