# I sold 8 businesses by age 32 [here's how]

## Summary

Solo talking-head walkthrough of how to sell a business to an institutional
buyer (private equity / family office) for millions. Alex uses his own eight
exits as credibility, then lays out the full M&A process end to end:
prerequisites (deal-killers), finding a banker/broker, prep (audited financials
+ CIM/SIM), marketing to buyers, LOIs, diligence, and the purchase agreement.
The video closes with real footage of one of his own ~$50M closings. This is a
biography-relevant source: it enumerates his businesses and exits.

## Key claims (the repeatable "how")

- **Enterprise value = a discount applied to future sales.** Total sales a
  business makes from now to the end of time, discounted by risk (likelihood
  those sales actually occur). High + likely future sales = high value.
  (paraphrase)
- **Buyers buy what the business will *become*, not what it is.** You must sell a
  credible growth story (how it 10x's), not just current numbers. (paraphrase)
- **~$5M is the smallest deal a real institutional buyer (PE / family office)
  will look at.** Below that you're in private-buyer territory (1x–3x, mostly
  asset sales). (self-reported / market claim)
- **Median M&A multiple cited: ~6.7x trailing-12-month for $5M+ institutional
  deals** (he flags he's recalling the stat imprecisely). (self-reported)
- **Deal-killers / prerequisites:** too small; customer concentration; no defined
  repeatable sales process; not talking to enough potential buyers ("talk to
  500, not 10"); wrong timing (most sell too late); overestimating value; not
  growing / small TAM / no growth story; **key-man risk**; **single-channel
  dependence**.
- **Banker vs. broker:** bankers for institutional (>$5M) sales; brokers usually
  for <$5M. Pick one who has sold businesses of *your* market and *your* size in
  *recent* history. (paraphrase)
- **Banker fee structure to watch:** upfronts, targets, ratchets, breakup fees —
  but the levers that matter most are the **minimums** (they don't get paid
  unless they beat a threshold) and **what number they're paid off** — negotiate
  their cut off **cash you receive**, not total enterprise value. (paraphrase)
- **Bankers behave like real-estate agents** — they want *a* deal closed more than
  *the best* deal, and will push both sides to meet in the middle; that's not
  necessarily in your interest.
- **The buyer-funnel math (his recommended targets):** contact ~500 buyers → ~98
  NDAs → ~14–15 management presentations → 2–4 IOIs/LOIs → 1 deal. Timeline: ~12
  weeks to find bankers, ~20 weeks of process, ~60–90 days diligence → **12–18
  months from start to money in the bank.**
- **Price vs. terms:** a headline number can be inflated with earn-outs, seller
  financing, rolled equity, escrow — good *terms* are worth more than a big
  *price*. A "$100M" deal can net ~$17M after earn-out/financing/roll/team
  splits/taxes/escrow. (paraphrase, illustrative math)
- **Win negotiations before they start by having options** — the best way to sell
  is to not need to sell; you must be willing to walk away or you get
  steamrolled. Say "if I don't get this point, the deal is off."
- **Don't let speed be used against you.** Buyers pretend a fast close is a favor;
  it's not. Diligence takes as long as they want; the only real way to speed a
  close is to keep growing fast (which raises valuation and your leverage).
- **Strategic buyers pay the best multiples** (they already have systems to make
  more from your business) but are rarer than financial buyers.
- **Purchase-agreement watch-items:** indemnities (negotiate the out-of-your-
  control ones out), non-competes (know your next move so you can scope the
  language narrowly; rolled equity can extend a non-compete), schedules (if a
  selling point isn't attached as a schedule, "it's as though it didn't
  happen"), definitions (redefining one term can reshape the whole document),
  net working capital left in the business, and escrow amount (aim to keep it
  under ~10%).
