# We Made a BIG Decision...

## Summary

Alex and Leila Hormozi announce that, for the first time in their careers, they have
brought an outside partner into their holding company, acquisition.com — a real-estate
executive they have been close friends with for six to seven years and who first served
as their informal "trusted confidant" for money after the Gym Launch sale. The
conversation (a three-way sit-down between Alex, Leila, and the new partner) is
structured as a business lesson on when and how to add a peer-level partner rather than a
hire, using Alex's "barrels vs. bullets" framing, the "I got it" culture, artifact-driven
onboarding, and decision speed. It doubles as the reveal of a new phase they call
"acquisition.com 3.0 / ACQ 3.0."

## Key claims

- The Hormozis have **never before brought a partner into their holding company**; this
  is the first in their career. (Alex, self-reported)
- The partnership was planned for **over a year** — a whiteboard session in Alex's office,
  a countdown timer on Alex's phone, then a formal announcement. (self-reported)
- **Barrels vs. bullets** (Alex's framework): resources ("bullets") should be handed to a
  "barrel" — a person who can create something from nothing and be as effective as you.
  You can't hire a barrel; a peer-level barrel is a partnership, not a hire. Alex, Leila,
  and the new partner are each "a barrel."
- **Aggregate skill-set thesis:** the potential of a business equals the aggregate skill
  set of the people in it; adding a second/third capable "brain" raises the ceiling
  dramatically. A solo operator caps the business at one brain's capacity. (Alex)
- **Under-talent, not over-expansion:** "You never overexpand, you just undertalent." The
  binding constraint on pursuing your ideas is too few barrels, not too many ideas. (Alex)
- **Decision speed = business speed:** compressing multi-week decisions into 30-minute
  ones (via someone with pattern recognition / prior data) is how a company grows 10–100x
  faster. In the absence of data you use patterns (past data extrapolated). (Alex)
- **"I got it" culture:** the phrase transfers leverage without loss of performance; the
  more people who can genuinely say "I got it," the faster the org moves. It also imposes
  a self-accountability / 100% say-do ratio on the speaker. (framework Alex has covered
  before; expanded here mostly by the partner and Leila)
- **Artifact-driven impact:** a new person should ship tangible artifacts (a memo, a
  video, a process, a vendor deal) as fast as possible — "the email is not the artifact,
  the artifact is the artifact" — to earn cultural buy-in. (attribution shared; see Notes)
- **Onboarding is the manager's responsibility**, not the new hire's, especially the first
  2–4 weeks. Leila wrote the partner a 16–20 page onboarding plan; A-players don't need a
  quarter to "get their bearings." (Leila + Alex)
- **Bottom-up learning:** the partner onboarded himself in ~10 days by meeting 100+ people
  from the bottom of the org chart up, and by reading every line item of the P&L, rather
  than accepting a 90-day ramp. Learn bottom-up, not top-down. (partner)
- **Unhirable experience:** some levels of skill/experience can't be hired or replicated
  in-house without effectively building a second company; Alex analogizes to OpenAI
  bringing in Jony Ive at co-founder status. (Alex)
- **acquisition.com 3.0:** framed as the natural progression — 1.0 = helping themselves
  directly; 2.0 = helping themselves directly + everyone else indirectly (documenting
  their playbooks); 3.0 = helping themselves directly + helping the audience directly.
  (Alex)
