## Summary

In this @MoreMozi interview clip Alex Hormozi explains the **mechanics** of how
Acquisition.com is structured and where the money actually comes from. He describes
**three intake paths** a company can take: **ACQ Ventures** (the venture arm — seed
capital for SaaS/software startups, check sizes ~$50K–$1M, ~3–4 deals/month); the
**private equity side** (the "big boy" side — bespoke deals, currently ~40% SaaS / 40%
B2B services / 20% consumer services, drifting toward a SaaS + professional-services
blend); and the **advisory division** (started January 2024, for "not portfolio-ready"
businesses roughly $1M–$100M/yr). On **how each arm makes money**: advisory is sold as a
plain **service** (fee for service); the **private equity** side throws off
**distributions**; **venture** checks he won't see a return on "for 10 years." He recounts
the **origin of the advisory arm**: for the three years prior, deals that weren't a fit
(he says "90 times out of 91") got free diligence + a change-list, founders called it the
most valuable thing they'd been through — so he decided to charge for that same
assessment. He also gives a long rationale for **why Acquisition.com has no public list of
its portfolio companies**: **keyman risk** (learned from the Prestige Labs sale, where
acquirers fretted over his 10K-follower Instagram driving a $20M/yr supplement business),
future non-compete/promotion provisions on exit, and not wanting to look like he's
"shilling." He says his **deal-size criteria have ~5x'd every ~18 months** — from ~$50M
opportunities at the start, to ~$250M, to **billion-dollar opportunities now** — and
plugs a free **10-stage 0→$100M roadmap** (8 business functions) at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims (dated 2026-02-16, paraphrased, Alex-attributed unless noted)

- **Criteria have escalated ~5x every ~18 months**: the target opportunity was ~$50M at
  the start, then ~$250M, and is **billion-dollar opportunities now**. "The businesses
  have to be a lot bigger."
- Companies come in via **three paths** on acquisition.com:
  - **ACQ Ventures** — the **venture arm**, for **seed capital / SaaS / software
    startups**; check sizes **~$50K to $1M**; **~3–4 deals a month** ("December did four,
    I think we did four in January"). Deals are lightweight: "meet the founder, understand
    the idea, cool, we can just deploy."
  - **Private equity** — the "big big… big boy side." Deals are **bespoke**, priced on the
    business's valuation, upside, and Acquisition.com's value-add. **Current PE portfolio
    mix ≈ 40% SaaS / 40% B2B services / 20% consumer services**, and he is **shifting over
    time toward a blend of SaaS + professional services** ("that just tends to be where we
    do really well").
  - **Advisory division** — started **January 2024** ("January of last year"), for
    companies that are **not portfolio-ready**, roughly the **$1M / $10M / $30M per-year**
    range (the "$1 to $100 million" band).
- **How each arm makes money** (asked directly):
  - **Advisory** — "we sell it as an advisory service. It's just a service." (Fee for
    service; **no equity taken**.)
  - **Private equity** — generates **distributions**.
  - **Venture** — "obviously I'm not going to see anything for that for 10 years."
- **Origin of the advisory arm**: for the ~3 years before Jan 2024, non-fit prospects
  ("90 times out of 91") got **4–6 free diligence calls** plus a list of things to change;
  founders repeatedly said it was **more valuable than anything they'd been through** and
  thanked him for doing it **free**. That was **expensive** for him, so he tested charging
  for the same assessment: invite founders to **fly to headquarters**, meet the
  **portfolio team** (head of marketing, head of sales, etc., by constraint), and get told
  the specific blockages to making the business more valuable/scalable.
- **Why the advisory works** (his read): there's **"just not a lot of help at that $1 to
  $100 million range."**
- **Why no public portfolio list** — several reasons, and he stresses he's **"gone back
  and forth"** on it:
  - **Keyman risk.** From selling Gym Launch / **Prestige Labs** (a sister supplements
    company doing **~$20M/yr** via the distribution base): in diligence, acquirers fixated
    on whether his then-**~10,000-follower Instagram** drove the sales, and worried about
    control and continued promotion. Seeing that sensitivity, he decided he doesn't want
    public association with portfolio companies, because he'd then be **tied to the deal
    later** on exit.
  - **Exit provisions**: a future acquirer of, e.g., an HVAC business he'd publicly
    associated with would demand **non-competes** and promotion provisions he doesn't want.
  - **Optics**: pseudo-promoting many businesses "almost feels like I'm shilling."
  - **Compliance/liability**: as an owner, if any portfolio company "does something
    stupid," a reporter or "clout-seeking YouTuber" pins it on him.
  - **Skool is the deliberate exception** — a purposeful **brand association + money**,
    because he's "in that for the long haul… many, many years," so he accepts the keyman
    risk there.
  - He thinks going public would net out only marginally positive ("51 to 49"), and says
    **ACQ Ventures deals may already be public** ("I think our ventures things are public…
    so it's really just the private equity side").
- **Interviewer's counter-arguments** (attribution: @MoreMozi interviewer, **not** persona
  data): a public "as-seen-on-Shark-Tank"-style page could raise portfolio valuations even
  net of keyman risk; would help attract businesses ("you started at this valuation, now
  look at you"); and would help negotiate better terms.
- **10-stage roadmap** plug: a free **"zero to $100 million plus" 10-stage roadmap** that
  "less than 1% of companies finish," which he's "now done multiple times," broken down by
  **8 business functions** (constraint symptoms + the steps taken to graduate), applied
  across software, physical products, service, and brick-and-mortar. Free at
  **acquisition.com/roadmap**.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "It's just much bigger. The businesses have to be a lot bigger. They have to be billion-dollar opportunities now… when I first started the goal was like $50 million opportunities, and then it became like $250 million opportunities. So it's almost like 5x'd every like 18 months or so."

> "For like seed capital, SaaS, software type startups, we have ACQ Ventures, which is our venture arm. Those are typically smaller checks, between like $50[K] and a million dollars… we do probably like three, four deals a month sometimes."

> "Right now we're 40% SaaS, 40% B2B services, and then 20% consumer services. And we're shifting over time towards just a blend of SaaS and professional services. That just tends to be where we do really well."

> "Then we have the advisory division, which we started in January of last year, which is companies that are like not really portfolio ready… that 1 million, 10 million, 30 million sometimes dollar per year business."

> "No, no. It's just — we sell it as an advisory service. It's just a service… We have distributions that come from the private equity side. The venture checks, obviously I'm not going to see anything for that for 10 years."

> "A lot of times, like 90 times out of 91, we'd be like, 'Not a fit for us right now, maybe change these two things.' … A lot of founders were like, 'This was more valuable than anything I've ever had to go through, and thanks for doing it for free.' And for me it was actually super expensive… I was like, I wonder if we could charge to do the same assessment."

> "There's just not a lot of help at that $1 to $100 million range. And I think we can provide that."

> "When I sold gym launch… I sold Prestige Labs, which was a sister company that did supplements… the business is doing like 20 million a year just the supplement side. And they were like, are all the sales coming from your Instagram? … seeing how sensitive they were to keyman risk around an acquisition, I was like, okay, if I do deals and grow my brand, I don't want people to know what the companies are."

> "Skool — that was purposely a brand association plus money… I'm in that for the long haul. I'm going to be with Skool for many, many years. So my keyman risk is something I'm willing to deal with. Whereas if I buy an HVAC business and publicly associate with it, a potential acquirer will want me to sign non-competes."

> "I think it's going to hurt you if a business does something that reflects on you, and if you do something it reflects on all the businesses… I think the net benefit is like 51 to 49."

> "The exact 10-stage roadmap from zero to 100 million plus that less than 1% of companies finish… I broke each of these down by eight different functions of the business… It's absolutely free. You just go acquisition.com/roadmap."
