# "How to Make Your Products Better Than Your Competitors"

## Summary

Short interview clip in which Alex is asked how he made School (Skool) so much better
than similar competitors like Circle or Kajabi, which have a similar mission but
"just suck compared to Skool." His answer is a product-quality philosophy: he simply
cared more and spent more time than anyone else, optimizing for making the best thing
rather than for revenue, growth, valuation, or profit (School had to "lose money forever"
as a result). He benchmarks against the highest-quality products on the internet (Google,
Google Maps, Chrome, MacBook Pro, iPhone, YouTube) and still sees "nothing but problems"
in his own product. The core differentiation method: don't stop at surface-level feature
parity — take every feature to its "hypothetical ideal" and resolve the hidden complexity
underneath, illustrated by the "green dot" online-status example. Competitors copy features
on the surface but not "at the depth," which is why users instantly sense the copy is "obviously
not Skool." Clip closes with the standard acquisition.com/roadmap promo.

Speaker note: solo Alex on the substantive content; an interviewer/host asks the questions
(and briefly interjects), and a co-founder ("Sam") is referenced. Only Alex's material below
trains the persona.

## Key claims (Alex's method — paraphrased, 2026-02-16)

- ★ **Care more, spend more time.** The simplest explanation for building a superior product:
  he just cared about it more than anything — more than commercial success, revenue, growth,
  valuation, or profit — and put in more time. Caring most is the root from which the other
  quality behaviors follow. (2026-02-16)
- The "doing of care" is observable, not a feeling: it shows up as resolving hidden complexity
  in features others treat as trivial. (2026-02-16)
- ★ **Optimize for the product, not the metric.** He optimized for making the best thing
  instead of revenue/growth/valuation/profit. The consequence: School had to lose money for a
  long time — an accepted cost of the standard. (2026-02-16)
- ★ **Benchmark to the highest bar there is.** He internally graded School against the best
  products on the internet — Google, Google Maps, Chrome, MacBook Pro, iPhone, YouTube — and
  aimed for that level. (2026-02-16)
- **Stay dissatisfied.** Even now he sees "flaws everywhere" and "nothing but problems" in
  School and is "far from satisfied," because that highest bar is the standard. (2026-02-16)
- ★ **The highest-standards person should lead.** Whoever has the highest standard should run
  the organization; the day someone has a higher standard than you, they should run it. It's a
  competition of who has the highest standards AND the ability to satisfy them — that combination.
  (2026-02-16)
- ★ **Take features to the hypothetical ideal, not to feature-parity.** The green-dot
  online-status example: a low-care team ships "tab open = green, done" and it doesn't work well;
  caring means defining what "online" actually means (tab open but on a different tab? on phone
  and computer? how long after switching tabs? active DM waiting on a reply vs. just posting?).
  Getting all these levels right is what makes the product quietly "just work." (2026-02-16)
- **Quality is the sum of avoided paper cuts.** A product that "just works" is the absence of a
  thousand small failures; each unresolved detail is a paper cut that erodes the feel of the product.
  (2026-02-16)
- ★ **Surface parity vs. depth parity.** Many competitors have tried to match School by creating
  feature parity on the surface, but not at the depth of what School does — which is why users
  quickly sense "this is obviously not School." (2026-02-16)
- **Apply this to any product.** Having a feature (e.g. "a thing on ads") isn't the point — the
  question is "how good is it?" It's all the details, all the way down. (2026-02-16)
