# How to Make Your Business Run Without You

/ [[../topics/business/scaling-and-enterprise-value|Scaling & Enterprise Value]] · [[../topics/business/communication-and-management|Communication & Management]] · [[../topics/business/hiring-and-recruiting|Hiring & Recruiting]]

## Summary

A mid-segment clip (opens and closes mid-sentence, part of a longer teaching) on
removing the founder as the bottleneck. Alex Hormozi reframes the "no one can do it as
well as me" belief, lays out the labor-leverage math of trading *doing* for
*managing/leading*, and gives a three-step handoff sequence (shadow → supervise →
support). The through-line is his keyman-risk litmus: the baseline is "does it burn down
without me," and the exciting version is a business that is *bigger* when you return —
which is what makes it saleable (a "faster growing annuity for an investor"). He closes
on a brick-and-mortar overexpansion warning: opening a second/third location before the
first can run without you doubles liability while spreading talent thin, so a flat
location can decline once the founder's attention moves elsewhere. Mostly a heavily-
covered thread (founder-bottleneck / scale-to-zero); the genuinely-distinct bits are his
talent-evaluation shift (measure *rate of improvement*, not starting level) and the
named-but-unexplained "phone test."

## Key claims (2026-02-14, paraphrased unless quoted)

- Belief reframe #1: no one can do it as well as *you* on one-tenth of the reps — but
  someone doing it with *all* their time should beat your part-time "5/10 that you call a
  10." Paraphrase: "If I can do it right with part of my time, someone else can do it
  better with all of their time."
- Belief reframe #2: you can outwork *anyone* in your business, but you cannot outwork
  *everyone* — realizing this late limited his growth. "I can do a mountain of work but I
  can't do Mount Everest."
- Every human is replaceable; if someone learns only the right lessons (skipping your
  bad mistakes) they can start at ~50% as good "right off the bat."
- Labor-leverage math: swap ~200 hours/month of doing (~50 hrs/week) for ~20 hours of
  managing → a 10x leverage step (50→5); then replace yourself again by hiring a manager,
  trading ~4 hours for ~1. Leverage through labor = turning your effort into an
  organization that produces without you.
- Litmus test (baseline): "does it burn down without me" — can you leave for a month and
  come back to it intact. The *next* level: come back and it is in a *better* position
  than when you left.
- A business that grows while you're away is what buyers want, because it functions as a
  "faster growing annuity for an investor," earning a big multiple.
- ★ Three-step handoff: (1) **shadow train** — they watch you do it; (2) **supervise** —
  they do it in front of you; (3) **support independence** — "available but not
  involved." Early on they need ad-hoc calls/consults; over time a competent employee's
  decisions "get automated back down to them," and full handoff = they own it completely.
- Accept 80% (sometimes 60%) as good as you at handoff, provided there's a clear path to
  improvement.
- ★ Talent-evaluation shift: he used to measure employees by *how well they started*; now
  by *how fast they improve*. A faster-improving hire overtakes a higher-starting flat
  one within 3–6 months. "Intelligence I measure as rate of learning" — he'll take a more
  intelligent (faster-learning) employee over a more experienced (slower-learning) one,
  role depending.
- The founder's job becomes attracting talent and aligning incentives: recruiting
  A-players rather than doing A-player work; strategy/prioritization rather than
  today's/next tactics.
- Ultimate owner test: can you take three months off and have the business *grow*.
- Referenced (from earlier in the talk): the systemization stack — big task inventory →
  decision trees → slide in existing people → scorecard for hires → a test at the end
  that says whether they pass.
- Brick-and-mortar / second-location warning: the simplest litmus is "the phone test"
  (named but deferred — "I'll tell you later"). If a location can be left alone and is
  bigger three months later, expand; if flat, fix growth first.
- ★ "Eye of Sauron" overexpansion mechanic: a flat location can go *down* once the
  founder's attention moves to the new site — leaving double the liability/debt/overhead,
  half the talent (spread thin), more risk and less profit. Overexpansion is called out
  as a top reason people go out of business; many shouldn't open a second location as soon
  as they think.
