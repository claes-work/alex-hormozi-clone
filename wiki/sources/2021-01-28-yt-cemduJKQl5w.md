# Recruiting and Hiring Tips — How to Hire The Best Employees (2021-01-28)

> Speaker: Alex Hormozi only (solo monologue). All claims below are Alex-attributed.
> Era note: Gym Launch–era, ~100-person company. Reflects Hormozi's 2021 views on HR,
> compensation, and hiring — DATE-stamp these; his framing may have evolved since.

## Summary

Solo podcast/YouTube monologue in which Hormozi recounts a lesson from a leadership
quarterly meetup and argues for how HR and hiring should actually serve a company. He
opens with a cautionary story of a former HR director who spent ~90 days and heavy
payroll on a company-wide salary analysis that only raised costs with no return, then
uses it to lay out his philosophy: the company (not the employees) is HR's customer,
compensation should only move to solve attraction or retention problems, and hiring/HR
value comes from recruiting top talent and protecting the company's profit. Despite the
"recruiting and hiring tips" title, the content is primarily an HR-role and
compensation-philosophy talk, with hiring framed as talent acquisition and gatekeeping.

## Hiring framework

Hormozi's 2021 model treats hiring/HR as a profit-protection and talent function, not an
employee-happiness function. Core components as stated in this source:

**1. Gatekeep new headcount — default answer to "let's hire" is "no."**
- When a manager requests a new hire, HR's first response should be "no": demand a
  reason the existing team is so overworked they can't just work more.
- Ask for hour-to-hour utilization data before approving headcount.
- Premise: many people aren't actually overworked — they "don't know how to work"
  (lack focus/discipline), so requested headcount is often unnecessary.

**2. Recruit the best talent — "the who, not the what."**
- The value of the company tracks the value of its people; a good "who" figures out
  the "what."
- "Really good HR is really good recruitment" — finding, developing, and retaining
  talent. HR owns recruiting; developing sits with departments; retaining is a function
  of department heads plus compensation packages.

**3. Screen for culture fit / positive inclination.**
- Not HR's job to make employees happy; it IS HR's job to find people with a positive
  inclination who fit the culture, so the environment retains and attracts talent.
- High-performance culture is self-policing: a strong core team ("this is how we do it
  — we wake up at 5, go over scripts, role-play, do game-day review of sales calls")
  causes new hires to either assimilate or be rejected by the team itself.

**4. Compensate by value contribution, only move pay to solve a problem.**
- Pay people based on value delivered to the bottom line, not equality-of-persons.
- Two market-value lenses: (a) what the market pays for the role, and (b) what THIS
  specific person yields for THIS company/product (a star may take 2–3 average hires to
  replace).
- The ONLY two reasons to raise an existing employee's pay: you cannot attract talent,
  or you cannot retain talent. Absent an attraction/retention problem, comp is fine —
  don't change it.
- Star performers (worth 3–5x in output) are usually worth paying above market to
  retain; make the economic argument ("if we lose this person we lose half our sales —
  how is that not worth 10% more base?").

**5. Priority order: marketing, sales, product first; operations (incl. HR) supporting.**
- Operations = everything non-core to value: IT, HR, finance, legal. Must happen, but
  not the primary value drivers.
- Operations should support and enable sales, marketing, and product — not lead them.

## Key claims (paraphrase; dated 2021-01-28)

- A former Gym Launch HR director ran a ~90-day company-wide salary analysis, concluded
  ~20–25% were overpaid, ~20–25% underpaid, ~50% appropriate; underpaid staff got raise
  emails, overpaid staff kept their pay. Hormozi calls this among the worst decisions
  he's ever seen. [paraphrase]
- He estimates the episode cost roughly $600,000/year in profit (raises with no return),
  plus the ~$100k of payroll spent producing the study. [paraphrase — his own estimate]
- HR at that time had 6 people in a ~100-person company; Hormozi attributes the salary
  project to that department not having enough real work. [paraphrase]
- HR's/managers'/owners' job is NOT to make employees happy; happiness is each
  individual's own responsibility. [paraphrase]
- The employee is not HR's customer — the company is. The company has three
  stakeholders: shareholders (own it), customers (pay it), employees (run it); all three
  must be balanced. [paraphrase]
- A company needs profit to grow, reinvest in new product lines, fund legal defense, and
  weather unforeseen storms; overpaying for no added value weakens that position.
  [paraphrase]
- Business goal restated bluntly: pay as little as possible for as much value as
  possible — the price-to-value discrepancy is where profit lives. [paraphrase]
- He explicitly values marketing, sales, and product MORE than operations, and says a
  past period of "operations first" (on mentors' advice) coincided with the company
  making less money until he reversed it. [paraphrase]
- Gym Launch had very low voluntary attrition ("we fire people like crazy, but people
  don't quit") due to a high-performance culture. [paraphrase]
- Cites Chet Holmes ("Chad Holmes" in transcript — likely misspeak) on superstar
  salespeople being worth well above market. [paraphrase — attribution to Holmes]
- Cites Grant Cardone for the line that Gym Launch is "a dangerous place to work."
  [paraphrase — attribution to Cardone]
- Anecdote: as a law-firm intern, a senior partner told him "you don't even know what
  work is yet"; watching lawyers work changed his standard for what "working" means.
  [paraphrase — self-reported biography]

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Your job is not to make employees happy... Employees['] job is to make employees
> happy. Human beings are responsible for making themselves happy."

> "Your customer is the company... the company has three stakeholders: you've got
> shareholders the people who own the company, you've got customers the people who pay
> the company, and then you've got employees the people who run the company."

> "The point of all businesses is to... get as much price. They want to have as big of a
> discrepancy as possible... value is what you get and price is what you pay."

> "A good who will figure out the what."

> "Really good HR is really good recruitment. It's talent... finding the talent,
> developing the talent, retaining the talent."

> "Give me a reason why your team is so overworked that they can't just work more.
> ...a lot of people don't work that hard."

> "There are some of you listening to this right now who spend your entire day not
> working, trying to work... That is not a company's fault. That is your lack of ability
> in being disciplined and learning the skills of learning how to work."

> "The only reason that you would want to move compensation is for one of two things.
> Either you cannot attract talent or you cannot retain talent."

> "We fire people like crazy, but people don't quit... low performers don't last."

> "Gym launch is a dangerous place to work... this is a winners' environment. And if you
> are a winner, you will love it here. And if you are not a winner, you will not."

> "If someone is new and comes in, they either assimilate or are rejected."

> "You need to think like an owner... What am I losing here?"

> "You get paid based on the value that you provide. And a good owner understands that."

> "The company is the goose... the mule that everyone is fed off of. And so we have to
> keep the mule healthy."
