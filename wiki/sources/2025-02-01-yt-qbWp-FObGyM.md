# The Heavy Burden of Unmade Decisions

## Summary

In a conversation on the @TheSkoolSchool channel, Alex Hormozi argues that unmade
decisions are the heaviest weight a person carries, and that the stress most people
feel is simply the accumulation of decisions they are avoiding. He defines commitment
as the elimination of alternatives, traces his own indecision back to fear of a single
person's judgment, and reframes ownership through a story about being 19 and realizing
that blaming his parents handed them control over his life. He closes by locating the
"activation energy" of high achievers in insecurity and, at its root, the fear of death.

## Key claims (his decision-making view)

- Unmade decisions are the heaviest burden in life; the stress a person feels is that
  they have decisions to make and aren't making them.
- Commitment is defined by the **elimination of alternatives** — you can say you're
  committed, but until you remove the other options (no "get out of jail free"), you're
  not, and removing them is what forces action.
- He distinguishes the *decision* ("I need to change this") from the *commitment*
  (actually changing it / killing off the alternatives); a decision only becomes real
  when the escape routes are gone.
- His own indecision usually traces to fear — and specifically to **one person's
  judgment**, not an amorphous "society." Naming that person ("Tom") makes the fear
  concrete and exposes how little power it actually holds.
- Blaming others hands them control over your life. At 19 he realized the people he
  resented most were the ones controlling him, and that disgust is what let him flip the
  narrative toward taking ownership.
- As a self-described "frame guy," he runs scenarios and reframes (zoom out, maybe the
  problem doesn't exist, maybe the right action is nothing), but recognizes the driver
  underneath is usually fear.
- The "activation energy" behind most high achievers is insecurity — a fear of being
  insufficient — which, regressed to its most basic form, is fear of death. He claims he
  has *more* insecurities than most people, and that they, not ambition, drove his
  achievement.
