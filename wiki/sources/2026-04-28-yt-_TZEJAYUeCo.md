# The Exact Roadmap From $0 to $10M

## Summary

In this short solo clip, Alex Hormozi maps the growth stages a business passes through from $0 to $10M and describes what changes at each. He frames the $0–$3M journey as first defeating ignorance (just sell something to one avatar through one channel), then making that repeatable, then getting the owner out of delivery and building a small core team. He explains why $3M is the minimum level his firm takes clients on — it signals a company has cleared three thresholds — and closes with a CTA for the free $100M scaling road map at acquisition.com/roadmap.

## Key claims (roadmap stages)

- **$0–$3M — the main barrier is ignorance.** "People just don't know what to do." Getting to six figures is "literally just like sell something to someone."
- **Six figures = one channel, one product, one avatar.** That's the entire requirement; nothing else is needed.
- **Seven figures ($1M) = do that same thing consistently.** Feed inputs into the system consistently so you get a consistent output — whatever acquisition method got the customers (reach-outs, content, paid ads, affiliates, referrals), just do it consistently.
- **$1M is only ~$20k/week** — framed as "not a huge number."
- **$1M–$3M = increasing output of that one main thing to a small degree**, plus baseline efficiencies and building out the core team.
- Going $1M→$3M is largely about **getting the owner out of delivery**, and hiring the first one or two competent individuals plus a handful of front-line people.
- **Companies at ~$3M are typically 5–15 employees**, of whom usually only about two are genuinely good and the rest are "okay."
- **$3M–$10M is the minimum level Acquisition.com takes people on** — not because $3M is magical, but because it typically signals three things.
- **The three boxes a ~$3M business checks:** (1) product–market fit at some level (people want what they sell, sales resonate); (2) at least one reliable acquisition channel they're currently running; (3) a core team in place — which may be dysfunctional with few key players, but has "some semblance of structure." Meeting these minimums gives enough leverage to start helping them grow.
