# Leveling Up: Why We Get Stuck

## Summary

In this 2020 talk, Hormozi argues that people plateau because the *nature* of what
they must improve changes as they level up — not just the difficulty. Early on the
bottleneck is acquiring skills (become proficient), then deepening those skills through
repetition, and finally the bottleneck becomes character traits (focus, patience, grit,
saying no). He frames the biggest late-stage constraint as the growing seduction of new
opportunity ("the woman in the red dress"), and preaches doing fewer things rather than
more. He cites his own decision to delay the supplement company as a discipline win, and
admits in hindsight he might not have launched it at all.

## Key claims

- (2020) Getting unstuck requires improving different *kinds* of things at each stage:
  first **skills**, then a change of **lens**, then **character traits** — people get
  stuck because they assume leveling up is linear/uniform when the nature of the required
  change itself changes.
- (2020) The progression is: (1) stack skills to reach **proficiency** — not expertise,
  just enough to work (e.g. know how to run an ad); (2) **deepen** skills through
  repetition; (3) develop the **character traits** that let you stay the course.
- (2020) Proficiency is cheap: most proficient skills take little time — ~2 hours of
  focused learning ("Google how to do X") gets you proficient. Most people won't spend
  the 2 hours because it's uncomfortable and they're bad at it at first.
- (2020) You learn far more from doing than studying — more in your first 20 sales than
  your first 20 hours studying sales; the first time in the ring teaches more than all
  the practice.
- (2020) As you climb, opportunity ("the woman in the red dress") looks progressively
  more attractive — becomes "a 10" — so higher levels demand a *new level* of discipline
  to say no. A bigger distribution base makes new opportunities even more tempting.
- (2020) The entrepreneur's fallacy: "if I do more things, I will make more money." The
  truth is the opposite — you need to do **fewer** things. Because people naturally think
  the additive way, that's why most people aren't successful.
- (2020) You can be successful at **anything** you want, just not **everything** you want.
  Each of several businesses can individually be a billion-dollar company, but you can't
  build all of them to a billion at once. (Illustrated with a friend running three
  businesses — contracting, roofing, flipping houses — whom he told to "pick one.")
- (2020) References Angela Duckworth's *Grit*: the most successful people have
  "stick-to-it-ness" — low likelihood of abandoning a path for another. Beginners chase
  shiny objects and never give any opportunity a chance to succeed.
- (2020) Fitness analogy: the effort to *build* muscle vs. *maintain* it rises
  dramatically as you advance, with a large "no man's land" between minimum-effective-dose
  and the threshold that forces adaptation. Most people (in skills and training) do enough
  to maintain but not enough to break through.
- (2020) Diagnostic: if your income has been flat for years, your skill set has been flat —
  usually because you lack the character traits to deepen skills. Sometimes the skill you
  need is to *not* do other things — to maintain the course and remove constraints.
- (2020, self-reported) Not launching the supplement company until ~1.5–2 years after Gym
  Launch was one of his best moves; he originally planned to start both simultaneously,
  and Leila talked him out of it, keeping him focused and enabling explosive growth.
- (2020, self-reported) He says he still might not launch the supplement company if
  redoing it, but notes Gym Launch pays ~$600,000/month in commission checks to community
  members ("gym lords") and that it solved a problem he had as a gym owner.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "You can be successful at anything you want, just not everything you want."

> "There's a fallacy that we tell ourselves as entrepreneurs, which is that if I do more
> things, I will make more money. When in fact, I need to do fewer things to get to where
> I want to go."

> "The higher up you go, the more seductive the woman in the red dress looks... when
> you're starting out, the woman in the red dress always looks attractive. And the thing
> is that the higher up you go, the more of a 10 she becomes."

> "Grit is stick-to-it-ness... how likely are you to abandon a certain path to pursue
> another thing. And the people in the beginning have very low stick-with-it-ness —
> entrepreneurs, shiny object, shiny object, shiny object."

> "In the beginning it's skills, but then you have to change your lens, and then after
> that it becomes traits... that's why people get stuck, is because the actual thing that
> you need to improve changes — not just skills, but the actual nature of the change
> itself changes."

> "You learn more in your first 20 sales than you do in the first 20 hours of learning
> about sales stuff."

> "Sometimes the skill itself is to not do other things... sometimes the skill itself is
> maintaining the course... the thing that you need to do to get to the next level is to
> do less and remove constraints."
