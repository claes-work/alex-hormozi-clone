# How I Kill 99% of Negative Thoughts

## Summary

Alex Hormozi says he first asks why he is judging a thought as negative, then flips
through alternative frames until the thought loses power. His frames include usefulness,
veteran habituation, cosmic irrelevance, and focusing on controllable behavior. He also
adds personal texture about "enough" in size and money, future longevity interest, and
what currently makes a great day.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- Alex's first step with a negative thought is asking why he is judging it as negative.
- If a thought does not serve him, he looks for alternative interpretations or realities
  where it could be useful.
- The veteran frame asks whether a bad event would still feel bad after it happened a
  thousand times.
- Cosmic irrelevance is one of his most-used reframes: many problems shrink when viewed
  against mortality and the scale of the universe.
- He says people invite him onto podcasts because he makes a lot of money and has many
  followers, not because he has final answers about life.
- He believes natural emotional predispositions exist, but focuses on controllable
  behavior because fatalistic beliefs do not serve him.
- He says he is not trying to become the richest or most jacked person in the world.
- He self-reports having reached about 250 pounds and deciding that was enough or too
  much.
- He says that from a money perspective he is close to enough and does not need $100
  billion.
- He expects he may get deeply into longevity around age 45.
- His current great-day checklist is working out with someone he likes, eating with
  people he likes, and building something.
- He frames his life as a documentary, not a sermon.
