# Building a $5,000,000 Business for a Stranger in 42 Mins

## Summary

A live-build/coaching episode in which Alex Hormozi diagnoses the business of
Raymond Coer, founder of a chain of six brick-and-mortar chiropractic clinics in
Washington ($5.2M trailing revenue, ~23% net margin, 75% insurance-based). Alex
probes the numbers, funnel, and operations, then lays out a prioritized ~6-month
plan: increase Google ad spend, add a low-ticket ($29) lead magnet, tighten
Facebook ad copy, move the sale to the point of greatest pain, streamline the
insurance-verification/sales process, and add local SEO. His recurring theme is
that a business already making money grows fastest by spending more on proven
channels, reducing friction in the sale, and installing repeatable operator-proof
systems rather than changing the model.

## Key claims (Alex's advice only)

- There are two expansion strategies — buy or build; the initial sorting question
  ("did you buy or open organically?") tells him which problems to look for.
- "Build it to sell" and "build it to keep" lead down the same path: both require
  building a better business; the difference only reflects how long the owner wants
  to work.
- Selling high-ticket packages (vs. one adjustment at a time) is the right direction
  for a premium service; his price points were already right.
- A healthy close rate is usually ~30–40% (the sweet spot of price vs. units sold);
  very high close rates (e.g. 71%) are a signal the market will bear higher prices.
- "Founder magic / owner magic": the first location is always more entrenched and
  special than any bought or opened afterward — a common cause of scaling pain.
- High ROAS channels are a money-printing machine: if a dollar in returns $24, put
  in as much money as you possibly can; ask the agency if you're maxed on spend.
- Google (search/intent-based) buyers outperform Facebook (interruption-based)
  because willingness to pay is proportional to spending power AND the pain being
  experienced; high-intent searchers buy faster and at bigger tickets.
- Working leads well is one of the biggest revenue levers small businesses ignore —
  respond quickly, personalize, multiple times, timely. 80% show rates prove Raymond
  already works leads well.
- Weak operations/execution is often the real constraint, not strategy — "the team's
  just not good enough"; fix the team before changing the model. This limits and
  reorders which recommendations he'll make.
- Sell at the point of greatest pain (deprivation), not the point of greatest
  satisfaction. Move the assessment + package sale before the treatment, not after.
- The steak analogy: sell two steaks to the starving man before he eats, not after
  he's full and satisfied.
- Upsells at the point of greatest satisfaction work only if they open the loop of
  pain for the next problem (e.g. more money → taxes → tax help).
- A $19–$29 low-ticket front-end offer (e.g. "$29 x-ray + assessment") gets the
  credit card on the phone and pushes show-up rates toward ~100%.
- Value equation (from $100M Offers): dream outcome, plus three levers of friction —
  perceived likelihood of achievement (risk), time delay, and effort/sacrifice
  (ease). Make it easy, fast, risk-free.
- "This is just how we've always done it" is a near-universal line for overcoming
  objections to small process changes (e.g. collecting a card/insurance info) —
  appeal to policy; employees stop questioning it.
- Machine the sales process into clicks/checkboxes: turn a laptop around and read the
  slides, OR use a clipboard with a hidden script — eliminates variability between
  reps. Model what the top ~80% closer does and turn it into the standard deck.
- Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate (from $100M Leads, p.207): make a checklist,
  do it in front of them, have them do it in front of you.
- Tell the agency to double ad spend and find more keywords; speak marketer language
  (CPA/CAC/CPL) and give a target cost-per-acquisition — "as long as it's under this,
  Make It Rain."
- Keep two numbers with agencies: the acceptable range you tell them and your real
  acceptable amount (build in padding); the more you trust them, the closer the two.
- Absolute return beats efficiency ratio: spending $10k to make $50k (5:1, +$40k net)
  beats $1k to make $10k (10:1, +$9k net). Expect efficiency to drop at scale — that's
  part of the game; stop over-optimizing and sometimes just spend more.
- Local SEO is winnable on long-tail local keywords (e.g. "chiropractor Everett,"
  "sciatica Everett") even though national terms are near-impossible; it's a
  multi-year, all-margin investment with the highest long-term ROI.
- Prioritization: do light-work/high-impact first; save heavy work for later unless
  it's a prerequisite. If something takes 5 minutes and can double the business, do
  it first.
- Doubling revenue per location without opening new stores captures most of the
  goal — fixed costs stay roughly the same, so doubled revenue flows to EBITDA.
