# How I Learned Sales

## Summary
Interview clip (an unnamed host asks where Alex studied sales; only Alex's words are used as voice data). Alex tells the origin story of how he learned to sell: entirely **face-to-face, in person, before he knew a "sales world" even existed.** He'd already done ~4,000 sales in his gyms before hearing of formal sales training. After college a family friend (a wealth manager) offered him a sales job; Alex refused, insisting "I'm not a salesman, I'm an academic." He got into fitness, opened a brick-and-mortar gym, and realized he had to get strangers to give him money. Having learned **Facebook ads in 2013** (very early), he got cheap leads and for ~4–5 years took **20 in-person consults a day.** Volume of reps made him close so high that his team would "stack" a week of sales for him — he'd drive between locations (La Habra, La Mirada, Huntington Beach) doing ~25 sales each per day. After six gyms (which he sold), he ran **gym turnarounds**: a fold-out table at the front door of an empty gym, ~30:1 on ad spend, averaging ~$100k cash collected in 21 days, **33 turnarounds over 2 years.** To scale, he built a sales team (up to eight reps taking 20 appointments/day across the country) — and his entire sales training was **60 minutes**, built to make people *close*, not to sell sales training. From this he distilled the **CLOSER framework** (Clarify, Label the problem, Overview past experiences, Sell the vacation, Explain away concerns, Reinforce the decision) and his core methodology: ask questions rather than make statements ("every time you make a statement you give the other person something to disagree with"), pre-handle the finite set of objections, and keep the human first (credited to Leila) — the end point of a sale is a *decision*, not a sale.

## Key claims
- As of March 2026, Alex says he only learned that a formal "sales world" existed *after* he had already done ~4,000 sales in his gyms; all of it was learned face-to-face, in person, with no sales training, courses, or gurus.
- As of March 2026, after graduating college a family friend (a wealth manager) offered him an office sales job; Alex declined, telling him "I'm not a salesman, I'm an academic / a student."
- As of March 2026, he learned Facebook ads in 2013 (early), got cheap leads, and for ~4–5 years personally took ~20 in-person consults per day, out-repping everyone and closing at a high percentage.
- As of March 2026, once he had multiple gym locations his team would "stack" a week's sales for him; he'd travel between locations (La Habra, La Mirada, Huntington Beach) doing ~25 sales each in a day.
- As of March 2026, after selling his six gyms he ran gym turnarounds: sitting at the front door of empty gyms with a fold-out table, pens, and contracts, getting ~30:1 on ad spend and averaging ~$100k cash collected in 21 days — 33 turnarounds over 2 years.
- As of March 2026, he set ~3 appointments per hour (every 30 min), delivered the same pitch repeatedly, and scaled a sales team to ~8 reps each taking 20 appointments/day across the country — trained in only ~60 minutes because the goal was to make them close, not to sell sales training.
- As of March 2026, his methodology: there is a finite, small set of objections ("offshoots") a prospect will raise, and because his team had had the conversation more times than anyone, there was "no excuse to be unprepared for anything Susie says."
- As of March 2026, his belief: "every time you make a statement, you give the other person something to disagree with" — so ask questions instead; asking questions about an objection usually sidesteps it, because people will only agree with themselves.
- As of March 2026, he frames the CLOSER framework: Clarify why they're there; Label them with a problem you can solve; Overview past experiences; Sell the vacation (not the plane flight) — usually ~3 dream outcomes; Explain away concerns; Reinforce the decision (a clean "handshake" to next steps to prevent chargebacks/refunds).
- As of March 2026, he says the best salespeople rarely use a "wicked closing line"; the close is easy if everything leading up to it is right — "don't attempt the close unless you intimately know you have the close" (compared to lawyers not asking questions they don't know the answer to).
- As of March 2026, he credits Leila with "keeping the human first," and states the person who cares more about the prospect wins; if it's genuinely not the best thing for them, push away — the end point of a sale is a **decision**, one way or the other, not a sale.
