# You Need to Start Doing Outbound

## Summary

Solo Alex Hormozi talk (short-form clip) arguing that most businesses drastically
overestimate how "saturated" or "crowded" their market is, and that this false
scarcity belief keeps them poor. He opens with an origin story from his Gym Launch
days: he didn't want to do outbound, but was convinced after interviewing a lead
sales rep from a gym-software company he'd never heard of that was doing $10M/month —
roughly 5x Alex's revenue at the time — entirely off outbound. That broke his belief
that outbound couldn't generate serious demand.

He then walks through a visual "pie" argument: entrepreneurs imagine the whole market
as their pie, feel it shrink as competitors enter, and conclude they only own a
quarter of it. In reality, the slice a business actually touches is a tiny sliver,
because it advertises in only one way, on one platform, in one medium. Alex maps the
full opportunity as three layers of channels — (1) content platforms, (2) paid ads,
(3) one-to-one outreach — most of which the average business ignores entirely.
Outbound (every way to contact another human one-on-one) is presented as a large,
mostly-untapped layer. He closes by pushing depth as well as breadth: even on the one
platform you use, you're likely not maximizing every format available.

## Key claims

All claims Alex Hormozi, paraphrased unless quoted, dated 2026-02-24.

- **Outbound origin story (Gym Launch era):** Alex initially did not want to do
  outbound; he was convinced to start after a specific conversation. (paraphrase)
- **The $10M/month story:** While recruiting a lead sales rep (who turned out to be in
  the gym business, selling gym software), Alex learned the rep's company was doing
  ~$10M/month, sourced entirely through outbound — a company Alex had never heard of,
  doing ~5x his revenue at the time. This "broke a belief" that outbound couldn't
  generate that kind of demand. (paraphrase)
- **The scarcity/pie mental model is false and costly:** Believing the market is a
  fixed pie you split with competitors "will keep you poor, or at least poorer than
  you would otherwise be." (near-verbatim / paraphrase)
- **You touch only a tiny sliver:** A business typically advertises "in one way using
  one specific platform and one specific medium on that platform" — so its real share
  of total market attention is barely visible, not the quarter-pie it imagines.
  (paraphrase)
- **Layer 1 — content channels are many:** If content is how you get customers, the
  platforms include Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, YouTube, X, radio, TV, Taboola,
  Google search, direct mail, email, and more (he calls "school" as another). Most
  businesses use only one. (paraphrase; channel list captured precisely)
- **Layer 2 — paid ads:** A whole additional layer ("ads circles") that most are
  "currently doing nothing with." (paraphrase)
- **Layer 3 — outbound/outreach:** "Every single way that you can contact another human
  being one-on-one" is a distinct outreach channel — direct mail, DMs on Facebook,
  LinkedIn, TikTok, phone calls, etc. This layer is largely untapped. (paraphrase;
  outreach methods captured precisely)
- **Depth within a channel, not just breadth:** Beyond adding channels, you should
  maximize every format on the one platform you already use — e.g., on Instagram:
  posts, stories, images, carousels; on YouTube: shorts, vlogs, community posts — and
  increase the volume of each. (paraphrase)
- **Reframe of competition:** Two or three competitors entering "your" market does not
  make it crowded; you were only ever occupying a tiny sliver of one channel, so the
  real market and channel mix remain vastly underexploited. (paraphrase)
