# 3 Proven Strategies That Make You Money with Skool

## Summary

Alex Hormozi lays out how to monetize a Skool community, framing the decision as "four life paths" a creator can take once they start a group. One path (staying free forever) is not monetization; the other three are the money-making strategies referenced in the title. He then teaches a pricing method: observe where most Skool groups sit ($19–$299/month), set the price by the value delivered relative to what the member will earn or save, then reverse-engineer the number of members (N) needed to hit a revenue goal. He uses a $10,000/month target as the worked example and reduces it further to a daily member-add quota. The talk closes with a CTA to join the Skool Games and his Monday calls.

## The 3 monetization strategies (the money-making paths)

Alex frames these as paths 2, 3, and 4 of "four life paths." Path 1 — keep the community free forever — is presented as valid but is *not* a monetization strategy (he praises free groups for engagement, guaranteed reach on posts, member-to-member interaction, leaderboards, and unlockable content that incentivizes sharing/referrals).

- **Strategy 1 — Start free, then switch to paid.** Begin free to build momentum, then flip the community to paid over time. Ethical constraint he stresses: you cannot start people for free and then bill them; everyone who was free gets **grandfathered in for free**. Pro tips: (a) when inviting people while still free, tell them the group *will* be paid in the future — this creates urgency and a "big reason why" to join now; (b) tell them they can keep the current (free) price forever *as long as they stay in*, which incentivizes both joining and staying engaged. Framing for late arrivals: once you switch to paid, "anyone who joins now joins the party late after the party is already booming," so you "charge a ticket for entry" — and that ticket is recurring monthly.

- **Strategy 2 — Keep the free community AND run a second paid community.** Leave the original free group intact and start a separate paid community alongside it. The paid tier offers additional features, additional access, additional events, and additional content that the free group doesn't have. His key conversion insight: "the best way to get people to want to join this one is to make this one awesome" — i.e., make the *free* community excellent so members want to level up into the paid one.

- **Strategy 3 — Just start a paid community from day one.** Skip free entirely and launch straight into a paid group. He notes he only lists it "for obviousness" and includes all paths deliberately "because people are starting at different points with different skill sets and different assets."

## Pricing method (how to set the price and hit a revenue goal)

- **Market range:** the majority of Skool groups currently price between **$19 and $299/month** — a deliberately wide range. You can go above or below it; this is just where most people sit.
- **Price by value discrepancy:** price is dictated by how much value you deliver relative to what the member gains. Example given — teaching digital-marketing tactics to agency owners doing $20,000+/month justifies a high price because of the large gap between price and the member's upside; teaching parents how to watercolor-paint with kids justifies a low price (small budget, and free alternatives exist online).
- **Reverse-engineer N from a revenue goal:** pick your goal, pick your price, then solve for the number of members (N) you need. His worked example uses a **$10,000/month** goal (stated as the most common goal new people bring): ~10 members at ~$1,000/mo, ~100 members at ~$100/mo, or ~1,000 members at ~$10/mo — all hit the same goal; the right combination depends on who you serve and the value/problem being solved. (Exact price points were garbled in the captions; see Notes.)
- **Break the goal into a daily quota:** he prefers thinking in daily adds — e.g., to reach 100 members in a month you need ~3 new members/day; spread the same 100 across a year and it drops to roughly one every other day. Then, once you hit the goal, raise the goal.
